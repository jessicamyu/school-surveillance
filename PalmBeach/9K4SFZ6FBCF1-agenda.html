FM-1 Negotiated Agreement for Financial Advisor Services
Meeting
Mar 7, 2012 - Special Meeting
Category
Consent Agenda
Type
Discussion

The District’s Financial Advisor provides advice and recommendation to the District regarding the issuance of debt, and other financial arrangements including COPS, TANS, Swaps, Leases, etc.  The services are critical to complete the pending transactions during the next 7 to 8 months.



The School District entered into a Negotiated Agreement with PFM for three years beginning September 20, 2006 through September 19, 2009 with an option to renew for two additional one year periods.  Both renewal options were approved by the School Board with the second option extending the contract through September 19, 2011.  The contract was extended for 90 days in September 2011 to allow time for a competitive selection process. 



While staff planned to move forward developing the invitation to negotiate, interest rates shifted lower again presenting the District with the opportunity to refund $50 million of COPs.  The transaction was approved by the School Board on 11/2/2011 and the completed transactions provided $2.4 million or 5.6 and 7.4% of present value savings.  Another change in market conditions allowed the District to terminate a basis swap which was previously approved by the School Board in 2010.  The transaction provided $1.8 million that will be used to help balance the FY 2013 Operating Budget.  While these transactions were of great benefit to the School District, they resulted in a delay in the release of the Invitation to Negotiate. 



Also on the agenda tonight is another refunding transaction that will produce present value savings of $800,000 to $4 million.  Another refunding required by the 2002D Swaption that must be completed prior to June 30, 2012 will be brought forward to the School Board in early May.  Finally, the 2011 TAN sale will be brought to the School Board in August and will be completed by mid-September to provide cash flow for operations this fall.



Staff has negotiated a one year contract with PFM to provide Financial Advisory Services for the next year.  This will allow the District to complete the financing transactions that will take place over the next 7 to 8 months.  The fee schedule from the past contract was compared with those from Orange, Hillsborough and Broward Schools.  A new maximum fee for each transaction was negotiated to ensure the fees proposed are competitive



Staff plans to release an Invitation to Negotiate for a new contract immediately following the TAN sale this fall. 



The Finance Committee discussed the contract at the February 22, 2012 meeting.  The Committee unanimously recommend a one (1) year negotiated contract with PFM.  The minutes from the meeting as well as the audio from the meeting are available on the Finance Committee website: http://www.palmbeachschools.org/treasury/FinanceCommittee.asp

Recommendation:

I recommend approval of a one (1) year contract with Public Financial Management, Inc. for Financial Advisor Services and authorize the Superintendent and Board Chairperson to sign all related documents. 

Contact:

Michael J. Burke, Chief Financial Officer (mike.burke@palmbeachschools.org)



Leanne Evans, Treasurer (leanne.evans@palmbeachschools.org)



 

Financial Impact:

Over the course of the year, staff estimates $39,000 will be paid to the Financial Advisor from the Treasury Department Budget.  Additional fees will be paid as part of any approved debt issue.  Each debt issue will be brought forward to the School Board for approval.

FM-2 Consider adoption of a resolution approving issuance of 2012 COPs
Meeting
Mar 7, 2012 - Special Meeting
Category
Consent Agenda
Type
Discussion

This Resolution delegates authority to the Superintendent to, with the District’s Financial Advisor, negotiate the terms of sale within the parameters set forth in the Resolution.  The Resolution also authorizes the execution and delivery of all instruments and documents necessary to refund the certificates.  Some of those parameters are:



 



Present value of the total savings to accrue to the District for each financing shall be at least 4.0 % for at least half of the par amount of the existing debt issues eligible for refunding.



 



Total principal amount shall not exceed $115,000,000.



 



Final maturity shall be no later than the original transactions.



 



Market conditions continue to change and interest rates are once again at new historic lows.  This Resolution allows for swift action to take advantage of favorable interest rates as long as the conditions above are met and market conditions are favorable.



 



The economic value to the District as a result of the refunding is the driving force behind this authorization. The 4% present value savings noted above is the minimum savings that would be acceptable.



 



The goal is to complete the refunding as a single issue; however that may not be possible.  The Resolution allows the refunding to be completed in multiple pieces, if needed, to comply with the parameters above.



 



The Finance Committee reviewed the proposed transaction at their meeting on February 22, 2012 and approved the transaction parameters as stated above. The presentation as well as the minutes and audio recording of the meeting are available on the Finance Committee website: http://www.palmbeachschools.org/treasury/FinanceCommittee.asp.

Recommendation:

I recommend the School Board adopt a Resolution authorizing issuance of 2012 COPs to refund portions of the 2002D, 2003A and 2004A COPS in an aggregate principal amount not to exceed $115 million.

Contact:

Michael J. Burke, Chief Financial Officer  (mike.burke@palmbeachschools.org)



Leanne Evans, Treasurer (leanne.evans@palmbeachschools.org)



 

Financial Impact:

Depending on interest rates and other market conditions at the time the COPs are sold, the approximate size of the transaction could range from $20 million to $115 million.  Accordingly, the net present value saving could range from approximately $800,000 to approximately $4 million.  Similarly, the costs associated with the transactions range from $137,000 to $400,000 depending on the size and number of transactions.  The projected savings are net of the costs of the transactions and they will be included in the debt issue.

On August 1, 2011, the Department of Charter Schools received an application from Pivot Charter School to open a charter school in the School District of Palm Beach County in August, 2012.



There were no other Pivot Charter School applications filed with the Department of Education.



Additionally, Pivot Charter School has two schools currently in operation; one school (grades 6-12) opened SY2011-2012 in Lee County.  Another opened FY2011-2012 (grades 6-12) in Hillsborough County.  There is no student performance data available.



According to Pivot Charter School's application submitted to the School District of Palm Beach County, the mission is, "To provide a rigorous standards-based, individualized online curriculum to students in grades 6-12, within a site-based instructional model is a unique "learning studio" environment."



The District's charter application process was explained to applicants during the New Applicants' Workshop held on May 27, 2011.  The applicant was invited and did not attend the workshop.



A review of the application was conducted using the required Florida Charter School Application Evaluation Instrument.



Projected Enrollment



First Year               6-12                  300 Projected Enrollment



Second Year          6-12                  700 Projected Enrollment



Third Year              6-12                  1,200 Projected Enrollment



Fourth Year            6-12                  1,600 Projected Enrollment



Fifth Year               6-12                  2,000 Projected Enrollment



Educational Program Design



The Educational Program Design at Pivot Charter School is Blended Learning.  Blended Learning combines traditional classroom instruction with online instruction.  This Educational Program Design is similar to two charter schools in the School District of Palm Beach County.



Location



Applicant has identified the intersection of I-95 and Lake Worth Road (Highway 802).  As per the applicant group, this area has the lowest density of competitive charter schools in the county, making it a prime area for supporting the District's growth needs and Pivot Charter School's enrollment goals.



Applicant's Founding Governing Board



One of the three Founding Board members is local, one resides in Tampa, Florida; it is not stated in the application the residence of the third member and the experience of the members includes economics, consulting for child welfare entities and legal.



Ratings of Pivot Charter School's Application



Designated District staff reviewed the 19 sections of Pivot Charter School's application.  There were two evaluations of this application.  After the first evaluation, Pivot Charter School had six of 19 sections that received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard," three sections that received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard," and one section that received a rating of "Not Applicable."



Listed below are the six sections that received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 5 - Assessment:  The school's educational goals and objectives were unclear.




  • Section 6 - Exceptional Students:  The specified policies and procedures used to develop and identify students with exceptional needs were unclear.  Also, there appears to be confusion between 504 Plans and an IEP.




  • Section 7 - English Language Learners:  The application referred to incorrect testing instruments and the applicant needs to refer to the ESOL Procedures Manual.




  • Section 15 - Transportation:  The School District of Palm Beach County no longer provides transportation for charter schools.




  • Section 18 - Financial Management and Oversight:  It was not described in detail how the required insurance coverage for the school will be obtained.




  • Section 19 - Action Plan:  There was no plan in place for unanticipated events.


Listed below are the three sections that received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard" with the reason for each rating.







  • Section 3 - Educational Program Design:  It was not clear if Pivot was proposing a Blended Learning or Virtual Instructional Model.  Additionally, the applicant did not address whether or not the submitted application was a replicate of an existing school design.




  • Section 10 - Management:  The management structure of the school was unclear.  Additionally, the staffing plan outlined on pages 149 and 150 was not aligned  with the projected student enrollment.




  • Section 17 - Budget:  The responses lacked meaningful detail and the budgetary projections are not consistent within the application.  Additionally, the applicant must adjust budget and application narrative to ensure the school has accounted for all necessary costs and is  not operating at a deficit.


Listed below is the section that received a rating of "Not Applicable" and the reason for the rating.







  • Section 11 - Education Service Provider:  The applicant does not intend to enter into a contract with an Education Service Provider.


A letter and 19 sections were emailed to the applicant group on January 18, 2012, and sent certified mail on January 18, 2012, asking for additional information in order to meet the standards.  (See Exhibit A - First Review)



Pivot Charter School then submitted additional information that cured the concerns that were outlined in Section 6 - Exceptional Students; Section 7 - English Language Learners; Section 10 - Management; Section 15 - Transportation; Section 18 - Financial Management and Oversight; and Section 19 - Action Plan.  (See Exhibit B - Second Review)



After the second review, two sections below still received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 3 - Educational Program Design:  The applicant did not address whether or not the submitted application was a replicate of an existing school design.




  • Section 5 - Assessment:  The school's educational goals and objectives were unclear.


After the second review, one section below still received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard."







  • Section 17 - Budget:  Many of the questions/concerns of the first review were unanswered by the applicant.  The adjustments made to the budget did not satisfy the requirements to operate a fiscally sound charter school and avoid operating in a deficit.


The Overall Assessment Summary/Checklist indicates the ratings for each of the 19 sections.  (See Exhibit C - Overall Assessment)



The charter school applicant granted the District an extension of time to vote upon its application.  (See Exhibit D - Extension Letter)

Pursuant to School Board Policy 8.122 (3), district instructional materials adoption committees were convened during 2011-2012 to evaluate and recommend new materials for adoption in the area of social studies, grades K-12.  Committee membership included: subject area classroom teachers and coaches, district level social studies curriculum administrators, and lay citizens.  Committees reviewed all instructional materials programs submitted for state adoption and some additional submissions that were considered appropriate for committee review.  Titles recommended for district adoption are contained in Attachment A.





The current-adopted texts are seven years old and do not comply with the new standards (NGSSS adopted 2008), nor do they address the increasing mandates of social studies instruction such as the Common Core State Standard Initiative (CCSSI), state end-of-course examinations, or the Sandra Day O'Connor Civics Education Act.  Additionally, Florida Statute 1003.42 mandates instruction in certain areas of history and social studies such as African and African-American Studies, Holocaust Studies, Hispanic, Gender, and Women's Studies; current texts do not support these areas.  





Publishers have realized the major impact of social studies on the 21st century learner and have provided clear guidelines for the teacher.  New texts include the Next Generation Sunshine State Standards, Common Core Standards, and civics instruction guidelines.  These new texts will also combine English Language Learner and Exceptional Student Education strategies.  The School District of Palm Beach County has an increasing number of dual language schools, and the next instructional materials adoption series will address the strategies necessary to educate students within these programs.





The recommended instructional materials programs will be provided in both print and/or online digital formats.  Student editions in the core courses will be available to students via their Edline accounts.  Teacher materials and resources will also be available on Learning Village.  The elementary and middle school recommended instructional materials are available in Spanish for dual language schools.





The 2012-2013 Instructional Materials Cost Analysis (Attachment B) represents estimated costs for implementation of new adoptions.  Funding is provided from carry-over balance from the 2011-2012 State Categorical Allocation for Instructional Materials, the projected 2012-2013 State Categorical Allocation, and projected collections for lost/damaged materials from FY2012.  The projected 2012-2013 State Categorical Allocation figure is an estimation and actual funding is subject to legislative appropriation.





The Instructional Materials Department has worked closely with the Purchasing Department to obtain bids from the various publishers.  This will secure the best possible price to help ensure a complete K-12 social studies implementation.





Staff development for instructional materials will be provided at no cost to the District by publishers and will be focused on the appropriate use of materials to implement the Next Generation Sunshine State Standards.  





 

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the 2012-2013 Instructional Materials Program budget of $10,246,000 and the list of instructional materials for the District adoption periods indicated.

Florida Charter School Application Evaluation Instrument



 



Each section presents criteria for a response that meets the standard and these criteria should guide the overall rating for the section.  The Strengths and Weaknesses boxes provide space to identify data and other evidence that supports the rating.  The rationale for each rating is important, especially if some of the data or evidence does not fit neatly into the criteria provided.



 



The following definitions should guide the ratings:



 



Meets the Standard:                                           The response reflects a thorough understanding of key issues and demonstrates capacity to open and operate a quality charter school.  It addresses the topic with specific and accurate information that shows thorough preparation and presents a clear, realistic picture of how the school expects to operate.



 



Partially Meets the Standard:                         The response addresses most of the criteria, but the responses lack meaningful detail and require important additional information.



 



Does Not Meet the Standard:                           The response lacks meaningful detail; demonstrates lack of preparation; or otherwise raises substantial concerns about the applicant’s understanding of the issue in concept and/or ability to meet the requirement in practice
