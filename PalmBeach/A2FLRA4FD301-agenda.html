PM1 Adult Education Center Modernization

Recommended Action
I recommend the Board approve the modernization project at the Adult Education Center at 2161 North Military Trail in West Palm Beach.

DESCRIPTION:

Modernize the Adult Education Center with traditional construction using the Design-Build method of contracting.  Raze the existing buildings and provide a new compact, efficient facility.

 

JUSTIFICATION: 

Adult Education sought to replace eight wooden portables with modular concrete units due to their condition.  On March 12, 2014, on Board Agenda Item PM3, the Board approved up to $1.3M to move the units, and increase drainage and parking as space allowed.  After further consideration, due to the extreme age of the facility (first occupied in 1941), staff recommends modernization of the facility would be more prudent should funds be available.

 

A Condition Assessment was therefore performed which revealed the degraded and fragile state of the facility and the cost of continuing operation. The results were reported to the Board within the May 7, 2015 CORC report, in which CORC supported replacing the facility.

 

On June 17, 2015, on Board Agenda item PM5, the Board approved the Castaldi Analysis for this facility, the summary of which was that the permanent buildings were qualified to be razed.  The Analysis was subsequently approved by the Department of Education on July 20, 2015 (attached).  On September 18, 2015 the Florida Department of State indicated that the facility is not of historic importance (attached).

 

Concepts to reduce cost per square foot will be included in this project such as reducing built-in cabinetry, reviewing the District Design Criteria and Master Specifications and not requiring formal certification by a green building rating system.   It is expected that the competitive Design-Build solicitation process will also reduce building costs.

 

The current Spending Plan formulated by the Adult & Community Education Department supports the funding of this project (attached).  The estimated cost of the project is based on the recommended Educational Specification for this facility, which is also on this School Board Agenda under Agenda Item PIR 1.

 

Construction could commence in the fall of 2016 and be completed in a year or less.

I recommend the Board approve the Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with the School Superintendents Association for Superintendent Leadership for PD Redesign and authorize the Superintendent to sign all required documents pertaining to this MOU.

DESCRIPTION:

The purpose of this MOU is to facilitate an initiative supported by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation.  The Foundation believes, as does the American Association of School Administrators (AASA), that the leadership of the superintendent is critical in building structures for effective professional development systems and processes that optimize existing resources (people, money, and time).  AASA will support the Superintendent and his staff to ensure that the professional development (PD) systems created are sustainable, teacher-supported, and teacher-led.

 

The goals of AASA's iPD efforts include challenging the Superintendent to candidly analyze his PD systems and collaborate with teachers and other staff to implement supportive policies and to create the conditions necessary for real, effective, lasting change.

 

AASA will work with the School District on activities to assess professional development systems and structures in the District with a focus on building enabling conditions for sustainability of redesigned teacher professional development systems.  Details of the work is outlined in the attached MOU.

DESCRIPTION:

About ERS

Education Resource Strategies (ERS) is a non-profit organization dedicated to transforming how urban school systems organize resources—people, time, and money—so that every school succeeds for every student. Since 2004, ERS has worked hand-in-hand with the leaders of over 30 school systems and several states on topics such as funding equity, budget development, strategic school design, central office support, and teacher compensation and career path. 

 

Project Overview

Over a ten-month period, ERS will work with SDPBC leadership to understand the big issues facing the district. That work began with the new School System 20/20 diagnostic.  System 20/20 is a vision for school system success across seven transformational areas—standards and instruction, teaching, school design, leadership, school support, funding, and partners—designed to help district leaders identify and track changes to system conditions and resource use that lead to improved student achievement. The assessment includes a diagnostic on system conditions, resource use and student outcomes, including both qualitative and quantitative measures generated through interviews, ERS analysis of district data and a survey of principals. System 20/20 is designed to be conducted over a 1-2 month period, with a focus on rapid diagnosis of critical issues that could lead to near-term action, while also laying the groundwork for deeper dives in the highest-priority and highest-potential areas for impact.

 

The second phase of work will be the creation of a strategic resource map which gives the district a picture of how well their resources are aligned with their strategic vision—and where the disconnects are. Whereas most organizations allocate costs sufficiently to create a school-level dollar per pupil metric, the ERS Resource Map takes allocation a step deeper, examining priority investments in specific students. ERS looks not only at the amount that is spent ($ per pupil) but also at how the resources are allocated (time in subjects, group sizes).  The information and context it provides can be invaluable in helping identify the most leveraged opportunities for realigning investments to support sustainable improvements in student performance. ERS will then work with district leadership to create a long-term resource and strategy alignment plan that builds on changes identified for School Year 2016 - 2017.

 

Team

The team dedicated to the District will be led by Karen Hawley Miles, president and executive director of Education Resource Strategies, Inc. She has worked intensively with school systems nationwide to analyze and improve their funding systems, school-level resource use, and human capital and professional development systems.  Karen has taught school leaders at Harvard University, in school districts, and for New Leaders for New Schools. She has authored numerous articles and co-authored The Strategic School: Making the Most of People, Time and Money with Stephen Frank. She serves as a Senior Advisor to the Aspen Institute Education and Society Program, and she served as a Commissioner on the Equity and Excellence Commission for the U.S. Department of Education.  Prior to her work at ERS, she worked at Bain & Company as a strategy and management consultant for hospitals and corporations. She has a B.A. in Economics and Political Science from Yale University and a Doctorate in Education from Harvard University, specializing in school organization, change, and finance.

 

Funding

Outside funding support is covering a portion of the cost.  ERS provided $30,000 in national matching funds and the Florida Association of District School Superintendents (FADSS) is expected to award a $50,000 grant which will be paid directly to ERS, reducing the net District cost to $520,000.  The District will utilize savings generated through the FY16 administrative hiring hold to fund this project.

Category

Recommended Action
I recommend the School Board receive and file the Internal Audit Report - Cash Handling Procedures at 14 Selected Schools (Report No. 2015-12).

DESCRIPTION:

During March 2015, the Office of Inspector General (OIG) performed unannounced cash counts and audited the cash handling procedures for fourteen (14) selected schools.  The primary objectives of the audit were to:  (1) determine if the amount of money to be deposited into the bank agreed with the collection records; (2) examine the school-based cash handling procedures and assess the adequacy of control procedures implemented; and, (3) determine the extent of compliance with procedures at the selected schools.

 

Three (3) of the fourteen (14) schools had no significant noncompliance.  The remaining eleven (11) schools demonstrated the following areas of noncompliance.

 

1.     Monies were missing at two (2) schools. After an expanded review, conclusions were forwarded to School Police and Office of Professional Standards (OPS) for further action.

 

2.     School treasurers at two (2) of the schools had full access to the Drop safe. The Internal Accounts Manual, Chapter 7, permits only the Principal to have the complete combination to a Drop-safe.  

 

3.     Noncompliance with Drop-safe Log Procedures.  Findings indicated:  (a) one (1) staff person signed/initialed another staff person’s name on the logs which was forwarded to OPS for further action; (b) missing Drop-safe Log pages at two (2) schools; (c) incomplete Drop-safe required information at five (5) schools and Official Receipts were not timely provided to the remitters to verify the accuracy of the deposits;  (d) five (5) schools failed to have the Document Custodian Assignment Register, or their Registers were not approved by the Principal; and (e) eleven (11) schools did not complete or maintain the Prenumbered Document Inventory Register to document the perpetual inventory and periodic inventory check of the Drop-safe Log.

 

4.     Collections not properly documented.  (a) At two (2) schools, all the yellow copies of Monies Collected Reports (MCRs) were not retained.  (b) At one (1) school, some MCRS were not completed with all the needed information.  (c)  On comparing the money collections in the drop-safe with Drop-safe Log records, OIG found some collections were not recorded on the Drop-safe Logs by the sponsors at two (2) schools and some collections did not have the required MCRs at two (2) schools.

 

5.     Untimely deposit on monies.       Four (4) schools failed to timely deposit monies into the banks, with delays ranging from three (3) to sixty-five (65) days.

 

The Special Review of Internal Audit Report-Cash Handling Procedures at 14 Selected Schools, was presented to, and accepted by, the Audit Committee during the September 17, 2015 meeting.

State Board of Education Rule 6A-5.071 states that:

  • Each District School Board shall develop and maintain a Master Inservice Plan for all district employees based on state adopted standards for high quality professional development as required under Section 1012.98, F.S.

The Plan must contain all training components leading to certification. Instructional personnel must obtain 120 inservice points during their 5-year validity period. Non-Instructional personnel, affiliated with certain employee groups, use inservice points toward incentive programs and to verify completion of required training(s).
