2012 Do The Write Thing Challenge
Meeting
Jun 20, 2012 - School Board Meeting
Category

The National Campaign to Stop Violence has developed a school program that gives middle school students an opportunity to examine the impact of violence on their lives and communicate that impact in writing. Students are asked what they can do to stop violence in the Do the Write Thing Challenge. The program involves a classroom discussion and a written "Challenge" to answer three questions through an essay, poem or song:
How has youth violence affected my life?
What are the causes of youth violence?
What can I do to reduce youth violence?
The goal of the program is to reduce violence in homes, schools and neighborhoods by encouraging students to make a personal commitment to do something about this problem.



Twenty-six district middle schools submitted writings. This year over 19,659 students from 36 schools submitted essays. Twenty-one schools were eligible to submit their top 10 essays.
All of the entries are read by multiple panels of volunteer judges who represent a cross section of Palm Beach County educators, parents, law enforcement and judicial leaders.



More than 600 people, including the 210 student finalists, their teachers, principals and families attended a luncheon at the Kravis Center for the Performing Arts in May. The six student finalists were honored and given cash prizes, and brief quotes from their writings. Two were selected as the Palm Beach County Ambassadors. The students are:

Throughout this school year a select group of students at H. L. Watkins Middle School have had unprecedented access and exposure to healthcare careers and patient care. Last year, the leadership of the T. Leroy Jefferson Medical Society, Good Samaritan Medical Center, Wells Fargo Bank, and H.L. Watkins Middle School met to formulate an innovative and unique Externship Program for a group of Health Career Academy students: Jessica Anderson, Zachary Franz, Breana Gadson, Mae Ghannouma, Ta'Nija Lark, Thuong Luu, Rebecca Murillo, Juna Purifoy and Vanessa Torres.



Throughout the school year, these 8th grade students had in-depth exposure to and visited many of the departments within Good Samaritan Medical Center. In the Human Resources Department, the students learned the importance of and underwent patient confidentiality training. In the Operating Room, they learned about sterile techniques; participated in the simulation of a patient being put to sleep and intubated; and performed robotic surgery on an artificial knee joint. In the Emergency Room and Cardiovascular Catheterization Lab, they learned about a variety of medical procedures from cardiac stress tests, to endovascular abdominal aortic aneurysm repair. On the Obstetrical Unit, the students had an in-depth review of the birth process; participated in mock deliveries (with full instrumentation) and newborn resuscitation; participated in in-depth discussions about maternal-fetal health and pathophysiology; and toured the newborn nursery. Through these experiences, the students gained perspectives to careers in medicine unrivaled to anywhere in our county.



In May, the students returned to the Good Samaritan Medical Center to share with the Executive Leadership, West Palm Beach Mayor Jeri Muoio, Wells Fargo Bank, and the T. Leroy Jefferson Medical Society Membership, the benefits of the Externship Program and what they had learned. From storyboards, DVDs, to poetry and dramatic readings, the students voiced their gratitude and appreciation for their experiences. According to Watkins Choice Academy Director Dr. Nathan Ringel, "For most, it was a pivotal event that has altered the trajectory of their career aspirations and scholarly pursuits."  Each of the students,  Dr. Ringel and Watkins Principal Dr. Glenda Sheffield were presented with personalized stethoscopes and Certificates of Completion. The Graduation ceremony was presided over by Mark Nosacka, C.E.O. Good Samaritan Medical Center, Dr. Roger L. Duncan, III, Mentorship and Community Outreach Chair, T. Leroy Jefferson Medical Society, and William Bourbeau, Wells Fargo Bank. Dr. Sheffield remarked, "We look forward to the program's continued growth and enhancement."

Nine (9) students have been residentially placed by the Agency for Persons with Disabilities (APD).  APD pays the cost of the residential placement.  The district is required to pay only the educational costs.  APD places students in residential facilities when it is determined that the least restrictive environment is not available within the community, due to serious behavioral issues in the home.  This decision is based by a multidisciplinary committee which determines that the students' behaviors at home and in the community are so severe as to render the students, the families, and the community at risk. 



Due to the severity of these students' needs, IEP teams have determined that the students could be educated in the facility chosen by APD.  These students require extensive educational and behavioral services.



These students have been appropriately identified as students with disabilities by the School District of Palm Beach County in compliance with state and local policies and regulations.  Prior to entering the residential facility, these students were attending community middle, high and/or center schools.



The district will pay the educational costs for those days the students are physically present and in attendance at the residential facility. The individual cost for the educational services is $18,136.24 per student.  The total cost to the school district is $163,226.16.  The total FTE to be earned by these students during the FY12 school year is $163,226.16.     



These students will remain in the facility until such time an APT team, with school district participation determines that these students are stabilized to the point where the students are no longer a danger to self or others.



This cooperative agreement is in compliance with School Board Rule 6A.6.0361, "Special Programs Contractual Agreements with Non-Public Schools".

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the cooperative agreement with Carlton Palms Educational Center in the amount of $163,226.16 from July 1, 2012 through June 30, 2013, and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all related documents.

The School Board of Seminole County will act as the lead district in a consortium of school districts and will serve as data manager and processor for Palm Beach County School District for the Medicaid School District Administrative Claiming (SDAC).



The School District of Palm Beach County, using the Seminole County School District data report, will determine and submit the Administrative Claim to Medicaid for reimbursement.



The School District of Palm Beach County has been participating in Medicaid School District Administrative Claiming (SDAC) since 1998 when the legislation was passed.



In 2002, we joined with other Florida School Districts in the Seminole County Data Management Partnership (SCDMP).  This process allows school districts to submit claims themselves without paying a private consultant.



Prior to 2002, the School District was paying a private consultant $146,000 annually for SDAC services.  Current cost to perform the same function with SCDMP is approximately $17,244 annually which amounts to a savings of  $128,756 annually.



Reimbursement amounts have increased annually from approximately $1.5 million in 1998 to receiving $ 5.1 million in 2011.

The purpose of this cooperative agreement is to provide educational programs to Part C (birth to three years) Exceptional Student Education students transitioning to Part B (ages three to five) under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act (IDEA). Specific to this cooperative agreement, Part C refers to the section of IDEA that provides services to children ages birth to three years of age. Part B refers to the section of IDEA that provides services to identified children with disabilities ages three to five years of age.



1.  Interagency Transition Agreement with Easter Seals Florida, Inc. - Under this agreement Easter Seals Florida, Inc.  (Treasure Coast Early Steps) is to collaborate and facilitate the transition of children eligible for IDEA Part C services to appropriate Part B IDEA services for three year old children throughout the year.  This agreement is a State initiative of the Florida Diagnostic and Learning Resources System (FDLRS). There is no financial impact to the district for these services.



2.  Cooperative Agreement with Easter Seals Florida, Inc. - Under this Agreement Easter Seals Florida, Inc. (Treasure Coast Early Steps) will provide educational and related services to students with disabilities in Part C programs upon their third birthday if the third birthday falls after May 1 of the school year.  By doing so, the students receive a seamless transition to Part B programs.  Eligible students will receive services from May 1 through the end of the school year and during an extended school year period.  The District will ensure that students served through the agency's personnel have been placed according to the District's Special Programs and Procedures document and student progress will be documented on the Individual Education Plan (IEP). The financial impact to the district for these services is projected at $75,000.



The cooperative agreement is in compliance with the State Board Rule 6A.6-03026, "Special Programs for Prekindergarten Children with Disabilities."

The following four (4) items are all ESE Entitlement Grants from the Department of Education, offered to the School District for specific services for the support of students with disabilities identified under the Individual  with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).  Each grant specifies to whom the services are delivered and how funding must be expended.  Grant dollars change from year to year, as well as specifications within the grants.  If the grant application is not written, submitted and accepted, the funds are forfeited.



We would like to make the Board aware of the goals, services, and deliverables of the following four grants.  The attached data panel shows the award amount for each grant for the past five years. 



IDEA:  Individual  with Disabilities Education Act: Entitlement and IDEA Pre-K Entitlement



Amount awarded based on a Base State Allocation with additional funding given for county-wide-size and population, number of free-reduced lunch students (survey 2), and number of private school students.



Purpose is to identify students with disabilities and provide for individualized academic and support services such as: specified learning plans, additional teacher support, additional hardware/software, paraprofessionals, interpreters, materials, supplies, substitute teachers, evaluations, counseling, occupational and physical therapy, psychological services, audiology and other medically related services.



Required to 'set-aside' a portion of funding to non-public school students (4%) $ 1,503,934; pre-school students (1%) $16,953, and projected charter school students (formula) $2,500,000.



2. SEDNET:  Students with Emotional/Behavior Disabilities Network



Amount awarded based on State discretionary funding within the IDEA Act.



Purpose is to promote a high quality system of care for children who have severe emotional behavioral disturbance, access to mental health treatment and residential services when needed; link community services to families, provide training;  coordinates implementation of school-based mental health services for students.  This includes the staffing of a Project Coordinator, substitute teachers, extra duty days, and travel for personnel.



3. FIN:   Florida Inclusion Network



Amount awarded based upon State discretionary funding within the IDEA Act.



Purpose is to train teachers in effective instructional inclusive practices for ESE students and increase the number of schools that utilize the model.  This includes the staffing of a project coordinator and resource teacher.



4. FDLRS:  Florida Diagnostic and Learning Resources System



Amount awarded based upon State discretionary funding within the IDEA Act.



Purpose is to provide Child Find Services (identification and evaluation of youth) Human Resource Development and effective implementation of IDEA as well as parent services and technology for students.



Complete copies of each of the above grants will be available in the Board Office for your viewing. 
These grants are written and required to be submitted to FDOE by June 30, 2012. 
They can be amended throughout the year due to need.

Florida Virtual School (FLVS) is an established leader in developing and providing virtual K-12 education solutions to Florida students, using a nationally recognized e-learning model.



In response to the growth in online education, Florida Virtual School developed the FLVS franchise program. As a franchise, Districts take advantage of the courses and learning management system developed by Florida Virtual School and implements the successful instructional model already in use, using local educators.



The franchise is implemented through the district's virtual school, Palm Beach Virtual. Palm Beach Virtual operates as a traditional full time school for students in grades 6-12 seeking a standard Palm Beach County diploma. Additionally Palm Beach Virtual is used for part time students in-county who need flexibility in their scheduling to take courses for graduation, acceleration, grade forgiveness or to raise their GPA.



The renewal of the FLVS franchise for FY13 will allow the District to continue the franchise and broaden the opportunities for students taking virtual education courses through Palm Beach Virtual.

Approve the contract which will allow KEV Group, Inc., to continue providing the School District of Palm Beach County the School Cash Online website application; a program that accepts online payments from parents and the public.



 



By Board action on February 16, 2011 a contract was entered into with KEV Group, Inc. to continue providing the School District of Palm Beach County the School Cash Online website application.



The software makes it possible for parents and employees to pay for lunches, student activities and other items with a credit card or debit card and view their payment history, including meal details, online.  Payments can be made from any location, 24 hours a day, seven days a week during the school year, using Master Card, VISA, or debit card.



 



The credit card processing, which averages 1.5% of purchases, is handled by Bank of America through a separate contract via the State of Florida.



 



The software interfaces with School Cash Accounting, an accounting program used by the school treasurers to manage internal accounts, eliminating data entry at the schools.



 



The software is used by all our schools and eight departments at this time, including Teacher Certification, Diversity in Business Practices, and After School Programming.  Staff expects the volumes to continue to grow as departments become more familiar with the program and parental acceptance increases.



 



The District pays a portion of the software costs and a portion is assessed to anyone using the system.  Parents and staff are charged $1.00 per month, per student or employee, for each month when an online payment is made.  No fee is assessed if a parent uses the system only to monitor their child's school food service account.  This results in approximately $140,000 that the parents pay to the vendor.



 



The Audit Committee has repeatedly recognized the advantages of using credit cards with School Cash Online system:  1.) Payment via credit cards relieves children from carrying cash; teachers and bookkeepers aren't counting as much cash; and parents don't have to worry about providing lunch money every day - they simply load money on their child's account.  2.) The interface with the accounting system automatically updates the child's account, with no action required by the teacher or bookkeeper once the payment is set up.  3.) Finally, parents can check their child's account to ensure payment was recorded completely and see how money was spent, including what their child chose to eat at lunch each day.



 



The Director of Diversity In Business Practices has confirmed that at this time, there are no M/WBE vendors registered with the District that provide this service.
