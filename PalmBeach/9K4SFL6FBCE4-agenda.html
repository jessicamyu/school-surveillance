PS1 Ongoing School Support for Reading Workshop Schools Contract
Meeting
May 26, 2010 - Spec Mtgs and Workshops re Operations
Category
Consent
Type
Action (Consent)

The purpose of this contract is to support ongoing professional development on Reading Workshop for approximately 80 teachers who will instruct over 1,000 kindergarten through second grade students in four Title I schools.   These four schools were selected based on evidence that they were implementing the Lucy Calkins Units of Study Writing Project with fidelity.  The Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund is currently providing funding for six other Title I schools to receive this ongoing professional development led by a staff developer from Columbia University's Teachers College. 



The workshop model is a daily, structured time that involves explicit instruction and both guided and independent practice.  Students are encouraged to be risk takers as they learn how reading and writing are connected by conversing, thinking and reading about personal and literature-related experiences. Students are given the tools to select and comprehend well-crafted narrative and informational texts. 



The workshop model is the structure that New York City schools have implemented to successfully turn around high needs schools.  New York City Department of Education research shows that from 2002 to 2008, the achievement gap for proficiency on the state-wide English Language Arts test between black and white fourth grade students closed 6.3 points.  The achievement gap between Hispanic and white fourth grade students closed 6.2 points.  Additional research shows that from 2006 to 2008, 13 out of 15 schools, specifically implementing the Lucy Calkins Units of Study writing curriculum, had a 3%-20% increase in student performance on this same assessment. 



A staff developer from Columbia University's Teachers College will visit the four schools, three times each, throughout the year to build capacity by  providing extensive on-site professional development and support to meet the school's needs. The staff developers will deliver professional development that includes lab-sites, discussion groups, lesson modeling and practical hands-on application. Teachers will work alongside the staff developer to develop curriculum calendars, analyze assessments, and discuss strategies to meet the needs of all their students. 



Additionally, the staff developers will be available to these teachers throughout the school year to troubleshoot and answer questions as they arise.  They will provide teachers with the support they need to implement strategy-based curriculum with fidelity. The overall goal is for teachers to sharpen their ability to provide high quality literacy instruction and develop the skills and confidence to meet the needs of every student in their class for years to come.   This along with the professional development their administration will receive will support the building of capacity in these schools.



The staff developers from Columbia University's Teachers College are leaders in the field of literacy. They provide professional development to schools throughout New York City and across the country in schools with similar demographics. Many of them are authors of high-quality literacy books. 



This contract will provide the reading professional development to accompany the writing professional development currently being offered at each school site by district staff members funded by the Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund.  This professional development will be funded by FDOE waived Title 1 ARRA funds targeted for Supplemental Education Services (SES) from the original application. These were redirected to support this project.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the board approve this contract for the staff developer for ongoing school support for schools implementing Reading Workshop in the amount of $77,400 and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents related to the contract which are required for implementation from August 2010 through May 2011.

A Technology Plan for the years 2010 – 2013 was developed to replace the current plan which will expire on June 30, 2010. The new plan will be submitted to the Florida Department of Education (FLDOE) by June 30, 2010. A FLDOE approved Technology Plan is essential for receiving Title 1 grants and E-Rate funds Universal Service Administrative Company.



The Technology Plan was developed with input from District Stakeholders including teachers, students, parents, community, technology specialists, and other end users. A comprehensive requirements gathering process was used to collect information on the current state of technology, technology  requirements for successful instruction, training, support, and efficient business processes.  Focus group sessions were held in the District’s four areas, providing all Stakeholders an opportunity to contribute to the technology vision.



The District’s technology objectives and priorities were derived from these findings, and the Technology Plan was developed to address the objectives and priorities. The Technology Plan outlines a three year strategy for instructional technologies, the seamless integration of technology into the classroom, and the development of a robust Infrastructure required that is required to support the growing needs.  The Plan defines training and support process improvements that will provide optimum and timely support to schools and end users.  The Plan also examines the strategy for replacing obsolete systems that are cost and resource intensive to maintain, as well as the integration of systems and applications to a common platform. The District has adopted governance and best practices that will be further infused into all aspects of technology including planning, procurement, project execution, and change management.



The Technology Plan lays the foundation for the District’s technology initiatives for the next three years. Technology projects and initiatives will be selected and implemented to address the priorities and objectives. Furthermore, criteria will be developed to ensure that initiatives are meeting the defined targets.

Please note the Technology Plan is pending Board and FLDOE approval.

The purpose of this contract is to provide staff developers from Columbia University's Teachers College who will provide professional development at the Homegrown Summer Reading Institute for teachers implementing Reading Workshop. The Institute will focus on improving the quality of teaching and student learning for approximately 250 teachers in 11 high needs schools.  The Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund is currently providing funding for six Title I schools to receive ongoing professional development on the workshop model led by a staff developer from Columbia University's Teachers College. 



The workshop model is the structure that New York City schools have implemented to successfully turn around high needs schools.  New York City Department of Education research shows that from 2002 to 2008, the achievement gap for proficiency on the state-wide English Language Arts test between black and white fourth grade students closed 6.3 points.  The achievement gap between Hispanic and white fourth grade students closed 6.2 points.  Additional research shows that from 2006 to 2008, 13 out of 15 schools, specifically implementing the Lucy Calkins Units of Study writing curriculum, had a 3%-20% increase in student performance on this same assessment. 



During this institute, teachers will learn about the structures, methods and expectations for the Reading Workshop model. Additionally, they will learn about the reading curriculum, effective strategy-based instruction as well as utilizing assessment as a means of differentiation and curriculum development.  The academic needs of students from these schools make this professional development critical.  The overall goal is for teachers to sharpen their ability to provide high quality literacy instruction for their students now and in the future.



It is cost effective to bring the staff developer to Palm Beach County.  If the district were to provide funds to send these teachers to an institute in New York City, the cost would be over $375,000.  This is a net savings of over $300,000.



The staff developers from Columbia University's Teachers College are leaders in the field of literacy. They provide professional development to schools throughout New York City and across the country. Many of them are authors of high-quality literacy books. During the two days, the staff developers will be delivering professional development that includes discussion groups and practical hands-on application.



This contract will provide the reading professional development to accompany the writing professional development currently being led at school sites by either staff developers from Columbia University's Teachers College or district staff members funded by the Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund.  This professional development will be funded by FDOE waived Title 1 ARRA funds targeted for Supplemental Education Services (SES) from the original application. These were redirected to support this project.
