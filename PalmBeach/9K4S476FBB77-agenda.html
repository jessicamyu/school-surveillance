Adolescent alcohol, tobacco and other drug (ATOD) use negatively impacts students' academic performance. The academic consequences of ATOD use, which include absenteeism, lower grades, disruptive behaviors, suspensions and below grade-level test scores, pose a significant and severe problem for the entire school community.  The presence of drugs in school is detrimental to the entire student body, increasing the risk that students will use drugs.  Families and communities are devastated by alcohol and other drug abuse.  Because there is such a strong link between ATOD prevention and school performance, schools need to help students prevent ATOD use to give students the best chance to perform well.  Each year the Prevention Center in the Department of Safe Schools coordinates the Red Ribbon Week campaign in our schools.  Red Ribbon Week provides us with an opportunity to renew our commitment to a healthy and drug-free lifestyle, and to show our dedication to education and prevention efforts in our schools and communities.



During the campaign the Prevention Center hosted a poster and an essay writing contest. Many students participated in these worthwhile contests.

Pursuant to F. S. 1002.33 (5) (c), "An applicant may appeal any denial of that applicant's application or failure to act on an application to the State Board of Education no later than 30 calendar days, after receipt of the sponsor's decision or failure to act and shall notify the sponsor of its appeal..."

The School District has partnered with the South Florida Water Management District (SFWMD) on endeavors over the years.

This is a request to amend the current agreement between the School District of Palm Beach County and Alice R. Hattendorf from $9,906.80 to $30,202.80. an increase of $20,296.00. The reason for this request is an increase in the scope of her responsibilities; thus increasing the number of hours from 376 hours to 1096, an increase of 720 hours; plus an increase from $300.00 for in-county and out-of-county travel to $2,200.00, an increase of $1,900.00. As a part of this grant, the funder requires attendance at grant-related, out-of-county conferences.



The contract is funded entirely by a U.S. Department of Education Grant to Reduce Alcohol Abuse, which was approved by the School Board on February 20, 2008. Per the direction of the grantor, two Program Coordinators are funded to provide direct services to the schools as they implement a multitude of alcohol prevention activities. This consultant will serve as one of these Program Coordinators.
The consultant will coordinate the schools' efforts to create a youth-led anti-alcohol social marketing campaign and collaborate with the community to change attitudes and behaviors around teen alcohol use. She will spearhead the development of both a Youth Leadership Council and Strategic Planning Councils at the schools.  These activities will ensure success of the grant-defined goals to:



o Increase the number of youth who believe that alcohol is harmful to their health;
o Increase the number of youth who disapprove in alcohol use; and
o Decrease the number of youth who binge drink.
" The grant addresses:
o School Board Goal #3 - SCHOOL SAFETY AND ENVIRONMENT - Provide safe and nurturing school environments that are free of drugs, alcohol, firearms, and harassment, and where standards of appropriate and ethical behavior are upheld. 
o Key Results #8 - DROPOUT AND GRADUATION RATES - All schools will decrease dropout rates and increase graduation rates for students of each racial/ethnic group.
o Key Results #9 - SUSPENSIONS - All schools will reduce suspensions and eliminate disproportionate suspension rates among student groups. 
Research shows the value of using a strategic planning committee to guide the schools' prevention efforts, as well as employing a social marketing campaign to impact youth and community attitudes regarding teens' alcohol use.
o Effective Prevention Programs should include multiple components and affect multiple settings to address a wide range of risk and protective factors of the target problem. (Nation, M., et. al. (2003). What works in prevention: Principles of Effective Prevention Programs. American Psychologist, 58, 449-456.)
o "Social marketing utilizes distinctive techniques to bolster the effectiveness of conventional health education and prevention programs. Its strategies can popularize positive ideas and attitudes and encourage favorable changes in social values and individual behavior. Social marketing examines the numerous and often subtle pressures to which students respond. Social marketing can best facilitate change as part of a strategic plan. Experience has shown that simply imparting knowledge about alcohol and other drugs and the risks associated with their use is not likely to lead to a change in either individual or collective student behavior." (Zimmerman, R. (1997) Social Marketing Strategies for Campus Prevention of Alcohol and Other Drug Problems. U.S. Department of Education)
o "The success among a growing number of colleges and universities in achieving significant reductions in high-risk drinking and related harmful behavior has paved the way for the social norms approach to be applied successfully in high schools and communities," said Michael Haines, Director of the National Social Norms Resource Center.



Alice is one of two Program Coordinators funded by the Grant to Reduce Alcohol Abuse. She works directly with the 4 Project SMART schools to develop and implement a comprehensive, innovative strategic plan addressing the school's and communities underage drinking concerns. The goal of this project is to change underage drinking attitudes and behaviors. A second goal of this project is to build capacity within the schools so they can continue the project beyond the grant period. Two Program Coordinators, as recommended by the funder, are responsible for working directly with their school contacts to develop infrastructures, knowledge, understanding, and skills that will promote ongoing involvement in underage drinking prevention and early intervention after the funding has ended. Because of the finite time of the project, both Program Coordinators were hired as consultants and they understand their positions will no longer be required with the end of the grant.



 



Alice is uniquely qualified for the job of Program Coordinator because she:



 Worked in schools for the past 11 years



 Taught a parenting substance abuse prevention class for Hanley Center for the past 10 years



 Trained and worked as a librarian giving her exceptional research capabilities and organizational skills



Worked with diverse audiences from elementary through high school youth, school staff, parents, and community.
