DESCRIPTION:

Calusa Elementary School was the second place winner out of 122 schools that were recognized by the Florida Atlantic University Pine Jog Environmental Education Center's Green Schools Recognition Program for Palm Beach and Martin Counties. Calusa Elementary achieved the second highest score from judges by documenting their impressive school-wide green initiatives through an annual application process. The school has a well-established garden program that is utilized by all grade levels, featuring a butterfly garden, a Garden Oasis, and a Flamingo Way Walking Path. 

 

Their school grounds are a certified wildlife habitat. Students frequently use the outdoor learning labs to study local flora and fauna and to make connections across the curriculum. Students participate in a number of initiatives to help keep Calusa green; serving as “Go Green!” patrols in each classroom, creating over 70 “Lights Off” posters this year to place throughout the campus, and taking turns to maintain the walking trail. This year, they were awarded the “Healthier U.S. School Challenge” award, and they raised over $47,000 in their school-wide Walk-A-Thon to promote health and well-being. Calusa students know that “There is no Planet B,” and their intermediate students create projects to propose solutions to our earth’s most pressing challenges.

DESCRIPTION:

Meadow Park Elementary School was the third place winner out of 122 schools that were recognized by the Florida Atlantic University Pine Jog Environmental Education Center's Green Schools Recognition Program for Palm Beach and Martin Counties. The school received the third highest score from judges by documenting their impressive school-wide green initiatives through an annual application process. 


This was Meadow Park’s seventh year participating in the Green Schools Recognition Program, and their commitment to being green just keeps getting stronger. The school demonstrates its commitment to being green by hosting their own annual school-wide “Green Competition” to boost student awareness of recycling. For the past four years, Meadow Park has held an annual “Book Swap” to promote both literacy and the ethics of reuse, for which students donate and swap more than 500 books a year. 

 

Meadow Park’s green initiatives have a very strong community service component. With their “Eagle’s Nest” initiative, they collect and redistribute gently used uniforms and other clothing for students, and their “Charlie’s Food Pantry” redistributes donated food and other basic necessities to school families in need.

DESCRIPTION:

Shriece Franks is the School Cafeteria Manager at H.L. Johnson Elementary School. She is a fundamental part of the Green Team at the school, demonstrating her commitment to green school initiatives. Ms. Franks has given students healthier snack options, established a mobile breakfast cart, and encourages serving produce grown in the school garden. She also eliminated the use of Styrofoam trays and replaced them with re-usable plastic trays, reducing the school’s solid waste and saving over $3,240 per year. She also works with students and cafeteria staff to maintain a sharing table, to recycle milk and juice cartons, and up to 25 cardboard boxes a day. Ms. Franks actively contributed to H.L. The U.S. Department of Education has named Gove Elementary School a Green Ribbon School for its commitment to student and community wellness, reducing environmental impacts, and for providing environmental and sustainability education. Gove Elementary was nominated by the Florida Department of Education to apply for this national award because of the local Green School recognition Gove has received, and because of the many grants and innovative initiatives Gove pursues.

 

Achieving this award is due to more than a decade of hard work by the entire Gove family with leadership provided by their principal, Kimberly Thomasson, Gove’s Green and Wellness Teams, and more than 50 business partners that support Gove’s “Whole School, Whole Community, Whole Child” approach. Fruits and vegetables have been included in the daily snacks, there’s a vegetable and herb garden on campus, and the students have participated in several health and wellness initiatives such as morning yoga, healthy eating forums, and fitness challenges. The school-wide commitment to conservation includes hands-on STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art and Math) lessons centered on the environment, recycling projects, carpooling and biking to school, and the reduction of pesticides used on the campus by incorporating ladybugs and marigolds in the landscaping.

 

The campus is LEED Silver-certified with energy and water-reducing materials such as native plants in the landscaping, green cleaning products, and ice storage air conditioning. Through these initiatives, it has reduced its energy use by 74% and its water use by 57% in two years. This award acknowledges Gove Elementary School’s commitment to sustainable practices and its leadership in facilities, health, and environmental education.

The current Collective Bargaining Agreement between SDPBC and CTA is being amended to reflect the following negotiated modifications:

 

1. Effective July 1, 2018, the Teachers' Salary Schedule is amended as negotiated in Appendix A. This settlement reflects an average salary increase of 3%. More specifically,

  • Teachers on the performance pay schedule who were rated highly effective for the 17-18 school year will receive a 3.5% increase to base pay inclusive of a 0.5% cost of living adjustment.
  • Teachers on the performance pay schedule who were rated effective for the 17-18 school year will receive a 2.75% increase to base pay inclusive of a 0.5% cost of living adjustment.
  • Teachers on the grandfathered pay schedule who were rated highly effective for the 17-18 school year will receive a 3.5% minus $1 increase to base pay inclusive of a 0.5% cost of living adjustment.
  • Teachers on the grandfathered pay schedule who were rated effective for the 17-18 school year will receive a 2.75% minus $1 increase to base pay inclusive of a 0.5% cost of living adjustment.
  • Teachers rated as developing/needs improvement or unsatisfactory for the 17-18 school year will receive a 0.5% increase to the base pay as a cost of living adjustment.
  • The starting salary remains at $41,000; however, the maximum salary was increased by 3% ($84,246) for those teachers on the performance pay schedule and by 3% minus $1 ($84,245) for those teachers on the grandfathered pay schedule.
  • Only teachers who are currently employed by the District on the date the School Board adopts this agreement are eligible to receive a retroactive salary increase back to July 1, 2018.

2. The District will extend the State’s Best and Brightest Scholarship (Bonus) program to approximately 1,400 non-classroom based instructional support teachers at a one-time cost of approximately $1.5 million.
