This Agreement will allow the college preparatory Advancement Via Individual Determination (AVID) Program to continue or begin implementation in the the following schools:

These sixteen schools are included in several USDOE Grants (Small Learning Communities (SLC). Advanced Placement Incentive Program (APIP), and High School Graduation Initiative (HSGI)) and local school-based grants that specify AVID as one of the strategies supported by the grants funding.  This Agreement includes the sixteen (16) school site memberships, summer institute team training, library of instructional materials that will be used in the AVID elective and content courses identified in the Florida Course Code Directory and other professional development.



AVID is a systematic change agent that works by infusing AVID strategies and methodologies in every classroom to allow all students to succeed in reading, math, rigorous courses of study and in their overall secondary/post-secondary endeavors.



AVID's mission is to close the achievement gap by preparing all students for college readiness and success in the global society.  AVID is designed to ensure that all students: 1) will succeed in rigorous curriculum; 2) will complete a college preparatory path; 3) Will enter mainstream activities of the school; 4) will increase enrollment in four-year colleges, and; 5) will become educated, responsible participants and leaders in a democratic society. 
 
The foundation of the AVID strategies is Writing/Inquiry/Collaboration/Organization/Reading (WICOR ).  By all staff receiving professional development in WICOR strategies the teachers are able to infuse these elements into all their lessons creating an academic single school culture among staff and students.



AVID is designed to ensure that all students:

                will enter mainstream activities of the school;

This Agreement will allow the implementation of AVID's college preparatory curriculum at all sixteen schools noted above for school year 2013.

Mr. David Porter, Chair of the Board's Construction and Oversight Review Committee, is the sole proprietor of David Porter Associates - Architects, Inc.  Mr. Porter has indicated that he occasionally needs the services of an engineer for his private practice.  Thus, he inquired if there would be a prohibited conflict of interest or voting conflict under Chapter 112, F.S., for him to hire an engineer who has been identified and retained by an architect within the provisions of the Board's competitive solicitation process for his firm's project(s) which may require engineering services.  Based upon an opinion from the Florida Commission on Ethics, Mr. Porter has been advised that if he will be hiring an engineer and the engineer will be subcontractor of architects who are in contract with the Board, that he should first obtain a waiver under Sec. 112,313(12) F.S. Please find attached the opinion from Mr. Chris Anderson, Deputy Executive Director/General Counsel, Florida Commission on Ethics.  See Attachment A.

As a member of CORC, Mr. Porter may receive a waiver in a particular instance by the Board when made in a public meeting after a written disclosure is made on Commission Form 4A.  See Attachment B.

Please note the grant of such a waiver requires at least a two-thirds vote of the Board.

Recommendation:

Mr. Porter be granted a waiver as provided under Sec. 112.313(12), F.S.; provided however:

  • Mr. Porter does not participate on a District review panel for the selection of an architectural firm that is considering engaging or has engaged as an engineer that Mr. Porter has hired or will be hiring.
  • Mr. Porter discloses during any meeting of the Construction Oversight and Review Committee (CORC) the working relationship he has with any engineer that may be connected with any construction-related project being reviewed by CORC pursuant to Policy 1.093.

This Agreement will allow the college preparatory Advancement Via Individual Determination (AVID) English Learner College Readiness (ELCR) Program to be implemented in the the following middle schools:



John F. Kennedy Middle
Okeeheelee Middle
Palm Springs Middle
Tradewinds Middle



Funding has been allocated and will pay for the four (4) school site memberships, local on-site professional development, and instructional materials that will be used in the AVID-ELCR course.



AVID's mission is to close the achievement gap by preparing all students for college readiness and success in the global society.  AVID is designed to ensure that all students: 1) will succeed in rigorous curriculum; 2) will complete a college preparatory path; 3) Will enter mainstream activities of the school; 4) will increase enrollment in four-year colleges, and; 5) will become educated, responsible participants and leaders in a democratic society. 



The AVID-ELCR Program is designed to assist English Language Learner (ELL) students with the necessary skills to not only achieve in school, but to pursue the most rigorous curriculum when entering high school.  The AVID-ELCR Program will focus on assisting the ELL students with increasing academic language, setting personal and academic goals, and learning about the hidden curriculum at school. 



Through a scaffold approach students begin the AVID-ELCR Program with a summer bridge between 6th and 7th grade.  The students then complete two years of the AVID-ELCR curriculum as an elective course in their schedule.  The students are supported using AVID strategies and teaching methodologies by their elective teacher, as well as their core area teachers.  The AVID-ELCR Program ends with a summer bridge between 8th and 9th grade.  The schools identified for participation in the AVID-ELCR all feed into high schools that currently offer the AVID Program  All ELL students who participate in the AVID-ELCR and elect to continue, will be placed into the AVID Program upon entering high school.



 



Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the AVID-ELCR Implementation Agreement between the AVID Center and the School Board of Palm Beach County effective April 19, 2012 through June 30, 2013, and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents related to the Agreement which are required for implementation.

AC3 - Cooperative Agreement with The Palm Beach County Sheriff's Office (Department of Corrections)

Apr 11, 2012 - Special Meeting

The School District, through the Department of Alternative Education, currently provides educational services to students who are juveniles incarcerated in Palm Beach County Jail, The Palm Beach Regional Detention Center, and the Palm Beach Juvenile Correctional Facility.



Pursuant to Florida Statute 951.176, provision of educational programs for youth, the District is responsible to offer educational services to "Minors who have not graduated from high school and eligible students with disabilitites under the age of 22 who have not graduated with a standard diploma or its equivalent who are detained in a county or municipal detention facility as defined in Florida Statute 951.23".  These educational services shall be based upon the estimated length of time the youth will be in the facility and the youth's current level of functioning.  In addition, Florida Statute 951.176 requires that "A cooperative agreement with the local school district and applicable law enforcement units shall be developed to address the notification requirement and the provision of educational services to these youth."



Two new programs entitled "Educational Services Programs" (ESP) will comply with the aforementioned Florida Statute 951.176.  The ESP will provide educational services to youth in the general adult population in the West and East Detention Centers who are under the age of 22 and have not graduated with a standard diploma or its equivalent.  Eligible students can voluntarily enroll in the ESP.  These new programs will meet many goals for the incarcerated youth:



            -  Prevents a disruption of eligible student's current educational  services
            -  Reduces or prevents high school dropouts
            -  Increases eligible students receiving a high school diploma



The School District will be responsible for providing the instructional staff and materials needed to provide the educational services to eligible youth.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the Cooperative Agreement with the Palm Beach County Sheriff's Office to provide educational services at the West Detention Center, Educational Services Program West - School #3091 and the East Detention Center, Educational Services Program East - School #3081 from March 29, 2012 through June 30, 2013.

AC5 - Statement of Work 3 (SOW 3) Between the School Board of Palm Beach County, Florida and CORE K12 Education (CORE K12), a Division of CORE Education and Consulting Solutions, Inc.
Meeting
Apr 11, 2012 - Special Meeting
Category
Consent Agenda
Type
Action (Consent)

In FY2012, the School Board of Palm Beach County approved the Statement of Work 2 (SOW 2) with CORE K12.  As a result of increased district and area support, there has been a substantial increase in the use of CORE K12 by teachers and students.



The FY2013 Statement of Work 3 (SOW 3) with CORE K12 provides an assessment for learning system that puts real time information about student performance into the hands of teachers and administrators at all public and charter schools.  CORE K12 enables teachers to administer, score, and immediately see the results of their student's performance on test questions and assessments and to access aligned instructional resources.  The questions and assessments are based on the Next Generation Sunshine State Standards (NGSSS) benchmarks and adhere to FCAT 2.0 Item Specifications.



There are several reasons why our District provides this system to all of its schools.





  • Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) Waiver Requirements:  The Florida Department of Education (FDOE) requires districts to "prescribe interim (Benchmark baseline, mid-year, and mini) assessments in reading, writing, mathematics, and science for Level 1-3 students."  Also, districts must ensure schools have "real-time access to student achievement data."

  • District Accreditation:  To be accredited by the Southern Association of Colleges and Schools Council on Accreditation and School Improvement (SACS-CASI), an accreditation division of AdvancED, districts must implement a comprehensive assessment system that generates a range of data about student learning and system effetiveness and uses the results to guide continuous improvement."

  • Research:  There is a substantial amount of research on the positive impact of assessments for learning.  For example, the National Center for Educational Achievement has determined that school districts which have increased student achievement and reduced student performance gaps have a balanced and comprehensive assessment system.  This research clearly indicates the importance of an assessment system that includes assessments of and assessments for learning that are aligned with the district's expectations for student learning.  The assessments for learning include:



    • Classroom-based assessments

    • School-wide benchmark assessments

    • District-wide benchmark baseline and mid-year assessments




One of the nation's leading educational researchers, Dr. Robert Marzano, has found that the following school-level practices, called Opportunity to Learn, have three times greater the impact on student acheivement than any other school practice:





  • Students and their teachers know what students are expected to learn and how well students are learning it.

  • Teachers know if they are teaching the expected content.


Continuity of Practices:  CORE K12 is an integral part of the District's assessment for learning system.  The substantial increase in usage by teachers and students in FY2012 indicates that there is growing adoption of CORE K12.  The time and resources invested to impact an even greater increase in adoption can be best leveraged by maintaining the existing system.  The District is best served by continuting to provide and support CORE K12 as an essential component in improving student achievement.



In order to comply with State ESEA Waiver requirements, AdvancED standards, and to adhere to research-based best practices, the FY2013 SOW 3 with CORE K12 provides the following deliverables:







  • Real-time results on teacher-developed and district-provided assessments of learning delivered through the Web, and/or by scanners and clickers using the Assessment Center (AC3) consisting of the following components:






    • National item bank (312,259 items)




    • Florida item bank (36,894 items)




    • FCAT Test Maker Item Bank (30,000 items focused on NGSSS benchmarks, and adhering to FCAT 2.0 item specifications)




    • 354 interim benchmark assessments focused on NGSSS benchmarks, adhering to FCAT 2.0 item specifications and aligned with the District's scope and sequence for Reading, Mathematics, Science, Algebra 1, Biology, and Geomerty




    • 92 multiple benchmark assessments focused on NGSSS benchmarks, adhering to FCAT 2.0 item specifications and aligned with the District's scope and sequence for Reading, Mathematics, Science, Algebra 1, and Geometry




    • 24 district-created fall and winter diagnostic assessments (The FDOE delivers these tests online.) for






      • Grade 6, 7, 9, and 10 Reading




      • Grade 5 Math




      • Algebra 1, Geometry, Biology, and U.S. History




      • Grade 10 Reading and Mathematics Sunshine State Standards Retakes




      • Grade 10 Reading NGSSS Retakes




  • Student, teacher, and parent instructional resources and community outreach through CORE K12




  • Data transfers from CORE K12 to the District's Educational Data Warehouse (EDW)


The CORE K12 price for the FY2013 SOW 3 is $684,500.  This is a price increase of $44,000 from the FY12 price of $640,500.  This price reflects the increase in the cost of the FCAT Test Maker Item Bank.  In addition, the District continues to work aggressively to control the costs through a partnership with CORE K12.  The partnership is evident in the following two ways:







  1. CORE K12 is crediting the District $425,250.00 for renewing the contract and offsetting by at least half the cost of FCAT Test Maker Item Bank.




  2. CORE K12 may provide the District additional funds for the sale of District-developed Diagnostic Assessments and/or norms, as outlined in the Cost Recovery Content Licensing Agreement.




 



 



 

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the Statement of Work 3 for the Assessment for Learning System in the amount of $684,500.

On June 22, 2011, Charter Schools of Boynton Beach, Inc. (CSBB) was granted a 15 year renewal of its charter school contract by the School Board of Palm Beach County. The charter became effective upon signing by both parties, and covered a term of fifteen (15) years commencing July 1, 2011, and ending June 30, 2026, except for reasons as provided in the charter.



CSBB serves students in Grades K-12.



CSBB sent an inquiry to the Sponsor regarding the process for the charter school to offer the Florida Retirement Program (FRS) to its employees.  Upon CSBB's submission of its application for FRS benefits to the State of Florida for approval, a scrivener's error was discovered on page 44.  The CSBB was informed that consideration to FRS benefits would not be granted unless corrected documents were forwarded to the State.  The CSBB then contacted and informed the District about the error.  After review by the attorneys for both parties, it has been recommended that the best way to address the correction is to amend the Charter to correct the language.  The Charter School's Governing Board has requested that we amend the Charter Agreement and has agreed to the revised language.



The Charter Contract on page 44 contained the scrivener's error at lines 16 and 17 that read: "The employees shall be a public employer."



The revised Charter Contract on page 44 will contain new language at lines 16 and 17 that reads: "The Charter School shall be a Public employer."



A copy of the Amended Charter Contract is attached as Exhibit A.



A copy of the First Amendment to the Charter Contract is attached as Exhibit B.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board of Palm Beach County approve the Amendment of the Fifteen (15) year Renewal Charter Contract for Charter Schools of Boynton Beach, Inc., in order to correct a scrivener's error and authorize the Superintendent and Board Chair to sign all relevant documents.

Ratings of Cornerstone Academy for High Standards, Inc.'s Application



Designated District staff reviewed the 19 sections of Cornerstone Academy for High Standards, Inc.'s application.  There were two evaluations of this application.  After the first evaluation, Cornerstone Academy for High Standards, Inc. had five of 19 sections that received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard," seven sections that received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard," and one section that received a rating of "Not Applicable."



Listed below are the seven sections that received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 1 - Mission, Guiding Principles, and Purpose:  It was not clear in the application how the financial responsibility and accountability were aligned.




  • Section 5 - Assessment:  The school's educational goals and objectives were unclear.




  • Section 6 - Exceptional Students (ESE):  In the application Section 504 was not specifically addressed.




  • Section 14 - Facilities:  The desired size of the facility did not support the budgeted lease.




  • Section 19 - Action Plan:  There was no emergency plan in place for unanticipated events.


Listed below are the seven sections that received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 3 - Educational Program Design:  It was not clear how 160 minutes of instructional time would ensure that the students earn the 24 credits needed for graduation.  Additionally, the research to support the proposed educational design was not addressed.




  • Section 7 - English Language Learners:  The ELL Plan does not follow the District guidelines in regard to ESOL strategies.




  • Section 8 - School Climate and Discipline:  The policies and procedures, as well as the school's Code of Conduct were not addressed.




  • Section 9 - Governance:  A clear understanding of the Governing Board's responsibilities was not evident.




  • Section 10 - Management:  The teacher responsibilities were not outlined.  Also, the staffing plan was not provided with the school's projected enrollment as detailed on the cover page of the application.




  • Section 17 - Budget:  The budget did not present a realistic projected source of revenue and expenses that will ensure the financial viability of the school.




  • Section 18 - Financial Management and Oversight:  The Asset policies and procedures, as well as the internal controls were not outlined in detail.


Listed below is the section that received a rating "Not Applicable" with the reason for the rating.







  • Section 11 - Education Service Provider:  The applicant does not intend to enter into a contract with an Education Service.


A letter and the 19 sections were emailed to the applicant group on December 13, 2011, and sent certified mail on December 13, 2011, asking for additional information in order to meet the standard.  (See Exhibit A - First Review.)



Cornerstone Academy for High Standards, Inc. then submitted additional information that cured the concerns that were outlined in Section 5 - Assessment; Section 6 - Exceptional Students; Section 7 - English Language Learners; Section 8 - School Climate and Discipline; Section 9 - Governance; Section 10 - Management; Section 14 - Facilities; and Section 19 - Action Plan.  (See Exhibit B - Second Review.)



After the second review, the two sections below still received a rating of "Partially Meets the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 1 - Mission, Guiding Principles and Purpose:  It is not clear in the application how the financial responsibility and accountability are aligned.




  • Section 18 - Financial Management and Oversight:  The applicant did not address the concerns outlined in the first budget review.


After the second review, the two sections below still received a rating of "Does Not Meet the Standard" and the reason for each rating.







  • Section 3 - Educational Program Design:  The research-based educational practices outlined in the application date back to 1992-1998 and are not current.  Also, the application referenced that six of the 18 credits that students earn will be in dual enrolled classes.  The dual enrolled classes should be Advanced Placement, Advanced International Certificate of Education or International Baccalaureate.  However, there was no mention of Professional Development or cost for the three programs outlined.




  • Section 17 - Budget:  The applicant did not address the concerns outlined in the first budget review.

Florida Charter School Application Evaluation Instrument



 



Each section presents criteria for a response that meets the standard and these criteria should guide the overall rating for the section.  The Strengths and Weaknesses boxes provide space to identify data and other evidence that supports the rating.  The rationale for each rating is important, especially if some of the data or evidence does not fit neatly into the criteria provided.



The following definitions should guide the ratings:



         Meets the Standard:                                            The response reflects a thorough understanding of key issues and demonstrates capacity to open and operate a quality charter school.  It addresses the topic with specific and accurate information that shows thorough preparation and presents a clear, realistic picture of how the school expects to operate.



Partially Meets the Standard:                             The response addresses most of the criteria, but the responses lack meaningful detail and require important additional information.



Does Not Meet the Standard:                              The response lacks meaningful detail; demonstrates lack of preparation; or otherwise raises substantial concerns about the applicant’s understanding of the issue in concept and/or ability to meet the requirement in practice
