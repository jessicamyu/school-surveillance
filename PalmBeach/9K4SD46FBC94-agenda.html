Boynton Beach High School and Santaluces High School have been chosen as Springboard National Demonstration Schools by the College Board.  To become a National Demonstration School, these distinguished schools have met criteria based on the implementation of SpringBoard for at least two years with ongoing professional development.  SpringBoard is the foundational instructional component of the College Board’s College Readiness System and is used in over 1,000 schools across the nation, by more than one million students. G1 - Advanced Placement Incentive Program (APIP) Grant
Meeting
Sep 21, 2011 - School Board Meeting
Category
Consent
Type
Action (Consent)

The School District of Palm Beach County has been awarded the Advanced Placement Incentive Program (APIP) Competitive Grant by the United States Department of Education (USDOE) in the amount of $1,940,262.  The APIP Grant Project targets four secondary schools with a high percentage of low income students, including Lake Shore Middle School, Glades Central Community High School, Crestwood Middle School, and Royal Palm Beach Community High School.  These schools were selected based on the criteria of the grant application. 



The APIP project goals are to: 1) Promote rigorous Pre-AP and AP Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math  (STEM) and English coursework at low-income schools; 2)  Expand access for low income students to Pre-Advanced Placement and Advanced Placement Programs and Incentives; and 3)  Increase successful performance of low income students in Pre-AP and Advanced Placement courses and on tests.



Services include:





  • Springboard Math in target schools

  • Springboard English/Language Arts established or expanded in target schools

  • AVID established or expanded in target schools

  • STEM  Coach to provide training, support, coaching, follow up to teachers

  • School-based lab and supervision for online AP courses

  • School-based staff (stipend) to support, facilitate,  and promote initiatives

  • Extensive Vertical Team, Advanced Placement, Springboard and AVID Training

  • Partnership with College Board

  • Partnerships with Scripps and Max Planck Institute for field trips, Neuroscience events, career panels, and integrated lessons

  • Tutoring

  • Mentoring by first generation college students to help with college readiness

  • College Readiness curricula from ECMC Foundation in middle and high schools

  • Parent and student college readiness workshops including efficacy training, financial aid, and college expectations

  • PSAT in 9th grade to generate AP Potential Report for use by high school principals in identifying potential students for AP classes


Although the grant is effective August 31, 2011, this does not prejudice the School Board. Congressional notification of the award was received on August 1, 2011. The three-year budget request from the USDOE is $1,940,262, and the required match from non-federal sources, including community partners, is for $433,826.   The community partners are the Scripps Research Institute, The Max Planck Florida Institute, ECMC - Believing the College Dream and Realizing the College, and College Board.



The District has had an existing APIP grant since October 1, 2008.  It was approved by the School Board on September 17, 2008.  The District has been granted a no-cost extension to June 30, 2012 at which time all remaining funds will be expended in support of the original grant.



The grant application is available in the Board Office for review.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the new APIP Grant award from the U.S.  Department of Education for up to $1,940,262, effective August 31, 2011 through August 30, 2014 and approve any no cost extension as applicable.  I also authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents and contracts related to and required for implementation of grant activities and recommend that the School Board approve the no-cost extension of the original APIP grant to June 30, 2012.

Contact:

Judith Klinek (judith.klinek@palmbeachschools.org)



Keith Oswald

Financial Impact:

The financial impact to the Special Revenue - Other Funds Budget is an increase of up to $1,940,262. All grant related expenditures and contracts are authorized for payment up to the amount of revenue. There is no financial impact to the Operating Budget.

Prevention Partnerships for Children, Inc., Quality Counts Coaches Agreement
Meeting
Sep 21, 2011 - School Board Meeting
Category

Action (Consent)

This Agreement with Prevention Partnerships for Children, Inc. provides $3,021,225 to the School District for the period effective October 1, 2011, through September 30, 2012, for the continuation of the Quality Counts (QC) Early Learning Coaches program.  The coaching program is one of a number of components that work together to create a comprehensive, voluntary early care and education quality rating improvement system for Palm Beach County.  The system partners work together to provide services to enhance the quality of early education programs so that children will enter school eager and ready to learn.  The Children’s Services Council of Palm Beach County and a number of stakeholders initiated the Quality Counts System.  System partners include Children’s Services Council, Palm Beach State College, Palm Beach County Early Learning Coalition, Family Central and the School District.



 



This grant funds 34 Quality Counts Early Learning Coaches (certified resource teachers) and related support/administrative staff to serve approximately 150 child care centers and school- based sites that voluntarily participate.  To be eligible to participate, child care centers must have been licensed for at least one year and must hold a rate agreement with Family Central.  School District classrooms offering the Voluntary Prekindergarten (VPK) program are participants as are those classrooms in the Village Academy Head Start program and the VPK/PreK Migrant Education partnership program.   



 



The Quality Counts coaches assist with the implementation of improvement plans and strategies developed collaboratively with classroom teachers and child care directors based on data from formal and informal assessments.  Assessors, who have been trained to “reliability” and are monitored for proficiency, administer the assessments.  Multiple assessment measures are used, including but not limited to, the Early Childhood Environment Rating Scale (ECERS-R) and the Infant/Toddler Environment Rating Scale (ITERS), both nationally recognized, research-based assessment tools.  The assessments provide for the award of a “Star Rating,” ranging from 1 to 5 stars.  The lowest rating, a 1-Star rating, equates to the attainment of current state and local child care licensing standards.



 



Coaching supports are provided using a three-level, consultation approach, based on an evidence-based coaching model developed by Pat Wesley, a research scientist at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Coaching levels include:



 



Level 1- Intensive







  • Intensive coaching services are based on the number of classrooms and the results of the informal baseline assessment.




  • Onsite intensive services range from 1 day/week up to 2.5 days/week.


Level 2 - Maintenance







  • Maintenance coaching services begin after sites have a formal assessment.  The formal assessment scores are used to award a star rating.




  • Onsite maintenance services range from 2 to 3 days per month for year 1 and from 1 to 2 days per month for year 2 participants.


Level 3 – Model







  • Quarterly visits are schedued with a child care center director or administrative leadership for sites that consistently maintain quality improvements. 




  • Follow-up supports may be provided via phone or e-mail.


The Quality Counts System currently has 113 participating private child care centers.  The District is both a partner and a voluntary participant and most participating school-based sites have earned the highest possible star rating.



 



Participating public schools include: Acreage Pines Elementary, Belle Glade Elementary, Benoist Farms Elementary, Berkshire Elementary, Cholee Lake Elementary, D.D. Eisenhower Elementary, Elbridge Gale Elementary, Equestrian Trails Elementary, Galaxy Elementary, Grassy Waters Elementary, Greenacres Elementary, Heritage Elementary, Highland Elementary, Hope-Centennial Elementary, Indian Pines Elementary, K.E. Cunningham /Canal Point Elementary, Lincoln Elementary, Pahokee Elementary, Palm Beach Gardens Elementary, Panther Run Elementary, Pine Grove Elementary, Roosevelt Elementary, Royal Palm School, Sandpiper Shores Elementary, Starlight Cove Elementary, U.B. Kinsey Elementary, Village Academy, West Riviera Elementary, Westward Elementary, and Wynnebrook Elementary.   Schools entering the program in October 1, 2011 include: Loxahatchee Groves Elementary, Manatee Elementary, North Palm Beach Elementary, Pleasant City Elementary, and Pioneer Park Elementary.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve a contract with Prevention Partnerships for Children, Inc. in the amount of $3,021,225 effective from October 1, 2011, until September 30, 2011, and authorize the superintendent or his designee to sign all documents and contracts related to the grant that are required for implementation of grant activities.

P2 Approve the Award of RFP 12C-002D Parent Notification System





  • On July 18, 2011 an Evaluation Committee consisting of District personnel, including a representative from the Office of Diversity in Business Practices, reviewed the eight proposals received and evaluated each of them based on the criteria outlined in the RFP. Prior to discussing and evaluating each of the responder's proposals, the Committee agreed that a recommendation of award would be made to the Proposer receiving the highest number of total points.  After discussing and scoring each proposal, Parlant Technology, Inc. received the highest overall score. Parlant Technology received the maximum number of points for minority participation based on their commitment to use M/WBE vendors for auxiliary services during the term of this contract. 

  • This system handles mass call-outs or emails for emergencies/hurricanes and calls to parents and staff from the Superintendent on a variety of subjects.  Each school can also use the system for daily automated calls to parents regarding student absences/tardiness, low lunch balances and reminders of school events, all delivered in a timely fashion.

  • The systems advantages include:



    • A large number of phone lines allowing notifications to be delivered to all parents and staff within hours.

    • During a disaster, this out-sourced (hosted) system will remain functional for communications, allowing staff to concentrate on critical local systems such as payroll and student systems.

    • Ability to configure the system to call students, staff and stakeholders in their native language and in unique ways such as detailed period-by-period attendance, cafeteria balances, overdue library books, etc.

    • Empowerment of parents and stakeholders to manage their messages via phone or Internet.

    • Increased productivity due to user-friendly nature of the system for schools, parents and staff.

    • Unlimited on-site user training provided by the vendor at no additional cost.


  • The current use of this system is expanding and reaping rewards. 



    • Recently, the Planning Department requested training to be able to use this system for Boundary Change notification rather than mailings.

    • PBCSD Food Service Department reported a 25% decrease in school lunch debt, directly correlating with the implementation of ParentLink lunch balance calls.

    • Transportation has also expressed interest in conducting a pilot program where ParentLink notifies parents of bus schedules, route changes, etc.  Parlant has agreed to host this pilot program at no additional cost to the District.

Recommendation:

I recommend approval of the contract for the notification services in the amount of $1.50 per student per year for the period of September 22, 2011 through September 21, 2013 and authorize the Superintendent and Board Chairman to sign all necessary documents.

·         Maintain a high quality rating as measured by the Quality Counts System



·         Improve the kindergarten readiness of the students as measured by components of the Florida Kindergarten Readiness Screener (FLKRS), reported as an annual VPK Readiness Rate Score



 



The Bridges Beacon Center project was initiated by CSC, which administers this grant and provides “pass through funding” to the collaborative partners for the implementation of the various strategies.  Highland Elementary provides space for the early childhood classrooms, for the managers employed by the Housing Partnership for the parent engagement and mentoring programs, and for a parent resource room with computers (provided by another partner).

This Agreement with the Palm Beach County Board of County Commissioners provides $1,052,115 for the continuation of full-day Head Start services for 185 three- and four-year-old students and their families effective October 1, 2011 through September 30, 2012.  Village Academy will host the Head Start program, creating a preschool to high school continuum of aligned services for the community.  The Village Academy Principal and Department of Early Childhood Education Director will be the academic and grant program leaders and will secure parent, community and District participation.  Currently, a diverse community group under the leadership of the Children’s Services Council is engaged in an enriched community collaborative process to support this program.    



 



Child development and family support services will be provided during the school year, Monday through Friday, between the hours of 7:30 a.m. and 5:30 p.m. Families will be provided with a full complement of Head Start services, including medical, dental and family social services supports.  The four-year-old classes will also participate in the Voluntary Prekindergarten (VPK) program provided under the auspice of the Department of Early Childhood Education, which will provide enhanced supports and oversight.  These supports include, but are not limited to, on-site coaching, along with financial assistance and management services.  The Village Academy Head Start program will also participate in and receive supports from the Palm Beach County Quality Counts System, a quality rating and improvement system.  The program will be subject to audits from local and federal Head Start agents, VPK auditors and Quality Counts assessors.



 



Over the past four years, a majority of the Head Start graduates have participated in the District’s Summer VPK program held at Delray Full Service Center.  These students were screened annually for Kindergarten readiness using the Florida Kindergarten Readiness Screener (FLKRS) upon entry into Kindergarten.  The State Board of Education disaggregated the FLKRS scores back to the Delray Full Service Center VPK site and awarded an annual Kindergarten Readiness Rate based on the FLKRS scores.  For the past two years, the site has earned a score above the minimum provider threshold.

IN5 ADOPTION OF HEALTH AND OTHER INSURANCE PROVISIONS OF THE COLLECTIVE BARGAINING AGREEMENTS
Meeting
Sep 21, 2011 - School Board Meeting
Category

Discussion

The Unions/Associations listed in the recommendation came together as a coalition to negotiate with the District over changes to the Health, Dental, Life, and Vision Insurances offered by the District to its employees.  Coalition bargaining on insurances has existed for approximately 17 years.  The coalition members and District negotiators reached a tentative agreement on the changes in the health insurance benefits to be effective January 1, 2012.



The District has shared it's expectation of an 8 to 10% increase in the cost of health insurance in 2012, and our higher-than-normal claim increases in 2011.  With that in mind, these coalition negotiations have resulted in a unified recognition that employees must become more aware of their health conditions and take positive steps to prevent further health problems in the future.  Thus, the adoption of the continuation and expansion of the Biometric and Health Assessment Program is a part of this tentative agreement.



Summary of Changes:





  • Continues and enhances the Healthy Rewards Program to continue the $50 per month premium discount for those employees who comply with the Healthy Rewards Program by published deadlines.  Also requires the covered spouses/domestic partners, as well as the employee to complete the requirements of the wellness physical, biometric screenings and on-line health assessment in 2012 for the discount in 2013.

  • Requires a premium increase of between $12.50 to $21.12 per month on the part of the employees.  However, the single Low Option HMO will have no premium for employee-only coverage as long as the employee completes the Health Rewards requirements.

  • Adds a $350 individual deductible/$700 family deductible for family for the High Option HMO which applies to inpatient and outpatient hospitalizations treatments, as well as most tests.  It does not apply to physician office visits; co-pays will continue for these visits.

  • Mail order co-pays for 90-day prescriptions will increase from 2.0 times the monthly co-pay to 2.5 times.

  • New employees (non-CTA) must work 30 hours per week or more to qualify for insurance (up from 20 hours).  Teachers must work at least 18.75 per week to receive health insurance.  Existing part-time employees will be grandfathered in and keep their eligibility for  benefits.  In addition, existing part-time employees that elect the High Option HMO Plan must pay $100 per month more than a full-time employees for coverage or $130 more per month for dependent coverage.  Existing part-time employees may avoid the additional $100/$130 per month increase by selecting the Low Option HMO.

Recommendation:

I recommend that pending ratification by the Association of Secretaries and Office Professionals, The Classroom Teachers' Association, The Police Benevolent Association, and the Service Employees International Union/Florida Public Services Union, the Board approve the Contract amendments on Health and other insurances to be effective January 1, 2012 and to adopt these same amended provisions for all eligible District non-bargaining employees effective January 1, 2012.

Subject
PR2 - Redistricting Criteria and Process
Meeting
Sep 21, 2011 - School Board Meeting
Category
New Business
Type
Discussion


  • On August 10, 2011, the School Board conducted a workshop regarding redistricting criteria and objectives.  At the workshop, the Board provided direction regarding objectives that should be accomplished during the redistricting process.  Several Board Members emphasized the need to reduce voter confusion through more sensible and compact districts with clear and understandable boundary lines.  There was also discussion about looking at potentially representing the Glades with one Board Member, placing the Acreage and Wellington in one district, making more sensible boundaries particularly as they pertain to Wellington, West Boynton and Boca Raton; using clear dividing lines for district boundaries to reduce confusion for constituents, including working on a more understandable northern boundary for District 5.  Staff was asked to look at the definition of communities of interest and give it top consideration; look at voting age demographics by district; and work with the Glades Tri-Cities Coalition regarding representation for the Glades.


 





  • At the August 10, 2011, School Board workshop, staff recommended that the School Board use essentially the same redistricting criteria adopted by the County Commission.  These criteria were used in the County's 1991 and 2001 redistricting exercises:



    • Achieve population equity (10% or less variance among districts population) among Commission Districts

    • Adhere to Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, as amended and interpreted through case law

    • Follow major natural and man-made boundaries

    • Maintain integrity of communities of interest, including municipal boundaries

    • Insure compactness

    • Contiguity of Districts

    • Preserve the core of the existing voter's districts


 





  • The County's criteria have been modified slightly to reflect Board priorities as expressed at the August 10th workshop.  Using similar criteria will maximize opportunities for coordination with the County's efforts.


 





  • The recommended criteria are based on traditional redistricting principles, legal redistricting requirements and similarity with the County Commission's criteria.


 





  • Staff is recommending the following components for public information and input:



    • A website where maps, Board materials, meeting dates, and related information can be posted (http://www.pbcredistricting.info/)

    • The ability for the public to submit e-mailed comments via the website

    • At least one community input workshop

    • Coordination of information with the City Managers and Neighborhood Coordinators/Planners

    • Other elements as directed by the Board


 





  • To affirm compliance with traditional redistricting criteria and applicable legal standards, the District has retained Jerry Wilson, Esq., as outside counsel for Redistricting.  Mr. Wilson was selected due to his role as the County's Redistricting consultant in 2001 and 2011, offering opportunities for greater familiarity with county-wide issues, economies of scale and coordination.  Mr. Wilson has reviewed the recommend criteria.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the Board adopt the following Redistricting criteria and direct staff to bring back at least two Redistricting scenarios that are prepared consistent with these criteria:





  • Achieve population equity among Districts (10% or less variance among districts' population)

  • Adhere to Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, as amended and interpreted through case law

  • Maintain integrity of communities of interest, including municipal boundaries where practicable

  • Follow major natural and man-made boundaries

  • Insure compactness

  • Contiguity of Districts

  • Preserve the core of the existing voter's districts

Meeting

The purpose of this agreement is to offer Palm Beach County students a learning opportunity in cooperation with Florida Atlantic University (FAU).  The agreement will offer a chance for students to explore interests in the health sciences and careers in medicine and to mentor and prepare them to successfully compete at the highest levels of college and post-graduate studies.



 



The program will initiate, upon Board approval, on September 22, 2011, at Lake Worth High School and Roosevelt Middle School with the intent to expand to other schools within the school district.



 



FAU and participating schools expect to engage in cooperative teaching and mentoring activities.  Such activities include joint/collaborative teaching of students by the FAU faculty and participating schools, field trips to FAU to attend lectures, involvement in informational panels and participation in activities at the FAU facilities such as laboratories and simulation centers.

FS-4 Crestwood Middle School Project Modifications
Meeting
Sep 21, 2011 - School Board Meeting
Category
New Business
Type
Action

This Project Modification contains the following items:



Item #1







  • Change Order Proposal Request (COPR) #2 in the not-to-exceed amount of $312,270.00 




  • Description:  Provide all labor and materials to patch penetrations and openings in rated wall partitions above existing ceilings.




  • Cause:  Unforeseen Condition




  • Justification:  The contract scope of work included replacing the light fixtures in corridors and classrooms.  To install the light fixtures, the contractor removed the existing ceiling tiles which exposed the wall partitions from the ceiling to the roof deck.  Numerous openings were identified in the wall partitions and were cited by the Building Department as code violations.  On future renovation projects, more detailed non-destructive testing during the design phase will be considered to identify these types of issues prior to contracting the work  


Item #2







  • Construction Change Directive (CCD) #3 in the not-to-exceed amount of $28,770.50




  • Description:  Increase landscape irrigation allowance from $30,000 to $58,770 to modify existing and add new irrigation for landscape areas that were modified and added as a result of the parking lot expansion, modifications to the school entrance, and the building addition.




  • Cause:  Outside Agency/Unforeseen Condition




  • Justification:  The Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP) included an allowance of $30,000 to provide landscape irrigation in and around the new construction.  The Village of Royal Palm Beach had not approved the site plan prior to the Board approving the Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP).  As part of the site plan approval process, the Village of Royal Palm Beach required that additional landscaping be provided around the perimeter of the site.  The increase in the allowance was required to provide irrigation in these landscape areas.

Recommendation:

I recommend the School Board approve Project Modifications in the not-to-exceed amount of $312,270.00 and ratify Project Modifications in the not-to-exceed amount of $28,770.50 to Moss & Associates, LLC for Crestwood Middle School, Project #1691-8307, and authorize the Chairman and Superintendent to finalize and execute all related contract documents.
