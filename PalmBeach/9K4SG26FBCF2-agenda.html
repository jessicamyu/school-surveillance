The Council for Exceptional Children (CEC) is the largest international professional organization dedicated to improving the educational success of individuals with disabilities and/or gifts and talents. CEC advocates for appropriate governmental policies, sets professional standards, provides professional development, advocates for individuals with exceptionalities, and helps professionals obtain conditions and resources necessary for effective professional practice.



2012 CEC Chapter 200 Distinguished Award Winners:
Dr. Ana Arce-Gonzales, CEC Chapter 200 Principal of the Year (Forest Hill Elementary)
Dr. Arce has embraced Multi-Tiered Systems of Support, School-Wide Positive Behavior Support, and Response to Intervention.  Her school is a showcase for these initiatives, the Owls give a HOOT by listening to each other, hands are for helping, and being responsible for what they say and do.   In addition to including ESE students in general education she has many special ESE programs with several dedicated staff.  She is truly a servant leader for ALL her students, community, and staff.



Kareem Thompson, CEC Chapter 200 Special Education Teacher of the Year (Palm Beach Lakes High School)
Mr. Thompson has been instrumental in bringing forth the school reform initiative, Multi-Tiered Systems of Support/ Response to Intervention, to our secondary schools where general education teachers and special education teachers tend to work in silos.  He has been a stalwart on problem-solving teams, making sure struggling students get the services they need to be successful in school.  He is currently working with ESE students who have dropped out or are close to dropping out of high school.  These students would normally be a casualty, but with his encouragement and guidance they continue.  He works with them on a daily basis, providing facilitative support, praise, and unending encouragement to keep their "eye on the prize", GRADUATION.  Mr. Thompson will represent Chapter 200 as their 2012 Marjorie Crick Teacher of the Year nominee, aspiring to  become the 2013 Florida CEC Teacher of the Year and represent the State of Florida at the National CEC conference in April 2014. 



Lieutenant Colonel James Rodriguez, CEC Chapter 200 General Education Teacher of the Year(Atlantic High School)

Approximately one in every 800 children are born with Down syndrome, representing approximately 5,000 births per year in the United States.  While research and early prevention have resulted in dramatic improvements in the lifespan and potential of those who are affected, more investigation is needed in the causes and treatment of Down syndrome.  People with Down syndrome possess a wide range of abilities, and are active participants in educational, occupational, social and recreational circles of the community.  Developed by the National Down Syndrome Society in 1995, the Buddy Walk is an annual event in cities across Florida and the nation celebrating the accomplishments of children and adults with Down syndrome. National Buddy Walks are promoted to increase understanding and acceptance of people with Down syndrome, while raising funds for scientific research into the causes and treatments.

G1 - 21st Century Community Learning Centers (CCLC) Grant
Meeting
Oct 17, 2012 - School Board Meeting 10-17-12

The School District of Palm Beach County has been awarded two (2) competitive grants from the Florida Department of Education for the 21st Century Community Learning Centers (CCLC) Program for up to $6,160,000 for both grants over a five-year period, from August 1, 2012 through July 31, 2017.



The 21st CCLC Grant supports community learning centers for academic enrichment opportunities during afterschool and summer. These programs will assist students in meeting standards in core academic subjects, such as reading and math; offer students a broad array of enrichment activities to complement their regular academic programs; and offer literacy and other educational services to the families of participating children.



The two (2) grant programs target a total of ten (10) elementary schools (five schools in each grant) with a high percentage of low income students.  The 21st CCLC Shining Starz Project includes Heritage Elementary, Highland Elementary, Indian Pines Elementary, Greenacres Elementary, and Rolling Green Elementary. The 21st CCLC Whiz Kidz Project includes Berkshire Elementary, D.D. Eisenhower Elementary, Forest Hill Elementary, Lake Park Elementary, and Washington Elementary.



These schools were selected based on the grant requirements.  Each is a Title I school with large populations of English Language Learners (ELL), and each school serves students from communities/neighborhoods that have been designated as high risk by the Children's Services Council.  



The 21st CCLC grant budgets will support 60 at-risk students in grades K-5 at each school. These students will be selected to participate based on one or more of the following criteria:







  • below grade level on recent school and/or state assessments




  • students who have been retained or in jeopardy of being retained




  • ELL and/or ESE students




  • vulnerable students: foster children or children displaced from the earthquakes or natural disasters


The 21st CCLC goals in both grants are: 1) improve academic performance; 2) increase social competence; 3) increase parental involvement and family literacy; and 4) improve physical fitness and lifestyle choices. The 21st CCLC Grant requires and funds an external evaluator who will assess quantitative and qualitative data to determine the project's impact on these goals.



Services will be provided free to students and their families, including afterschool and summer programs. Student learning activities include the following components: literacy enrichment; Science, Technology, Engineering and Math (STEM) education; arts and music; technology; tutoring and mentoring; service learning; physical education, recreation, and wellness; and dropout prevention, character education, and drug/violence prevention.  Additionally, Parent/Caregiver activities focus on family literacy, including parent education in literacy and computer skills; parenting skills to support the child's learning; and inter-generational literacy: Parent and Child Together Time (PACT) to promote literacy and language development.



Each grant has been awarded up to $3,080,000 for a project period of five years, totaling up to $6,160,000 for both grants for five years.  The maximum award per grant is $700,000 per award for years 1 and 2; and $560,000 for years 3, 4, & 5.  Copies of both grants are available for review in the Board Minutes Office. 



The District received notification of these two grants on September 7, 2012. The Florida Department of Education will release the grant funds toward the end of October 2012, following attendance at a required state project meeting for all awardees.  Therefore, the 21st CCLC programs for the students will begin the second week in November.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve two (2) grant awards for the 21st Century Community Learning Centers (CCLC) Grants from the Florida Department of Education for up to $6,160,000, effective August 1, 2012 through July 31, 2017, and approve any no cost extension as applicable; and I authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents and contracts related to and required for implementation of grant activities.

The School District of Palm Beach County has filed a Notice of Intent to Apply for the new Race to the Top - District (RttT-D) Grant competition from the U. S. Department of Education, with a grant deadline of October 31, 2012.



The District intends to submit a four-year grant proposal, with a project budget of up to $40,000,000, to include teachers and students in all K-12 District schools.  The Classroom Teachers Association, as the District's primary partner, has collaborated to prepare this application. The application requires the signature of the Superintendent, the School Board Chair, and the President of the Classroom Teachers Association.



The Race to the Top proposal is centered on four core educational reform areas:
(a) Adopting standards and assessments that prepare students to succeed in college and the workplace and to compete in the global economy;
(b) building data systems that measure student growth and success and inform teachers and principals how they can improve instruction;
(c) recruiting, developing, rewarding, and retaining effective teachers and principals, especially where they are needed most; and
(d) turning around the Nation's lowest achieving schools.



The purpose of the RttT-D Grant competition is to build on these four core assurance areas to support bold, locally directed reforms and improvements in learning and teaching that will directly improve student achievement and educator effectiveness. 



The RttT Grant focuses on the following goals:





  • Create personalized  learning environments for all students in all of schools;


  • provide students and teachers with personalized  strategies, tools, and supports aligned with college and career ready standards;


  • accelerate student achievement and deepen student learning by meeting the academic needs of each students;


  • make equity and access a priority and provide each student the opportunity to pursue a rigorous course of study;


  • prepare each student to master content and skills required for college-and-career-readiness;


  • increase educator effectiveness and expand student access to the most effective educators;


  • decrease achievement gaps across all student groups; and


  • increase rates at which students graduate from high school prepared for college and careers.


The RttT-D Grant aligns with the District's Strategic Plan and specifically supports: Goal 1) Student First, Goal 2) Family Matters, and Goal 3) Highly Effective Workforce.



A copy of the grant proposal will be available for review in the Board Office.
