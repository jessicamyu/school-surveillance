DESCRIPTION:

Students from Boca Raton Community High School have won first place in the inaugural AP Global Challenge, a College Board pilot program that encourages Advanced Placement students to extend their learning beyond the classroom and connect their knowledge and skills to address globally relevant challenges through research, innovation, collaboration, and creative problem solving. Additionally, a team of students from Spanish River High School tied for third place.

 

During the Global Challenge program, student teams worked with a faculty advisor for three months to complete a final project — including a research-based proposal paper and multimedia presentation — in which they propose a solution that mitigates or solves the team’s chosen global challenge. The AP Global Challenge was piloted in 17 schools across the nation (including two Palm Beach County schools) from January to April 2014 and a total of 30 final team projects were submitted and scored by an international panel of interdisciplinary judges.

 

The third place team members from Spanish River High School include: Emmalyn Dalton, Alexander Deckler, Danielle Feinstein, Bethany Malzman, Jamie Shear and Aaron Siegel. Their faculty advisor was Caitlyn Calenzani. The team’s project was a thoughtful approach to issues relating to the beef industry. The team was complimented by the judges for their analysis and research and for evaluating how the project might work in several national contexts. Each of the students on the team will be awarded a $1,000 scholarship.

 

Boca Raton Community High School’s grand prize winning team, “Oodles of Utils”, examined a unique labeling strategy to inform consumers about the cultural, economic, and environmental impact of products they might purchase. Team members include: Phineas Agar, Ellis Galyon, Matthew Kirchman, Lawrence Richardson, Isabelle Tersio and Libby Vilmar. Their faculty advisor was Meredith Miller, AP Micro and Macroeconomics teacher.

I recommend the School Board approve receipt of the 2014-2015 competitive Title X, Part C - Education for Homeless Children and Youth Program grant funds in the amount of $100,000 effective immediately, and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents and contracts related to and required for implementation of grant activities.

DESCRIPTION:

The Title X, Part C - Education for Homeless Children and Youth 2014-15 grant funds help to provide services that comply with the McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act. The services facilitate the enrollment, attendance, and success in school of children and youth experiencing homelessness, including foster care children awaiting placement. Services to be provided include:

  • Provision of school supplies and uniforms to homeless students;
  • Identification of homeless children and youth through communication with schools, shelters, community agencies, and the Department of Children and Families/ChildNet;
  • Removal of barriers to school enrollment for homeless children, including the coordination of transportation to maintain educational stability;
  • Referral to appropriate social service agencies in order to receive additional services, including summer camp scholarships, child care, medical, food, and housing assistance;
  • Homelessness awareness training for school-based and community service personnel;
  • Referral to School Based Teams; and
  • Academic tutoring for homeless students who reside in homeless shelters.

I recommend the School Board approve the Cooperative Agreement with Youth Services International to provide alternative educational services from July 1, 2014 through June 30, 2015.

DESCRIPTION:

 

This new cooperative agreement between Youth Services International (YSI) and the School District is written to provide services to incarcerated youth. This program supports students in a residential secure facility for high risk males located in West Palm Beach, FL. The Palm Beach Juvenile Correctional Facility is a 98-bed residential treatment facility for male offenders between the ages of 13-18. Youth Services International (“YSI”) operates the program for the Department of Juvenile Justice. This facility is considered a Level 8 (high risk) residential facility. The average length of stay for the youth is 9-12 months. YSI offers many services to our residential youth, including case management, mental health and substance abuse services, medical services and psychiatric services. While in the program, youth receive public school services on site, provided by the Palm Beach County Public School District.

  • The goal of the program is the improvement of students’ behavior, social skills and academic proficiency to ensure a successful transition to  their home school or community after completion of the program.
  • Students receive career counseling and career skills training which improves their chance for employment.
  • Students have the opportunity to prepare and successfully take and pass the GED exam.
  • The School District is responsible for the educational staff and materials needed to provide the educational program.

I recommend the School Board approve renewal of the Cooperative Agreement with the Palm Beach Sheriff’s Office to provide educational services at the West (Belle Glade) and East (Main Jail) Detention Facilities from July 1, 2014 through June 30, 2015.

DESCRIPTION:   

This is the renewal of a Cooperative Agreement between the Palm Beach County Sheriff’s Office and the School District of Palm Beach County for the Educational Services Program (ESP).  

 

The ESP program serves eligible youths incarcerated in the PBSO Department of Corrections in the West Detention Facility in Belle Glade, Florida and the East Detention Facility at the Main Jail on Gun Club Road in West Palm Beach. Youths identified as non-ESE up to age 18 and 20 and youths who have been previously determined to be eligible for ESE services up to age 22 are served.

 

The School District reserves the right to determine if a youth is eligible to participate in the Educational Services Program until the age of 22, provided he/she was enrolled in a K-12 school prior to being incarcerated in PBSO’s Detention Facility.  The eligibility criteria serves many educational goals that are beneficial to our students and are necessary to:

  • Reduce or prevent a disruption of eligible student’s current educational services
  • Reduce or prevent high school dropouts

The School District is responsible for the instructional staff and materials needed to provide the educational services.
