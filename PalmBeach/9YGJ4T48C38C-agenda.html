2.     The Revised Schedule of Values Form was not Submitted to the Reports and Forms Review Committee for Review as Required.

Our Follow-up Audit revealed that Program Management staff utilized multiple versions of the Schedule of Values Form (Formerly PBSD Form 2349), and the most current version of the form being utilized had not been submitted to the Reports and Forms Review Committee for review as required by Board Policy 2.341 and its related procedures,  the District’s Forms Management Manual.  Management concurred with these findings; thus, the necessary forms have been approved by the Reports and Forms Review Committee and are accessible on the forms website as of April 30, 2015.

I recommend the Board receive and file the Internal Audit Report on District’s Technology System Acquisition Procedures.

DESCRIPTION:

The District’s Technology System Acquisition Procedures Audit was part of the Office of Inspector General’s Annual Work Plan for FY2014-2015, and the report was accepted by the Audit Committee at it's June 11, 2015 meeting.  The audit examined a twenty (20) systems sampling which incurred a total expenditure of $11,864,547 (21 percent of the total technology expenditures during 2009 through 2013), and included: 1.) Fourteen systems purchased and implemented by the Division of Information Technology (IT); and 2.) Six systems purchased and implemented by departments outside the Division.

 

Major conclusions of the audit are the following:

1.     IT System Acquisition Review/Approval Needs Improvement.   Eighteen (18) of the 20 sample systems were purchased during or after Fiscal Year 2009, i.e. after the three technology committees were established by the School Board or Superintendent.  A review of the meeting records and documentation of the three committees revealed the following:

 

·   One system was reviewed/approved by the three technology advisory committees.

 

·    Twelve systems, totaling $3,742,239 in cost, were not reviewed by the Superintendent Technology Committee (STC).  STC reviews and provides comprehensive based recommendations to District stakeholders on technology plans, projects, and acquisitions that have a significant impact on the District's operations, and provides guidance in establishing technology standards and policies.

 

·    Nine systems, totaling $2,544,678 in cost, were not reviewed by the Technology Clearinghouse Committee (TCC).  TCC reviews all new technology requests prior to purchase, to assure alignment with the District’s mission and goals and make certain the proposed technology serves the function intended without duplication.

 

·   Eleven systems, totaling $5,853,560 in cost, were not reviewed by the Technology Advisory Committee (TAC).  TAC, appointed by the Board, provides advice on the District’s technical infrastructure consistent with industry best practices and emerging technologies; the District’s Annual Technology Plan and the Five-Year Capital Plan for infrastructure technology elements; and, other technology matters as requested by Board or Superintendent.

 

2.     Improvements Needed for Technology System Acquisition Procedures. The audit reviewed the acquisition process for the 20 sampled systems against recommended best practices for technology systems acquisition.  The audit findings indicate:

·   Sixteen systems did not have documentation to demonstrate that any of the above essential phases for technology system acquisition and development was conducted.

·   One system had proof that some of the essential phases were conducted.

·   Three systems had records of all the phases and appeared adequate.

·   In 2012, the Division of Information Technology purchased and implemented the ProjectManager software to manage large technology project acquisition and development. However, the system was not utilized by non-IT departments for technology acquisitions.

 

3.     Technology System Acquired But Not Utilized.    During 2010, the Department of IT Infrastructure and Security spent $55,905 for the purchase of SnapManager software for Virtual Infrastructure.  Although the software was implemented, it was never utilized by the department. 

 

         Audit recommendations made, and agreed or concurred with by management, were as follows:

·   Technology system acquisitions should be timely presented to all technology committees for review and input; and relevant project information should be timely presented to the Technology Advisory Committee and School Board for constructive input for the system selection.

·   The Division of Information Technology (IT) should develop and implement a comprehensive district-wide project management process for technology system acquisition and development.

·   IT should provide input and participate in all acquisition and/or development of technology systems managed by non-IT departments.

·   Non-IT departments and schools should consider using the ProjectManager software for managing major technology acquisitions.

·   To ensure proper fiscal accountability, technology acquisition should be adequately planned and documented with justification accordingly.

 

Based upon the recommendation of the Audit Committee, the audit report has been forwarded to the District’s three technology advisory committees for review.

Twenty-eight (28) of the 249 students in the District’s Career Education 9-12 (OJT) test had exceptions involving reporting errors or records that were not properly or accurately prepared or were missing and could not be located.  None of the students in the Career Education 9-12 (OJT) test attended charter schools.

Recommendations Related to FTE Reporting.    The Auditor General recommended that District management exercise more care and take corrective action in sixteen (16) areas related to  FTE reporting and documentation.

 

Recommendations Related to Students Transported.  Based upon its audit findings, the Auditor General recommended that District management exercise more care and take corrective action, as appropriate, in eight (8) areas related to reporting and documents of students transported.

  • School Concurrency required the District maintain the adopted level of service (LOS) of no more than 10% above capacity at any given school (110% of capacity). As a result, the District's 5 Year Plan and associated construction program dramatically reduced crowding in District schools during the term of the agreement. ​
  • Many factors affecting school planning have occurred since the Concurrency agreement was first adopted in 2001. In the initial years of the agreement, the District was growing by about 5,000 students per year. Current enrollment remains relatively flat. In addition, the 2002 Class Size Reduction (CSR) mandate, which was phased in and became fully effective in 2010, is more restrictive than the School Concurrency 110% LOS requirements. Adding to this is the impact of charter school enrollment.
  • Due to changes to Florida Statutes, School Concurrency became optional in 2011, allowing local governments to explore other options for coordinated school planning. Through adopted provisions in local Comprehensive Plans, School Board policies and land development regulations, School Concurrency will continue to be in effect in Palm Beach County, until local Comprehensive Plans  are revised to discontinue or replace School Concurrency and related School Board policies are updated.
  • In November 2011, the School Board, Board of County Commissioners and League of Cities held a Tri-Board meeting to discuss topics of mutual concern.   At that meeting, the Intergovernmental Plan Amendment Review Committee (IPARC), which is comprised of all local government Planning Directors, was asked to work on a draft Interlocal Agreement that would update implementation of School Concurrency in Palm Beach County.
  • An IPARC subcommittee was formed, comprised of County, City and School District staff. Numerous meetings were held over several years and pertinent issues discussed.
  • To ensure transparency of the process and to allow others to follow the subcommittee's progress, a website was maintained: http://www.palmbeachschools.org/planning/SCCHome.asp
  • Three options were considered by the subcommittee, all of which included mandatory coordinated Planning requirements.  These options were:

1. Optional: Continue with optional school concurrency and make minor changes to the agreement for re-adoption.

 

2. Optional:  Implement an alternative to School Concurrency – School Capacity Availability Determination (“SCAD”). District staff would conduct an analysis regarding the impacts on local schools, including potential boundary changes required, and make recommendations that could be incorporated as conditions of development approval, dependent on the local government approving Board.

 

3. Mandatory: Enter into the required Inter-local Agreement with the County and Municipalities for Coordinated Planning to address population projections, joint use of facilities, school site selection, infrastructure planning, and school site consistency with local government comprehensive plans. (Required by F.S. 163.31777)

  • The Draft ILA recommended by the IPARC subcommittee is a combination of Options 2 and 3. The subcommittee determined the substantial changes to the school planning environment warranted a new approach. IPARC was kept appraised throughout the process and its input was incorporated.
  • On August 25, 2014, the IPARC subcommittee shared the draft agreement with the Executive Committee of the Issues Forum, comprised of elected officials from the League of Cities, County and School Board. Chairman Shaw represented the District at this meeting.
  • The Executive Committee recommended the following process for unveiling the new agreement:

1.      Place this topic on the September agenda for the PBC League of Cities Board of Directors for an update.  It was reviewed in September, 2014;

2.      Present to the School Board for approval before taking it directly to any of the other potential signatories; 

4.      Present the ILA to the Board of County Commissioners (BCC) for adoption; and 

5.      Allow interested Municipalities to participate in the agreement. (Cities that opt not to participate must enter into a separate agreement with the School Board in order to remain compliant with Sections 1013.33 and 163.31777, Florida Statutes.)

  • On April 30, 2015, IPARC reviewed the Agreement and suggested additional minor changes, which have all been incorporated.
  • The recommended Interlocal will be the successor to School Concurrency.​

Because of this increased revenue, the fee structure changed from the original maximum of $150,000 per quarter to the following:  The School District passes to the Safety Council 70% of the funds received from the County Commission. The remaining 30% is used by the School District as designated in the Dori Slosberg Driver Education Act for other Driver Education necessities. Since recent quarterly collections are approaching $250,000, pass-through funding to the Safety Council up to one million dollars is allowed.
