October is Down Syndrome Awareness Month.  One in every 733 babies is born with Down syndrome.  There are more than 350,000 people living with Down syndrome in the United States.  Down syndrome affects people of all ages, races and economic levels.   Life expectancy for people with Down syndrome has increased dramatically in recent decades - from 25 in 1983 to 56 today.  Quality educational programs, a stimulating home environment, good health care, and positive support from family, friends and the community enable people with Down syndrome to develop their full potential and lead fulfilling lives.

The Palm Beach County Parks and Recreation Department has developed quality, creative and high interest recreational modules for middle school students.  The funding from the County Commissioners is used to purchase these modules.  This unique partnership has afforded our after-school program and students with varied recreation opportunities; such as, computer building, animation, digital arts and video game design, drumming, fencing, karate, pottery, various types of dance, forensic science, etiquette, tennis, the Brain Game, flag football and other sports, Poetry Slam and Robotics to name a new.
Three recent studies cited below continue to support the positive impact participation in afterschool programs has for youth.
    1. .According to a 2007 report by the Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning (CASEL 2007), "youth who participate in after-school programs improve significantly in three major areas:  feelings and attitudes, indicators of behavioral adjustment, and school performance."
    2. The After School Corporation (TASC) programs demonstrated that participation by middle school students in afterschool is associated with positive outcome for youth, particularly with greater attachment to school.  (Russell, C., Meike, M., Miller, T & Johnson, J. (2007).  After-school programs and high school success.  Analysis of post-program education patterns of former middle grades TASC participants.  Washington, DC:  Policy Studies Associates, Inc.)
    3. Excerpt from the April 2007 After-school Alert Issue Brief, published by the National After-school Alliance. 
            o Young people face a number of dangers during the hours after school.
            o There are approximately 20 to 25 hours per week that children are out of school while most parents are at work, creating an after-school gap.
            o Self-care and boredom can increase the likelihood that a young person will experiment with drugs and alcohol by as much as 50 percent.  Youth tend to develop patterns of alcohol, tobacco, and other drug use - or non use - from ages 12 to 15.
            o Teens who do not participate in after-school programs are nearly three times more likely to skip classes at school than teens who do participate.  They are three times more likely to use marijuana or other drugs, and also more likely to drink alcohol, smoke cigarettes and engage in sexual activity than teens not in after-school programs.
            o After-school programs are a powerful antidote to youth crime.  They provide a safe haven that keeps kids away from violence.  They provide an alternative to gangs and street life, allowing kids to develop new skills and interact positively with peers.  They offer youth hope and opportunities, offsetting the sense of nihilism can cause youth to turn to crime.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the Recreation Consultant Agreements named within this agenda item to provided services in the Middle School Afterschool Programs and authorize the Superintendent and Board Chairman to sign all necessary documents.

This amended Cooperative Agreement with the School Board of Seminole County due to legislative changes in the Medicaid billing process.  The original agreement was entered into on June 4, 2008, and was based on the premise that Medicaid Administrative Claiming would officially end on 6/30/08.



Although backdating agreements is not our standard practice, it was necessary in this particular case due to new legislation that passed on June 30, 2008, which included the moratoria to extend Medicaid Administrative Claiming until April 1, 2009.  It does not predjudice the rights of the School Board.
 
School Districts have been encouraged to assume the Medicaid billing responsibilities in lieu of contracting with outside consultants. The School Board of Seminole County acts as the lead district in a consortium of school districts and serves as data manager and processor for Palm Beach County School District.



The cost to the District to implement this agreement will be as originally approved $3,000.  Preparation of the Medicaid Administrate claim for reimbursement was previously provided by an outside vendor at a cost of $146,000.  Approval of this cooperative agreement will result in a savings to the District of approximately $142,760 annually. Administrative claiming earned $3.5 million in revenue for the School District in FY07.

Recommendation:

I recommend approval of the confirming cooperative agreement with the School Board of Seminole County Public Schools to continue participation in a consortium for the Medicaid administrative claiming process and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all related documents.  The effective dates of the cooperative agreement are July 1, 2008 through June 30, 2009.

AC4-Master Affiliation Agreement FIAT Programs
Meeting

In order to address the need for new and well-trained teachers, FAU and its regional partners, which includes the School District of Palm Beach County, have created a three-prong, innovative approach that is practical, research-based and scalable.



Project FIT increases the number of education majors by paying college freshmen, sophomores and others interested in teaching to provide instructional support with model teachers.  This will increase the number and quality of students entering and remaining in the teacher preparation pipeline at all levels.  Undergraduate students will be receiving extensive classroom experiences and intensive, multi-year support with retired educator mentors, as well as a minimal stipend.  The students in the school classrooms will be provided with additional, qualified and caring undergraduates to directly support mutual learning in concert with the classroom teachers.



The Substituting with Mentors and Realistic Teaching (SMaRT) Program provides classroom experience to college juniors and seniors to work as substitute teachers with mentors.  A SMaRT site is defined as an elementary, middle, or high school where public school and university personnel work together to facilitate high levels of learning by all children of the school by using Florida Atlantic University students as substitute teachers to work under the direction of an FAU mentor. The relationship will be dedicated to improving the quality of pre-service and inservice teaching.  The School District of Palm Beach County and Florida Atlantic University will combine resources and provide leadership for the development of the SMaRT sites. 



The purpose of this relationship is to enable the parties to







  • improve the performance of students respective of each institution;




  • extend mutual research and grant capacity;




  • apply and model promising teaching practices;




  • promote targeted pre-professional instruction and continuous professional development to improve student performance;




  • inform and improve school and university instruction, and;




  • test innovative means to develop new education professionals.


The Accelerated Induction into Teaching (AIT) Program fills high needs' teacher vacancies with selected university seniors who are ready to student teach as the actual classroom teacher with daily support from a mentor.  Both the District's Academic Business Plan and Staffing Plan call for intensive and targeted recruitment strategies to insure the availability of highly qualified teachers for high needs schools. The AIT Program is an initiative that provides for the identification and placement of high-quality teacher candidates in high needs' schools.







  • AIT candidates will be selected from Florida Atlantic University's Teacher Training Program;




  • All AIT candidates will have successfully passed the CLAST and the Florida Teacher Training Program;




  • AIT will be supervised by an experienced Master Mentor hired by FAU and reviewed by the School District.  The Master Mentor will provide direct supervision/observation.  (see agreement);




  • The AIT Program management model incorporates three levels of supervision: Master Mentor, University Supervision, and Cooperating Teacher.




  • The AIT Program will reduce the need to employ alternative certification applicants and substitute teachers.


Recommendation:

Consider approval of the Master Affiliation Agreement FIAT Programs between the School District of Palm Beach County and Florida Atlantic University effective October 15, 2008.
