Norton Museum of Art-National Medal for Museum and Library Service
Meeting
Nov 18, 2008 - School Board Meeting
Category
Presentations

The Norton Museum of Art was recently nominated by Senator Mel Martinez and awarded the 2008 National Medal for Museum and Library Service. Each year, the National Medal is awarded to five out of the 17,500 museums nationwide, and it is the nation's highest honor for museums and libraries presented for outstanding community programs.  At the Norton Museum of Art, these programs include:



* Partnerships with Conniston Community Middle School and U.B. Kinsey/Palmview Elementary School of the Arts in West Palm Beach: Norton staff work with teachers to develop curriculum meeting state standards that integrates art in the museum's collection. The museum develops ongoing relationships with students, school staff and their families through workshops, tours, performances, exhibitions and family programs.
* Progressive Afterschool Art Community Education: The program serves 500 to 750 elementary through high-school students annually at six sites in under-served communities throughout the county. It is designed to promote self-esteem, creativity and critical thinking skills through art activities and museum tours.
* School Tours: More than 12,000 students toured the museum last school year. The museum provides lesson packets meeting state curriculum standards so teachers can prepare students for their visits.
* Last season's In the Hands of African American Collectors: The Personal Treasures of Bernard and Shirley Kinsey broke a record by attracting 6,700 students - more than twice as many as toured any other Norton exhibition.
* Professional Development Workshops for Teachers: The Norton trains public and private school teachers to integrate the visual arts into lessons.



All of these Norton Museum programs are provided free to students and the participating school teachers and staff as a service to the community.

Ms. Monahan's enthusiasm for teaching has also been recognized as she has earned the Outstanding Young Educator of the Year Award and Teacher of the Year Awards from her community.  She was also one of five finalists for the William T. Dwyer Award for Excellence in Education.

·      The District currently insures 23 locations with 138 buildings valued at approximately $531 million dollars and contents valued at $33.6 million dollars located in the applicable Flood Zones.   



·      Without this flood coverage, in the event of a federally declared flood disaster, the School District would not meet all Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) requirements for federal reimbursement.  Based upon current Stafford Act regulations, all locations in Flood Zones A or V are required to purchase coverage from the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP).  Without NFIP, FEMA will apply the maximum amount that could have been purchased (up to $500,000 per building and $500,000 contents) as a deductible prior to any FEMA reimbursement.



·      NFIP requires the District to purchase one policy per building, for all 214 buildings housed at these 23 locations.  The total insurance coverage is $82.0 million with individual policy deductibles of $50,000 for the building and another $50,000 for the contents.



·      Five years ago FEMA allowed increased deductibles (retentions) from $5,000 to $50,000 with a corresponding reduced premium of $167,779.  We again recommend the $50,000 deductibles with an annual savings of $234,505.

Subject
G2 - FDOE Application: Enhanced Instructional Opportunities for Recently Arrived Immigrant Children and Youth Grant
Meeting
Nov 18, 2008 - School Board Meeting
Category
New Business
Type
Action (Consent)
The School District of Palm Beach County is applying for the Enhanced Instructional Opportunities for Recently Arrived Immigrant Children and Youth Grant.  This grant is being offered by the State of Florida Department of Education for the amount of $597,909.00  The revenue from this grant will be used to continue and expand the Newcomer/New Beginnings Program in seven high schools.  This program will serve selected Haitian and Hispanic immigrant students (English Language Learners - ELLs) who have come to the United States with limited or interrupted formal education.  The goal of this project is to address the extreme academic and social needs of our immigrant students, thus closing the achievement gap and increasing graduation rates.  The funding from this grant will be utilized primarily to provide school-based, bilingual support personnel, tutorials and supplementary instructional materials for ELLs, parent involvement activities and professional development for teachers of ELLs.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the School Board approve the Florida Department of Education Enhanced Instructional Opportunities for Recently Arrived Immigrant Children and Youth Grant in the estimated amount of $597,909.00 effective January 2009-June 30,2009 and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents and contracts related to the grant which are required for implementation of the grant activities..

Financial Impact:

The financial impact to the Special Revenue-Other Fund is an increase in the estimated amount of $597,909.00 from the Enhanced Instructional Opportunities for Recently Arrived Immigrant Children and Youth Grant funds.  These funds will be allocated to the Department of Multicultural Education's budget.  All grant related expenditures and contracts are authorized for payment to the amount of revenue.

AC4 - High Touch High Tech In-School Field Trips
Meeting
Nov 18, 2008 - School Board Meeting
Category
New Business
Type
Action (Consent)
Consultant shall perform the following services:  In-School Field Trips that are a hands-on science enrichment program that transforms the classroom into a Living Laboratory.  Students can become a real scientist and learn by doing actual experiments.  The Programs are aligned with National and State Standards in Science and help build confidence for standardized testing.  They offer problem solving skills as well as build self-esteem and a positive regard for learning.  High Touch High Tech programs provide students with the opportunity to interact with their peers, share ideas and contribute to inquiry based discussions that support the way in which students naturally learn.  Funding will come from fees collected from the parents of the students who attend and will serve 17 elementary schools.
