I recommend the Board approve the application of Somerset Academy Avenir for any and/or all of the reasons stated by the evaluation instruments and the overall assessment, and authorize the Superintendent and/or his designee to sign all related correspondence and documents.

DESCRIPTION:

 

Overview

On February 3, 2020 the Department of Charter Schools received an application from Somerset Academy Avenir to open Somerset Academy Avenir in the School District of Palm Beach County in August 2021.

A review of the application was conducted using the required Florida Charter School Application Evaluation Instrument. 

Projected Enrollment

          First Year                K-5                 242 Projected Enrollment

          Second Year           K-5                 282 Projected Enrollment

          Third Year               K-5                 398 Projected Enrollment

          Fourth Year             K-5                 514 Projected Enrollment

          Fifth Year                K-5                  652 Projected Enrollment

 

Education Program Design

Mission Driven:  The proposed school’s educational program intends to fulfill the school’s mission of promoting a culture that maximizes student achievement and fosters the development of responsible, self-directed life-long learners in a safe and enriching environment.

 

Somerset Academy Avenir will implement the best practices of the Somerset Academy network of schools through a rigorous and well-rounded educational program, in accordance with the Palm Beach County Student Progression Plan and following a standards-based scope and sequence (see attached sample).

Since 1997, the Somerset family of charter schools has promoted a “one school culture” through a common philosophy of high expectations for all that leads to high student achievement through the use of data-driven differentiated instruction, individual accountability, a commitment to upholding the student code of conduct, an expectation of parental involvement, the neighborhood school paradigm, the use of common board configuration, experiential learning and collaborative infrastructure.

 

Data-Driven, Differentiated Instruction:  To ensure the needs of all learners are met and that each child realizes his/her highest potential, data from all available assessments will be used to develop and target instruction as well as monitor student progress. While all students will receive high-quality, data-informed instruction, the needs of diverse learners will be met through the implementation of differentiated supports and interventions. Students in need will receive additional instruction and remediation in targeted areas and more advanced learners will receive appropriately challenging instruction and enrichment. (Please refer to Section 3E and Section 4 for detailed curriculum, support services, and strategies to serve students entering below grade level, at level and above-grade learners as well as students with special needs).  Using this approach, the curriculum used will prioritize mastery of all applicable Florida State Standards, Next Generation Sunshine State Standards (NGSS), Florida Standards Access Points, and ACCEL options that provide academically challenging coursework to students in Kindergarten through 5th grade in all core subject areas. Additionally, the school will offer a rich array of special/electives in the arts, health, physical education, and world languages.

 

Certified teachers will deliver instruction directly aligned to the FS/NGSSS, in accordance with the Palm Beach County School District’s Pupil Progression Plan and will employ effective research-based strategies to maximize learning (referenced in detail by core subject in Section 4 below). The school’s master schedule will be designed as follows:
 

Instruction:

Kindergarten:  Core and electives courses are taught in a self-contained classroom setting.

1st - 3rd Grade:  Core courses are taught either in a self-contained setting or departmentalized according to student data, indicating specific needs. Electives are taught by special area teachers certified in the specific area of instruction given.

4th -5th Grade: Core courses are taught in a departmentalized setting by teachers certified in the specific course area when applicable. Electives are taught by special area teachers certified in the specific area of instruction given.

 

Subject Area Didactic Contact Time:

 

ELA: Reading:

K-5:  Standard: 90 minutes, consecutive, uninterrupted daily instruction. Intervention/Enrichment: 30 minutes’ minimum of additional instruction.

Writing: K-5: Standard: 150 minutes per week; Intervention: as needed through small group or one on one instruction.


Math:

K-5: Standard: 60 minutes consecutive, uninterrupted, daily instruction intervention: remediation must occur in one of the following ways: in a before/after school tutoring program, through small group or one on one instruction, or computer program to address specific student needs.


Science:

K-5:  Standard: 40 minutes consecutive, uninterrupted daily instruction. An additional 40 minutes of science lab time will be provided during the electives block each week. Intervention: remediation may be integrated into the standard science instructional time.

 

Social Studies:

K-5:  Standard: 40 minutes consecutive, uninterrupted daily instruction. An additional 40 minutes of global studies will be provided during the electives block each week. Intervention: remediation may be integrated into the standard social studies instructional time.


Electives:

Elective 1: Physical Education:                                                                                    

K-5: 150 minutes per week, 30 consecutive minutes per day, unless waived according to Florida Statute 1003.455(9), separate from the electives as described below.


Elective 2:

K-5: An electives wheel of 40 minutes per day in various special area fields of study and/or acceleration which will include the Physical Education requirement.


Homeroom:  Homeroom for K-5 character education which will address the statutorily required topics of Bullying Curriculum, Sexting Curriculum, Kindness to animals, the effects of intoxicants on the human body, mindfulness, as well as a comprehensive program in line with the district’s student progression plan.


Individual Accountability:  Students are held to a high standard and monitored individually through the use of progress monitoring plans that are specific to each student’s specific needs.


Student Code of Conduct:  Somerset Academy Avenir will use the School District of Palm Beach County’s code of conduct and disciplinary matrices for elementary and secondary education. In an effort to create an environment that is most conducive to learning and upholding high expectations, classroom disciplinary plans are created for each grade. Somerset schools believe in educating the whole child and will implement a character education program with the goal of instilling essential character traits that we believe are necessary for life success.

 

Parental Involvement:  Parental/Guardian involvement is a critical component of educational success. Somerset Academy prides itself on the success of our students and therefore requires that parents/guardians become active stakeholders in their child’s future. All Somerset Academy parents/guardians are asked to complete thirty (30) volunteer hours, per academic year.


Neighborhood School Paradigm:  Somerset schools ascribe to the idea that schools should be an active part of the neighborhood in which they operate. This includes drawing students into the program from the surrounding area, taking part in community events, and using local business when feasible.


Common Board Configuration:  Somerset Academy Avenir will use the common board configuration approach to help create a one school culture environment where students stay aware of what is expected of them while in school. The components of this approach include using student agendas and a pre-prescribed list of information to include: Date, benchmarks, essential question, vocabulary, bell ringer activity, exit quiz, and home connection.


Experiential Learning:

 

Collaborative Infrastructure:  To ensure the school implements the core practices of the Somerset Network, the governing board, and Somerset Academy Inc.’s administrative staff, will work together to oversee and implement the following practices/activities:

· Teachers/representatives from the Somerset network of schools will meet periodically (in person, conference call, or online) to plan and share best practices.

· Teachers will be trained on common board configuration - outlining the objectives, agenda, essential questions, and home learning assignments for each class.

· Principals will ensure implementation through modeling, mentoring, and classroom walkthroughs.

            · School will implement the same curriculum, materials, and scheduling process.

· Implementation of the same cornerstone elements: High expectations, individualized instructional models, targeted tutoring, and parental involvement through the School Advisory Council and an active Parent Organization.

· Fidelity of instructional programming by using appropriate grade/subject level pacing guides, focus calendars, and implementing the same state-approved curriculum in content areas.

· Ensuring teachers are on task and effective as measured by various processes such as formal observations conducted annually (bi-annually for new teachers), informal observations through classroom walkthroughs conducted daily, and ongoing review (at least quarterly) of student performance data and results as a means to inform instruction.

           · Using qualitative and quantitative data to inform and guide instructional planning.

DESCRIPTION:

 

Overview

On February 1, 2020, the Department of Charter Schools received an application from The Collegiate School of Riviera Beach to open The Collegiate School of Riviera Beach in the School District of Palm Beach County in August 2021.

A review of the application was conducted using the required Florida Charter School Application Evaluation Instrument.

            Second Year                K-8                     626 Projected Enrollment

Education Program Design

The school’s mission is to empower students to take ownership of their learning and inspire students to develop intrinsic motivation to achieve scholarly potential, for college and career readiness to lead in a global society. The guiding vision and philosophy of the School is to build an exceptional, world-class public school that proves students from all backgrounds can succeed in college and in life, along with showing that all students can learn when they are challenged, supported, respected, and motivated. Students benefit socially and emotionally, as well as intellectually, from being challenged academically. The School will target the lowest-achieving students in underserved communities to close the achievement gap and prepare students for college and a career. The School is founded on the belief that all students are capable of developing the skills and motivation required to complete college and to pursue the careers and lives of their choosing. Students will receive innovative, rigorous instruction combined with a positive culture for self-motivation to allow all students to become college and career ready. The School will organize around three key design elements to achieve our mission and vision: Academic rigor, a strong school culture, and structure and order. The School’s core set of principles, gathered from research are central to the school’s educational program to meet the mission and vision of preparing students for success in college and beyond. The School will use a “No Excuse” model where students will be held to high standards no matter what circumstances they come from. We believe all students are capable of achieving on the same level as their college-competitive peers. The School will provide a nurturing environment for students to demonstrate perseverance, which leads to achievement. The School’s “No Excuse” model has been proven extremely effective in raising student achievement with at-risk populations.

The model embodies the idea that all students will be successful, go on to college, and have the opportunities that follow the path of academic achievement. By focusing students on the task at hand and improving literacy (extended day with rigorous literacy curriculum) and cognitive ability (brain-based learning/visible thinking), “No Excuse” schools are unquestionably successful in raising achievement for all students. The flipped classroom, project-based learning, flexible grouping, and differentiated instruction will benefit underserved students, including students with disabilities, gifted students, and ELLs by creating an exceptional learning environment where active learning is taking place throughout the school day to provide the skills necessary to succeed in the 21st-century global economy. The innovative focus, data-driven decision-making, instructional program monitoring, instructional teaching strategies, ongoing evaluation of student learning, and timely and effective interventions will ensure a rapid closure of the existing achievement gap. Every student will develop leadership skills, good citizenship, a strong work ethic, and core values through our education through service-learning, community time, and character education. A focused, timely, and comprehensive system of supports will ensure that each student has the knowledge, skills, and resources necessary to be successful in school, college, and career. The School will provide the staff with ongoing professional development to meet the needs of each student.

The School’s education program is designed and will be executed with the following beliefs.

  • Students need a rigorous, challenging curriculum to successfully prepare them for both college and beyond.
  • Students need an engaging, relevant curriculum that fosters skill development, intrinsic learning, and critical thinking.
  • Students learn both from the community in which they reside and the global community; both are rich in real-life learning connections.
  • Students all have individual learning styles and instruction, must be differentiated so all students can succeed.

The Schools innovative educational program will meet the academic needs and interest of each student by providing:

Structure and Order with High Expectations: Creating an environment of order and structure, where the focus is teaching, learning, and achieving high expectations by mastering state standards.

Academic Rigor and Achievement: We focus on data-driven results and do whatever it takes to accomplish our goals.

Respect: A cultural atmosphere fostered where every individual and everything will be treated as each individual would in turn expect to be treated.

Integrity: The learning community is based on honesty and professionalism.

Perseverance: We never give up, for every challenge, there is a solution.

Self-Motivation: We remain positive and drive ourselves to achieve.

Strong Positive School Culture/Community: Every member of our community is treated as a stakeholder and takes ownership and responsibility to make sure students are achieving at their highest standards. Students, staff, and parents will feel valued, cared for, and respected. The School will create a partnership with parents for students to achieve.

The School’s educational program will empower students and will offer the following:

Academic Rigor:

The School will aim to serve the needs of all students. Our educational program will continuously improve to tailor to each student’s needs. As required by 1002.33, F.S., the School’s rigorous college-preparatory curriculum will be achieved by implementing the Florida Standards (FS) and Next Generation Sunshine State Standards (NGSSS). The school will deliver researched-based instruction and learning strategies that is driven by data, in accordance with 1002.33(7)(a)2, F.S. The School’s Educational Program will close student achievement gaps, including the areas of Reading/ELA and Mathematics. The School’s educational program is student-centered where the instructional approaches and academic strategies are intended to address the distinct learning needs and interests of the individual student. The School will focus on the academic achievement of elementary students, middle school students, ELLs students, students with disabilities, and gifted students, including preparing them for a successful transition to high school and college.

The School will keep all students engaged and meet the needs of students on, below, or above grade level, including students with disabilities, gifted learners, and ELLS. In accordance with the requirements of s. 1008.25, F.S., each student who does not achieve a Level 3 or higher on the FSA in ELA and the FSA in Math will receive additional diagnostic assessments to determine the nature of the student’s difficulty, the areas of academic need, and strategies for appropriate interventions and instruction. The School will adjust interventions depending on the student’s academic growth and attainment of grade-level/course standards through the PS/MTSS/RtI process. To maximize accessibility to the curriculum, students will access the state standards through appropriate programming, support from special education teachers, gifted teachers, ESOL teachers, regular education teachers, support in the use of assistive technology, and through the use of the universal design for learning. The School will serve the needs of ELLs across the curriculum, which is aligned with state standards. ELL students will be served in accordance with the district's ELL plan, 1003.56 and the students' ELL plan. The School will provide services and accommodations (if applicable) and serve students with disabilities and gifted learners to support attainment of the state-adopted standards, as required by section with 1002.33, F.S. The School will provide gifted or advanced learners enrichment or advanced courses. The School will feature the following student-centered, innovative instruction:

Visible Thinking:

The School will implement research-based Visible Thinking, through Harvard University’s Project Zero. Adopted from Harvard University, thinking about thinking builds learning skills as active processing time. The school will build a thinking culture with constant learning across subjects. Visible thinking will cultivate students thinking skills and dispositions, along with deepening content learning. The flexible and systematic research-based conceptual framework aims to integrate the development of students’ thinking with content learning across subject areas. The staff will be trained in thinking depositions. They will guide instruction with three core practices: thinking routines, the documentation of student thinking, and reflective professional practice. The School will create a thinking culture by using student engagement and understanding to show students thinking can be made visible through questioning, listening, documentation, and facilitative structures called thinking routines, which will support students who perform below, on, or above grade level, including students with disabilities, gifted learners, and ELLs. These routines are researched-based and were comprised of researchers at Project Zero Harvard, to scaffold and support one’s thinking, which will create higher-order thinking. Teachers will implement visible thinking during instruction as a tool for students to succeed with Webb’s Depth of Knowledge to increase rigor. By applying these processes, thinking will become visible as our student’s ideas will be expressed, discussed, and reflected upon. The School will create a culture of thinking using high-leverage thinking moves that are integral to understanding content:

Observing closely and describing what’s there

Building explanations and interpretations

Reasoning with evidence

Making connections

Considering different viewpoints and perspectives

Capturing the heart and forming conclusions

Wondering and asking questions

Uncovering complexity and going below the surface of things

Identifying patterns and making generalizations

Generating possibilities and alternatives

Evaluating evidence, arguments, and actions

Formulating plans and monitoring actions

Identifying claims, assumptions, and bias

Clarifying priorities, conditions, and what is known

Brain-Based Learning Strategies:

The School will implement a movement program, which is a school-wide recess from grades K-8 for passive processing for students to consolidate the content. Physical activity, movement, and positive social conditions increase brain activity. Incorporating movement and positive social conditions throughout the day makes students less fidgety and more focused on learning. Students will be able to participate in sports, clubs, dance, chorus, band, computer lab, and other activities for 45 minutes daily. Our movement program will improve on-task behavior, increase student attendance and motivation, and reduce classroom management challenges.

Blended Learning:

Students will learn in part through online delivery of content and instruction with the element of the students being able to control their learning over time, place, path, and/or pace. The School will provide a mix of technology and face-to-face instruction. Blended learning will be an integral part of in-class instruction and will be data and teacher-driven. The teacher will adopt technology and other classroom structural changes as a pedagogical tool and as a key mode of delivery of information in order to best individualize instruction to meet the differentiated needs of each student.

Flipped Classroom:

The school will feature this pedagogical model in which the typical lecture and homework elements are reversed. The school will implement instructional technology to “flip” (Bergman & Sams, 2011) the use of classroom time away from lecturing to facilitate active learning and individualized and differentiated instruction. This will allow teachers to facilitate increased active learning during class time and to deliver content outside of class time. The teacher will assign short video lectures to students at home or before or after school, or during movement time, while in-class time is devoted to exercises, projects, or discussions. The flipped classroom will provide active learning, student engagement, hybrid content design, and podcasting. The value of a flipped class is in the repurposing of class time into a workshop where students can inquire about lecture content and test their skills in applying knowledge. The teacher will function as a coach or advisor. Students will have equal access to technology through laptop carts or the computer lab. Most students and/or parents have cell phones that will support the videos/podcasts. Students who do not have access to phones or computers will have time to use the computer lab daily before or after school and during movement time. Students can also check out laptops at the media center. Teachers will ensure all students will have access to technology for the videos and podcasts. The School will have two (2) computer labs with twenty-four (24) computers in each lab. The School will purchase laptops for at least half of the student body for the first year of operation. Teachers will rotate throughout the day to provide blended learning. By the third year of operation, the goal is for each student to have a laptop for use during the school day (one-to-one). Students will only be able to use school-issued laptops during the school day. All videos will be accessible with tablets and cell phones.

Personalized Learning Plans:

Each student will develop their own personalized learning plan where the student, parent, and teacher will set their learning goals based on personal, academic, and career interests. The goal of the plan is to ensure equity, increase student achievement, and inspire student agency by providing a framework to help learners set, plan, and persist toward their academic, professional goals, social, and college goals. The plan will talk about the students. It will discuss their specific academic journey, goals, and motivations. This will be paired with performance data, and wrap it in a framework that records, monitors, and offers feedback as change occurs. The School will collaborate with parents when implementing the PLP, especially for younger students.

Connections Beyond the School Day: Extended Day/Enrichment and Intervention:

The School will offer an extended day with a focus on literacy and mathematics. It takes time, effort, and practice to meet the challenge of high academic standards. Additionally, recent studies suggest that there is a direct link between extended learning time and student achievement. Armed with evidence and examples of other high performing schools, the school day will be extended, running from 8:00 am to 4:00 pm. Research shows that schools in which high-risk students reach mastery have a relentless focus on developing literacy and math skills. In high performing schools, the majority of the time was dedicated daily to both English Language Arts/Reading and Mathematics. For students at the Collegiate School in grades K – 5 at least four hours per day will be dedicated to English Language Arts/Reading and Mathematics, for grades 6-8 at least 3 hours will be dedicated to English Language Arts/Reading and Mathematics. The added instructional time will help reinforce skills that students lack while simultaneously preparing students for rigorous high-quality expectations and standards when they enter high school and beyond.

A student who attends the school will receive more learning time than a student in the typical school district. This extended time will also allow for grades K – 8 to receive a second 45-minute recess/movement time and middle school grades to receive 45 minutes for recess/movement time daily. During this time, students can receive target tutoring, small group, and one-on-one instruction for students who need remediation in order to master skills and state standards. Students who are not in need of remediation will be able to meet for clubs, co-curricular classes (dance, band, chorus, sports), or study hall in the computer lab (for students who do not have technology at home to support the flipped classroom).

The School will provide quality extended learning opportunities that provide students with academic remediation, enrichment, and supervised activities and clubs. Research suggests that regular participation in programs that provide academic and social activities contribute positively to student’s academic and social development. Students will receive tutoring, academic enrichment target tutoring, and co-curricular activities daily. The School will provide students with extra support both academically and creatively, to help boost student performance, along with keep students engaged, motivated, and increase attendance.

Project-Based Learning:

The School will implement this dynamic approach in the classroom in which students will actively explore real-world problems and challenges and acquire a deeper knowledge.

Flexible Grouping:

Flexible grouping is a range of grouping students together for delivering instruction. This grouping can be whole group, small group, or with a partner. This strategy will meet the academic, social, and emotional needs of each student, and will allow the teacher to zero in on any specific needs of one student.

Differentiated Instruction:

A framework for effective teaching that involves providing different students with different avenues to learning. Instruction will benefit students below, on, or above grade level, ELLs, students with disabilities, and gifted students.

Data-Driven Decision Making:

The school is committed to using data to inform and guide instruction. The School will use data to give the teacher guidance to design lessons with a clear understanding of current student mastery. The school will continuously progress monitor students to measure student progress over the course of the year to reach mastery of state standards.

The School will use the Florida Continuous Improvement Model to monitor, evaluate, and analyze student data and drive teaching and learning. Teachers will have one full week to collaborate with the leadership team to analyze the results of assessments and design their lessons. Specific focus will focus on developing strategies for meeting the needs of students significantly below grade level. The significant amount of data generated by these assessments will not find itself in a binder in the school office. Rather, each teacher will use this data to generate action plans that are connected to lesson plans. The format of these lesson plans will be uniform throughout the school and will allow teachers to design targeted tutoring sessions and different small groups.

Intense Focus on Literacy and Math:

It is not surprising that schools that serve undeserved students reach mastery have a relentless focus on developing literacy and math skills. Students will receive 150 minutes (K-5th grade and) and 90 minutes (6-8th grades) of uninterrupted of ELA/Reading and 90 minutes of Math. The additional time will be broken down into separate ELA/Reading and writing classes. Math will be broken into problem-solving, remediation, and procedures classes. It is anticipated that the school’s student population will be significantly below grade level in both subject areas. This added time will help reinforce skills that students lack while simultaneously preparing students for rigorous college preparatory expectations and standards when they enter high school.

College Bound Culture/College Preparatory:

Studies show that strong college-bound culture, one that ensures high school is not viewed as the endpoint of education is essential for success in the 21st-century workplace. Every aspect of the school will emphasize attending and succeeding in college. Posters and slogans will be displayed on the walls of the school to broadcast the ultimate goal of graduating from college.

The School’s curriculum is aligned with Florida Standards and NGSSS, which prepares students for college success. Reminders of this strong college culture will be evident throughout the school. With banners in the classroom and halls, college class names, and college school visits every year; students will realize that college is an expected goal and that success in college is attainable with proper work ethic. Students will have the opportunity to participate in internships where they are not only shadowing successful college students but also learn what the college experience looks like.

High Expectations:

Schools that produce outstanding academic outcomes do so in large part because they expect outstanding outcomes of both staff and students. The faculty will set measurable goals for improved student outcomes on assessments. Improved student outcomes will be a goal for every student, regardless of proficiency level for every grade and in all core subject areas. By 8th grade, it is expected that students will meet or exceed state and federal accountability targets. All adults will be held accountable and take responsibility for their student’s outcomes.

Professional Development:

The School will offer a strong academic program that has the ability to support teachers in promoting high achievement amongst students with on-going professional development. The leadership team will provide strong coaching and feedback to teachers and conduct daily walk-throughs. The leadership team will also provide “in the moment’ feedback during classroom observations, as well as in weekly one-on-one meetings to encourage teacher development. The School’s curriculum supports instructional planning and the school’s assessment system improves instructional effectiveness and student learning. The leadership team will provide support by launching every unit with an explanation of the goals and related standards teachers will teach during the unit, thus allowing teachers to know what to teach and when to teach it. The school will administer weekly assessments in vocabulary, spelling, and no-hesitation math facts. Staff and parents will be able to access student achievement data with the School’s robust Student Information System.

Strong School Culture:

Community Time/Self-Motivation/Perseverance:

The School will provide students with social and emotional support necessary for school success. Students will participate in weekly sessions structured to express and address their needs and learn social and personal skills necessary for college and career success. Grade level students and staff will meet with the leadership team to discuss current challenges, academic or personal. Each student will also be assigned a small advisory group, named after the advisory’s alma mater (instill college and career readiness). Students will meet in small groups with mentors/advisors to talk and share their feelings and goals. This will help nurture a sense of belonging, competence, empowerment, and usefulness advisory will provide a connection for students and families to a single staff member to guide their education and support them socially and emotionally. Community time is designed to serve as a supportive family within the school. The School will foster family and community engagement. The School will provide an intimate setting and a family-like environment. The School will implement a school-wide commitment to student connections through its Community Time. Time is designed to serve as a supportive family within the school.

Education through Service Learning/Community Support:

The School will partner with local businesses and universities for students to volunteer and intern to receive hands-on experience and to learn about the many careers that are offered in the community. Every grade level will engage in service-learning activities. These activities will be most often conducted as an entire class or grade level and organized by the students and staff.

Character Education/Positive Behavior

The School believes that building both character and a sense of responsibility is crucial to a student’s education. The School will teach core values and provide consistent positive recognition of student success for motivation and a positive school-wide culture. All students will also participate in the character program to build positive life learning skills. Students will be expected to have high standards of behavior, ethics, and citizenship both in our educational community and the complex and diverse world. The School will challenge students to develop and practice qualities of respect, responsibility, honesty, and other elements of character.

Parental Involvement

Students' chances of success are more likely improved with active parental support. The result of meta-analysis studies indicated that parental involvement is associated with higher student achievement outcomes. The School will form a relationship with parents as partners in their child’s education. The School will extend its partnership to the larger community.

Provide Structure and Order:

Effective learning cannot occur in an atmosphere of chaos and disorder. Creating an environment of order and structure, in which teachers can focus on teaching and students can focus on learning, is essential to the ability of the school to deliver its mission. The school will instill a high code of conduct that is clear, detailed, and consistent throughout the school. The school will adhere to the “Broken Windows” theory of school discipline, believing that if a big deal is made about small issues, many big problems can be avoided. All students will be expected to wear uniforms, adhere to behavior requirements listed in the student code of conduct handbook during the school day, and actively participate in classroom activities. Various positive reinforcements will be used to support these high behavioral expectations.

Comprehensive System of Learning Supports: The School will provide learning support.

The School will have strong support for students who are performing below grade level. The school will offer resources and strategies to serve each student’s best interests and meet each student’s needs, including ELLs, students with disabilities, and gifted learners. The School will support its students by featuring an intimate and family-oriented school setting that creates a community atmosphere of courtesy and respect. Students will feel that they have individuals who care about them. The staff will be highly qualified to maintain and define behavioral and academic expectations and a constructivists focus on student’s needs, interests, and abilities, by adapting the curriculum to the student’s needs. For students who require additional support, we will provide the appropriately qualified staff and use interventions through the MTSS/RtI process to address each students’ needs.

The school’s learning support staffs’ philosophy is that every student has a basic right to a high-quality academic program to meet their unique needs to achieve their full scholarly potential, including students performing below, at, or on grade level, students with disabilities, ELLs, and gifted learners. The School will provide early intervention and prevention with students experiencing learning and other problems through the Schools Special Education and English Language Learner programs. The School will provide qualified personnel to provide services, resources, strategies, and practices that support, physical, social, emotional, and intellectual development and the well-being to enable all students to have an equal opportunity for success at school. The School will enable the effective use of learning supports by using instructional efforts, interventions, and professional development. These supports will be implemented in the classroom and school-wide to address barriers to learning and teaching and re-engage disconnected students. The school will use five approaches to achieve its learning support system:

  • "Classroom-based services approaches to learning" refer to efforts to improve or maintain the excellence of classroom learning. The School will provide qualified staff to meet the needs of each student including special education students, English language learners, and students performing below grade level.
  • "Supports for transitions" is an area that addresses major transitions between grades, schools, individual classes, or even family/life transitions such as divorce or moving.
  • "Home and family engagement in schooling" seeks to help families feel more involved in the education process.
  • "Community engagement" makes efforts to ensure local communities and the school district are unified as part of larger communities.
  • "Student and family interventions" serve to help students or families in social, psychological, physical, or financial need when other measures are not enough.

Recommended Action
I recommend the Board approve and adopt the FY 2021 through 2030 Capital Plan.

DESCRIPTION:

FY 2021 Capital Plan

  • Each year, the School Board is required to adopt a five-year capital plan prior to the adoption of the budget.  The District has expanded the plan to cover ten years so that all referendum projects can be included.
     
  • The first year of the plan is the FY 2021 capital budget which is included the budget that will be presented for approval later today.
     
  • As required by law, the capital plan is balanced.
     
  • The School Board last reviewed the capital plan on August 26, 2020.  Adjustments have been made to correct project budgets and ensure they align with the referendum project list, including plan modifications that were reviewed by the Independent Sales Surtax Oversight Committee (ISSOC) at their meeting on August 28, 2020.
     
  • The projects planned for FY 2022 through FY 2030 (years 2 through 10) can and will be amended as needed during the budget process each year.  Any future changes that impact projects on the referendum project list will be reviewed by ISSOC.
     
  • The Tentative District Education Facilities Plan, which is the state version of the capital plan, will be brought to the School Board at a future meeting after FDOE makes the online forms available.
