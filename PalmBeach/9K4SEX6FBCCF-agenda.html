Robert Pew-Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund
Meeting
Dec 15, 2010 - School Board Meeting
Category
Presentations
Type
Discussion

Since late 1998, the Pew Fund has awarded over $18,000,000 in grants to improve public education for economically disadvantaged children in Palm Beach County by developing, testing, and implementing new strategies for learning in classrooms and schools by supporting educational enrichment programs unavailable as part of the regular school curriculum.



Believing that it is skilled teachers and principal leadership that makes the most critical difference in improving academic achievement for each and every student, the Fund’s current grant making focuses on professional development for teachers, leadership development for principals and senior executives, and enhancements which include arts integration projects, efforts to address the summer slide in reading, and field trips.   The Fund’s staff works with potential grantees that have the vision, expertise, and talent to implement projects within schools and within the district. 



Originally, the majority of the Fund’s work targeted high-needs elementary schools that serve student populations in which 85% or more qualify for free or reduced-rate lunches.  The Fund now supports efforts to improve academic achievement in grades K-12.



The Fund was the first philanthropic entity to support Single School Culture for Academics, a locally-designed process in which weekly, facilitated learning team meetings were implemented in the Palm Beach County School District’s highest-needs schools.   The process has engaged and trained over 8,600 teachers in 108 schools in the use of data to drive instructional practice and ensures that instruction is based on State standards.  Between 2004 and 2008, the high-poverty, high-needs schools that participated in the Single School Culture for Academics process experienced a 9% change in students reaching proficiency as defined by state standards in reading, and a 12% change in students reaching proficiency as defined by state standards in math. 



Quality professional development has become the hallmark of the Mary and Robert Pew Public Education Fund.   The Fund continuously seeks to institutionalize our most effective grants within the schools or the District, as reflected in the Single School Culture for Academics work.  Current efforts with Columbia University’s Teachers College Readers and Writers Workshop appear promising in helping to raise achievement in reading and writing literacy in high-poverty schools.   High-quality, on site, professional development is provided using the Writers and Readers Workshop models in Palm Beach County public schools.  The Fund is taking a regional approach in which, after the schools have mastered the model, they will then serve as lab schools to transmit the techniques to surrounding schools. 



The Fund has supported arts education and served as a convener of community and public school stakeholders to improve arts integration in public schools.  Currently, an arts integration pilot project in three elementary schools is transitioning into a lab school approach to expand arts integration strategies into additional schools.



The Pew Fund is gratified with some success in our enhancements grants focused on music and band for elementary schools students.  Grants to increase music programs by providing band instruments at three schools serving 90 students per year, and band experiences for elementary school students in partnership with Florida Atlantic University, have provided these same students with opportunities to participate in individual and small group music lessons conducted by FAU College of Education graduate students, and one-week summer band camp experiences at FAU’s Boca campus.  These are opportunities that students from South Grade Elementary, Highland Elementary and KEC/Canal Point Elementary, would have never experienced without assistance from Mary and Robert Pew.   Recently, an instrument grant for strings and band instruments was awarded to North Palm Beach Elementary.



An evaluation of the Fund’s World Music Drumming replication project in 18 high-needs schools indicates that the cohort of at-risk children participating in the program had 45% fewer total absences.  Of the sixty-two children not on grade level in reading at the start of the program, 29.6% showed significant academic improvement after one year of participation, and 42.9% showed significant academic achievement in math.   



Pew Fund’s enhancement efforts include field trips for all Glades area elementary school students.  Over $200,000 is granted annually to ensure that each of the over 3,000 children attending these seven schools participates in at least one or more quality field trip experiences not normally offered in these high-poverty schools.  Aligned with the Sunshine State Standards, the field trips provide the opportunity for children to expand their minds, providing the background knowledge these young students need to better comprehend what they read, and improves social skills. 



Since 1998, Robert Pew’s philanthropy has impacted tens of thousands of students, hundreds of teachers, and nearly 100 administrators.

November 24, 2010 marked the 35th Anniversary of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act, the federal law that governs our ESE students with disabilities.  In 1975 Congress passed what was then known as Public Law 94-142 or the Education for All Handicapped Children Act.  This law guaranteed all children with disabilities access to free, appropriate, public education in the least restrictive environment.     Subsequent amendments to the law now known as the IDEA have lead to an increased emphasis on access to the general education curriculum, the provision of services for young children from birth to five, transition planning and accountability for the achievement of students with disabilities.  Thanks to the IDEA, there are 6.6 million infants, toddlers, children and youths with disabilities nationwide who are achieving at levels that would not have been imagined in previous decades.  Here in Palm Beach County, we proudly provide a free, appropriate, public education to over 26,000 ESE students with disabilities.



To assist in recognizing this landmark anniversary, we have some individuals who will share how the IDEA has impacted their lives

This is an agreement between HABITAT for HUMANITY and the SCHOOL BOARD of PALM BEACH COUNTY’s Construction Academy at Seminole Ridge Community High School whereby the two parties will work together to provide HABITAT with a pre-fabricated, modular House unit to be delivered to and installed onto a foundation to be provided and installed by HABITAT.



 



·        SCHOOL BOARD AND HABITAT will jointly develop architectural drawings (no cost to SCHOOL BOARD) for the house that will be completed with the understanding that the house will be constructed in 4 parts: complete, rectangular modular units consisting of 4 walls each, a structural wood framed floor and ceiling joists so that the interior of the units can be completely finished and painted prior to shipping the modular units to the property.  The interiors will be fully plumbed and wired, inspected, drywalled, and fully painted.  Cabinets will be put in place, plumbing fixtures installed, doors, trim, and hardware installed, air conditioning, heating unit (AHU) in place (no ductwork), toilet accessories installed, windows and doors installed and trimmed on the inside.  Each modular unit will be fully wired and electrical covers installed on switches and receptacles.  All will be inspected during the rough-in stage during construction at the school site.  The electrical systems for the separate modular units will be connected together by HABITAT at the Property.  The roof will be built in sections of pre-fabricated wood trusses with plywood sheathing and then dried in, ready for shipping and assembly as whole roof sections onto the house boxes at the property.



 



·      HABITAT will provide all necessary materials and disposable tools to the school for the proper construction of the modular house units in a timely manner.



  



·        HABITAT will pay for the on-site services of the project electrical contractor, plumbing contractor, and air conditioning contractor for their respective work at the property for making all final connections of their systems at the assembled House at the Property. 



 



·       HABITAT will provide the SCHOOL BOARD with a payment to reimburse for the electrical tools, equipment, and supplies consumed by the SCHOOL BOARD’s process of constructing the modular units (i.e. saw blades, broken drills, drill bits, damaged nail guns, etc.)  Said payment shall be paid by HABITAT upon the SCHOOL BOARD’s presentation of invoices for replacement tools, parts, service, and disposable tools and to not exceed $5,000 for the term of this contract.



 



·        Each student working on this Project is covered by the SCHOOL BOARD’s insurance policies.  Additional medical insurance will be purchased by Seminole Ridge Community High School.



 



·        The start of construction of the modular house shall commence one day after the issurance of a single family home construction permit.

I recommend that the SCHOOL BOARD approve the Agreement with the HABITAT for Humanity effective January 4, 2011 through January 4, 2013 and authorize the Superintendent or designee to sign all documents and amendments related to the agreement which are required for implementation.

The Plumosa School of the Arts project was originally planned for the old Plumosa Elementary School site.  During the planning process it was decided to build a kindergarten thru-5th grade (K-5) school of the arts and provide space to expand it to a kindergarten thru 8th grade (K-8) school of the arts at a later date.  The old Atlantic High School site had sufficient land for a K-8 school and already had an auditorium.  The decision was made to locate the Plumosa School of the Arts on the old Atlantic High School site.



Plumosa School of the Arts, K-5 is a Leadership through Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) "green" project and will at a minimum achieve a silver rating.  The new structure at Plumosa SOA is a K-5 two (2) story building that included classrooms, administration offices, clinic, and media center.  There is also a new physical education center, play courts and play equipment.  This comprises 105,087 square feet.



The existing cafeteria from the old Atlantic High School was renovated to accommodate both the K-5 and future 6-8 middle school of the arts.  The existing auditorium from the old Atlantic High School was renovated to include Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requirements, curtains, rigging, minor lighting and sound upgrades.  The existing square footage of the two (2) buildings is 35,827 bringing the total square footage for Plumosa SOA to 140,914.  The project was occupied in August 2010.



This Project Modification contains the following items:







  • Construction Change Directive (CCD) #1 for the addition of one hundred eighty (180) calendar days, the replacement of the cafeteria windows and the replacement of the auditorium stage floor in the amount of $281,100.00.




  • Description:  Extend the Substantial Completion date from June 19, 2010 to December16, 2010 and extend the Final Completion date from October 2, 2010 to March 31, 2011.  Increase the Construction Phase Fee (Construction Manager Staff Costs) and General Conditions (trailers, phones, etc. required for the Construction Manager staff) as a result of the project duration being extended because of concealed conditions. Replace the cafeteria windows and the auditorium stage floor.




  • Cause:  Unforeseen (Concealed) Conditions




  • Justification:  There were two (2) concealed conditions that impacted the completion of the project:  (1) the replacement of the exterior windows in the cafeteria due to water damage and (2) the replacement of the stage flooring in the auditorium due to termite damage.


Item #1 - Cafeteria Windows
The Construction Manager's scope of work included painting the exterior and completely renovating the interior of the existing kitchen/cafeteria.  The windows were to be reused.  When the contractor removed the drywall and caulking around the windows, we discovered that the concealed portion of the window frames were badly rusted and the windows needed to be replaced.



Item #2 - Auditorium Stage Floor
The scope of work in the auditorium included the removal of the orchestra pit.  During the removal of the orchestra pit (safety issue) the contractor encountered termites.  The entire stage was infested.  The stage had to be removed, the area treated for termites and a new stage installed.  The stage floor was removed and a new one was installed, however, the sanding, staining, and finishing had to be delayed until the work on the stage rigging and lighting was completed to avoid damaging the new floor.  This caused the completion of the floor to be delayed until the week of November 8, 2010.



This CCD pays for the general conditions, phase fees, and the replacement of the cafeteria windows, and auditorium stage floor.
