BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue our improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting district wide, we are implementing a new process streamlining our Independent Contractor Agreements. Our goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

BACKGROUND:  In September 2003, the district went live with a new Student Information System (SIS) named Zangle.  Over the years, Information Technology and Support Services (ITSS) staff have kept the Zangle system current by applying scheduled upgrades, patches, etc. in order to fix, enhance, or stay current with mandated changes.

 

In late November 2010, the district was notified that C-Innovation, Inc., the company that had provided and was supporting the Zangle SIS system, had gone out of business.  This unexpected circumstance resulted in an immediate urgency to develop plans to both support the existing Zangle system in the short term and to replace the existing Zangle SIS system with a new student information system in the long term.

 

The plan developed in the short term to support the existing Zangle system was for ITSS to immediately freeze any programmatic future and pending changes to the Zangle system.  By freezing any proposed changes, then the district significantly reduced the possibility of unrecoverable problems with the existing Zangle system. This action was implemented and remains in place at this time.

 

The plan developed in the long term to replace the existing Zangle system was to develop a Request for Proposals (RFP) for a new SIS in order to make this recommendation to the school board at this time for a replacement SIS.

 

On September 13, 2011, the board approved an agreement with Gartner, Inc. to provide a review of the district's five-year Enterprise Software Plan for Oracle PeopleSoft, Oracle Business Intelligence, Zangle, and Non-Instructional Computer Refresh.  On December 13, 2011, the board approved Gartner's review of the district's five-year Enterprise Software Plan.  In its review, Gartner identified current situations, challenges, and the Strategic Goal of the district to replace the existing SIS (Zangle).  

 

On February 16, 2012, the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department released an RFP for a new Student Information System.  The RFP was developed based upon a thorough understanding of the needs of the district for its new SIS.  It also incorporated information received from an earlier Request for Information (RFI) by the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department in May 2011, which resulted in the receipt of information which identified the technology available in student information systems today, including an online student registration module, an informative parent portal, and comprehensive school site reporting tools.  A pre-proposal conference was conducted on March 2, 2012, to ensure that prospective proposers completely understood the requirements of the RFP in order to submit their best proposals to the district.

In order to maximize the usefulness of the presentation, a cross-section of the district's SIS user community was invited to attend the presentations and to provide feedback to the evaluation committee by utilizing a questionnaire developed by ITSS.  This feedback from the SIS user community was then compiled and presented to the evaluation committee.

 

After the evaluation committee viewed the presentation of both proposers, reviewed the feedback about the presentations from the SIS user community, and completed its oral interviews of each proposer, then the members of the evaluation committee ranked NCS Pearson, Inc. as the better proposer to provide the district with a new Student Information System.

 

Upon approval of this recommendation, ITSS will then begin the process of configuring the NCS Pearson Student Information System, perform a data conversion, complete the necessary interface development, and establish a training program.  Upon completion of these initiatives, preparation for the conversion of the existing SIS operations to the new NCS Pearson SIS will commence.  Afer this conversion is completed, then the process of converting all schools onto the NCS Pearson SIS will begin.

BACKGROUND:  Government Code section 4217.10 et seq. allows a school district to enter into an energy management agreement on terms that are determined by its governing board to be in its best interest.  In or about 2009, six companies responded to the district’s Request for Proposals.

 

On May 25, 2010, the board approved a non-binding Letter of Intent that allowed Main Street Power Company to secure rebates through the California Solar Initiative and gave its approval to enter into negotiations with Main Street Power Company to establish a Solar Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) with the district.   The negotiations resulted into two PPA’s: one PPA is designated as “New Markets Tax Credits” (NMTC) available to Main Street Power for qualified low income areas as defined in IRS Code and, another PPA for the balance of the areas designated as non-NTMC. 

 

On September 14, 2010, the board approved these two PPA’s for NMTC sites and non-NTMC sites, allowing Main Street Power Company to own, install, and maintain solar panels on roofs of designated district properties, and then sell the energy generated from the solar panels back to the district at a discounted rate compared to SDG&E’s rate.  The solar panels pertain to 36 district sites.  At the end of the 20 year agreement, unless otherwise agreed by the parties, Main Street Power Company will remove the solar panels at its own expense.

 

The rooftop system designs required to meet the structural requirements of the Division of the State Architect (DSA) prompted a need to clarify and modify the PPA’s as originally approved. As such, the First Amendments clarify the maintenance and inspection obligations and also more clearly define the roles and responsibilities of the district and the Main Street Power Company, Inc. in the implementation and ongoing administration of these agreements.

BACKGROUND: For most students across the country, the harsh reality is that presence of technology is the norm for students in every aspect of their lives except at school. In fact, some equate going to school with students having to take a seat and power down for six hours.  Not so in i21 classrooms across the San Diego Unified School District and this Learning On the Go (LOGO) video highlights 10 diverse neighborhood schools around the city, where teachers have fully utilized their 21st century classrooms and have

taken digital instruction to the next dimension!  San Diego Unified’s i21 interactive classroom initiative enhanced by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) pilot program, Learning On the Go, has set the stage for dramatic changes in teaching and learning.  At these LOGO schools, Netbooks with 3G mobile broadband access are distributed to all students for 24/7 internet connection with their schoolwork, teachers and classmates.  Knowing that every student, regardless of background or socioeconomic status, has access anytime/anywhere to robust online curriculum has enabled teachers to truly innovate their teaching practices and change their instructional design. This allows students to develop  21st Century skills on a daily basis, at school, at home, and anywhere in between.

It is also having a dramatic effect on teaching and learning. Teachers at these LOGO schools have embraced the robust use of the district-supported Learning Management System, Moodle, as well as the utilization of multiple Web 2.0 tools to foster collaboration, critical thinking, and creativity by dramatically turning traditional instruction “upside down.” Math teachers, Julie Garcia and Mike Salamanca, were the first teachers in the district to share their ideas involving the “flipped” classroom. Flipped learning happens when the teacher’s lecture is delivered to students via video outside of the classroom. Then traditional class time is used for active problem solving and one-to-one or small group tutoring with the teacher. Students can watch the short video lectures as many times as they wish to grasp the content and then come to class ready to jump into the lesson, ask questions, work on collaborative projects and explore the content further. Students take a quick online assessment based on the video and are placed in differentiated groups, allowing the teacher to provide a more personalized learning environment with one-on-one attention and small group instruction.  About “flipping”,  Julie Garcia says, “I feel like I really know my students now because I can tell you after a 50 minute period I’ve probably talked to every single student at least three or four times and that kind of personal attention for students you don’t necessarily get with direct instruction.”  These “flipping” advocates at our schools have inspired others across San Diego County and beyond through their presentations and workshops involving “flipping” at the San Diego CUE Conference, the National CUE Conference, among others.

BACKGROUND: Passage of this resolution will allow the district to use state school facilities funds at a different grade level than the proposed project.  Specifically, this resolution allows the district to transfer funds eligible for high school construction to elementary school construction to help build Salk Elementary.

 

After the Scripps Ranch/Mira Mesa High School Attendance Area (SR/MM HSAA) elementary school eligibility was used for the construction of Ellen Browning Scripps Elementary School in 2005, the eligibility left for elementary school new construction is 212 pupil grants.  For Salk Elementary, the district is building at least 32 classrooms which equates to 800 pupil grants at 25 students per classroom.  To maximize the basic grant we can get from the state (approximately $9.5 million), the district will need an additional 588 pupil grants.  The high school new construction eligibility for SR/MM HSAA is 1150 pupil grants. Under Section 1859.77.3(b) of Title 2 of the California Code of Regulations relating to the Leroy F. Greene School Facilities Act of 1998, the district will use 588 of the high school new construction eligibility to apply for state funding/reimbursement for the Salk project, leaving a total of 562 pupil grants eligibility for future new high school construction use at this area.   Thus, the district will be able to construct Salk Elementary with funds based on the eligibility of SR/MM HSAA.
