  • District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education: Reviewed draft LEA Plan - November 19, 2014.  Additional information and discussion on January 21, 2015.

BACKGROUND: The California Department of Education (CDE) requires that districts annually review their LEA Plans and update them as necessary. The district LEA Plan was last updated and approved by the board on October 8, 2013. This updated LEA plan (attached) was generated from the state's California Accountability and Improvement System (CAIS) that allows us to enter data electronically to monitor implementation. It is important to note that our LEA Plan was the first to be given approval to depart from the state's template and instead, use the district's 12 indicators of quality schools as the structure for the plan.

 

During the 2013-14 school year, San Diego Unified collaborated with stakeholders across the district to create the state-required Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP). The resulting LCAP, like the LEA Plan, provides an overview of key actions and services to promote the achievement of all students, while also addressing the needs of identified student groups (e.g., low income, English learners). In addition, both the LEA Plan and the LCAP use the district's 12 indicators of quality schools as the structure for the plans. It is intentional that these two plans should align and provide a coherent and cohesive structure and plan for improvement that are grounded in, and reflect, the district’s Vision 2020 approach to ensuring Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood. Although the CDE is working to align, or combine in some way, the LEA Plan and LCAP, there is no near-term resolution. We, however, have begun this work through this initial revision/alignment of the LEA Plan.
 

Following the review by subject matter experts, most of the LEA Plan remains substantially unchanged – but reflects the alignment of key LCAP actions and services, as well as adjustments for details such as time, duplicate tasks, dates, and names of responsible parties.

BACKGROUND: The Child Nutrition and WIC Reauthorization Act of 2004, mandated that all local educational agencies participating in the federal school meals program have an established Local Wellness Policy. These Wellness policies are unique to each district and include goals for nutrition education, physical activity and nutrition guidelines for all foods available on campus.

 

The Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010

On December 13, 2010, President Obama signed the Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010 (HHFKA) reauthorizing the Child Nutrition Programs. Section 204 added Section 9A to the Richard B. Russell National School Lunch Act (Title 42, U.S. Code 1758b), Local School Wellness Policy Implementation. The provisions enhanced the previous Local School Wellness Policy requirements, strengthening requirements for ongoing implementation, assessment, and public reporting of wellness policies and expanding the team of collaborators participating in the wellness policy development to include more members from the community. The HHFKA now requires that the local school wellness policy, at a minimum, include:

  • Goals for nutrition promotion* and education, physical activity, and other school based-activities that promote student wellness.
  • Nutrition guidelines for all foods and beverages available on school campus during the school day.
  • Requirements that Stakeholders be provided opportunities to participate in the development, implementation*, and periodic review and update* of the wellness policy.
  • A plan for measuring effectiveness, that is measured periodically and that the assessment is made available to the public.*
  • Public notification informing and updating the public (parents, students, and others in the community) periodically about the content and implementation of the local school wellness policy.*
  • Local designation must include one or more local education agency officials or school officials to ensure that each school complies* with the local school wellness policies.

*These are new requirements.

 

It is the Wellness Committee's belief that healthy students not only excel academically but are more likely to be positively engaged in school. According to the National School Board Association, research shows that when school districts and schools have effective policies and practices that support the health of their students and staff; student and staff absenteeism decrease, student concentration improves, student behavior problems are reduced, and children and adolescents establish lifelong health promoting behaviors. The committee believes this policy to be clear and comprehensive. Which will promote the health of students and staff. As well a support the district's goals as stated in Vision 2020, and in the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP).

 

Additionally, this agenda item includes a resolution to incorporate San Diego Unified School District into the Live Well San Diego Initiative.  Live Well San Diego is the County of San Diego's roadmap to achieve its vision of a safe, healthy, and thriving county.  This resolution will be put forward for action at a future board meeting.

RECOMMENDATION: Ratification of Agreement with Streamlink Software, Inc. to provide Amplifund-Full Cycle and Grant Finder Software Access to support the district's grant management process (SW-15-0836-19).  The term of this Agreement is December 19, 2014 through December 18, 2017.

BACKGROUND: The use of Streamlink Software, Inc. applications (Amplifund-Full Cycle and Grant Finder) brings consistency and efficiency to the district’s grants management process by replacing paper-based processes that require multiple people to generate, review, and approve documents. 

 

In addition, the applications have replaced manual financial and outcomes tracking and reporting tasks that involved considerable time creating and maintaining offline excel sheets.  Financial information is being extracted from PeopleSoft and uploaded to Amplifund so that grant managers and Finance Division staff have current information on grant expenditures, balances, and reporting benchmarks.  In addition, potential grant applicants (e.g., site staff members), have a more streamlined and prompt process to monitor if their requests for grants have been reviewed and approved. A reporting module will allow for reports to be quickly and easily generated to show the status of grant applications in addition to open/active grants. The tools also provide for the tracking and ability to report on significant donations or anticipated donations made to individual schools or departments in order to provide the board an opportunity to review anticipated donations prior to acceptance.

 

As part of research into grant-management tools, the Public Information Division had three different vendors provide a demo and pricing:  Ampliifund, eCivis, and Abila.  Preliminary research on pricing for such tools was conducted to see if the anticipated costs would be such that a competitive bid process would be required.  This research showed the pricing was well under the required threshold, but multiple vendors were contacted to ensure research was thorough and inclusive. Upon review of the pricing and features, and after seeking input from businesses (including school districts where available) as to their experiences with these tools, and based on the pricing and functionality, Amplifund was selected.

 

The applications include:

  • Robust pre-award to award tracking and reporting
  • Automated reminders
  • Automated grant search capability
  • Integration with PeopleSoft financials via file transfer/upload
  • Unlimited file space
  • Unlimited users
