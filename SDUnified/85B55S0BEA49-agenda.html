Subject
F.02. Proposal to Eliminate Non-Teaching Half Days, effective 2010-11 and for the Duration of the SDEA Collective Bargaining Agreement (de Beck)
Meeting
Mar 30, 2010 - - 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No
RECOMMENDATION: Eliminate all non-instructional days when teachers are on duty, no matter what the activity is called, or what its funding may be, for the duration of the recently ratified San Diego Education Association (SDEA) collective bargaining agreement.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Yes, certificated teaching staff.

BACKGROUND: Board member John de Beck proposes that San Diego Unified should eliminate all non-teaching or minimum days, commonly called “staff development days.” The purpose of this proposal is to increase instructional time for students.

The district and SDEA recently agreed to shorten the instructional year by having up to five furlough days. The Association chose for the furlough days to be days when teachers and students would normally interact in their classrooms. This meant that many students were deprived of many half days of instructional time even though the teachers were on duty and available. While the fractional days vary, the fact is that students will be losing valuable instructional time when teachers are available. Any loss of instruction must be considered detrimental to student achievement according to research about time and its effects on instruction.

The Association has maintained that “teachers are the experts in instruction,” and the district certifies that we have “highly qualified” teachers in our classrooms. To send kids home while these expert and highly qualified teachers engage “essential professional development” seems particularly ironic. Essential staff development for professionals can be arranged and paid for outside the school day.

Our students deserve all the instructional time they can get, and sending them home on “minimum days” seems counterproductive.

Board member John de Beck proposes that the board unanimously approve eliminating all non-instructional workdays, starting in September 2010, and throughout the currently bargained teaching contract.

Subject
H.03. Proposition S Use of Potential Construction Savings; BUDGETED: YES, PROPOSITION S FUNDS (OE-9)
Meeting
Mar 30, 2010 - - 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Option B for the use of early program savings in Proposition S.


Option B: Return all cost avoidance dollars from the near-term favorable bid climate, to the program to accommodate future cost overruns, “pop up” requirements, and anticipated inflation for projects later in the program.

FISCAL IMPACT: Option A may cause the scope of future projects in the Prop. S program to be reduced.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: On March 16, 2010, district staff presented a board workshop on Prop. S that provided an overview of the current accomplishments and status of the Prop. S program. Staff requested board direction regarding the use of cost savings on the occasion that individual project costs come in under budgeted amounts.

Since the passage of Prop. S in November 2008, the program has enjoyed an owner favorable bidding climate that has resulted in a number of projects receiving construction bids that are below both district estimates and budgets. All cost avoidance savings have been retained in the Prop. S account and have not been used to increase scope at the site where costs came in lower than anticipated.

While this favorable bidding climate has provided cost avoidance for these early projects, the economic downturn has also reduced available revenues for the Prop. S program in the near term, and increased the estimated duration of the program from 10 years to 16 to 18 years.

The effect of this increase in duration increases the probability (certainty) that the district will experience an inflationary period before the program completes, and that projects later in the program will have to reduce scope and be reprogrammed. In the long term, staff projects that the total indicated cost for the entire scope of Prop. S work will exceed total available revenues by approximately $79 million if the bond program lasts 16 years.

In the short term (next five years), due to a reduction in net assessed property values that has reduced our bonding capacity and less certainty that state matching funds will be available for use as programmed, the district projects a net reduction in revenues of $134 million over the next five years.

In summary, both in the short and long term, the district must carefully manage its revenues, expenditures, and savings. Pertaining to short term savings, there are two options for their use:

  • Option A: Keep any construction savings at the campus where the cost avoidance occurred and increase scope at the site. Savings could either be placed in reserve until the entire bond project list has been completed and then spent at the site, or savings could be spent immediately at the site by increasing scope. In either case, cost savings would remain at the site.
  • Option B: Return all cost avoidance dollars from the near-term favorable bid climate, to the program to accommodate future cost overruns, “pop up” requirements, and anticipated inflation for projects later in the program.

Discussion: Option A presents the greatest benefit to sites that are scheduled towards the beginning of the bond. This benefit would be overridden by the likelihood (certainty) that construction cost inflation will create challenges to the program in the out years, and unless savings are retained in the Prop. S account, significant scope will not be accomplished at sites scheduled towards the end of the bond. Please note that currently our projected total indicated cost projected for a 16 year program is $2.379 billion, and we are programmed for only $2.3 billion.

Option B retains all cost avoidance dollars in the Prop. S account for use in future projects. These savings would be consumed on scheduled projects and would not be used to increase scope at any site. Returning these funds to the account allows staff to mitigate the effects of certain inflationary pressures.

ICOC Recommendation: The Independent Citizen’s Oversight Committee opined at the March 16, 2010, Prop. S Board of Education Workshop that they were in strong support of Option B.

Staff Recommendation: Staff most strongly recommends selection of Option B. This option increases the likelihood that program scope promised to the district’s voters would be accomplished. This option has been used successfully in all previous bond projects. District staff is requesting that the board selects Option B.

RECOMMENDATION:
1. Reject all bids for the construction of the New Woodshop (Green) building at Hoover High School project, and
2. Re-bid the New Woodshop (Green) building at Hoover High School project without the Project Stabilization Agreement (PSA) requirement.

FISCAL IMPACT: There is no fiscal impact associated with this recommendation.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The rejection of all bids does not impact district personnel. This project is intended to be re-bid upon receiving direction from the board.

BACKGROUND: This project was the first Prop. S project publicly bid that incorporated the board-approved Project Stabilization Agreement (PSA).

On this project, five contractors submitted bids. The lowest responsive and responsible bid came in nearly 20 percent above government estimate. These results are inconsistent with the brief history of the Proposition S (Prop. S) program. Prior to this bid, bids for the eight previous school construction projects under Prop. S received between 7-14 bidders and bid estimates came in between 5-49 percent below the government estimate.

Under section 2.3 the PSA, if a bid exceeds the government estimate by 10 percent or more, and the increase is due to application of the PSA, the district has the choice of rebidding the project with or without application of the PSA.

To inform the district’s decision, two analyses must be completed: verifying that the bid exceeded the government estimate by 10 percent, and attributing the additional cost to application of the PSA.

Validity of the Government Estimate:
The government estimate for this project, as with all Prop. S construction projects, was developed by an independent, professional cost estimating firm, with additional validation from district staff.

To further validate the government estimate for this project, staff, at the request of a special ad-hoc subcommittee of the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee (ICOC), prepared data supporting costs for the scope of work comprising the project and compared those costs to other Prop. S projects. After three meetings, the subcommittee, comprised of experienced construction professionals, concluded that the government estimate accurately reflected the value of the work required to complete the project, and forwarded its finding to the full ICOC. The ICOC agreed with the conclusion of the subcommittee. Accordingly, staff is confident in the accuracy of the government estimate and that the low bid exceeded the value of the scope of work required by the project.

Attribution of Increased Costs
Staff believes that application of the PSA accounted for the increased costs in this bid for following reasons:

  1. Over the brief history of the Prop. S program, school construction projects have consistently attracted between 7-14 bidders. On this project, five contractors submitted bids, despite 15 contractors attending the mandatory pre-job site walk.
  2. Anecdotal evidence suggests that prospective bidders avoided bidding on this project specifically because it was covered by the PSA, including the contractor currently working on the site. The PSA's new set of unknown conditions likely caused some bidders to include a greater contingency, and likely caused other prospective bidders to decline to bid.
  3. Staff analyzed and rejected, as not supported by the evidence, other factors that could have explained to increased costs, including:
    • ambiguities within the bid document-the relative uniformity in bid prices suggest that bidders all saw the project the same way, and that the documents were clearly understood
    • changes in the bidding climate-the fact that bids subsequent to this project came in under project estimate suggest that the increased cost was not the result of a fundamental change in the bidding climate for construction projects in San Diego
    • increases in construction, materials or labor costs-trend data from industry sources show no radical increases in construction, materials or labor costs which would explain the bids
    • complexity of the project-although the project incorporates “Green” design, materials, and technologies, neither the project nor the site pose any extraordinary construction challenges
Conclusion and Recommendation
Staff is hereby recommending that the Board of Education take the following actions
  1. Reject all bids for the Hoover Woodshop project
  2. Re-bid the Hoover Woodshop project without application of the PSA.


In support of the above, staff considered the following factors in developing its recommendation:
  • It is the district's responsibility to deliver safe, well constructed facilities at the best value to the district. It is more probable that this goal would be achieved by rebidding outside without the PSA requirement.
  • Staff has examined this project and found no substantial reason for cost escalation other than the PSA.
  • It is the staff’s opinion that the increased cost is due to application of a risk premium by the bid submitters due to unknown costs associated with the PSA.
  • When examined, there is no factual reason to believe that if re-bid under the PSA agreement, that the bid results will be substantially different.
  • It is staff's opinion that the bid results from the Madison Multimedia and Clairemont Automotive projects are independent of, and would have no bearing on, future bid results for the Hoover Green construction project.

Per Public Contract Code section 20111 (b) a school district is required to competitively bid a public works project valued at $15,000 or more. Per Public Contract Code Section 20114, the district must contract this work because it exceeds the force account limitations for performing the work with in-house forces.
