2. REVISED 7-13-20: Proclamation in Recognition and Celebration of the Life of Dr. Bob Matthews (Whitehurst-Payne/McQuary)

RECOMMENDATION: Approve proclamation in recognition and celebration of the life of Dr. Bob Matthews and recognize his lifelong commitment to serving his community.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Robert Louis Matthews, was born in the small town of Tonganoxie, Kansas on June 2, 1930. Matthews, often called Bob, grew up seeing the injustices of racism and discrimination firsthand. Matthews was the only African-American in his class from grade school through high school, he went on to graduate from college, where he met his wife Ardelle. With a passion for service in his community, Matthews taught at the Booker T. Washington Elementary School in Kansas City, Missouri. A year later he was drafted into the U.S. Army during the Korean War. After returning from the war, Matthews earned a masters degree in childhood education and, in 1955, he and Ardelle headed west to San Diego. When they arrived in San Diego they found housing and lending discrimination keeping Blacks out of some neighborhoods, highly segregated schools, and restaurants that refused to serve people of color. Initially Matthews took a job as a teacher at Frontier Elementary School, where he prided himself on being an accessible and eager ally to students. He went on to take several teaching and administrative assignments in the San Diego Unified School District over nearly three decades, became the second African-American to be named principal in the district, and earned a doctoral degree. Dr. Matthews and his wife were founders and members of the Association of American Administrators, and he joined the San Diego Community College District as President of Continuing Education at the Educational Cultural Complex in 1984. The impact of Bob and Ardelle Matthews on the San Diego community went well beyond education. The duo were fierce civil rights and social justice advocates. They were leaders and members of many community organizations and city committees, including the NAACP, the Urban League, the Kiwanis Club, the Elementary Institute of Science, the San Diego Zoological Society, Christ United Presbyterian Church and the Alpha Phi Alpha Fraternity and Delta Sigma Theta Sorority. Dr. Matthews was a lifelong educator, civil rights activist and icon in San Diego’s African-American community – who was influential in the creation of the Jackie Robinson YMCA, the growth of the San Diego Community College system and of the Martin Luther King Jr. Parade – sadly passed away on July 3, 2020.

BACKGROUND: Emerson-Bandini Elementary School is a two-campus school that reflects a previous merger of the historic Ralph Waldo Emerson Elementary School, which was built in 1939 and is located at 3510 Newton Avenue, and Bandini Elementary School, which opened in 1991 and is located a few blocks north in the Mountain View community. The two schools merged in 1992 and became known as Emerson-Bandini Elementary School.
 

Emerson-Bandini Elementary School is currently undergoing a major site modernization featuring new classroom and student services facilities, expanded outdoor play and athletic areas, an outdoor amphitheater, a renovated multi-purpose room, library media center and food services facilities.
 

The ongoing modernization will also consolidate all Emerson-Bandini Elementary students onto the south campus previously known as Ralph Waldo Emerson Elementary School. The Bandini campus is now home to the new Mountain View Elementary pilot school, formerly the San Diego Cooperative Charter School 2, which was co-located on the campus.

Celebrating the union of all Emerson-Bandini Elementary students onto the Emerson campus, the district has received a proposal to revert the name of Emerson-Bandini Elementary School to the original and historic name of Ralph Waldo Emerson Elementary School, abbreviated as Emerson Elementary School.
 

Pursuant to Administrative Procedure 3300, Naming of Schools and School Facilities, the School Names Committee has reviewed the renaming request and supporting documents submitted by the school community and has voted in support of the name change proposal. There were no votes in opposition.
