BACKGROUND:  The proposed Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC) and Auxiliary Services reorganization is designed to provide improved efficiency, effectiveness and customer service throughout the organization.  Under the leadership of the Deputy Superintendent Business, Phil Stover, the reorganized FPC and Auxiliary Services groups will be closely integrated, collaborate functionally, and share resources with increased efficiency.

 

This reorganization addresses some of the known shortcomings of the current organization structure such as:

  • Isolated facility and maintenance planning between Physical Plant Operations (PPO) and FPC
  • Varying levels of operational focus on financial performance across departments
  • No coordinated view of environmental programs
  • No focused training or professional development for staff

In summary, the proposed FPC and Auxiliary Services reorganization will provide an improved and more efficient organization to support the district’s mission.  It will address known shortcomings of the current organization.  In so doing, the reorganization presents a more streamlined structure yielding a net decrease of eight positions, including a net of three fewer managers and supervisors, and a savings of more than $728,000 (all funds). 

 

Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC) is a capital facilities project-driven organization.  The functional groups within FPC support the mission of the district by providing new facilities and by upgrading and modernizing existing schools and support facilities.  The numbers and types of projects depend on the needs of the district and availability of funds.  While the types of business functions remain constant, the staffing level of each group may change as program requirements change.

 

Facilities Planning and Construction manages three types of projects:  (1) Smaller capital facilities improvement projects including major repair and replacement typically initiated by PPO; (2) Bond projects are typically large and more complex with funding provided by a bond program; and (3) Larger capital facilities projects funded by grants and other sources of funds.

Auxiliary Services provides operational support to the district’s schools and sites through the four key departments that make up the Auxiliary Services group: Physical Plant Operations, Food Services, Transportation Services and Distribution Services.  The departments provide services ranging from building and grounds maintenance and repair; serving 120,000 meals a day; transporting nearly 18,000 students on a given day; and storing and moving mail and supplies throughout the district.  This work is accomplished by approximately 2,500 employees (2,200 FTE) and an annual budget of more than $200 million.  

 

Through the proposed reorganization, many of the key and common functions performed within the Auxiliary Services departments are centralized to bring a greater level of consistency, professionalism, cross training, and support.  This creates an opportunity to leverage the support that has been siloed in each department, to share best practices, to be able to focus more resources at critical times and develop a peer group of professionals within the functional areas.

 

In addition to the centralized Facility Planning and Program Controls functions identified above, the reorganization also establishes an Auxiliary Services Manager for Safety, Training, Personnel, and Environmental Compliance, which brings together the existing resources within the Auxiliary Services departments to create a consolidated team.

 

This unit consolidates personnel from safety, maintenance, and facilities planning who deal with environmental issues and brings them together under one unit where they can leverage each other’s skills and abilities to best ensure the district complies with ever increasing environmental regulations.  Impending changes in stormwater regulations affecting every district site are on the horizon and will require dedicated attention from district staff.  As another example of the benefits of the reorganization, this new department brings together the functions that share commonalities such as the safety and environmental requirements of dealing with hazardous materials, which are in use at the school sites (auto shops, chemistry classes, etc.) as well as maintenance.  This unit will have the ability to address the life cycle of the hazardous material, from training in proper handling and storage, safe use, compliance with environmental regulations, and ultimately proper disposal.  Additionally, this unit will focus on our biggest asset, Auxiliary Services personnel, by developing and implementing training and professional development to ensure all staff are growing and current in their skills.  This area has been long overlooked -- a strong training program shows the employees that their work is valued and appreciated, improves morale and will create a more effective and productive staff.

 

Finally, this reorganization incorporates the recommendations of the PPO Zone Task Force, a workgroup of labor and management that recently studied the operation of PPO under the zone structure.  The Task Force recommended creating four new positions, while eliminating eight positions to fund the newly created positions and generate additional savings.

BACKGROUND: In September 2010, Governor Schwarzenegger signed the Kindergarten Readiness Act.  SB 1381, enacted as Education Code section 48000, changed California’s kindergarten entry date from December 2 to September 1 so children enter kindergarten at age 5.  The legislation will phase in the new age requirement by moving the cutoff date one month a year for three years, beginning in 2012.  It also established a new transitional kindergarten, the first year of a two-year kindergarten program of those children between September and December.

 

To help the youngest kindergartners be successful, the two-year kindergarten program is intended for children whose birthdays fall between July 1 and December 2. Transitional kindergarten will serve as a bridge for children who need the gift of time, essential to gaining confidence in their social and academic lives.  Research demonstrates that children who attend high-quality early learning programs are 11 percent less likely to be placed in special education.  Transitional kindergarten could potentially reduce the number of children receiving special education services in Grades K-12.  Additionally, children who attend high-quality, early learning programs are less likely to drop out of high school or be held back a grade, and are more likely to score better on reading and math tests.

 

Many districts throughout California have already implemented transitional kindergarten programs.  Los Angeles Unified is in their first year of a transitional kindergarten program.  Districts in Poway, Fresno, Palo Alto, and Orange City have also implemented pilot programs for two-year kindergarten programs.

 

There is a need in the district for transitional kindergarten.  Currently, there are three elementary schools in the district that offer a Primary Enrichment Program (PEP), Scripps E.B., Hickman, and Marvin.  This program offers a developmental kindergarten class designed for children who would benefit from two years of a kindergarten program.

 

Transitional kindergarten will be available on a voluntary basis. Prospective pilot sites include: Balboa, Ericson, Freese, Hancock, Horton, Johnson, Kimbrough, Lindbergh/Schweitzer, Marvin, Mason, Rosa Parks, Porter, Ross, Silver Gate, Sunset View, and Zamorano elementary schools.  If the numbers are not sufficient at a site and it would incur a cost, a transitional kindergarten class will not be held at that site.

  • One member that is active in a business organization representing the business community located within the district
  • One member who is active in a senior citizens’ organization 
  • One member of a bona fide taxpayers’ association
  • One member who is the parent or guardian of a child enrolled in the district

Board members each choose one appointee from the above criteria and each select an individual of their own choosing, "at-large."  The superintendent provides one appointee to serve "at-large."

The City intends to terminate early both of the 1993 and 1994 joint use agreements due to various reasons; one of them being that the current joint use area of the 1994 agreement is well below the standard two acres desirable for a park and the off-leash dog park will cause the acreage to become extremely substandard.

  • Association shall provide ongoing maintenance responsibilities;
  • Association shall provide utilities (i.e. water area for pets, any utilities for snack shack/restroom structure).
  • Association shall provide indemnity, defense, and required insurance.
  • Association shall pay fair market value for use of property (Education Code section 17535(a) states that except for public entities, no agreement entered into by a school district  under this article shall rent or lease vacant classrooms or other space in operating schools for less than fair market value for comparable facilities);
  • Board must approve an exception to Administrative Procedure 5150;
  • Board has made the findings described in Education Code section 17529.
