BACKGROUND:  Under the federal Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) legislation, San Diego Unified School District must have a Title I Parent Involvement Policy that is developed in collaboration with parents of Title I students and reviewed every year.  [NCLB section 1118, section 2]

 

The policy establishes the LEA’s expectations for parent involvement, and describes how it will –

  • Involve parents in the joint development of the plan under NCLB, Title I, Part A, Section 1112;
  • Provide the coordination, technical assistance, and other support necessary to assist participating schools in planning and implementing effective parent involvement activities to improve student academic achievement;
  • Build the schools’ and parents’ capacity for strong parental involvement;
  • Coordinate and integrate parental involvement strategies;
  • Conduct, with the involvement of parents, an annual evaluation of the content and effectiveness of the parental involvement policy; and
  • Involve parents in the activities of the schools.

The Title I Parent Involvement Policy was approved by the board on June 26, 2007.

 

A subcommittee was convened during the 2009-10 school year, consisting of members from the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education and district staff, to review the policy and determine what, if any, changes needed to be made.  Subcommittee members are listed on the attached exhibit. The subcommittee met several times during the year to review and update the policy.  The DAC general body voted to approve the revised, district level Parent Involvement Policy on February 17, 2010.

BACKGROUND:    

 

General Information:  Program Improvement (PI) Status:  Under the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 (NCLB), a Title I school that, for two consecutive years, does not meet or exceed a series of annual academic performance goals known as Adequate Yearly Progress (AYP) is designated Program Improvement. Such schools must provide specified types of services and/or interventions during each year that they are identified as PI.  A school is eligible to exit PI if it makes AYP for two consecutive years.

 

District Schools in PI Year 4, 2009–10:  School Restructuring:  A school that enters its fourth consecutive year in PI status is required to develop a plan to restructure that will be implemented the following year, in this case in 2010–11. The restructuring plan must describe in detail the changes in governance, instructional practice and staffing that will be required to improve students’ academic achievement and enable the school to meet its AYP goals.  District schools submitting a PI Year 4 restructuring plan are:  Carson; Horton; Language Academy and Rosa Parks elementary schools; Wangenheim Middle; Riley/New Dawn; and San Diego Communications, Investigations in a Multicultural Atmosphere; and San Diego Media, Visual and Performing Arts.    Encanto Elementary School entered PI Year 4 status in 2008-09 with the board approving their restructuring plan in June 2009.  Encanto met AYP for the 2008-09 year and is on its second year of PI Year 4 status with the possibility of exiting PI if AYP targets are met this year.

 

The restructuring proposals were developed by School Program Improvement Leadership Teams composed of teachers, administrators, classified staff, and parents, in collaboration with the Office of Federal and Special Programs.

 

District Schools in PI Year 5, 2009–10:  Federal and Special Programs met with the 24 PI Year 5+ schools to review their existing restructuring plans.  All stakeholders engaged in discussion and reviewed student data to analyze the success of the strategies outlined in the plans.  The instructional teams made data driven tentative decisions about what strategies/programs improved student achievement.  These decisions will be reflected in their 2010-11 Single Plan for Student Achievement.

  2. Approve associated agreement (attached) for a Reading Corps Volunteer Program Coordinator, who will create and implement the program. (Scope of work and responsibilities are specified in Section II of the attached agreement.)

PROPOSED READING CORPS VOLUNTEER PROGRAM OVERVIEW:

 

The purpose of the proposed Reading Corps Volunteer Program is to support the district’s Goals for Student Achievement in Academics (GSA-2), and the Operational Expectations for Instruction (OE-2), by targeting students in Grades K-3 who are identified as achieving below expectations in reading to encourage their development of a love of reading, and to provide direct support to students who are not yet reading at grade level.   Students with a demonstrated need to develop and improve their reading skills can receive one-on-one tutoring with volunteers that includes independent reading, and increasing fluency and improving sight reading. In fall 2009, approximately 56.6 percent of Grade 1 students; 53.1 percent of Grade 2 students; and 61.9 percent of Grade 3 students performed below expectations on district reading assessments (see attachment).  The program is inspired by and is based on the district’s emphasis on improving student literacy and raising awareness of the tremendous need for and the strategic use of parent and community volunteers in increasing student achievement.

 

The program will be available to all elementary and K-8 schools.  Principals, in consultation with their Area Superintendents, will implement the program depending upon students’ needs.  The program can also be tailored to best meet the needs of students in a cluster of schools.  For example, individual schools or clusters could choose to participate during the school day or after school as part of an established after school program, in an arrangement that best works for their students and the volunteers.

 

A Reading Corps Volunteer Program Coordinator will actively work to create and implement the program through hands-on collaboration with district staff.  The Coordinator will be responsible for recruiting, training, and assigning volunteers; documenting student progress/activities/outcomes at participating elementary and K-8 sites; providing feedback to key stakeholders including students, parents, teachers, principals, Area Superintendents, and Board members regarding student progress, expectations, goals, etc., and serving as public relations spokesperson for the program. 

 

A timeline for implementation of the program will be developed to ensure the success of the program at each elementary and K-8 school.  An evaluation process and sustainability plan will also be developed and presented to the Board in Fall 2010.  At the end of the program, it is expected that students will show demonstrated improvement in reading skills and increased confidence in reading, that there will be significantly increased numbers of volunteers working on independent reading with K-3 students at school sites, and that qualitative and quantitative evaluations of the program will be performed that include pre- and post- data; and surveys of students, volunteers, and site staff.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  The estimated cost for the program in 2010-2011 is $90,000. Funding has been budgeted for a Reading Corps Volunteer Program Coordinator ($85,000), and for volunteer tool kits that will contain basic supplies ($5,000).  The funding sources are ARRA SFSF and ARRA Title I funds (District Budget Nos. 5635 32000 00 5150 2100 00000 01000, 5635 30110 00 5150 2100 00000 01000, 5635 32000 00 4301 1000 1110 01000 0000, and 5635 30110 00 4301 1000 1110 01000 0000).

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:   Area Superintendents, principals, teachers, and school site staff will be involved in and assist with the effort.  Central office departments will provide integrated support for the program.  These departments include: Deputy Superintendent of Academics, Instructional Support Services, Student Programs and Professional Learning (specifically the English/Language Arts Program Manager), Community Relations, Instructional Resources and Materials, Extended Learning Opportunities, and Accountability.

 

BACKGROUND:  In support of the Board of Education’s 2009 Year of Literacy initiative, and its 2009-2010 Year of the Volunteer initiative, on March 9, 2010, the Board approved the pilot of a six-week volunteer program to boost third grade reading levels.  The Perfecting Our Words Enhances Reading to Unlimited Proportions (POWER UP) Program encouraged volunteers to assist elementary school staff and third grade students in reading and literacy at Bayview Terrace, Fletcher, Lee, Sherman, and Whitman Elementary Schools.  Parent and community volunteers, including Board members, participated in the highly successful six-week pilot, contributing over 400 hours and working with 70 students.  The volunteers worked with students on decoding strategies, high frequency words, and fluency.   The proposed Reading Corps Volunteer Program will be modeled on the successful POWER UP Program. 

 

The Detroit Public Schools, a fellow member district of the Council of the Great City Schools, has also initiated a Reading Corps Program in an effort to ensure that every Grade 3 student is reading at grade level by 2015.  Over 3,000 volunteers have provided more than 360,000 volunteer hours in support of this successful program since January 2010.  Volunteers are asked to commit at least an hour each week to the program, and all volunteers receive training and undergo a background check.  The program was highlighted in the January/February 2010 edition of the Council of the Great City Schools’ Urban Educator publication.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve agreements with the San Diego County Superintendent of Schools for participation in the Marine Science Floating Lab and Science Outreach Programs, available to district elementary and middle-level schools, during the 2010-11 school year.  The Agreements authorize district schools to participate in the Marine Science Floating Laboratory, Green Machine, Splash Science Mobile Lab, and Salk Institute Genetics Lab from September 6, 2010 through June 30, 2011.  These programs are designed to meet elementary and middle-level California Science Content Standards.


FISCAL IMPACT:  Individual schools are responsible for the cost of participation in these programs; to participate, they must make arrangements directly with the San Diego County Office of Education.  Funding may come from school site budgets, Parent-Teacher-Student Associations, grants, donations, etc.  The costs of the programs are:

  • Marine Science Floating Laboratory:  $660.00 per half-day trip, serving a maximum of 36 students and 4 adults per trip
  • Green Machine:  $305.00 per trip, serving four one-hour elementary classes (Grades K-4) of up to 30 students each
  • Splash Science Mobile Lab:  $550.00 per trip, serving four one-hour elementary classes (Grades 4-6) of up to 36 students each
  • Salk Institute Mobile Genetics Lab:  No charge, serving 90-160 middle-level students in four class sessions

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  Individual schools that participated in these programs during 2009-10 paid for the trips from site funds, PTSA funds, grants, donations, etc.


IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  Sites are responsible for making arrangements with the San Diego County Office of Education to participate in these programs.


BACKGROUND:  

  • The Marine Science Floating Laboratory program consists of a half-day field trip on San Diego Bay with hands-on science instructional and materials.  It provides students with the opportunity to study marine biology, oceanography, history, and economics in a "real world" environment.  
  • The Green Machine curriculum aligns with the California Science Content Standards for Grades K-4.  The presentation describes the journey from seed to dinner table through hands-on learning stations that enable students to learn where their food comes from.  A musical and dramatic wrap-up reinforces the learning.
  • The Splash Science Mobile Lab curriculum aligns with the California Science Content Standards for Grades 4-6.  The presentation offers hands-on learning opportunities for students at multiple learning stations (i.e., watershed/stormdrain, water conservation, San Diego Estuary, and microscope).
  • The Salk Institute Mobile Genetics Lab provides a unique opportunity for middle-level students to learn about genetics and DNA from real Salk Institute scientists.
