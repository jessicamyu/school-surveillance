RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Supporting the San Diego Community Schools Coalition.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Possible future impact.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: San Diego has high levels of socioeconomic and racial inequality leading to negative effects on communities and students that include unmet health, mental health, and social welfare needs.

 

The San Diego Unified School District’s mission is, “...all San Diego students will graduate with the skills, motivation, curiosity and resilience to succeed in their choice of college and career in order to lead and participate in the society of tomorrow.”  Vision 2020 is a community-based school reform plan that engages all parents, staff, students and community members that commits to creating a school district that develops schools as neighborhood learning centers and engages parents and community volunteers in the educational process. 

 

San Diego Unified students should have access to an array of enriching curricular and extra-curricular learning opportunities that promote holistic, positive student development and community engagement. 

 

San Diego Unified students, families, and communities should have access to an array of health and social supports that enable positive student and community development, where resources will be readily available, accessible and inclusionary to community members, families, and students.

 

San Diego Unified students, families, and community members should understand that they are valued members of communities and that they have a role to play in improving and contributing to their communities.

 

San Diego Unified students can develop critical analytic skills and civic engagement commitments by studying the strengths and challenges of their communities and developing evidence-based plans for community improvement and building community power towards equity and inclusion.

 

The holistic development of district students must be an integrated effort of educators, school staff, families, community partners, and community members provided with time, support, and training. District schools will utilize shared decision-making processes that involve all stakeholders groups as democratically elected members of Site Governance Teams (SGT), School Site Committees (SSC), and English Language Advisory Committees (ELAC). The district recognizes that the Community Schools model allows for a systematic process that integrates educational activities with local community needs to effectively identify and foster community assets. 

 

Previous education reform strategies focused narrowly on market-based academic competition that further erodes the relationships and understanding needed to promote a sense of community. Community schools close well-documented racial and economic gaps by increasing racial and economic equity in San Diego community schools. The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires federally funded interventions in communities to be evidence-based.  Community schools use evidence-based curriculum with an integrated focus on academics, health, social services, and students for equitable school improvement and can reduce the impacts from challenges derived outside of communities’ schools. The Learning Policy Institute and the National Education Policy Center developed research that shaped the following San Diego pillars:

 

1. A strong and proven culturally relevant curriculum: Educators provide a rich and varied academic program allowing students to acquire both foundational and advanced knowledge and skills in many content areas. Students learn with challenging, culturally relevant materials that address their learning needs and expand their experience. They also learn how to analyze and understand the unique experiences and perspectives of others. The curriculum embraces all content areas including the arts, second languages, and physical education.

 

2. High-quality teaching and learning: Teachers are fully licensed, knowledgeable about their content, and skillful in their practice. Instructional time focuses on learning rather than testing. Students’ individual needs are identified, and learning opportunities are designed to address them. Higher-order thinking skills are at the core of instruction so that all students acquire problem solving, critical thinking, and reasoning skills. Educators work collaboratively to plan lessons, analyze student work, and adjust curriculum as required. 

 

3. Inclusive leadership: Leadership teams with educators, the community school coordinator, and other school staff share the responsibility of school operations with the principal, parents, and community stakeholders. This leadership team ensures that the community school strategy remains central in the decision-making process. The team plans development and implementation that includes thinking about sustainability that involves not just fund-raising but also organizing resources in new and more effective ways. A community school committee inclusive of families, community partners, school staff, youth, and other stakeholders from the school’s various constituencies works alongside and in collaboration with the leadership team.

 

4. Positive behavior practices (including restorative justice): Community school educators emphasize positive relationships and interactions and model these through their own behavior. Negative behaviors and truancy are acknowledged and addressed in ways that hold students accountable while showing them they are still valued members of the school community. All members of the faculty and staff are responsible for ensuring a climate where all students can learn. Restorative behavior practices such as peer mediation, community service, and post-conflict resolution help students learn from their mistakes and foster positive, healthy school climates where respect and compassion are core principles. Zero-tolerance practices leading to suspension and expulsion are avoided. 

 

5. Family and community partnerships: Families, parents, caregivers, and community members are partners in creating dynamic, flexible community schools. Their engagement is not related to a specific project or program but is ongoing and extends beyond volunteerism to roles in decision-making, governance, and advocacy. Education support professionals, teachers, community stakeholders, parents, and student leaders are responsible to develop family engagement strategies, and they are supported through professional learning opportunities. Their voices are critical to articulating and achieving the school’s overall mission and goals. When families and educators work together, students are more engaged learners who earn higher grades and enroll in more classes that are challenging; student attendance and grade and school completion rates improve.

 

6. Coordinated and integrated wrap-around supports (community support services): Community school educators recognize that students often come to school with challenges that affect their ability to learn, explore, and develop in the classroom. Because learning does not happen in isolation, community schools provide meals, health care, mental health counseling, and other services before, during, and after school. Staff members support the identification of services that children need. These wraparound services integrate into the fabric of the school that follows the whole child tenets. Connections to the community are critically important, so support services and referrals are available for families and other community members.

 

A combination of federal, state, and local funding can and should support each of the pillars. Research demonstrates that the mechanisms, practices, and activities for successful community schools include: 

 

1. An annual collaborative asset and needs assessment of and by schools and communities.

 

2. Creation of a strategic plan that defines how educators, school staff and community partners will use all available assets to meet specific student needs and get better results.

 

3. The engagement of partners who bring assets and expertise to help implement 

elements of the strategic plan.

 

4. Assigning a dedicated staff member at each community school site whose primary job is to facilitate the development and implementation of the strategic plan in collaboration with school and community members/partners, and to ensure alignment of solutions to needs. 

 

Transparent decision-making and accountable governance are necessary pre-conditions for sustainable community engagement and are essential components of a community school. 

San Diego Unified has adopted policies, programs, and practices that support the principles and vision of community schools through “Quality Schools/Vision 2020” and should include restorative racial justice, environmental justice (i.e., school climate bill of rights), social emotional and trauma-informed practices, wellness centers, culturally relevant curricula (i.e., ethnic studies), and high-quality parent and community engagement.

Brown Act and the Bagley-Keene Open Meetings Act, the California Public Records Act, California Government Code 1090 and the Political Reform Act. SB126 further clarifies that entities managing charter schools are also subject to these. AB75 went into effect on July 1, 2019, making clear that a "charter school shall not discourage a pupil from enrolling or seeking to enroll in a charter school for any reason...," and "[a] charter school shall not encourage a pupil currently attending the charter school to disenroll from the charter school or transfer to another school for any reason..." AB75 further requires that charter schools' governing bodies adopt their Local Control Accountability Plans in a public meeting. Certain provisions of AB1505 go into effect on July 1, 2020, that amend the authorization and renewal process in significant ways, including allowing districts to take into account the community and fiscal impact on all district students, programs and services when determining whether a new charter authorization or expansion is appropriate. Other provisions strengthen the need to assess whether charter schools are serving all students as part of the oversight and renewal process. Further provisions of AB 1505 establishing a two-year moratorium on new non-classroom based charter schools went into effect on January 1, 2020. AB1507 further requires charter schools already in operation as of January 1, 2020, to comply with the provisions of the law upon renewal of their charter. Inadequate oversight by some authorizers has resulted in harm to students, criminal prosecutions and significant financial losses of state education funds. It is the responsibility of the San Diego Unified School District to implement these new laws in its capacity as a charter authorizer.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, special education status, expansion of grade levels, or other significant revision.

On April 3, 2018, the district granted the petition to establish the NUA 1001 charter school at the 3678 Aero Court site, which is a building owned and operated by the National University as a college campus.   • Merge with another charter school named “NUA Homeschool” which is authorized by the Orange Department of Education;
  • Create a new board that combines NUA 1001 board members and NUA Homeschool board members;
  • Terminate its relationship with National University; and
  • Amend its bylaws.

On July 10, 2020, the district issued a “request for information” letter to NUA 1001 to clarify ongoing concerns about the governance and operations.

 

On July 17, 2020, NUA provided its response to the district.  

 

Based on the district staff's review and analysis, the information submitted by NUA 1001 fails to clarify the school’s organizational structure, fails to address the district’s governance and operational concerns, and increases the risk of a negative fiscal status.

 

During NUA 1001’s past two years of operation, the district’s oversight of NUA 1001 has been characterized by the school’s failure to produce and/or correct compliance documents, including new employee screening and tuberculosis documentation. NUA 1001 is currently in “not in good standing” status due to its lack of a facility and has failed to notify its families of its facility status and separation from National University. Allowing NUA 1001 to merge with another charter school and join a governing board based outside San Diego County would hamper the district’s efforts to monitor NUA 1001.

 

NUA 1001 is proposing to merge with NUA Homeschool. However, documents provided indicate NUA Homeschool has significant outstanding debt owed to its former back office provider SMG. District staff does not recommend approval of a merger with a charter school with a negative financial status.

 

NUA 1001 has failed to provide minimal information about the proposed new board members, their term dates, and other requested documents.

 

District staff has notified NUA 1001 that the school is in danger of nonrenewal of its charter if the governance and operational concerns are not addressed.

 

In sum, it appears the charter revision submitted by NUA 1001 amounts to a new charter petition to establish an altogether different charter school than that granted by the district on April 3, 2018.

Staff recommends the board grant the proposed revision to the charter of National University Academy 1001 STEAM insofar as to rename the charter school as “Suncoast STEAM Academy” effective July 29, 2020; however, staff recommends the board deny the proposed merger and all other aspects of the charter revision.

BACKGROUND: For the California School Dashboard (Dashboard), certain schools are automatically designated as Dashboard Alternative School Status (DASS) given the special populations served and the unique programs offered. Schools in this group include Community Day and Continuation schools. DASS schools receive colors on the Dashboard like other schools; however, modified measures have been developed for the academic and graduation rate indicators. For example, for most schools the adjusted 4-year cohort graduation rate is used, however DASS schools use a one-year, grade 12 graduation rate. Other alternative schools serve high-risk students, but are not explicitly required to do so in the Education Code. These schools must have an unduplicated count of at least 70 percent of the school's total enrollment (upon first entry to the school) comprised of high-risk student groups to be eligible for DASS.

 

Riley/New Dawn meets the criteria as an "other alternative school" as shown on page three of the attachment (largely given the percentages of suspended and habitually truant students). As such, Riley/New Dawn will benefit from having an accountability designation that adjusts for its unique student population.

  • Audubon K-8 (PS-21-0206-99, Attachment 1)
  • Innovation Middle (PS-21-0207-99, Attachment 2)
  • Longfellow K-8 (SV-21-0110-99, Attachment 3)
  • Rolando Park Elementary (PS-21-0209-99, Attachment 4)

FISCAL IMPACT: Under the terms of the agreements, the district is funding the Joint Use Projects’ construction. BACKGROUND: The district, in collaboration with the City, will construct and operate new joint use facilities at:

  1. Audubon K-8: 2.13 acre joint use facility with 1.59 acres of natural turf field, walking track, parking lot and a drinking fountain.
  2. Innovation Middle: 4.47 acre joint use facility with 1.95 acres of natural turf field, decomposed granite field, walking track, hardcourts, and a drinking fountain.
  3. Longfellow K-8: 1.43 acre joint use facility with 0.58 acres of natural turf field, walking track, hardcourts and a drinking fountain.
  4. Rolando Park Elementary: 5.4 acre joint use facility with 3.5 acres of natural turf, parking lot, walking tract and a drinking fountain.

The district will construct the Joint Use Projects. The City will operate and maintain the joint use fields for the contract term, while the district will continue to maintain landscaped areas outside the school, all perimeter fencing, and hardscape areas. District and City staff coordinated the facility designs with each school and their community.

Usage of each joint use area is as follows, with any modifications being mutually agreed upon by district and City staff to accommodate each site’s specific needs:

  • Each day school is in session, the district has exclusive use of the area 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day until 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • Each day school is in session, the City will have use of the area up to 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day and 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • Innovation Middle School will be a passive park (no organized sporting events) on Sundays and basketball hardcourts are not joint use until 5:00 pm on school days to provide exclusive use for PrimeTime.
  • City shall have exclusive use of the area on holidays, weekends and other days when school is not in session.

On September 28, 2018, the board approved Joint Use Agreements for Audubon K-8 and Innovation Middle. These agreements were not brought to City Council for approval, as district and City staff reviewed the Joint Use agreement template and made revisions which were mutually agreed to by district and City Legal. Now, all four agreements are based on the new template.

RECOMMENDATION: Determine that the Sherman Elementary Joint Use Agreement is Exempt from Further Environmental Review Pursuant to California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines Sections 15301 – Existing Facilities and 15323 – Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings; and Approve new Joint Use Agreement (Agreement) with San Diego Unified and the City of San Diego (City) for the operation and maintenance of natural turf fields, parking and recreation facilities for a 25-year term at Sherman Elementary (PS-21-0210-99, Attachment 1).

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Under the terms of the agreement, the City at their cost, will maintain the joint use facility through the term of the Agreement.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: Sherman Elementary has been a joint use natural turf field with the City since 1993 under an agreement that expired in 2018. This new agreement continues the joint use and expands the joint use area to include approximately .25 acres of existing hardcourts and a play structure. While the hardcourts and play structure were not included in the previous joint use agreement, they have been in public use since the joint use area was constructed in 1994. The new joint use area as part of this Agreement is:

  • 1.44 acre joint use facility with 0.74 acres of natural turf field, 0.21 acres of decomposed granite infield, 0.24 acres of decomposed granite walking track, 0.25 acres of concrete hardcourts and a play structure.

The City will continue to operate and maintain the joint use area for the contract term, while the district will continue to maintain landscaped areas outside the joint use area, all perimeter fencing, and hardscape areas.

 

Usage of the joint use area is as follows, with any modifications being mutually agreed upon by district and City staff to accommodate the site’s specific needs:

  • Each day school is in session, the District has exclusive use of the area 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day until 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • Each day school is in session, the City will have use of the area up to 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day and 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • City shall have exclusive use of the area on holidays, weekends and other days when school is not in session.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the proposed Joint Use Agreement with the City of San Diego at Sherman Elementary School (Proposed Project) is subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities and 15323 (Class 23) – Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings.

 

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

 

The Proposed Project includes the approval of a new joint use agreement at Sherman Elementary School that would result in negligible expansion of the existing joint use area but would not result in any operational changes compared with the existing use. As previously described, the proposed joint use area expansion would not result in increased use of the joint use area because the public already uses this area. Furthermore, the proposed joint use area expansion would not require the construction of new facilities. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Class 23 consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose.  Past history is defined as a similar kind of activity that has been occurring for at least three years and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323).

 

The Proposed Project includes the approval of a new joint use agreement to replace an existing, expired agreement, to support recreational uses by students and residents. The site has operated as a joint use field since 1993. Therefore, the application of a Class 23 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may have an impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not applicable.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of an existing school site and existing joint use area. The Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with operation of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Operation of the joint use field is consistent with existing use and would not contribute to a cumulatively considerable impact. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes approval of a joint use agreement for an existing recreational uses. No construction activity is proposed on- or off-site that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the existing school site and joint use area, or surrounding area. Operation of the joint use area would be consistent with existing use. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at the project site is anticipated.Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

Approval of this item approves the Proposed Project as described, authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption (Attachment 2) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and approves a new Joint Use Agreement with San Diego Unified and the City of San Diego a 25-year term at Sherman Elementary.
