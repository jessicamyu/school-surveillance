BACKGROUND:  In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. SPSAs are created under this part may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeed Act (public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.


The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA. 
 
Additionally, FPMA staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs,  approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs.  Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the FPMA Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.  The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA. 
 

The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires state educational agencies to determine school eligibility for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI). Local educational agencies (LEAs) with schools that meet the criteria for CSI must partner with stakeholders to locally develop and implement a plan to improve student outcomes. The county office of education (COE) also plays a role in providing technical assistance and support to LEAs within its county that serve schools that meet the criteria for CSI.  CSI schools are identified by the state education agency as one of the lowest-performing schools in the state based mostly on studentsâ€™ academic performance using the California Dashboard. Also, any high school with a graduation rate of 66 percent or less is a CSI school. 

 

SPSAs for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2019. This report brings forward for board approval the 2019-20 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for the 14 identified CSI schools. SPSAs from all remaining district schools will be presented to the board on December 10, 2019.  Electronic copies of each 2019-20 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item, and hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103.

BACKGROUND:  Youth Will, a San Diego based organization, believes in youth having the opportunity to shape the world and its future by bringing people together to build power in pursuit of a shared vision. Youth Will worked with youth from across San Diego to draft the Youth Bill of Rights and hundreds of students have signed on in support. The first right listed in the Youth Bill of Rights is that youth will be seen heard, and treated as equal members of our community with dignity and respect and free from abuse neglect, and exploitation. Youth Will and over a dozen youth organizations and student groups came together over the summer of 2019 to voice their common struggles and shared needs. Youth expressed that their voices are not taken into account or valued unless doing so benefits others. Studies have shown groups of people with less power benefit from sharing their perspective. Students have limited means of independently reviewing the conduct of vendors who serve students. It is a priority for students to feel they are receiving quality service and being treated well. Strong Families, Thriving Communities was formed in 2017 through a partnership between the San Diego Foundation, the County of San Diego and Clinton Health Matters Initiative (CHMI). CHMI built and facilitates a coalition of leaders and youth in San Diego's child-serving systems who came together to set bold action steps for improving health and reducing racial disproportionality. One of these bold action steps aims to make service providers and communities of support more trauma-informed and responsive to the unique needs of families impacted by trauma. To accomplish this, youth have lived experience in child welfare and juvenile justice created the Trauma-Informed Code of Conduct, which aligns with the Youth Bil of Rights. The Trauma-Informed Code of Conduct has been adopted by 35 organizations that are integrating the trauma-informed principles into organizational policies and practices in ways that are customized to how each one operates. Therefore, the San Diego Unified School District will encourage vendors to use the Trauma Informed Code of Conduct on behalf of district children, youth and families. The Superintendent's staff shall participate with the County of San Diego, San Diego County Office of Education, and youth-led organizations, such as Youth Will to develop a survey that will regularly be administered to youth participating in or receiving services from district vendors for the purpose of assessing the outcomes of using the Trauma Informed Code of Conduct approach. After survey development and implementation, the Superintendent shall return to the board with the survey results. The Superintendent shall consider the survey results as a factor in determining eligibility for renewal of vendor contracts. The board will make it a goal to see improved results on a quarterly and annual basis.

BACKGROUND:

 

As part of California's school accountability system aligned with the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) and the Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP), accountability results will be reported via the California School Dashboard (Dashboard). In addition to several state indicators (e.g., student scores from the Smarter Balanced assessments, English learner progress, graduation rates), Local Educational Agencies (LEAs) are required to report outcomes on local indicators that apply to LEAs only, not schools:

  1. Appropriately Assigned Teachers, Access to Curriculum-Aligned Instructional Materials, and Safe, Clean and Functional School Facilities (State Priority 1)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress in meeting the Williams Settlement requirements at all of its school sites, as applicable, and promptly addresses any complaints or other deficiencies identified throughout the academic year, as applicable.
  2. Implementation of State Academic Standards (State Priority 2)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress implementing state academic standards.
  3. Parent Engagement (State Priority 3)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress in (1) seeking input from parents in decision-making and (2) promoting parental participation in programs.
  4. School Climate (State Priority 6)
    • Standard: LEA administers a local climate survey at least every other year that provides a valid measure of perceptions of school safety and connectedness, such as the California Healthy Kids Survey, to students in at least one grade within the grade span(s) that the LEA serves (e.g., K-5, 6-8, 9-12).
  5. Access to a Broad Course of Study (State Priority 7)
    • Standard: Local educational agencies (LEAs) annually measure their progress in the extent to which students have access to, and are enrolled in, a broad course of study.

Prior to finalizing information reported via the Dashboard, LEAs are required to have their governing boards review the information at a regularly scheduled public meeting. The information included in this item presents some new information and also supplements information that was included in the 2019–20 LCAP that was adopted by the Board of Education on June 25, 2019. 

 

The three performance levels for the local indicators are: Met, Not Met, and Not Met for Two or More Years. To report that the standard is met on a local indicator, LEAs must demonstrate that they have collected and reported performance data for each local indicator. This agenda item fulfills the requirements and therefore, the district has met the standard for each of the local indicators.

The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314). The Proposed Project consists of school modernization, minor expansion, and upgrades that would not result in an increase in enrollment or classroom capacity beyond the criteria set forth in CEQA Guidelines Section 15314. The existing and projected number of students and classrooms is more fully discussed in Attachment A. Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern were designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.

  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

BACKGROUND: The district’s state-funded Early Childhood Education Programs and the Neighborhood House Association’s (NHA) federally-funded Head Start Program, have a long history of collaboration in educational and supportive services to children and families enrolled in the district’s early childhood education programs.

 

Under this Agreement, the district is required to:

 

Model 1-Extended Day: The district will provide an additional three and one half hours of instruction to Head Start eligible children that currently participate in the State preschool program. This model will increase the child development services to a minimum of six and one half hours each day.

 

Model 2-Wraparound Service at CDCs: The district will operate Child Development Centers (CDCs) for children three to five years of age, at which NHA agrees to provide health, social, and supportive services to Head Start eligible children and families enrolled. The district must ensure that one hundred percent enrollment is achieved at least six days prior to the first class day of Head Start, as established by and reported to the Office of Head Start, and maintained for the duration of the Agreement by providing Head Start services to children and their families (“Maximum Enrollment”). If the district fails to provide evidence of progress in meeting the foregoing enrollment targets, NHA in its discretion may reduce accordingly, the Maximum Enrollment and Maximum Reimbursable Amount, unless the target of one hundred percent enrollment is achieved, in which case no funding will be reduced for failing to meet the prior targets. The three and one-half hours of center-based services must be provided for a minimum of 37 weeks per year.

 

NHA agrees to reimburse the district in an amount not to exceed $4,677,838.00. Such reimbursements shall cover all program operation costs including, but not limited to, (i) Personnel; (ii) Fringe; (iii) Supplies and Materials; (iv) Equipment; (v) Training and Staff Development; (vi) Travel; (vii) Printing and Publications; (viii) Refreshments for Parent Meetings; and (ix) Such other allowable costs associated with the districts' performance of the Services. 

 

This Agreement will provide NHA staff at the district’s early childhood sites to oversee the provision of referral services, track the services provided, educate parents in parenting skills, and provide care for its children. The district’s early childhood teachers and classified staff provide the instructional component and assist in carrying out referrals and tracking student and family progress through these services. In addition, funding provided under this Agreement enables the district to offer an extended instructional day lasting six and one-half hours in the state preschool setting and to offer wrap-around services. Central leadership staff for both programs meets twice monthly to follow requirements, respond to concerns, and plan for the work being performed by both partners in the collaborative. This program enhances the lives of qualifying children and their families to achieve academically and socially through this highly supportive early intervention process.

BACKGROUND: The board approved the Innovations Academy project on July 26, 2016. In addition, the board approved the Purchase and Sale Agreement to purchase the facility for Innovations Academy on September 4, 2018.

 

On June 18, 2019, the board adopted a resolution for procedures for evaluating the qualifications of lease-leaseback contractors and the issuance of a request to prequalify for Group D, which are projects at or below $26,000,000 in estimated value. On April 9, 2019, the district issued a Request to Prequalify and for Statements of Qualification for Preliminary and Construction Services for Group D Lease-Leaseback Projects.

 

On May 15, 2019, thirteen contractors submitted Statements of Qualifications (SOQs) to the district’s RFQ. After evaluating each of the SOQs under the established criteria and procedures, district staff determined that ten of the contractors met the requirements of the RFQ for Group D Projects. The district issued an RFP for Construction Services for Innovations Academy Whole Site Modernization Project LLB, utilizing the LLB delivery method pursuant to Education Code section 17406. et. seq. The best value criteria for scoring proposals included but was not limited to demonstrated competence, relevant experience, compensation, timeliness, and staffing capacity to perform the required work.

 

Sealed proposals for the RFP were received on September 26, 2019. The district received a total of one proposal from Balfour Beatty Construction. The district assembled an evaluation panel consisting of the Architect of Record's Job Captain, Project Management Supervisor, Project Manager, Facilities Planning and Construction Project Coordinator and Senior Construction Manager, to evaluate and rate the proposals.

 

Based on the strengths of the proposal submitted, Balfour Beatty Construction, was deemed a responsive and responsible proposer, has been determined to provide the best value to the district for the Project located at the 5454 Ruffin Road, San Diego, CA 92123.

 

Innovations Academy has been on a Multi-Year Facilities Use Permit Agreement at the Scripps Mesa Conference Center at Spring Canyon, which has been amended nine times since 2011 to extend the term of the agreement and increase classroom space. The Scripps Mesa Conference Center is a temporary site containing relocatable buildings that was established for E.B. Scripps Elementary School in 2001 prior to the relocation to their permanent facility. The current Innovations Academy temporary school buildings are nearing the end of their useful life.

 

The new location will provide Innovations Academy with a new permanent facility containing 26 classrooms, collaboration spaces, a gymnasium, a multi-purpose room, administrative offices, a small food service facility and outdoor play areas to accommodate student learning and achievement. Innovations Academy will enter into a long-term lease for the facility wherein the school will be responsible for all maintenance and utilities, ultimately reducing district operational expenses.

 

The existing building at 5454 Ruffin Road was constructed in 1984. The existing building is a tilt-up concrete shell with an open second floor encompassing approximately one-half of the building interior. The district purchased the building in October 2018 for occupancy by Innovations Academy Charter School.

 

It will be Balfour Beatty Construction’s responsibility to sequence and schedule the work required in the plans, specifications and RFP documents to achieve the substantial completion date of March 23, 2020, or before. Balfour Beatty Construction will be responsible as part of this contract to relocate the school from the existing site in Scripps Ranch to the new location.

 

On May 10, 2011, the board approved a resolution in support of service-disabled, veterans-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a 3 percent required participation of DVBEs for all district construction contracting opportunities. The recommended contractor, Balfour Beatty Construction submitted a 6.3 percent DVBE participation in its proposal.

 

Environmental Analysis: On September 4, 2018, the board determined that the Innovations Academy New Site Acquisition and Whole Site Modernization Project was exempt from environmental review under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) pursuant to CEQA Guidelines Section 15301. The board also directed staff to file a Notice of Exemption (NOE)(Attached) for the project with the Recorder/Clerk of San Diego County, which was completed on September 6, 2018. Based on the foregoing, staff has determined that the project remains as described and analyzed in the previously approved NOE and thus no further CEQA review is required.
