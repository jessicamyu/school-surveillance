RECOMMENDATION: First Reading of the 2010-2011 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the San Diego Unified School District. This report presents four scenarios for the 2010-11 Title I eligibility ranking and school participation.

FISCAL IMPACT: Approximately $23,050,000.00, Title I Entitlement.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: $24,000,000.00

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001, and signed by President Bush on January 8, 2002, for implementation on July 1, 2002. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty.

Title I includes legislation that requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The Federal Census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty that are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

The educational and administrative leadership of the district are challenged with designing a plan that meets strict legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources.

Key factors that affect the projected reduction in school allocations are:

  • Projected addition of 25 schools to Program Improvement status
  • Student enrollment; enrollment declined by 617 students from 133,097 in 2008-09 to 132,480 in the 2009-10 school year
  • Free and Reduced lunch students increased over prior year by 1.4 percent from 58.53 percent in 2008-09 to 59.96 percent in 2009-10
  • Impact of entering district Program Improvement


The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education members discussed several Title I Ranking scenarios at the meeting on November 18, 2009, and voted on these scenarios at the December 16, 2009 meeting.

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct public hearing and approve General Waiver Request to the California Department of Education for Roosevelt Middle School to raise class size targets required in the Quality Education Investment Act (QEIA) for Grade 6 from 15.9 to 20.9, and Grade 7 from 15.7 to 20.7.

FISCAL IMPACT: There is no fiscal impact associated with conducting a public hearing or submitting the waiver request.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: Roosevelt Middle School has been in Program Improvement (PI) for six years. Roosevelt's Program Improvement Plan included reduced class sizes in key content areas. In 2006-07, the district provided additional teachers to the school, in addition to those allocated by the district's formula, to help the school implement the class size component of their Program Improvement Plan. Roosevelt's 2006-07 baseline of 20.9 for Grade 6 and 20.7 for Grade 7 was already significantly lower than a typical middle school when they began receiving QEIA funding. (This waiver does not affect the Grade 8 target). The facilities at Roosevelt cannot accommodate a further reduction in class size.

Due to the state budget situation, the funds that were provided by the district to reduce class size at Roosevelt in 2006-07 are no longer available. Additionally, to balance the 2009-10 budget, the district raised class sizes at all grade levels, thus reducing the number of teachers allocated to the school by the district's formula.

In April 2009, Roosevelt teachers voted favorably to raise class size targets to 20.9 for Grade 6 and 20.7 for Grade 7. The Roosevelt teachers' vote was followed by a presentation to the school's site council, which also agreed with the proposal to raise the QEIA ratios as stated in the waiver request.

On several occasions in spring and summer 2009, the San Diego Education Association (SDEA) was asked for its position on the submission of this waiver request. In September 2009, SDEA indicated that it did not support the waiver. Thus, the waiver was submitted to and approved by the Board of Education on September 29, 2009, without bargaining unit support.

On October 8, 2009, SDEA indicated that it had changed its position and was in support of the waiver. The waiver has been revised to show bargaining unit support and is re-submitted to the Board of Education for approval. RECOMMENDATION: Adoption of the 2010-11 school year calendar and associated single-track year-round calendar as recommended by the district Calendar Committee and provided in the item exhibits. The proposed 2010-11 calendars follow the current district models with one exception. The Calendar Committee recommends that Spring Break be scheduled April 4-8 rather than the week preceding Easter, which is April 25-29 in 2011.

FISCAL IMPACT: There is no fiscal impact associated with the calendar recommendation.

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Operational Expectation OE-10, Instructional Program, the district Calendar Committee (membership list is attached) is made up of voting and non-voting members. The voting members of the committee include equitable representation from San Diego Education Association (SDEA), Administrators Association San Diego (AASD), California School Employees Association (CSEA) and parent groups, as well as representatives from the district’s curriculum and school improvement staff. Non-voting members include central office staff from Human Resources, Payroll, Testing, Special Education, Pupil Accounting, Transportation, Summer School, and Communications.

The proposed 2010-11 calendars are based on 180 days of instruction, contract negotiations, and mandated and declared holidays. All calendar dates were reviewed and approved by the committee. The majority of voting members endorsed the following recommendations to the Board of Education:

  1. In the 2010-2011 school year, Spring Break would be scheduled the week of April 4-8 on the traditional calendar rather than the week preceding Easter, which is April 25-29, 2011. Spring Break for single track year-round calendar would be April 4-26, 2011. The committee based this recommendation on what they feel is in the best interest of students. This change would give students and teachers more time to prepare for spring testing without interruption as well as a more even distribution of time off between winter break and the end of the school year. The committee recognizes that this recommendation represents a change from a long-standing district practice to schedule Spring Break, both traditional and year-round, the week preceding Easter. The committee believes that if approved by the Board of Education, there would be adequate time to advise parents of the change.
  2. The district establish a standard Spring Break for all upcoming school years. A standard Spring Break would better serve students by ensuring that there would consistently be adequate time to prepare for spring testing. A fixed schedule would also ensure that the number of days between breaks would be more evenly distributed, giving students a better balance of days in and off school.
  3. The district move toward a common modified year-round calendar instead of one traditional and one year-round, with an anticipated start date of the 2011-2012 school year.

The above recommendations reflect the conclusions and recommendations made by previous Calendar Committees in years past.

These agreements are for services provided in the second half of the school year.

With a yearly deadline of October 31 set by the state for all California nonpublic schools (NPS) and agencies to submit applications for second term (January – June) CDE certification, updated CDE documentation is not received by most NPS schools prior to the end of the calendar year. Certification is extended in writing to those schools for which the process is delayed and schools begin to receive updated certification documents and/or extension letters from late November through January of the following year depending upon when their applications were submitted. This, along with the timing of the district winter break, makes ratification of NPS agreements necessary.
