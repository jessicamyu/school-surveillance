RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution recognizing October 2017, as National Bullying Prevention Month for all schools and students in San Diego Unified School District. The Board uses this opportunity to highlight our ongoing commitment to promote an improved school environment, enforce positive interactions between students, and raise awareness for teachers if a student is being bullied.

 

BACKGROUND: The National Bullying Prevention Month campaign was founded in 2006.

 

San Diego Unified School District Vision 2020 calls for all schools to be “safe, attractive neighborhood learning centers,” and each quality neighborhood school to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.”

 

School district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing updated and revised protocols and procedures, participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved.

 

San Diego Unified has partnered with the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and their No Place for Hate program which provides participating schools with anti-bias and anti-bullying resources to help create sustainable change that leads to an improved school environment.

 

The San Diego Unified Council of ASB Presidents fully supports October as National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within the month and throughout the year. Students’ participation in anti-bullying activities provide opportunities for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and find solace in the knowledge that others have been in similar situations.
  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying which people may experience including, but not limited to, cyber, verbal, physical, and exclusion; on the basis of actual or perceived race, religion, national origin, disability, sexuality, gender identity and/or socio-economic status.
  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus and the consequences of these actions.
  • Support students to come up with solutions to stop bullying and enforce positive interactions between students.
  • Engage teachers and parents in raising awareness if a student is being bullied.
  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying.
  • Help empower students in the recognition of the role everyone plays in addressing and reducing bullying with the goal to eradicate bullying on all school campuses; and

All elementary, middle, and high schools shall coordinate anti-bullying activities at the beginning of each new school year to be held in the month of October, during a week or days of their choosing.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution recognizing October 2017 as Filipino American Heritage Month.

 

BACKGROUND: In 1988, Filipino American History Month was first proclaimed by the 103rd Congress of the United States through House Resolution 155.

 

The Filipino American community is the second largest Asian American group in the United States with a population of approximately 3,100,000 people and over 5,000 students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

Filipino Americans have contributed greatly to the fine arts, music, dance, literature, business, journalism, education, science, technology, government, politics, fashion, health care, the armed forces, and many other fields that enrich the landscape of the country.

 

Efforts should continue to promote Filipino American history and culture, because the roles of Filipino Americans and other people of color have been overlooked in the writing, teaching, and learning of United States history.

 

In September 1988, Bell Junior High School was the largest junior high school west of the Mississippi, and had the largest concentration of Filipino students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

In 1989, the Filipino community rallied to advocate the offering of Filipino language classes at Morse High School and Bell Junior High School.

 

Filipino language classes are now offered at Challenger Middle School and Mira Mesa High School as well.

 

Filipino Heritage Month is a time of reflection and remembrance, and provides an opportunity for all people to learn and appreciate Filipino Americans’ historic contributions.

BACKGROUND:

Proposed 2018 Summer School Program

A proposed six-week program will be offered at an estimated nine high school sites to be located district-wide, for students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects). Tentative sites are: Clairemont, Crawford, Garfield, Henry, iHigh, Lincoln, Mira Mesa, Morse, and Twain. Historical data indicates that approximately 5,000 students in Grades 9 - 12 receive F grades in core subjects at the first semester, which will make them eligible for the program. Proposed program dates are June 19, 2018 – July 30, 2018. Transportation will not be provided.

 

Background/History

In 2014, 2015, 2016, and 2017, Summer School was offered only to Grades 9-12 general education students for credit recovery. The general education program was paid for out of Title 1 Funds. Transportation was not provided for general education students. In 2014, we had an average 3,511 high school students attend summer school; in 2015 we had an average of 3,837 high school students attend summer school; in 2016 we had an average of 3,907 students attend summer school; and in 2017 we had an average of 4,100 students attend summer school. The Special Education department continued to fund Summer School and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2013, Summer School was offered only to Grades 9-12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy. The Special Education department continued to fund Summer School and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2012, Summer School was offered only to Grade 11 and 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy. Transportation was not provided for general education students. The Special Education department continued to fund Summer School and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2011, Summer School was offered only to Grade 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy. Transportation was not provided for general education students. The Special Education department continued to fund Summer School and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2009 and 2010, Summer School was offered to general education students in Grades 9-12 for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy. Transportation was no longer provided for general education students. The Special Education department began funding Summer School and providing transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

Through 2007, the San Diego Unified School District offered a Summer School Program to a percentage of general education students in Grades 1-8 who performed below and significantly below grade level based on teacher judgment, instructional level, and assessment data; general education students in Grades 9-12 that needed to make up core courses to graduate; and to students with disabilities whose Individual Education Program (IEP) were found eligible for “Extended School Year.” Transportation services for all students were included in the program.

BACKGROUND: An election was held for the San Diego Unified School District, County of San Diego, California on November 4, 2008, at which electors of the district approved, by more than 55 percent of the votes cast, Proposition S authorizing the district to issue up to $2.1 billion in general obligation bonds pursuant to California Constitution Article XIII (A), to be used for the specific school facilities projects set forth in Proposition S.

 

Pursuant to Proposition S, the district shall only issue bonds if it projects that the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on the proposed bonds, when combined with the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on presently outstanding bonds of the district in each year, will not exceed the rate levied in 2007-08 (being $66.70 per $100,000 of assessed valuation) to pay debt service on the presently outstanding bonds alone, referring to bonds issued under Propositions MM and S. Proposition Z, which was passed by electors of the district on November 6, 2012, has a separate tax rate.  Due to the tax rate limit the majority of Proposition S bonds are issued as Capital Appreciation Bonds (CAB) that do not pay current interest, but compound interest and pay it at maturity.

 

Assembly Bill 182 (AB 182), effective January 1, 2014, requires that any time a CAB is issued that the board consider the item at two consecutive meetings.  This is the second of the two meetings; the first was held on September 12, 2017.  Other key requirements in AB 182 are a 25-year maximum term of the bonds, prepayment option for any bonds longer than 10 years, and maximum repayment ratio of 4:1 for debt service to principal amount. The bond issue is being structured to be in compliance with AB 182 with a term of 25 years, call feature after year 10, and a projected repayment ratio of less than 4:1 for all series of bonds.

 

As part of the current plan of finance for the Proposition S Bond Program, the district anticipates issuing one or more series of bonds, as needed, every other year, until the full authorization has been issued.  The plan proposes the sale of bonds that allow for the compounding of interest.  On September 12, 2017, the bond resolution authorizing issuance of Proposition S was presented as an information item.
