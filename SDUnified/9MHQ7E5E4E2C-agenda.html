RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board on the implementation of LCAP/Vision 2020 Quality Indicators 2, 4, and 5: Quality Teaching, Professional Learning for All Staff, and Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All, with a focus on early learning.

 

Quality Teaching

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 2, Quality Teaching, Destination:  Effective teaching requires careful crafting of learning communities built on trust and respect, as well as routines, resources, and strategies that support diverse students' intellectual engagement in learning. Effective teaching happens through thoughtful planning focused on defined outcomes for student learning and leveraging of teachable moments.  Effective teaching is revealed in the ways in which teachers respond to the strengths and needs of individual students while engaging and supporting all students in their learning.  Effective teaching is a form of inquiry wherein teachers use evidence and analysis of students' strengths and struggles, and their own performance, to guide their practice in support of student learning.  Effective teaching relies on engagement within a professional learning community.  Teachers in California have a professional responsibility to provide students with safe and caring learning environments, where students' differences are celebrated and supported, and they acquire the knowledge, skills, strategies, and concepts they will need for successful participation in an increasingly technological and global society.  Teachers are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.

 

LCAP Goal:  Students have access to a highly effective teacher in every course and in every year.

 

LCAP Actions and Services - Year 1:

  1. Provide for effective teacher hiring, recruitment (including partnerships with local universities), and retention practices (including Special Education, bilingual, and other hard to staff positions) and assure that teachers are properly assigned and supported.
  2. Provide district support for beginning teachers, interns, and peer assistance to effectively teach diverse student groups.
  3. Provide supports and training for special and general education staff who teach and support students with special needs.
  4. Provide systems of support at the school and district level to enhance instructional practices, professional learning, attendance, and success.
  5. Design Visiting Teacher Academies to build capacity of visiting teachers and develop a cohort of quality teacher candidates.
  6. Cluster support teachers work alongside principals, teachers, and school staffs to build capacity around the Common Core and ELD State Standards, adult learning structures (Professional Learning Communities, Instructional Leadership Teams), technology integration, and effective teacher practice.
  7. The Special Education Division works in concert with the Office of Leadership Development, area superintendents, and principals to support schools in providing quality professional learning and instructional supports for students with disabilities.

Professional Learning for All Staff

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 4, Professional Learning for All Staff, Destination:  Schools hold professional learning among their top priorities for the school community.  They recognize that universal high expectations for all students require ambitious and continual improvements in curriculum, instruction, assessment, leadership practices and support systems.  These improvements require effective professional learning to expand educators' knowledge, skills, practices and dispositions.  Schools demand effective professional learning focused on substantive results for themselves, their colleagues, and their students.  Schools artfully combine deep understanding of and cultural responsiveness to the community they serve with high expectations and support for adult learning in order to achieve school and school system goals.  They embed professional learning into the organization's vision by communicating that it is a core function for improvement and by establlishing and maintaining a public and persistent focus on educator professional learning.

 

LCAP Goal:  All staff members engage in ongoing professional study directed at standards-aligned teaching and learning.

 

LCAP Actions and Services - Year 1:  

  1. Support site-based professional learning through Professional Learning Communities.
  2. Provide cluster-level support and learning opportunities through Common Core Cluster Support Teachers and other district staff.
  3. Schools develop, monitor, and refine a Site Professional Development Plan with a clear emphasis on the successful implementation of the Common Core and ELD State Standards and high levels of student achievement.
  4. Establish Visiting Teacher Academies to develop a pool of well-trained visiting teachers. 

Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 5, Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All, Destination:  Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language, or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring that every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gap goals are implemented.  All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly or indirectly, to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.

 

LCAP Goal:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.

 

LCAP Actions and Services - Year 1:

  1. Schools use multiple assessment measures to gauge student understanding and application of learning.
  2. Schools set and monitor progress goals for all students and student groups.
  3. High schools participate in Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) processes and will develop strategic plans to improve both school climate and student behavior outcomes.
  4. Early learning programs will define, develop, and assure the implementation of a well-articulated pathway from pre-school through Grade 1.
  5. Provide strategic supports at the earliest levels by lowering class size in Grades TK-3; provide targeted intervention and supports for at-risk learners; and, provide professional development for teachers directed at best first teaching, intervention, and support.
  6. Improve outcomes for TK and Kindergarten students with disabilities by providing access to age-appropriate educational opportunities with non-disabled peers.
  7. Counselors and support staff monitor student access to advanced pathways.
  8. Central office staff supports area superintendents, principals, and school staffs to access data and build a culture of data analysis across all schools.
  9. Create and monitor the implementation of appropriate programs and services for students with disabilities.
  10. All middle and high school students have a 6- or 4-year plan, respectively, that clearly articulates the courses students must complete to meet the 'a-g' requirements, including World Language.
  11. Central office and secondary school staffs work collaboratively to identify and implement best practices that improve outcomes for Grade 9 students at every high school.
  12. Improve the outcome for students with disabilities.  Specifically:  (a) increase the percentage of students who earn high school diplomas, and (b) increase the percentage of students participating in functional curricula, who by the age of 22, transition into the community as contributing members.

BACKGROUND:  Each month, Superintendent Marten provides a District Accountability Report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting Year 1 LCAP actions, services and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's District Accountability Report each month, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report, and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation and suggested next steps.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in support of AB 101, Ethnic Studies for California High Schools, to promote understanding and acceptance of diverse cultures.

 

BACKGROUND: California has one of the largest and most diverse student populations in the nation.

 

People from historically, underrepresented ethnic backgrounds make up over 76 percent of the student population in San Diego Unified, with more than 45 languages spoken in district schools.

 

Given San Diego Unified’s annual increase in diversity, it is especially important that students build knowledge of the various racial and ethnic groups in the nation.

 

Expanding the high school curriculum to include ethnic studies will help students relate to historical events and have a better understanding of their own history and history of their neighbors. Learning of the struggles for equality, will teach students what it means to be an American.

 

The National Education Association confirms that ethnic studies has a positive impact on students of color. The Academic and Social Value of Ethnic Studies reports that ethnic studies benefited students in observable ways: they became more academically engaged, increased outcomes on academic tests, improved graduation rates, and developed a sense of self-efficacy and personal empowerment.

 

Developing ethnic studies programs in public high schools is an integral part of cultivating a classroom environment that is accepting of diverse cultures. It is vital for young people to learn about their history, but also important for them to feel like they can change their communities in positive ways.

 

 Assembly Bill 101, Ethnic Studies for California High Schools (AB 101), instructs the Superintendent to establish an Ethnic Studies Advisory Committee beginning in the 2016-17 school year. The advisory committee will make recommendations to the State Board of Education (SBE) on issues related to ethnic studies. The Superintendent will then submit a plan to the SBE to fully implement an ethnic studies curriculum for all California high schools.

 

Assembly Bill 101 is a natural extension of current efforts of the San Diego Unified School District with cultural proficiency and San Diego UNIFIED through Diversity Committee where students cannot wait until 2016-17.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District directs the Superintendent to establish an Ethnic Studies Advisory Committee (ESAC) as a subcommittee to San Diego UNIFIED through Diversity to make recommendations to the Superintendent on issues related to ethnic studies. The subcommittee will be a cross-functional, diverse team of internal and external district stakeholders. The Superintendent will present the ESAC recommendations to the board within nine months of this resolution.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in support of AB 288, Public Schools: College and Career Access Pathways Act, to better prepare students for success at the post-secondary level.

 

BACKGROUND: California has a long history of allowing high school students to concurrently enroll in college courses to receive instruction necessary to pass the California High School Exit Exam, take general education courses, or enroll in occupational courses.

 

Numerous studies have concluded that these programs effectively help low achieving students integrate into a college environment, increase the likelihood a degree program will be completed, decrease the length of time to complete a degree program, and stimulate interest in higher education among high school students.

 

Under existing law, high school pupils may be granted admission to California community college courses as special part-time or full-time students with the authorization of the K-12 district governing board, with the principal’s permission, and parental consent.

 

AB 288 establishes the College and Career Access Pathways Act to authorize California community college districts to enter into formal partnership agreements with local school districts to expand access to concurrent enrollment opportunities for high school students.

 

AB 288 seeks to build upon existing concurrent enrollment efforts to better prepare high school students for college and career success.

 

The district supports dual enrollment as an effective way to prepare students to complete high school and enter college.

 

San Diego Unified and the San Diego Community College District currently have many partnership programs in place such as Advanced College Placement (ACP) at Mesa College, Early Middle College (EMC) at City College, San Diego Metropolitan Regional Career Technical High School (MET) at Mesa College, and Lincoln STEAM Middle College program with City College.

 

Beginning with the 2015-16 school year, additional partnerships will be implemented at Scripps Ranch and Mira Mesa high schools with Miramar College.

Services by the four firms include program analysis, fiscal management, environmental planning (including CEQA), design and construction management, specialized environmental and technical services and other related services for planned and ongoing projects on an “as required” basis.  Theses contracts are required to procure specialized and technical services not performed by district staff, support short term specialized project requirements and capacity to staff peak periods of program activity.  These contracts permit the district to quickly respond to fluctuations in bond funded project requirements.  A program staffing plan has been developed and shared with bargaining units.  In accordance with the staffing plan 27 OTBS and 12 OSS positions were created to meet long term program staffing needs, 11 positions have been filled. Scheduled bond projects at traditional and charter schools throughout the District encompass capital facilities improvements, classroom technology, safety and security upgrades, Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) upgrades, new/renovated facilities for College, Career and Technical Education, temporary classrooms replacements, air conditioning and upgrades and improvements to athletic facilities and fields.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve purchases less than $86,000 for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment, and non-construction services; purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and contracts, leases or other documents less than $86,000 for the period February 1, 2015 through February 28, 2015.  These transactions are submitted to the board for review pursuant to Education Code section 17605 and under its delegation of authority to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Each purchase order has sufficient funds from available budgets for the purchase.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: For the period February 1, 2014 through February 28, 2014:  $1,184,624.70.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $86,000 (for the year 2015) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and for contracts, leases or other documents no greater than $86,000 (for the year 2015) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111. The purchase orders, contracts, leases, or other documents are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders, contracts, leases or other documents. For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 10, 2014, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

 

Thus, the district has met the requirements of Education Code section 17605 with this process of reporting out the specified purchase orders under $86,000 (for the year 2015) that are not associated with any previously-approved board contracts.
