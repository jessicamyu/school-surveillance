(Source: “Report: Teacher qualifications the best predictor of student success,” By Amelia Harper, May 16, 2019, Education Dive). PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The California Government Code and Education Code recognize the complexities in the day-to-day operation of school districts and authorize boards of education to delegate authority and responsibility to the superintendent and staff members to perform the many routine functions of a school district. Annually, the Board of Education delegates authority and assigns administrative responsibility to staff members by adopting a series of resolutions before July 1, specifying the responsibility and authority authorized by the California Government and Education Codes.

 

These resolutions cover such items as signing contracts, acceptance and recording of deeds, certification of board meeting actions and minutes, rejection of claims against the district, employee mileage allowances, execution of personnel action forms, travel allowances within the amount budgeted by the board, execution of loyalty oaths, financial report documents, etc. The resolutions delegating authority are attached.  Changes made to the resolutions include names and titles to reflect organizational structure and position title changes and increased financial settlement authority amounts delegated to staff, made effective for the 2021-22 year.

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount: $48,600.00. Budget String: 5467-00000-00-5100-2490-0000-01000-0000. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Miller Educational Consulting Services, Inc., (Miller Consulting) will support the district's Leadership and Learning Team in their efforts to identify and adopt a framework for the observation and assessment of teacher practice, develop a teacher observation model that has teacher growth as a central core component of the model, and develop and facilitate training for school district teacher observers that establish roles and responsibilities to be followed in the observation of teachers.

 

Short-term objectives: Miller Consulting will assist the district team with a revision of the district rubrics. The revisions include language to address Equity as a component of instruction and to reflect the district A Square work (Academics through Agency). Miller Consulting will consult with the team to build an in-depth understanding of the work of teacher observation and assessment with an emphasis on teacher growth, and to assist in defining the components of an observation model that would emphasize teacher growth as the main objective. Miller Consulting will additionally review and establish components and processes of a growth model of observation and assessment, articulate steps in a goal-oriented growth model, consider the role of objective evidence as a tool and voice for use in professional conversations, establish roles and responsibilities for observers/assessors and teachers in observation processes relative to the goal-oriented growth processes, articulate and define the role of the observer as coach in a growth model, and the role of the observer as an assessor in a growth model, identify objectives for long-term development and implementation of the model, including training for observers on the content embedded in the model (e.g., the rubrics and criteria) and the observer skills needed to facilitate the model (e.g., coaching, diagnostic skills, and assessor skills).

 

Long-term Objectives: Miller Consulting will analyze and identify training priorities and objectives for observers and teachers. The analysis includes a review of what training observers and teachers may have had related to this work in a process to build a plan for the training. Training objectives may include: building coaching skills to facilitate evidence-based observations and conversations; building a consistent understanding and interpretation of the rubric language; articulate the scoring priorities of the rubrics to distinguish between the varying levels of performance; engage with the district team to develop a training program for observers and teachers to meet the program objectives; assist as needed to facilitate training; train district trainers to facilitate, or any combination as identified by the district.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Advanced Via Individual Determination (AVID) is an in-school academic support program to help prepare students for college and career readiness and success. The online tutoring program emphasizes growing writing, critical thinking, teamwork, organization, and reading skills. AVID is implemented in almost all district high schools and middle schools.

 

To implement the AVID program with fidelity, a major component is having college tutors support the class with tutorials. Most sites struggle to meet this component due to lack of funds to pay tutors, or inability to find and train tutors. UCSD's Center for Student Involvement has multiple service groups that want to support the district's AVID programs. By adopting a wholly virtual tutoring model, the district and UCSD will work together to connect college students with district students in a sustainable way, overcoming many hurdles related to remote learning. The district will be better positioned to allocate tutoring and mentoring services, allowing students, regardless of geographic location, to benefit from UCSD’s tutoring program.

 

The district's AVID District Director will train all UCSD volunteers at no cost. UCSD tutors will provide tutorials via zoom for AVID classes with the AVID teacher supervising. Historically, only sites with funds to pay tutors and/or available community members were able to meet the tutor requirement. Now with virtual tutorials, this Agreement allows AVID sites across the district to have the support of UCSD tutors, providing more equitable AVID programs.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): Section 15300.2 of the State CEQA Guidelines specifies several exceptions to the use of a categorical exemption.

 

  1. Location. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: Approval and implementation of the Proposed Project is subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines section 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities. Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. Examples cited by the CEQA guidelines relevant to the Proposed Project include interior or exterior alterations involving such things as interior partitions, plumbing, and electrical conveyances. The Proposed Project consists of the maintenance, replacement, and repair of existing facilities at the Kearny High School campus that would not result in the expansion of existing use. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school campus. The Proposed Project is consistent with the existing use on the project site. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction timeframe of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Additionally, construction activities, including staging, would be contained to the project site. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes maintenance, replacement, and repair of existing facilities at the Kearny High School campus. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. The closest designated state scenic highway is the portion of State Route 163 through Balboa Park, located approximately four miles south of the project site and the project site is not visible from this area. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not substantially damage scenic resources within a state scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

The district will enter into a contract with the San Diego Education Research Alliance (SanDERA) at UCSD to perform an evaluation of the district's implementation of standards-based grading in middle and high schools in the 2021-22 and 2022-23 school years.

 

The evaluation will compare trends in student outcomes in years before the reform (and prior to the pandemic), that is, 2016-17 through 2018-19, with outcomes in 2021-22 and 2022-23. Metrics included in the evaluation include:

  • Academic and citizenship grades,
  • attendance rates, as a measure of student engagement,
  • Smarter Balanced test scores in grades 6-8 and 11 in math and English Language Arts, as measures of academic achievement, and
  • the number of 'a-g' courses completed with grades of C or higher, by grade.

 

The study will also examine gaps in these outcomes and test whether gaps narrowed or widened after the implementation of the standards-based grading reform in middle and high schools. Gaps related to the following student and school characteristics will be examined:

  • Student characteristics:
    • Race/ethnicity
    • Gender
    • English Learner status
    • Parental education
  • School characteristics:
    • Percentage of students at the school eligible for free/reduced-price meals
    • Middle versus high schools
