BACKGROUND: Operation Samahan’s Y2Y Center will implement required instruction, as well as state education code and district requirements, for HIV and sexual health education curricula.  The district and Y2Y will also collaborate to increase the rate of high school graduation, enhance parenting skills, prevent teen pregnancy and HIV/STD infection,and promote youth development without fragmenting, overlapping or duplicating services in San Diego County.

The governing board shall prepare an annual plan to increase the participation of these pupils unless it determines that there are no additional pupils enrolled in the district who would benefit from this participation. BACKGROUND: Education Code Section 52304.1 requires districts offering courses in ROP to annually review and assess participation of eleventh and twelfth grade pupils in ROP and to prepare an annual plan to increase enrollment of these pupils.

 

Established in 1969, ROP is a state-funded program designed to prepare individuals 16 years of age or older to qualify for: entry-level employment opportunities, career advancement, and/or entry into advanced education programs in community colleges or four-year colleges.  State funding granted to San Diego Unified School District by ROP is overseen by the district Office of College, Career and Technical Education (CCTE). This structure and office has been implemented to ensure a confluence of efforts in the development of innovative programs and courses of study. Each sequence of courses will have high quality, standards and competencies-based curriculum that lead to a mastery of skills and a continued passion for life-long learning.  The integration of core academics, career technical content and career exploration are presented in a personalized, supportive and caring environment of high expectations for all students. These programs prepare students for the challenges and opportunities of the 21st century. To ensure a successful transition from the critical years of childhood to young adulthood, CCTE equips students with the skills they need to become responsible citizens, contributing members of society and gainfully employed.

 

Over the years, district staff has implemented ROP promotion and recruitment practices that have resulted in annual enrollment increases of eleventh and twelfth grade students.  The total percentage of high school to adult student enrollments has steadily increased over the past fifteen years.  High school enrollment has increased from a low of 37 percent in the 1992-93 school year to 99 percent in the 2008-10 school years.  The district is in compliance with the passage of AB 2448 which required that adult students comprise no more than 10 percent of ROP enrollment by July 1, 2010. District recruitment efforts will continue to be directed toward eleventh and twelfth grade students, with strategic planning for the implementation of the Career Ready and College Prepared graduation requirements, through the enrollment priority procedures and the recruitment plan activities presented in this report.

Subject
3. Proposal to Continue Current 2011-12 Thresholds for Title I School Allocations (Evans)
Meeting
Nov 29, 2011 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION:  To continue the current weighted allocation thresholds, which include a strong priority for high poverty schools:

 

$434 $341 to $425 per student for schools having 85-100 percent Free and Reduced Lunch (FRL)

$274 $191 to $335 per student for schools having 60-84 percent FRL

$168 $71 to $185 per student for schools for 40-59 percent FRL

 

As an example, School X with 1,000 students and 90 100 percent FRL receives $434,000 $425,000.  School Y with 1,000 students and 70 percent FRL receives $191,800 $175,700.  School Z with 1,000 students and 50 40 percent FRL receives $84,000 $28,400 for its programs.

 

BACKGROUND:  In December 2010, the board approved a plan to: 1) evaluate the current effectiveness of Title I programs and 2) to raise the threshold of participation from 40 percent FRL to 75 percent FRL and to develop a plan to phase that in over five years. 

 

Title I funding is for school-wide programs.  According to federal guidelines, to participate in Title I funding a school must have at least 40 percent FRL.  The Title I Tiger Team and staff review of Title I was presented on November 1, 2011.  The decisions on how to spend Title I funds is determined by the School Site Council.  The conclusion in the staff review stated that it was “determined that lack of appropriate data precluded conducting an evaluation of the effectiveness of Title I funding.”  The Tiger Team report stated, “The Tiger Team has determined that existing data cannot demonstrate the effectiveness of past expenditures on student achievement.”  Determining school-wide effect is difficult due to the multiple, complex factors related to student achievement. No information was presented that could differentiate between achievement results at 40 percent, 60 percent or 85 percent thresholds.

 

It is anticipated that, as the district continues its strategic process to develop a quality school in every neighborhood over the next five years, certain schools at the lower threshold will eventually fall below 40 percent.  They will then be below the federal minimum and no longer qualify for Title I funds.  This will result in a gradual process of an increase in population at some of the highest poverty schools and, therefore a higher portion of Title I funds.  Equity requires that all those schools that have a substantial number of students living in poverty (and in most cases a majority of students living in poverty) receive funds to assist those students.  However, the highest poverty schools have the greatest challenges and this justifies continuing with the much higher per pupil funding formula for those schools.

Subject
4. Title I School Funding Threshold Increase: Five-Year Phasing, Second Reading; BUDGETED: N/A (OE-2)
Meeting
Nov 29, 2011 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action, Second Reading
Preferred Date
Nov 29, 2011
Budget Source
Title I, Part A

RECOMMENDATION:  The Superintendent recommends that the threshold be increased using an equal interval change of seven points per year over five years. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: This report does not reflect any overall fiscal changes to the district. Some schools will lose Title I each year as the threshold increase is phased in over five years.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: Title I funds are distributed to 156 schools in 2011-12. At the end of the five-year period, based on current free and reduced-price meal percentages, 97 schools will continue to receive Title I funds.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Changes to school-level Title I funding could affect school choices and ability to purchase certain types of staff to support supplementary programs.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: District staff consulted with the District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education. The school funding threshold and Title I priorities were discussed at the January, February, March, and October 2011 meetings.

 

BACKGROUND:  Title I is a federal program that provides funding to local school districts to improve the academic achievement of disadvantaged students. It is part of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, currently known as the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act.

 

Part A of Title I provides supplemental funding that may be used to provide additional instructional staff, professional development, parental involvement, extended-time programs, and other support to help ensure that all children have the opportunity to obtain a high-quality education. For 2011–12, San Diego Unified is projected to receive about $41 million.

 

On December 14, 2010, the Board of Education adopted a 40 percent threshold and sliding scale methodology for allocating Title I funds to sites for the 2011-12 school year. The threshold is based on the percentage of students at a school who are eligible for free or reduced-price meals. Included in the board action was an action to increase the minimum funding threshold to 75 percent (the highest minimum level permitted in law) within five years thereafter, and a directive to staff to develop a phase-in proposal. This directive was further clarified at the January 11, 2011, Board of Education meeting at which outreach efforts, priorities for Title I spending, and analysis of school outcomes were further delineated.

 

A work group comprised of district staff was formed to develop a phase-in plan to reach the 75 percent threshold by 2016-17 and to generate a report documenting Title I spending for the last five years and describing Title I school performance. The work group included area superintendents and staff members from the Finance Division, Chief of Staff and District Relations, and Office of Accountability. This agenda item presents several example scenarios for funding Title I schools as well as an example of the impact to the number of Title I schools using one phase-in model.
