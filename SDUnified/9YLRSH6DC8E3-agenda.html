In alignment with the provisions of AB 288, as approved by the Governor on October 8, 2015, community college and K-12 districts offering college courses on high school campuses must enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships that include specific language regarding the total number of high school students to be served and the total number of full-time equivalent students (FTES) projected to be claimed by the community college district for those students. Agreement SV-17-0166-99, for classes offered by Miramar College on the campuses of Mira Mesa, Scripps Ranch, Serra, and University City high schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreements SV-16-1543-13, SV-16-0045-14, SV-15-0789-13, SV-15-0776-13, SV-17-0094-99, and SV-17-0095-99.  Agreements SV-17-0167-99 and SV-17-0168-99, for classes offered by Mesa College on the campuses of Clairemont, Henry, Kearny, La Jolla, Madison, Mission Bay, Morse, Point Loma, Serra, and University City high schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreements SV-16-1066-99 and SV-16-1067-99.  Agreement SV-17-0165-99, for classes offered by City College on the campuses of Crawford, Garfield, Hoover, Lincoln, Morse and San Diego High Schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreement SV-15-0712-13.  

 

The first amended master Memorandum of Understanding SV-14-0291-13 between the San Diego Community College District and the San Diego Unified School District remains in effect and is applicable to programs in which San Diego Unified students participate in classes on the Miramar, Mesa, or City College campuses.  

 

This item is submitted as an informational first reading, and was heard as a first reading and public hearing by the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees on June 9 May 12, 2016.  The agreements will be agendized for second reading, public comment, and action by the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education on June 28, 2016.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND:  AB 288 authorizes community college and K-12 districts to enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships with the goal of developing seamless pathways from high school to community college for career technical education or preparation for transfer, improving high school graduation rates, or helping high school students achieve college and career readiness.  Through dual enrollment partnerships, school districts and community college districts can create clear pathways of aligned, sequenced coursework that allows students to more easily and successfully transition to for-credit, college-level coursework leading to an associate degree, transfer to the University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) systems, or to a program leading to a career technical education certificate.  The intent of AB 288 is to offer or expand dual enrollment opportunities for students who may not already be college bound or who are underrepresented in higher education.  AB 288 was supported by both the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees and the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve continuation of early admission at the beginning of the 2016-17 school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a Transitional Kindergarten (TK) program as per flexibility offered to districts by changes to the California Education Code. At the beginning of the 2015-16 school year eight schools in the Henry Cluster (Benchley-Weinberger, Dailard, Foster, Gage, Green, Hardy, Hearst, and Marvin Elementary Schools) offered enrollment to children whose fifth birthday fell within the six-month window of September 2 to March 2. All stakeholders – parents, principals, and teachers found this to be a successful model with a positive impact on student learning.

 

This pilot warrants an additional year of implementation and study. District staff will work to revise the current TK curricula in both math and English language arts to assure that it is developmentally appropriate for the youngest learners.  This revision work will include input from current pilot TK teachers and other interested TK teachers.  This curricular work will have important implications for district TK and preschool programs. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The program could be subject to a potential staffing adjustment based on actual TK enrollment in the Fall.  If enrollment necessitates a staffing adjustment, approximate cost to the district of $109,397.00 per FTE as needed for 2016-17. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $427,568.00.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Potential staffing adjustment of up to four teachers based on actual TK enrollment.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The laws applicable to transitional kindergarten were changed as a result of Assembly Bill (AB) 104, the Education Omnibus Trailer Bill. The new law, effective July 1, 2015, permits local educational agencies and charter schools for the first time the option to grant early admission, at the beginning of the school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a TK program. AB 104 amended the California Education Code (EC) to add subsection (B) to EC 48000(c)(3) and states the following.

 

(B) (i) For the 2015-16 school year and each school year thereafter, a school district or charter school may, at any time during a school year, admit a child to a transitional kindergarten program who will have his or her fifth birthday after December 2, but during that same school year, with the approval of the parent or guardian, subject to the following conditions:

 

(I) The governing board of the school district or the governing body of the charter school determines that the admittance is in the best interest of the child.

 

(II) The parent or guardian is given information regarding the advantages and disadvantages and any other explanatory information about the effect of this early admittance.

 

(ii) Notwithstanding any other law, a pupil admitted to a transitional kindergarten program pursuant to clause (i) shall not generate average daily attendance for purposes of Section 46300, or be included in the enrollment or unduplicated pupil count pursuant to Section 42238.02, until the pupil has attained his or her fifth birthday, regardless of when the pupil was admitted during the school year.

 

Approximately 50 students were admitted through the expanded TK program.  Enrollment was limited to students who resided within the Henry Cluster and all students who applied were admitted.  The community was delighted that their children had access to the program.  Feedback from teachers indicated that the children made progress in kindergarten readiness, social-emotional skills/self-regulation/confidence, and the children prospered.  The numbers are expected to be comparable or slightly higher in the 2016-17 year as the community is more aware of this early learning opportunity.

 

We would like to continue with the pilot program within the Henry Cluster, specifically Benchley-Weinberger, Dailard, Foster, Gage, Green, Hardy, Hearst and Marvin elementary schools. These schools were selected because there are no district preschool options within this cluster and the principals expressed a strong interest.

 

This small-scale pilot will allow the district to continue studying the community interest, impact on teaching and learning, and the impact on enrollment of extending the TK eligibility date to March 2, 2017. It will help us determine both the challenges and benefits in moving this expanded program into additional schools the following year.

 

By continuing with this pilot program at the start of the 2016-17 school year, Leadership and Learning will be able to provide the pilot schools with the supports and resources they will need to assure that our youngest learners have access to TK programs by the first day of school.

In February 2016, the City of San Diego Charter Review Committee (CRC) discussed San Diego City Charter section 55, which affects the lease for San Diego High School.

 

The San Diego Unified School District addressed the CRC to ask for clarifying language in the City Charter that would allow San Diego High School to continue to operate on the site indefinitely, however the CRC failed to take any action.

 

San Diego High School is the only high school that serves the communities of Logan Heights, North Park, Normal Heights, and Barrio Logan.

 

San Diego High School has a top ranked International Baccalaureate program that benefits students from all over San Diego Unified.

 

As San Diego’s oldest high school, San Diego High School has become one of district’s most used school sites for youth and adult sports leagues and community events benefiting all citizens of San Diego, such as Stand Down for Homeless Veterans.

 

The site must be permanently secured for San Diego High School in order for San Diego Unified to move forward with the approved campus modernization projects totaling over $30 million of Proposition Z bond funds approved by the voters in 2012.

The Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) is an essential element of California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF).  It is a three-year, district-level plan that is updated annually.  The plan describes the school district's key goals for students as well as the actions (with expenditures) the district will take to achieve the goals and the measures used to indicate progress.  The LCAP also includes an Annual Update, which reflects on implementation of the current year's LCAP (2015-16).  The San Diego Unified School District has aligned its six LCAP goals and supporting actions and expenditures to the board-adopted Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.     

 

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and first reading of the district's proposed 2016-19 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP).  A workshop was conducted at 9:00 a.m. on June 14, 2016, at which the Superintendent and staff provided information to the board on the implementation of Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood and its alignment with the proposed 2016-19 LCAP. Each goal was reviewed, and plans for implementation of each goal in the 2016-17 school year were presented. The 2016-19 LCAP will be presented to the Board of Education for final reading and adoption on June 28, 2016.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The 2016-19 LCAP is submitted for first reading in conjunction with the first reading of the proposed 2016-17 budget.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: The Annual Update section of the 2016-19 LCAP includes 2015-16 actions, services, and estimated actual expenditures.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) and District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC) serve as the district's LCAP advisory committees.  During 2015-16, regular updates on LCAP baseline data and implementation were presented to the DAC and DELAC.  The draft 2016-19 LCAP was presented to the DAC on May 18, 2016, and to the DELAC on May 19, 2016, for review and comment, and is posted on the district website at www.sandi.net/lcap for public review.

 

BACKGROUND:  The LCAP is intended to be a comprehensive planning tool.  For school districts, pursuant to Education Code Section 52060, the LCAP must describe, for the school district and each school within the district, goals and specific actions to achieve those goals for all students and each subgroup of students in Education Code Section 52052, including students with disabilities, for each of the state priorities and any locally identified priorities.  

 

The district's local priorities (LCAP Goals) are:

  1. Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.
  2. Access to a Broad and Challenging Curriculum:  Students have equitable access to rigorous, well-rounded, standards-aligned curricula and access to and skill in applying technologies to leverage learning, assuring readiness for a full range of post-graduation options.
  3. Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning:  Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.
  4. Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture:  Schools provide safe and well-maintained facilities and positive learning climates and instructional practices that are culturally responsive, challenge bias, and support the academic, social, emotional, and physical needs of students.
  5. Parent and Community Engagement with Highly Regarded Neighborhood Schools that Serve Students, Families, and Communities:  Parents and community members are engaged and work within and across schools to support student learning.  Neighborhood schools are highly regarded for their quality as well as their service as centers for extended learning and enrichment opportunities, and academic and social services.
  6. Well-Orchestrated Districtwide Support Services and Communications:  All parts of the organization - personnel and infrastructure - work together to support student success through quality schools in every neighborhood.

The state priorities are:

  1. Basic:  The degree to which teachers are appropriately assigned pursuant to Education Code Section 44258.9, and fully credentialed in the subject areas and for the student they are teaching; students have access to standards-aligned instructional materials pursuant to Education Code Section 60119; and school facilities are maintained in good repair pursuant to Education Code Section 17002(d).
  2. Implementation of State Standards:  The implementation of academic content and performance standards and English language development standards adopted by the state board for all students, including English Learners.
  3. Parental Involvement:  Description of efforts to seek parent input in decision-making at the district and each school site, promotion of parent participation in programs for unduplicated students and special need subgroups.
  4. Student Achievement:  Performance on standardized tests, score on Academic Performance Index, share of students that are college and career ready, share of English Learners that become English proficient, English Learner reclassification rate, share of students that pass Advanced Placement exams with a 3 or higher, and share of students determined prepared for college by the Early Assessment Program.
  5. Student Engagement:  School attendance rates, chronic absenteeism rates, middle school dropout rates, high school dropout rates, and high school graduation rates.
  6. School Climate:  Student suspension rates, student expulsion rates, other local measures including surveys of students, parents, and teachers on the sense of safety and school connectedness.
  7. Course Access:  Student enrollment in a broad course of study that includes all of the subject areas described in Education Code Section 51210 and subdivisions (a) to (i), inclusive, of Section 51220, as applicable.
  8. Other Student Outcomes:  Student outcomes in the subject areas described in Education Code Section 51210 and subdivisions (a) to (i), inclusive, of Education Code Section 51220, as applicable.

BACKGROUND: Statement 54 of the Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB 54) established accounting and financial reporting standards for all governments that report governmental funds.  GASB 54 seeks to improve financial reporting by establishing criteria for classifying fund balances and clarifies definitions for governmental fund types.  Essentially, it establishes a hierarchy of fund classifications based largely on the extent to which the government agency is restricted in the use of the funds.   GASB 54 establishes the following five classifications of ending fund balances:

  • Non-Spendable Fund Balance consists of funds that cannot be spent because they are either not in spendable form (i.e. inventory and prepaid) or amounts that are legally or contractually required to be maintained intact.

  • Restricted Fund Balance consists of funds that are mandated for a specific purpose by external parties, constitutional provisions or enabling legislation.

     

  • Committed Fund Balance consists of funds that are set aside for a specific purpose by the district’s highest level of decision making authority.  Formal action must be taken prior to the end of the fiscal year.  These committed amounts cannot be used for any other purpose unless the same formal action is taken to remove or change the limitations placed on the funds.

     

  • Assigned Fund Balance consists of funds that are set aside with the intent to be used for a specific purpose, but are neither restricted nor committed.  Assigned fund balance includes all positive remaining amounts in governmental funds, other than the General Fund, that are not considered nonspendable, restricted, or committed.  Assigned funds cannot cause a deficit in unassigned fund balance.

     

  • Unassigned Fund Balance consists of excess funds that have not been classified in the previous four categories.  All funds in this category are considered spendable resources.  This category also provides the resources necessary to meet unexpected expenditures and revenue shortfalls.

Governmental agencies—including school districts— are required to adopt policies and protocols to establish the processes that shall determine amounts relegated to these classifications, e.g.- through board action.  Agencies are required to implement Statement 54 for fiscal years first ending June 30, 2011.  The attached resolution shall establish district policy and processes as required by GASB 54.

This project leverages $4.9 million in Propositions S and Z bond funds with over $19 million in Department of Defense grant funds. Federal funds were awarded to San Diego Unified pursuant to a Department of Defense program to construct, renovate, repair, or expand elementary and secondary schools located on military installations throughout the country.  A key requirement for participation in the program is the ability of a school district to provide local matching funds.  Because of the Propositions S and Z bond programs, San Diego Unified is one of the few school districts able to meet the local match requirement to take advantage of the grant program.

 

District staff has evaluated the type of improvements necessary to ensure that Miller ES aligns with the goal of Vision 2020 to provide a quality school in every neighborhood.  The whole site modernization of Miller ES, which is located on federal land leased from the United States Navy (USN), would include the demolition of seven existing portable classrooms at Miller ES and six portable classrooms at Miller Child Development Center (CDC), renovation of four existing, loft-style classrooms to include permanent partitions, improved site path of travel, construction of a new on-site student drop off area, and installation of a new artificial turf play field.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111 (b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more.  The district advertised for a minimum of two weeks and bids were opened on March 9, 2016.  The district received bids from 2H Construction, Inc., Balfour Beatty Construction, LLC., Barnhart-Reese Construction, Inc., C. W. Driver, Inc., Solpac Construction Inc., dba Soltek Pacific Construction Company, and USS Cal Builders, Inc.  Balfour Beatty Construction, LLC. has been determined to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.   On May 10, 2011, the board approved a resolution in support of service-disabled, veterans-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled, veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a 3 percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities.  The recommended contractor, Balfour Beatty Construction, LLC. submitted a 6.41 percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA Analysis:

The project would replace a total of 13 existing portable classrooms with a new 10-classroom building, and thus would not result in a net increase in classroom space greater than 1O new classrooms. The existing total enrollment at Miller ES is approximately 725 students. The proposed project would not increase the net student capacity of the campus. In addition, the school was built between 1975 and 1978; therefore, the buildings proposed for demolition are less than 45 years old and not considered historical structures.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

BACKGROUND:  On May 10, 2016, the board authorized the purchase of district-adopted and supplemental instructional materials and textbooks for the 2016-17 school year to ensure that sufficient standards-aligned textbooks and/or instructional materials are available to each pupil in accordance with Education Code Section 60119 and in compliance with Williams Settlement Legislation. 

 

The district moved to CCSS 2012 Version of K-2 student materials in the 2013-14 school year, but continued use of the California version for Grades 3-5. This year, the district has the opportunity to expand CCSS enVision Math to elementary Grades K-5 with this updated 2016 CCSS version of the adopted enVision Math program.  The updated version has been reviewed by Leadership and Learning Department curriculum-area experts and teachers and has been determined to support the district’s vision.  The move to CCSS enVision Math 2.0, 2016 will provide consumable student texts for Grade K-5. 

 

The new curriculum has a very strong problem-solving component which aligns perfectly with launch, explore, summarize and guides teachers how to summarize the problem solving component including sample student work.  Within the pacing there will be days to incorporate the conceptual based tasks that many teachers love about the concept guides.  This curriculum is Promethean board friendly. Another strength of the new curriculum is the online testing component in which students can take end-of-topic tests online with items that are similar to SBAC (Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium) items and are much better than the Illuminate items.  There is also the ability to collect the data to use it to inform instruction.

 

Pearson/Scott Foresman-Addison Wesley is the exclusive publisher of its enVision Math product.  Given the unique needs of the district and the single source from which the district can obtain the required materials, the board finds that the needs of the district are best served by acquiring the product as a sole source procurement of materials.
