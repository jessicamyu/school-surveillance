2. 2021-22 School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSAs) and Categorical Budget Summaries for 16 Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI) Schools

Oct 26, 2021 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2021-22 School Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) and Categorical Budget Summaries for 16 Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI) schools listed below.

 

School Plans for Student Achievement for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2021.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:

The District Advisory Council (DAC) received information on the timeline and components of developing the SPSA at their September 15, 2021, and October 20, 2021, meetings.

 

In addition, site principals received training regarding specific plan requirements and consulted with their school stakeholders through the School Site Council to develop these plans on September 14 & 15, 2021. In addition to these trainings, principals were provided one-on-one support by the Strategic Planning for Student Achievement (SPSA) Department beginning shortly after the training occurred, and this support continued through the submission of the plans. For the first time this year, principals were provided an opportunity to work ob their SPSA during a summer phase with support from the SPSA Department.

 

BACKGROUND:

In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. SPSAs are created under this part may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeed Act (public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.


The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.

 

Additionally, Strategic Planning for Student Achievement staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the SPSA Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment. The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA.

 

The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires state educational agencies to determine school eligibility for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI). Local educational agencies (LEAs) with schools that meet the criteria for CSI must partner with stakeholders to locally develop and implement a plan to improve student outcomes. The county office of education (COE) also plays a role in providing technical assistance and support to LEAs within its county that serve schools that meet the criteria for CSI. CSI schools are identified by the state education agency as one of the lowest-performing schools in the state based mostly on students’ academic performance using the California Dashboard. Also, any high school with a graduation rate of 66 percent or less is a CSI school.

 

SPSAs for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2021. This report brings forward for board approval the 2021-22 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for the 16 identified CSI schools. Electronic copies of each 2021-22 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item, and hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103. SPSAs from all remaining district schools will be presented to the board on December 14, 2021.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of addressing antisemitism and affirming the value of Jewish students, staff, and families and denounce the rise in antisemitic rhetoric and hate-motivated crimes and incidents that denigrate Jewish students and staff in the communities served by SDUSD.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The City of San Diego is home to 50,000 Jewish people, including thousands of students within San Diego Unified School District boundaries who are themselves or have family members who are Jewish. The Jewish people have maintained a unique ethnic, religious, social, and cultural identity, and diverse ethnic history that spans more than 3,000 years, have become a vibrant and diverse community over the course of that history, and have experienced everything from acceptance and prosperity to hatred and discrimination since their arrival in the United States. As members of an ethno-religious group, students who identify as Jewish at schools across the district come from many different racial and ethnic backgrounds, immigrant communities, levels of religiosity, and more, and include those with overlapping identities, and other inclusionary identities. What we teach our youth about their ancestral legacies and cultures is essential in the fight for racial and ethnic justice and equity. San Diego has a long history of centering the positive contributions of the Black, Latinx, Asian American and Pacific Islander, Native American, Jews, Muslims, women, LGBTQ+, and other communities. No individual or group within our District should fear becoming a target of discrimination, retaliation, derision, bullying, isolation, or violence. There has been a significant rise in hate crimes against many racial, ethnic, religious, cultural, and other minority groups here in San Diego and across the country in recent years. This discrimination, racism, xenophobia, sexism, homophobia, islamophobia, and antisemitism has not only generated heated rhetoric, but has resulted in harassment, vandalism, physical assaults, emotional trauma, and worse. Reports of white supremacist, antisemitic, anti-Jewish, and anti-Israeli graffiti, bullying, harassment, and violence on SDUSD campuses has been on the rise in recent years such that administrators, teachers, and student leaders need updated and readily available training and resources to prevent and address antisemitism in all its forms. In 2019 San Diego Unified School District graduate Lori Kaye was murdered by an anti-Semite in local synagogue Chabad of Poway. Property and vandalism crimes against Jewish places of worship and community centers, and physical assaults against people who “look Jewish” or dine at restaurants frequented by Jewish people has increased significantly in recent years and most recently due to the May, 2021 war between Hamas and Israel. These hate crimes show how anti-Zionism and anti-Israel bias can descend into antisemitism when they promote demonization, discriminatory double standards, and/or delegitimization of Israel and its existence. These hate crimes were denounced by President Biden and San Diego Mayor Todd Gloria, the City Council, and faith leaders from across the San Diego region. The Biden Administration and the U.S. Department of Education’s Office for Civil Rights have adopted the IHRA (International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance) Working Definition of Antisemitism. The definition of antisemitism is an important educational tool which covers antisemitism old and new, from across the political spectrum. SDUSD successfully partners with the Anti-Defamation League to create No Place For Hate® schools, which work to create campuses free of bullying, bias, and discrimination for students of all religions and ethnic groups. SDUSD operates a robust ethnic studies program, seeking to recognize the incredible contributions of minority communities and to promote cultural inclusion. The Board of Education supports creating inclusive and accepting communities in order for students and staff on SDUSD campuses in order to have a welcoming environment for all students, staff, and families. The social content of instructional materials used in California public schools must comply with California Education Code section 60040-60045 and 60048 as well as the State Board of Education guidelines in the Standards for Evaluating Instructional Materials for Social Content; which require that they portray accurately and equitably the cultural and racial diversity of American society. Education Code section 51500 states that “teachers shall not give instruction and a school district shall not sponsor any activity that promotes a discriminatory bias on the basis of race or ethnicity, gender, religion, disability, nationality, or sexual orientation”, or other protected characteristic.

Public Contract Code sections 10298 et seq., 20118.2, and Government Code section 53060 authorize the district to contract with system integrators using the California Multiple Award Schedule (CMAS), allowing the district, without further competitive bidding, to choose a vendor(s) based on best value, which includes price (as the most heavily weighted factor), past performance and experience, qualifications, work plan, methodology, schedule, and overall responsiveness.

 

On July 15, 2021, the district publicly advertised Request for Proposal (RFP) PS21-0617-40 for competitive proposals for CMAS Certified Vendors in Systems Integration to Provide Technology Upgrades Services. The advertisement appeared in The Daily Journal and was also posted on PlanetBids to 371 vendors. All proposers were required to submit systems integration and technology upgrades service plans for all school, administrative, and data center sites, along with customer references, and a fixed pricing proposal.

 

Seven vendors responded by the closing date of August 23, 2021: AT&T DataComm, LLC., ConvergeOne, Digital Scepter, Eplus Technology, Inc., Aruba, NetXperts, Inc., and Vector Resources, Inc. The Committee evaluated the vendors’ technical proposals, as well as their experience, implementation strategies, and customer service approaches. Strategic Sourcing and Contracts worked with Facilities, Planning, and Construction to complete a cost comparison among all cost proposals received to determine the initial two-year contract value.

 

The Committee determined it is in the best interest of the district to award multiple contracts to balance the projected workload and complete work in a timely manner. At this time, the district has completed negotiations with two vendors, Vector Resources, Inc., and NetXperts, Inc., and is recommending them for contract award.

 

The contracts with Vector Resources, Inc., and NetXperts, Inc., are each in the amount not-to-exceed amount of $50 million dollars, based on the estimated scope of technology integration work and associated design, management, contingency, and other soft costs. On January 26, 2021, the board approved agreements with A-Z Bus Sales, Inc., and Creative Bus Sales, Inc., for the purchase of electric school buses per the California Air Resources Board (CARB) Grant (GD21-0615-40 and GD21-0616-40).

 

In February 2021, the district applied to SDG&E’s Power Your Drive for Fleets Make-Ready Infrastructure Program (Program), as approved by the California Public Utilities Commission, in which SDG&E offers participants a rebate of up to 80 percent of the cost to design and install the "Customer-Side Make-Ready Infrastructure" at selected locations operating qualified Medium-Duty/Heavy-Duty Electric Vehicles. Under the Program, SDG&E will install equipment and infrastructure on its side of the meter at zero cost to the district, who will receive a rebate of up to 80 percent of the cost to design and install needed infrastructure on the district's side of the meter for bus charging. Under the Participation Agreement, SDG&E will provide the installation of the civil and electrical infrastructure required of the charging stations for the electric buses at no cost to the district. (Please also see companion Items H.3 and H.4 under Operations on the October 26, 2021 board agenda to grant an easement and right of way for medium-to-heavy-duty electrical vehicle charging infrastructure to SDG&E at the district Transportation Bus Yard at Cardin Lane and district Supply Center at Cardinal Lane).

 

The Participation Agreement requires the district to contract with a qualified Electric Vehicle Service Provider (EVSP) to procure, install, operate and maintain at least one Electric Vehicle Service Equipment in good working order for a certain term and provide usage data for five years. Nuvve is a qualified EVSP on SDG&E’s approved list.

 

Under the Nuvve agreement, Nuvve will provide vehicle-to-grid equipment and infrastructure, vehicle-to-grid chargers, level 2 chargers, software, and support to enable energy storage resource aggregation services and utility bill savings at no cost to the district. During the 12-year agreement, Nuvve will own and maintain the equipment and manage the software/grid services at no cost to the district. The district and Nuvve will share in the net revenues generated from Carbon Credits and Vehicle to Grid on a 50/50 basis. The district has the right to purchase all ownership rights as well as any revenues generated through Carbon Credits and Vehicle to Grid Revenues at any time during this 12 year period. After the end of the 12 years, Nuvve will transfer the ownership of the charging stations as well as revenues from Carbon Credits and Vehicle to Grid to the district.

 

Approval of the Participation Agreement with SDG&E and the Energy Services Agreement with Nuvve is in support of California’s goal to reach 1.5 million zero-emission vehicles by 2025, and at least five million zero-emission vehicles by 2030.

 

As the district is developing a strategy to achieve zero-net-energy by 2030, these Agreements will lend themselves to the catalyst. For example, the Lincoln High School cluster is identified within a disadvantaged community near the Port of San Diego — an area within San Diego County's worst air quality. These electric vehicle charging stations will charge the electric buses that will be used within the established disadvantaged area, thus cultivating mobility and support the adaption of improved air quality for children, families, staff, and the community. The reduction of greenhouse emissions from the disadvantaged community aids California's ambitious goals of improving air quality near San Diego's port.

 

These electric vehicle charging stations will help ensure that the district implements an e-mobility program that will serve as an example of best practices to school districts across the state, and ultimately across the U.S.

The term of this Agreement is unchanged and remains November 14, 2018 through November 13, 2022.

BACKGROUND: On November 13, 2018, the board approved an Agreement with BakerNowicki Design Studio, LLP, (PS19-0549-24) to provide architectural design services in support of the Propositions S and Z and Measure YY programs. Firm selection was based on a competitive solicitation process.

 

On November 10, 2020, the board approved a First Amendment to the Agreement with BakerNowicki Design Studio, LLP, to increase the consideration by $5,000,000.00 to continue providing architectural design services.  The new total not-to-exceed amount was $15,000,000.00 and the term remained unchanged.

This Novation Agreement will assign all rights and obligations of BakerNowicki Design Studio, LLP, to DLR Group, Inc. This will allow the district to continue to conduct business with DLR Group, Inc., in place of BakerNowicki Design Studio, LLP, for the remainder of the Agreement term.  All terms, conditions, and pricing of the Agreement remain unchanged and in full force and effect, except as modified by this Novation Agreement.
