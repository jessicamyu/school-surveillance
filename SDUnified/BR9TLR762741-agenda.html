Proposed 2021 Summer School Program: Our vision for summer programming is to reimagine enrichment opportunities and meaningful learning experiences to accelerate student learning. San Diego Unified is dedicated to supporting the development of wellbeing, knowledge, skills, and abilities through this summer and beyond.

 

Secondary Summer Experience: The "Summer Experience" for secondary students is a human-centered learning opportunity that will meet students where they are, with the purpose of re-engaging, igniting passion, and providing options for credit through engaging interdisciplinary projects. 

 

Program Structure: The "Summer Experience" will be designed around interdisciplinary hands-on, minds-on projects (English, History/Social Science, Math and Science) connected to key industry/community partners in San Diego.

 

  • Students are grouped in grade bands 6-8, 9-10 and 11-12.
  • Student learning outcomes will be personalized first to the students' credit recovery needs and interests.
  • Students will gain work-based learning or internship experience that clearly connect to the academic learning outcomes.

Who: Priority enrollment will be given to students who need credit recovery in core content classes. Additional enrollments will be based on course availability at each host site.  

 

Location: A maximum of 15 middle school sites and 15 high school sites will be selected based on facility availability. Final sites will be determined after working with PPO/Facilities to ensure no conflicts, and in collaboration with Leadership and Learning, the Area Superintendents and in consultation with the PPO and Transportation Departments.  

 

Timeline High School: Three weeks, half-day, onsite program (two sessions); total of six weeks for high school. Program dates are June 21 - July 30, 2021.

 

Timeline Middle School: Four weeks, four hours per day, onsite program (one session). Program dates are June 21 - July 16, 2021. 

 

Online Credit Recovery: A proposed six-week program will be offered through iHigh, for students in grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects). Historical data indicates that approximately 5,000 students in grades 9 through 12 receive F grades in core subjects at the first semester, which determines eligibility to participate in the program. Proposed program dates are June 21 - July 30, 2021.

 

Elementary Summer Experience: The "Summer Experience" will be designed for elementary students as a human-centered learning opportunity that will meet students where they are, with the purpose of re-engaging, igniting passion, including foundational literacy, numeracy and social-emotional learning skills through an integrated approach. 

 

Program Structure: The "Summer Experience" will be designed around an integrated STEAM approach with a strategic emphasis.

 

  • Students are grouped by grade level, avoiding combination classes, if possible (grades K-5)
  • Student learning outcomes will be aligned to individualized foundational and numeracy skills 

Who: Priority enrollment will be given to students who currently qualify for Phase 1 onsite services. Additional enrollments will be based on class availability at each host site. 

 

Location: At least one elementary school in each cluster will be open. More elementary sites will be open in areas where there are higher numbers of eligible students. Final sites will be determined after working with PPO/Facilities to ensure no conflicts, and in collaboration with Leadership and Learning, the Area Superintendents, and in consultation with PPO and Transportation Departments. 

 

Timeline: Four weeks, four hours per day, onsite program. Program dates are June 12 - July 16, 2021. 

 

Proposed 2021 Summer School/Extended School Year Sites: It is proposed that summer programs for both general education students and students with disabilities be held at districtwide sites. Due to major construction projects in the district, all final host summer school and ESY sites have not been selected at this time. Final sites will be determined after working with PPO/Facilities to ensure no conflicts and in collaboration with Leadership and learning, Area Superintendents, and in consultation with PPO and Transportation Departments, taking into consideration actual geographic patterns of ESY eligible students. 

 

Proposed 2021 Extended School Year (ESY) Program for Students with Disabilities: The California Code of Regulations Section 3043(f) requires that Extended School Year (ESY) services be provided for each individual with exceptional needs who has unique needs and requires special education and related services in excess of the regular academic year. Such individuals shall have disabilities which are likely to continue indefinitely or for a prolonged period, and interruption of the pupils educational programming may cause regression, when coupled with limited recoupment capacity, rendering it impossible or unlikely that the pupil will attain the level of self-sufficiency and independence that would otherwise be expected in view of his or her disabling condition. The lack of clear evidence of such factors may ot be used to deny an individual an ESY program if the student's Individual Education Program (IEP) team determines the need for such a program and includes ESY in the IEP. The proposed program for high school students is June 21–July 30, 2021 and for elementary and middle school students the proposed dates are June 21–July 16, 2021. Transportation services will be provided.

 

Community Partners: In addition to the instructional opportunities provided this summer, San Diego Unified plans to forge new partnerships with community-based organizations to expand the range of enrichment activities available to our students to accelerate their learning.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Community Schools Implementation Team members and timeline for implementation of Community Schools. Staff will provide an update on the work of the Community Schools Implementation Team. The recommendations presented in this item include the steps to be taken in the 2020-21 school year to seat and train the Community Schools Implementation Team members and begin to develop the San Diego Unified guidelines for Community Schools implementation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On July 28, 2020 the Board of Education adopted a Community Schools Resolution with a focus on identifying two to five schools within San Diego Unified that would benefit from implementing a Community Schools model. Community schools close well-documented racial and economic gaps by increasing racial and economic equity. The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires federally funded interventions in communities to be evidence-based. Community schools use evidence-based curriculum with an integrated focus on academics, health, social services, and students for equitable school improvement and can reduce the impacts from challenges derived outside of communities’ schools. The Learning Policy Institute and the National Education Policy Center developed research that shaped the following San Diego pillars:

 

1. A strong and proven culturally relevant curriculum: Educators provide a rich and varied academic program allowing students to acquire both foundational and advanced knowledge and skills in many content areas. Students learn with challenging, culturally relevant materials that address their learning needs and expand their experience. They also learn how to analyze and understand the unique experiences and perspectives of others. The curriculum embraces all content areas including the arts, second languages, and physical education.

 

2. High-quality teaching and learning: Teachers are fully licensed, knowledgeable about their content, and skillful in their practice. Instructional time focuses on learning rather than testing. Students’ individual needs are identified, and learning opportunities are designed to address them. Higher-order thinking skills are at the core of instruction so that all students acquire problem solving, critical thinking, and reasoning skills. Educators work collaboratively to plan lessons, analyze student work, and adjust curriculum as required.

 

3. Inclusive leadership: Leadership teams with educators, the community school coordinator, and other school staff share the responsibility of school operations with the principal, parents, and community stakeholders. This leadership team ensures that the community school strategy remains central in the decision-making process. The team plans development and implementation that includes thinking about sustainability that involves not just fundraising but also organizing resources in new and more effective ways. A community school committee inclusive of families, community partners, school staff, youth, and other stakeholders from the school’s various constituencies works alongside and in collaboration with the leadership team.

 

4. Positive behavior practices (including restorative justice): Community school educators emphasize positive relationships and interactions and model these through their own behavior. Negative behaviors and truancy are acknowledged and addressed in ways that hold students accountable while showing them they are still valued members of the school community. All members of the faculty and staff are responsible for ensuring a climate where all students can learn. Restorative behavior practices such as peer mediation, community service, and post-conflict resolution help students learn from their mistakes and foster positive, healthy school climates where respect and compassion are core principles. Zero-tolerance practices leading to suspension and expulsion are avoided.

 

5. Family and community partnerships: Families, parents, caregivers, and community members are partners in creating dynamic, flexible community schools. Their engagement is not related to a specific project or program but is ongoing and extends beyond volunteerism to roles in decision-making, governance, and advocacy. Education support professionals, teachers, community stakeholders, parents, and student leaders are responsible to develop family engagement strategies, and they are supported through professional learning opportunities. Their voices are critical to articulating and achieving the school’s overall mission and goals. When families and educators work together, students are more engaged learners who earn higher grades and enroll in more classes that are challenging; student attendance and grade and school completion rates improve.

 

6. Coordinated and integrated wrap-around supports (community support services): Community school educators recognize that students often come to school with challenges that affect their ability to learn, explore, and develop in the classroom. Because learning does not happen in isolation, community schools provide meals, health care, mental health counseling, and other services before, during, and after school. Staff members support the identification of services that children need. These wraparound services integrate into the fabric of the school that follows the whole child tenets. Connections to the community are critically important, so support services and referrals are available for families and other community members.

 

A combination of federal, state, and local funding can and should support each of the pillars. Research demonstrates that the mechanisms, practices, and activities for successful community schools include:

 

1. An annual collaborative asset and needs assessment of and by schools and communities.

 

2. Creation of a strategic plan that defines how educators, school staff and community partners will use all available assets to meet specific student needs and get better results.

 

3. The engagement of partners who bring assets and expertise to help implement elements of the strategic plan.

 

4. Assigning a dedicated staff member at each community school site whose primary job is to facilitate the development and implementation of the strategic plan in collaboration with school and community members/partners, and to ensure alignment of solutions to needs.

 

Transparent decision-making and accountable governance are necessary pre-conditions for sustainable community engagement and are essential components of a community school.

 

San Diego Unified has adopted policies, programs, and practices that support the principles and vision of community schools through “Quality Schools/Vision 2020” and should include restorative racial justice, environmental justice (i.e., school climate bill of rights), social emotional and trauma-informed practices, wellness centers, culturally relevant curricula (i.e., ethnic studies), and high-quality parent and community engagement.

2. Resolution in Support of AB22 (McCarty) - Universal Transitional Kindergarten

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of Assembly Bill 22, and urge the Governor and the Legislature to approve AB 22 and to fully fund expanded TK to increase program availability, enhance quality and provide a clearly differentiated TK curriculum that bridges the Preschool Learning Foundation standards to the kindergarten Common Core State Standards.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes that access to high quality early learning opportunities improves childrens social-emotional development, school readiness, and long-term academic achievement. A study by the American Institute of Research found that transitional kindergarten (TK) significantly improves kindergarten readiness, putting children ahead of their non-TK peers by up to six months in some academic skills, while also giving TK students an advantage in literacy, language, math, and problem solving skills compared to non-TK students even those with other preschool experiences. The study found that the benefits of TK are most significant for disadvantaged students: low-income children and English learners show the greatest learning improvements at the end of TK. These findings demonstrate that high-quality TK can be a powerful tool for narrowing opportunity and achievement gaps, and setting Californias neediest students on a path to success. Ten years ago, the Legislature approved the Kindergarten Readiness Act, which established TK to serve and educate four-year-olds within the public K-12 system, and yet, only 25 percent of four-year-olds (those with birthdays falling between September and December) are eligible to enroll in TK. The remaining 75 percent of four-year-olds are missing out on this opportunity for an additional year of high quality public education, which means many families lack access to high quality pre-K programs, and too many California children enter kindergarten unprepared for school and never catch up. San Diego Unified is addressing this gap by voluntarily expanding TK to prioritize access to children in communities with the highest need for a high-quality preschool education program, which includes low-income and often emerging bilingual children who need additional educational and co-curricular supports to ensure their developmental and academic success -- costs that districts bear with insufficient state funding. With historic rates of declining enrollment in most school districts, California has an opportunity to leverage existing school infrastructure for preventing the achievement gap and addressing the needs of working families. The Governors Master Plan for Early Learning and Care recommends achieving universal preschool for the states youngest learners by leveraging the existing TK program, and begins to implement this vision in the Governors 2021-22 Proposed Budget by offering one-time incentive grants to school districts that voluntarily expand TK. This is the time for California to do more, amid a pandemic, to invest in a recovery for our youngest learners by fully funding the cost of expanded TK through the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) because doing so retains LCFFs paramount equity goals of directing resources to students who need them the most. Despite the fiscal instability created by the pandemic, funding is available to make a meaningful investment in universal TK when the amount of funding to be deposited into the rainy day fund is adjusted for student attendance, as constitutionally required, allowing more funding to instead be used for necessary investments in Californias early education system. AB 22 will extend universal access to full-day TK programs to all four-year-olds statewide at no cost to families, while also implementing TK quality improvements to address the social-emotional and early academic development of Californias youngest learners. AB 22 will build upon and strengthens Californias existing TK law by phasing in full-day, universal TK for all four-year-olds while also implementing quality improvements including lower adult-to-student ratios and developmentally appropriate learning expectations. AB 22 will give families an additional option to ensure their children enter kindergarten with the maturity, confidence and skills they need to excel.

  • Community forums will be held in early November 2021 for the public to meet the finalists and provide feedback.
  • A permanent Superintendent will be selected by the beginning of December 2021 to allow for a month-long transition into the position.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve recommendation to name district facilities in honor of the life and service of Reverend George Walker Smith, who was a titan in San Diego civic life for six decades and a trailblazing leader in the region’s African-American community, as follows:

 

  • The "George Walker Smith Education Campus" will house Central Elementary School and Wilson Middle School.
  • The district's new Education Center will be named, "George Walker Smith Education Center." 

 

Reverend Smith’s life story, starting as a child of sharecroppers in rural, segregated Alabama, rising through his intelligence, academic excellence and discipline to transcend these limits, complete school and college and graduate from theological seminary to begin a life-long career of community service and activism, exemplifies the values and inspirations the San Diego Unified School District seeks to instill in every student.

 

When Reverend Smith arrived in San Diego in 1956, he founded the Golden Hill United Presbyterian Church, which eventually became the Christ United Presbyterian Church of San Diego, where he served as pastor until his retirement in 2000. Soon after moving to San Diego, Reverend Smith became one of the City’s foremost community leaders, and in 1963, he became the first African American to hold political office in San Diego with his election to the San Diego Unified School District’s Board of Education. During Reverend Smith’s four consecutive terms on the San Diego Unified board he served as President and advocated for a change in the district’s employment practices which led to more inclusive staffing and the employment of almost 800 African American and Latino teachers by the end of his fourth term in 1979.

 

With the intervention of the United States Office of Education and the Superior Court, Reverend Smith was able to overcome board opposition and establish the Voluntary Ethnic Enrollment Program (VEEP) and Magnet program to help eliminate racial imbalance in San Diego schools. Reverend Smith served on the National Advisory Commission on Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention, the White House Committee on Education and the Arts, the California School Boards Association, the board of the San Diego Community District the San Diego Crime Commission and as the first president of the police Citizens Advisory Review Board, and became a nationally recognized advocate for social justice. In 1989, Reverend Smith co-founded the Innovative Preschool Project, subsequently establishing the School of Success, now known as the McGill School of Success Charter. Sadly Reverend Smith passed on February 15, 2020, at 91 years old.

RECOMMENDATION: Determine that the Miramar Ranch Elementary School Block Wall and Switchgear Project is Exempt from Further Environmental Review Pursuant to California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines Sections 15301 – Existing Facilities. Approve Agreement with Byrom-Davey, Inc., to remove and replace block wall and main switchgear at Miramar Ranch Elementary School (CZ21-0621-23). The term of this Agreement is March 10, 2021, through completion of the project.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount: $910,027.00. Budget String: 9210-90404-00-6200-8500-0000-21400-8640 - Proposition Z Funds.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The project generally consists of replacing and moving existing electrical switchboard and concrete block retaining wall, and replacing electrical equipment boxes on the roof of the main building at Miramar Ranch Elementary School at 10770 Red Cedar Drive, San Diego, CA 92131.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code Section 20111(b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000.00 or more. The notice calling for bids was published once a week for two successive weeks in accordance with Public Contract Code section 20112. Bids were due and opened on February 11, 2021. The district received a total of seven bids from Byrom-Davey, Inc., MCV Enterprises, Inc., Nexgen Building Group, Inc., SOLPAC CONSTRUCTION, INC., dba Soltek Pacific Construction Company, Straight Line General Contractors, Inc., Team C Construction, and TL Veterans Construction. After evaluating the bids, staff determined Byrom-Davey, Inc., to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.

 

Additionally, consistent with the May 10, 2011 board approved resolution in support of service-disabled veteran-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE), which established a three percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities, the recommended contractor, Byrom-Davey, Inc., submitted a three percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Agreement to remove and replace a block wall and main switchgear at Miramar Ranch Elementary School (Proposed Project) is subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities. Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. Examples cited by the CEQA guidelines relevant to the Proposed Project include interior or exterior alterations including interior partitions, plumbing, and electrical conveyances. The Proposed Project involves the replacement and repair of existing facilities and would not result the expansion of the existing use at the school site. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school campus. The Proposed Project is consistent with the existing use on the project site. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction timeframe of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the replacement of an existing block wall and existing electrical switchgear. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. The closest designated state scenic highway is the portion of State Route 52 near Santo Road, located approximately 4.5 miles south of the project site. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not substantially damage scenic resources within a state scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Furthermore, according to the National Register of Historic Places (NRHP) guidelines, properties completed at least 50 years ago must be considered for eligibility for designation in the NRHP. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking because grading and trenching into fossil bearing soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.

Approval of this item approves the Proposed Project as described, and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and approves the proposed Agreement with Byrom-Davey, Inc., to remove and replace a block wall and main switchgear at Miramar Ranch Elementary School.
