RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of recognizing October 2018, as Filipino American Heritage Month for all schools and students in San Diego Unified.

 

BACKGROUND: In 1988, Filipino American History Month was first proclaimed by the 103rd Congress of the United States through House Resolution 155.

 

The Filipino American community is the second largest Asian American group in the United States with a population of approximately 3,100,000 people and over 5,000 students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

Filipino Americans have contributed greatly to the fine arts, music, dance, literature, business, journalism, education, science, technology, government, politics, fashion, health care, the armed forces, and many other fields that enrich the landscape of the country.

 

Efforts should continue to promote Filipino American history and culture, because the roles of Filipino Americans and other people of color have been overlooked in the writing, teaching, and learning of United States history.

 

In September 1988, Bell Junior High School was the largest junior high school west of the Mississippi, and had the largest concentration of Filipino students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

In 1989, the Filipino community rallied to advocate the offering of Filipino language classes at Morse High School and Bell Junior High School.

 

Filipino language classes are now offered at Challenger Middle School and Mira Mesa High School as well.

 

Filipino Heritage Month is a time of reflection and remembrance, and provides an opportunity for all people to learn and appreciate Filipino Americans’ historic contributions.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of recognizing October 2018, as National Bullying Prevention Month for all school and students in San Diego Unified.

 

BACKGROUND: The National Bullying Prevention Month campaign was founded in 2006.

 

San Diego Unified School District Vision 2020 calls for all schools to be “safe, attractive neighborhood learning centers,” and each quality neighborhood school to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.”

 

School district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing updated and revised protocols and procedures, participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved.

 

San Diego Unified has partnered with the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and their No Place for Hate program which provides participating schools with anti-bias and anti-bullying resources to help create sustainable change that leads to an improved school environment.

 

The San Diego Unified Council of ASB Presidents fully supports October as National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within the month and throughout the year. Students’ participation in anti-bullying activities provide opportunities for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and find solace in the knowledge that others have been in similar situations.
  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying which people may experience including, but not limited to, cyber, verbal, physical, and exclusion; on the basis of actual or perceived race, religion, national origin, disability, sexuality, gender identity and/or socio-economic status.
  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus and the consequences of these actions.
  • Support students to come up with solutions to stop bullying and enforce positive interactions between students.
  • Engage teachers and parents in raising awareness if a student is being bullied.
  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying.
  • Help empower students in the recognition of the role everyone plays in addressing and reducing bullying with the goal to eradicate bullying on all school campuses.

All elementary, middle, and high schools shall coordinate anti-bullying activities at the beginning of each new school year to be held in the month of October, during a week or days of their choosing.

BACKGROUND:

 

History

In 2008, the Board of Education adopted a Mission Statement; however there was no clear cut plan to carry out that mission.  In 2009, at the time of the adoption of Vision 2020, the Class of 2020 was in first grade.  The Board committed to achieving Vision 2020's goals by the time of their graduation. This document was to serve as our report card. We are now two years away from 2020.  From 2009-2013, in spite of the financial crisis and reduced education funding, we continued to discuss and refine our goals.  By 2013, with the appointment of a new Superintendent, Cindy Marten, Vision 2020 forums were held across the district and by 2015, we stared to see real results from this initiative.

 

Mission Statement

All San Diego students will graduate with the skills, motivation, curiosity and resilience to succeed in their choice of college and career in order to lead and participate in the society of tomorrow.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood

To prepare our students for the competitive global economy they will face in years ahead the San Diego Unified Board of Education has developed a focused, long term plan for student achievement. It culminates with graduation of the Class of 2020. Vision 2020 is a community-based school reform plan that engages all parents, staff, students and community members. It will result in a high level of confidence in our public schools. Between now and 2020 we intend to develop a world-class school system for all of our students.  The Board will hold itself responsible for maintaining the focus of the staff and the community on Vision 2020. The Board will review Vision 2020 at the end of each school year to assess progress towards the goals of the vision. The Board designates to the Superintendent the development of the strategic process for the implementation of the goals of Vision 2020.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. BACKGROUND: In 2013, the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee (ESAC) was formed at the request of the district’s Board of Education to discuss a wide range of environmental sustainability initiatives to support Vision 2020. On July 8, 2014 and January 20, 2015, the ESAC presented 13 “Dream Big” ideas to the board which included solar energy installations as one of the most effective ways of reducing greenhouse gas emissions and utility costs. The 13 "Dream Big" ideas, including solar energy, were subsequently approved by the board on July 14, 2015.

 

Thereafter, on September 14, 2015, the district publicly advertised Request for Qualifications (RFQ) No. PS-16-0433-02 for the prequalification of firms to participate in subsequent Request for Proposals (RFP) for the Design-Build Delivery of Solar Power Generation Systems. The RFQ was advertised in the San Diego Daily Transcript and posted on DemandStar to 163 subscribers.

 

Five firms responded by the closing date of October 8, 2015, and were evaluated on their prequalification submittals. Four (4) firms qualified to participate in the RFP process for the initial eight (8) sites (PS-17-0144-02 and PS-17-0128-02) in Round 1, and additional projects being added in subsequent years. The four (4) prequalified firms are: Borrego Solar Systems, Inc. (Borrego), ENGIE Services U.S. Inc. (formerly Opterra Energy Services, hereinafter “ENGIE”), SunPower Corporation Systems (SunPower), and Rosendin Electric, Inc. (Rosendin).

 

On September 13, 2016, the Board approved Master Agreements with ENGIE (PS-17-0144-02) and SunPower (PS-17-0128-02) to provide design-build services for installation of solar power generation systems at seven (7) sites. Firm selection was based on a competitive solicitation process which was detailed on the September 13, 2016 board report.

 

The eighth site from Round 1, Penn Elementary School, was not assigned to a firm on the aforementioned Master Agreements and was reserved for a firm that ultimately decided not to enter into an agreement with the district. Thus, on December 13, 2016, the Board approved assignment of Penn Elementary School site to ENGIE for completion of the solar project under the terms of the agreement (PS-17-0512-02).

 

On September 14, 2017 the subsequent RFP (phase two) No. PS-18-0281-06 for Design-Build Solar power generation at 10 sites was issued to the four (4) prequalified firms.

 

Three of the four prequalified firms submitted their RFP proposals (ENGIE, SunPower, Rosendin) by the closing date of October 23, 2017 and were scored by a qualified evaluation committee on the basis of best value. Eight categories were scored for a possible total of 100 points for each vendor. The committee considered; (1) Overall Costs; (2) Overall Responsiveness of the Proposal; (3) Technical Expertise; (4) Component Quality; (5) Technical Proposal; (6) Management Plan; (7) Qualifications for Subcontractors; (8) Past Performance/Customer Satisfaction. The members of the evaluation committee consisted of Bernize Gil, Financial Planning and Development Analyst; Mike Clark, Project Manager; Joel Hermosillo, Project Manager; Darin Vey, Manager (TOC), Safety, Training & Environmental Compliance; and Francisco Campuzano, Senior Project Manager. Based on the final ratings of the panel, two firms were recommended for award in order to balance the projected workload at the following school sites: Kearny High School, Bell Junior High School, University City High School, School of Creative and Performing Arts (SCPA), Chollas/Mead Elementary School, Knox Elementary School, Henry High School, Creative Performing and Media Arts (CPMA), De Portola Middle School, and Madison High School. Rosendin’s proposal scored the lowest and the Committee rejected their proposal.

 

Contract negotiations are continuing for three (3) of the 10 sites at this time, and will be submitted to a future board meeting for approval upon completion.

 

Generally, the solar power generation systems would consist of photovoltaic (PV) arrays mounted onto canopy structures and/or a ground-mount array system in existing graded and disturbed areas within the campus, such as playgrounds, parking lots, and decomposed granite playfields. All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. The Proposed Project would be designed to ensure that all existing ADA accessible paths of travel are maintained or reconfigured as prescribed by code. Electrical transmission lines would be installed in an underground conduit and would extend from the combiner to the main electrical service in one of the existing school buildings. All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

 

Procedurally, the district plans to issue separate Notices to Proceed (NTP) against the Agreements as the work arises at each site. The NTPs are incorporated by reference into the Agreements and may be amended or ratified from time to time, as Amendments to the Agreements. The selected solar firms will also be tasked with commissioning each solar system for two years. After the initial two-year commissioning period, district staff will perform any necessary maintenance and operation services.

 

Once complete, the solar systems at the aforementioned 10 phase two schools are estimated to produce approximately 3.8178 Million kilowatt-hours (3,817,800 kWh) electricity each year that will offset CO2 greenhouse gas emissions and utility costs (approximately $775,767.00 annually based on current utility rates).

 

The proposed award of this contract for design build services for solar power generation projects at Chollas-Mead Elementary School, Henry High School, Kearny High School, Knox Middle School, School of Creative and Performing Arts, and University City High School are subject to the requirements of the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et. seq., as amended), because it authorizes construction of the above mentioned projects in accordance with the terms and specifications that were publicly bid and no further discretionary action is required by the board in order for these projects to proceed.

 

On July 11, 2017 and February 27, 2018, staff filed Notice of Exemptions for solar power generation projects at Chollas-Mead Elementary, CPMA Middle School, De Portola Middle School, Kearny High School, Knox Middle School, Madison High School, School of Creative and Performing Arts, University City High School, and Henry High School with the County of San Diego Recorder/Clerk (See attachment 1 and 2).

BACKGROUND: On November 6, 2013, Proposition 39, the California Clean Energy Jobs Act, was passed by voters. Under this initiative, schools are eligible for funding that must be fully executed by June 30, 2020. The California Energy Commission (CEC) is tasked to administer Prop 39 with the overarching approach that local education agencies first maximize energy efficiency, and then install clean, on-site energy generation, and lastly, consider non-renewable energy projects. 

 

This agreement focuses on five district sites with a total of 11 projects. The goal of these projects is to save energy and money that will allow the District to redirect funds toward educational purposes for many years to come.

 

The district publicly advertised Request for Qualifications (RFQ) No. PS-16-0541-57 for prequalification of firms to participate in an RFP for the design-build delivery of energy efficient projects as turnkey operations. The RFQ was advertised in the San Diego Daily Transcript and posted on DemandStar.

 

Seven (7) firms responded by the closing date, and were evaluated on their prequalification submittals. The three firms that qualified (Schneider Electric, Noresco, and Barnhart-Reese) were invited to participate in the RFP No. PS-18-0280-06, which was issued on December 15, 2017.

 

The prequalified firms submitted their proposals by the closing date of January 30, 2018, and were scored by a qualified Evaluation Committee on the basis of best value. Five (5) categories were scored for a possible total of 100 points for each vendor. The Committee considered (1) project budget, (2) schedule, (3) design, (4) support/training, and (5) overall value. The members of the Evaluation Committee consisted of Mike Clark, Project Manager; Bernize Gil, Financial Planning and Development Analyst; Curtis Hall, Lead Planner Estimator Inspector; Chris Palis, Energy/Utilities Program Supervisor; and Darin Vey, Manager (TOC), Safety, Training & Environmental Compliance. Based on the final ratings of the panel, Schneider Electric was recommended for award in order to complete 11 projects at five (5) sites: Physical Plant Operations (PPO), Vista Grande Elementary School, Supply Center/Warehouse, Lincoln High School, and Green Elementary School. The Committee rejected proposals from Noresco and Barnhart-Reese because they did not propose the best value.

 

Procedurally, the district plans to issue separate Notices to Proceed (NTP) against the Agreement as the work arises at each site. The NTPs are incorporated by reference into the Agreement and may be amended or ratified from time to time, as Amendments to the Agreement. Schneider Electric will also be tasked with third-party commissioning of each energy efficient measure and solar photovoltaic system, as well as retro-commissioning of existing equipment. Upon completion of installation, commissioning, and acceptance of the installed system as functioning as designed, district staff will perform any necessary maintenance and operations services.

 

Once complete, the Prop 39 projects at the aforementioned five Phase 1 sites are estimated to produce approximately 1,222,049 kWH of energy in year one of operation that will offset $267,101.00 in utility costs based on current electricity rates.

 

Following installation of the Phase 1 Prop 39 projects at the five sites, district staff anticipates a second phase of 12 sites with a total of 16 projects to be installed between 2018 and 2019. For the second phase of Prop 39 projects, staff will issue a new RFP to the prequalified firms for response and evaluation.
