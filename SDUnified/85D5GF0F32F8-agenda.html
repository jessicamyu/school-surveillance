BACKGROUND:  District practice has been for sites to develop their Single Plans for Student Achievement (SPSA) during the spring preceding the year of implementation. Problems last spring with the newly adopted Site-Based Budgeting (SBB) tool, that includes a function to develop SPSAs, required delaying SPSA development until this fall.

 

As a result of SB 374, the state holds each school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA.  Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

Additionally, Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to the respective area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their smart goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

Prior to implementation, the local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2010–11 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 52 schools.

Copies of each SPSA, budget summary, and required, signed assurances page are available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve Scenario 1 (status quo) identified in the 2011–12 Title I Eligibility Ranking and Three Allocation Scenarios report as the method for distribution of Title I funds to district schools. This method maintains stability for schools’ categorical budgets in a year when they will be subjected to reductions from other funding sources. Additionally, staff also recommends, as permitted in Title I regulations, to fund, for one more year, three schools (Marvin Elementary, Standley Middle, and Whittier/Del Sol) that will drop below the 40 percent threshold.
 
FISCAL IMPACT:  $21,850,814 Title I Entitlement.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  $23,008,670
 
IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.
 
BACKGROUND:  The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires that funding is allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources. Key factors that affect the school allocations are:

  • Potential addition of 35 schools to Program Improvement status.
  • K-12 student enrollment at district-managed schools decreased by 1009 students from 117,010 in 2009-10 to 116,001 in 2010-11.
  • The number of free and reduced-price meal eligible students has increased over the past several years.
  • Impact of the district continuing in Program Improvement.


The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). The District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC), at its November 17 meeting, surveyed its members regarding Title I Ranking minimum funding level percentage (e.g., 40 percent, 60 percent, 75 percent) and a tiered versus sliding scale methodology. A majority of members supported continuing to fund schools at the 40 percent and higher level of students eligible for free or reduced-price meals and changing from a tiered to a sliding scale method.

5. Resolution Clarifying the Statutory Role of the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee (ICOC) and Process for Appointment of Members (First Reading); BUDGETED: N/A (GC-5)
Meeting
Dec 14, 2010 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information, First Reading

 

RECOMMENDATION: Consider this item and recommendations as a first reading on December 14, 2010 and as an action item on January 11, 2011 to adopt the following recommendations:
(a) Duties and Authorized Activities of the Independent Citizen’s Oversight Committee” (Attachment A)
(b) Resolution Clarifying Statutory Role (Attachment B)
(c) Proposed “Application, screening, interview, recommendation and approval process for membership on the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee” (See Attachment “C”)

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

BACKGROUND:  The purpose of this item is to clarify the statutorily-defined role of the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee (ICOC) and to provide a process for selecting and appointing community members to serve on the ICOC.

 

On November 4, 2008, the voters of the San Diego Unified School District approved a ballot measure authorizing the district to issue up to $2.1 billion dollars in general obligation bonds (Prop S) to be used as identified in the ballot measure.  The election was conducted under Proposition 39, chaptered as the Strict Accountability in Local School Construction Bonds Act of 2000, at Section 15264 et seq. of the California Education Education Code.  Pursuant to Section 15278 of the Education Code, the District is obligated to establish an Independent Citizen's Oversight Committee in order to satisfy the accountability requirements of Proposition 39.  The Board of Education established the Prop S ICOC on November 18, 2008 and approved its Bylaws most recently on June 8, 2010.   

 

The ICOC is comprised of 11 members, eight of whose terms expire in February 2011.  The proposed "Application, screening, interview, recommendation and approval process for membership on the Independent Citizen's Oversight Committee" provides that further appointments to the ICOC shall be made by the Board of Education at a regularly scheduled, properly noticed public meeting through the following process:  (a) appropriate local and professional groups will be solicited for applications; (b) the Superintendent/President or his or her designee will review the applications; (c) the Superintendent/President or his or her designee will submit the qualifications of each applicant to the board and make appointment recommendations to the board, and (d) the board shall appoint members by majority vote following public disclosure of each applicant’s qualifications and solicitation of public comment regarding the nominees.  Any individual wishing to be considered for appointment or re-appointment to the ICOC will be required to complete an application form and undergo an interview with the Board of Education, and will be selected in accordance with the following criteria for board membership and composition. 

  

Proposition 39 requires that the composition of the ICOC include at least one of the following:

  • One member that is active in a business organization representing the business community located with the district
  • One member who is active in a senior citizens' organization
  • One member of a bona fide taxpayers’ association
  • One member who is the parent or guardian of a child enrolled in the district
  • One member who is the parent or guardian of a child enrolled in the district and is active in a parent/teacher organization.

The current Bylaws include the above requirements and additionally require that one ICOC member have previously been a member of the Prop MM ICOC.  The requirement for previous service on the Prop MM ICOC will be eliminated under the proposed amendment to the Bylaws. 

 

Under the proposed amendment, while meeting the above statutory requirements, district staff recommends that eight of the ICOC members be selected from the following labor organizations, professional organizations, trade associations and public sector organizations whose members perform their duties in the commercial construction industry or organizations in the public sector that execute large scale capital programs.  Inclusion of individuals having these skills will contribute to the efficient and effective execution of program. 

  • Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers 
  • American Institute of Architects
  • Association of General Contractors
  • The American Institute of Certified Public Accounting/CalCPA
  • Construction Managers Association of America
  • San Diego Regional Chamber of Commerce
  • San Diego and Imperial County Building and Construction Trades Council
  • City of San Diego, County of San Diego, State of California, or Federal Agency Representatives

The Resolution Clarifying the Statutory Duties of the ICOC is intended to provide further clarity to the ICOC members, the board, district staff and members of the community regarding the powers and duties assigned to the ICOC under Proposition 39; thereby increasing the efficiency and effectiveness of the ICOC.  In specific, the document includes language that details duties of the Committee, authorized activities of the Committee, and authority specifically reserved by the Board of Education.  While in general, these items are outlined in the governing statutes in the California Constitution (Article XIIIA) and are required under Proposition 39, the district believes it is productive to clarify these duties and in activities in a manner consistent with applicable laws.

Reeves (2003).  “90/90/90" is a term used to describe schools that have 90 percent or more of the students eligible for free and reduced lunch, 90 percent or more of the students are members of ethnic minority groups, and 90 percent or more of the students meet the district or state academic standards in reading or another area (Reeves, 2000).

 

Rowan meets the first two 90/90/90 identifiers, but is far from having 90 percent of its students reach academic proficiency.  Among the common characteristics of high achievement schools are: Focus on Academic Achievement, Frequent Assessment of Student Progress with Multiple Opportunities for Improvement, Written Responses in Performance Assessments and External Scoring. 

 

Rowan’s main focus within these common characteristics will be on written responses in performance assessments, with an emphasis on non-fiction, informative writing scored with district rubrics.  Teacher collaborative team will be focused on analyzing student written work.  The teaching artists will create visual and performing art experiences to extend non-fiction writing learning.

  • The Impact of Collaboration
  • The Value of Feedback
  •  Action Research
  •  Mid-Course Corrections
  • Constructive Data Analysis
  • Using Common Assessmentsand
  • Cross-Disciplinary Integration

Maintenance Strategy
Physical Plant Operations (PPO) maintains and repairs the district’s asphalt systems utilizing in-house and contract resources.  Primary aspects of the asphalt maintenance program include: planned maintenance (PM) and reactive maintenance (site-generated repair orders). Planned maintenance (PM) is the most predictable and cost-effective method of maintaining a usable and appropriate surface for students and staff.  PPO supports a four-year maintenance cycle that includes some combination of asphalt repair, slurry seal and line striping for every district site. Reactive maintenance consists mainly of small, isolated asphalt repairs and safety concerns such as potholes and cracks. Each year the planned maintenance of the district’s asphalt is evaluated for updates and improvements by PPO planning staff. With this separation, a seal coat crew of three to four professional district staff and seal coat equipment can re-seal approximately 25 percent of the district each year, visiting almost every district site in a four year cycle. The cost of sealing the asphalt is approximately 4 percent of the cost of removal and replacement and is by far the most economical way to preserve and maximize the life cycle of this district asset.
