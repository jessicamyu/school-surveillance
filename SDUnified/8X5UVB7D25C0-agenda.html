BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue the improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting districtwide, a new process will be implemented for streamlining Independent Contractor Agreements. The goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

RECOMMENDATION: Accept petitioner's voluntary withdrawal. Deny the charter petition to establish a new charter school, the San Diego Military Academy, and adopt the Findings of Fact resolution, attached hereto as Exhibit A.  The proposed Findings of Fact include the following:

  1. The San Diego Military Academy petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because the petitioners’ past history of involvement in charter schools has been unsuccessful. Previously, in 2012, the San Diego Military Academy petitioners submitted parent signatures that were determined to be for individuals who do not exist and/or their names were fabricated; in addition, the contact information listed was for local bars or medical offices that were not connected to the named signor.  (See Exhibit B)  Of the current charter petition, only 4 of 71 signatures could be verified as originating from parents who are meaningfully interested in having their children attend the proposed charter school, which demonstrates the petitioners are unlikely to attract a sufficient number of students to the program.
     
  2. The San Diego Military Academy petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because the petitioners are unfamiliar with the content of the Petition and the requirements of law that would apply to the proposed charter school.  In addition to numerous typographical errors, the charter petition includes statements that are inaccurate, are not applicable to charter schools authorized by the San Diego Unified School District, appear to be “copy and pasted” from other charter petitions, and/or are contrary to law.  For example, the charter petition includes references to the San Bernardino County Board of Education and language from the High Tech High Middle Media Arts charter petition.
     
  3. The San Diego Military Academy petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because the petitioners have presented an unrealistic financial and operational plan.  In the area of financial administration, the petitioners did not include the charter school’s first-year cash flow, the first-year operational budget shows a negative net income, and the budget assumptions do not clearly identify expenditures.
     
  4. The San Diego Military Academy charter petition does not contain reasonably comprehensive descriptions of multiple elements required by law, including the educational program, governance structure, and closure procedures.

    With regard to the educational program, the San Diego Military Academy charter petition does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of how the charter school will meet the needs of students with disabilities.  Specifically, the San Diego Military Academy charter petition contains conflicting information with respect to the provision of special education and related services.  Page 16 of the charter petition states that the San Diego Military Academy “will participate as an LEA member of the San Diego Unified School District SELPA,” however, page 43 states, “San Diego Military Academy will be deemed a public school for the purposes of special education within the district and not as an LEA.”  (Underline added.)  Moreover, on page 57 of the San Diego Military Academy charter petition includes the following language that appears to be “copy and pasted” from another charter school, “In concert with other HTH Schools, the School participates as an LEA member of the San Diego City Unified School District SELPA.”

    With regard to the governance structure, the San Diego Military Academy charter petition does not provide a reasonably comprehensive description of the affiliation between the charter school and True North Education, the nonprofit corporation that is described as having the right to approve the appointment of up to one half of the trustees of the charter school governing board.

    With regard to closure, the San Diego Military Academy charter petition does not provide a reasonably comprehensive description of the procedures to be used if the school closes.  For example, the San Diego Military Academy charter petition does not include the identification of funding for closure-related activities.

RECOMMENDATION:  To direct Superintendent and staff to utilize the name “San Diego Unified” or “SD Unified” when abbreviating San Diego Unified School District, rather than SDUSD, a confusing acronym. To direct the Superintendent and staff to expand the usage already started by the Communications Department to utilize “San Diego Unified”  that began when the new “Unified” logo was introduced a few years ago.  This effort will avoid confusion regarding the district’s identity, while emphasizing the concept of a unified district of diverse neighborhoods.  Furthermore, this effort will support the identity of Vision 2020: A Quality School in Every Neighborhood. This emphasizes that ours is a diverse district and that all of our neighborhood schools and clusters are made stronger by a “unified” district.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: “San Diego Unified” has often been abbreviated to “SDUSD.”  (To further confuse matters the district was referred to as “San Diego City Schools” for a period of time.) The school district is referred to as a “unified” district because it is comprised of both elementary and secondary schools along the K-12 continuum.

“SDUSD” is not an easily decipherable acronym.  It is very similar to SDSU (San Diego State University) and close to USD (University of San Diego) and not too different from the University of California (UCSD).

 

San Diego Unified is unique among school districts in the nation that tend to be divided between urban and suburban districts.  Our city includes both high poverty inner city neighborhoods, as well as affluent suburban neighborhoods.  Besides its broad geographic and economic diversity, it includes diversity of race, ethnicity, and national origin, as well as diversity in language and cultural background.  In the 1970's San Diego Unified began an extensive voluntary integration program and kept the district “unified” as a diverse district.
