BACKGROUND: Harvey Milk was born May 22, 1930 in Woodmere, New York.

 

Harvey served in the United States Navy during the Korean War as a diving officer aboard the submarine rescue ship USS Kittiwake and was later transferred to Naval Station, San Diego to serve as a diving instructor with the rank of Lieutenant.

 

Harvey was a tireless advocate of fairness for all Americans, both as a candidate for office and as a San Francisco Supervisor.

 

In 1977, Harvey was elected to the San Francisco Board of Supervisors, becoming the first openly gay man to be elected to public office in California. This milestone achievement gave hope to millions of gays and lesbians across the country that a day would come when they could live their lives openly and honestly without fear of discrimination.

 

As a Supervisor, Harvey worked with others to secure passage of San Francisco’s landmark Gay Rights Ordinance. This ordinance extended employment protections to gays and lesbians in San Francisco, and it became a model for anti-discrimination legislation throughout California and the nation.

 

In 1978, Harvey traversed the state to campaign against Proposition 6, which was known as the Briggs Initiative. Had it passed, Proposition 6 would have required California school districts to fire openly gay and lesbian teachers solely because of their sexual orientation. The proposition was defeated in the November 1978 election in part because Harvey successfully appealed to Californians’ basic sense of fairness.

 

Although Harvey’s life was cut short, he was a hero and role model, and his legacy of hope, tolerance, and equality lives on.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in support of Assembly Bill 1721 (Medina) and Assembly Bill 1892 (Medina), Cal Grants and College Affordability, both of which will reform the Cal Grant system to address completion barriers by helping California community college students take more classes and be academically successful.

 

At the annual joint meeting of the San Diego Unified School District and San Diego Community College District Boards, the Boards agreed to support this legislative initiative.

 

BACKGROUND: California is projected to face a significant gap in both skilled workers and college graduates; and increasing the number of college graduates and skilled workers is a priority for Governor Brown and the Legislature.

 

California has made an important investment in need-based, financial aid for California community college students through the California College Community College Board of Governors (BOG) Fee Waiver Program.

 

Students at California community colleges are more likely to be the first in their family to attend college, to be in need of retraining, to come from an immigrant background, or to be economically disadvantaged.

 

A major factor in a student’s enrollment and ultimate success in college is affordability, including fees and tuition, textbooks, transportation, housing, food, and other access costs; and the total cost of attendance, including financial aid available for students (federal, state, and institutional), for students attending a California community college is more expensive than a University of California or California State University.

 

The lack of state financial aid support has forced community college students to work more and take fewer classes, reducing the chances of achieving academic success.

 

AB 1721 (Medina) will help reform the Cal Grant B program to better serve California community college students by increasing the access award level and number of competitive Cal Grants available, and expand the access to the transfer entitlement program by raising the age limit and extending the application deadline.

 

AB 1892 (Medina) will help reform the Cal Grant C program to better serve California community college students in Career Technical Education programs by creating an entitlement program for economically disadvantage students and increase the access award level.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in support of Assembly Bill 2364 (Holden) Public Postsecondary Education: Exemption from Nonresident Tuition, to ensure that all of California’s high school students are able to access concurrent or dual enrollment programs without being charged nonresident tuition fees.

 

At the annual joint meeting of the San Diego Unified School District and San Diego Community College District Boards, the Boards agreed to support this legislative initiative.

 

BACKGROUND: AB 2364 will ensure that all of California’s high school students are able to access concurrent or dual enrollment programs without being charged nonresident tuition fees and would also allow community college districts to claim apportionment for students who are eligible for the nonresident tuition exemption.

 

AB 2364 corrects an oversight in the 2015 dual-enrollment reform measure, AB 288 (Holden), which resulted in some undocumented students in California being charged nonresident tuition fees.

 

AB 2364 ensures that concurrent and dual enrollment programs are accessible by explicitly stating that any student, regardless of immigration status, who meets the requirements of a concurrent or dual enrollment program is eligible for resident tuition.

 

This will ensure that students who arrive at college academically prepared and with some accumulation of college credit have a greater chance of success and maximizing their lifetime educational and career achievements.

 

AB 288 did not expressly authorize a community college district to claim state apportionment for nonresident, high school students participating in a Certified Community Action Professional (CCAP) partnership; and corrects this apportionment eligibility deficiency in existing California Education Code, which presents a barrier to the full implementation of CCAP partnerships.

 

Concurrent and dual enrollment programs help to close the gap between students who need basic remediation and those who do not. California should be doing all it can to promote access to these innovative opportunities.

The Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department utilizes a web-based reverse auction process to competitively solicit electronic bids for xerographic paper.  On March 17, 2016, a reverse auction was held. There were five bidders that participated in this reverse auction with 63 changes in price occurring during the auction.  Bids were received from LinkMax Paper, Ltd., Liberty Paper, Unisource, Contract Paper Group, AFP Industries, Costco Wholesale, CPS HR Consulting, Elite Textile Trading, LLC., Exert Technology Solutions, Express Office Products, Quill Corporation, Paper 101, Ribbons Express, Sunrise Press, Veritiv, and Xpedx.  The final bid price was $22.45 per case which is $0.53 lower than the last contract award.
