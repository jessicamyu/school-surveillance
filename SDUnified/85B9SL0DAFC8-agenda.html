Budget Source
Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) funds from the State of California and Proposition 20 lottery funds
Recommendations:
The Superintendent and the Instructional Materials Adoption Committees recommend board approval of the attached list of textbooks and accompanying digital instructional materials. These new materials are needed to replace older curriculum textbooks with new, more explicitly standards-based and technologically-advanced materials. After a thorough analysis of the most critical instructional needs in the district, over $2 million in purchases previously recommended have been withdrawn: Included in this list of delayed (or cancelled) purchases are Economics, American Literature, British Literature, and Graphic Communications textbooks.

Budget Impact Summary:
This recommendation will require an expenditure of $6,118,321. This amount is funded from non-flexible “carry-over” funds from the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) funds and lottery funds.

Curriculum Areas/Need Summary

1. Middle School Mathematics: $2,725,086
The current Grade 6 math, pre-Algebra, and Algebra titles were “first generation” math materials created in response to new math standards over a decade ago. Approval of the proposed curriculum will place in our students’ hands more explicit standards-based instructional materials, and provide teachers with an articulated sequence of textbooks across the middle grades. Electronic versions of the materials, online student tutorials, workbooks, and extensive teacher training are included.

2. World Languages: $740,198
French, Spanish, and German language books are needed for next year. The present materials are eight years old and do not explicitly address the current framework and standards for this subject area. New textbooks will include electronic formats, online support, and student workbooks for the “life of the adoption.” The Education Code requires that world language be offered beginning at Grade 7, and as the district moves toward a-g education for all students, these materials will be crucial.

3. High School Geometry, Chemistry, and Chemistry Honors: $1,974,553
The new materials provide a much closer correlation and explicit connection to California standards. Technology-based instructional materials characterize the materials included in this recommendation.

4. Advanced Placement: $611,219
New books (with technology) for over 2,000 Advanced Placement (AP) students of AP English Lit/ Composition, Art History, and Chemistry across the district; includes support for more AP courses in SDUSD.

5. Music Appreciation: $67,265
Over 700 students in fifteen different secondary schools take this Visual and Performing Arts (VAPA) course each year. To date, there has not been a common textbook available to meet the needs of students across the district. This purchase will formalize instruction in music appreciation classes across the district.

School Site Involvement:
This year, 157 teachers served as voting committee members on 23 adoption committees. Textbook Adoption Committee members were recruited districtwide through memos to every principal, flyers sent to every school for posting in teacher and parent areas, and numerous announcements in Newsline and the Operations Update. Principals approved the teachers’ participation. Committees were balanced first by geographical representation so all stakeholders were represented. Approximately $115,000 dollars was spent to pay for teacher substitute days and hourly pay for after school meetings and independent evaluation time.

RECOMMENDATION: Review and accept the School Assistance and Intervention Team (SAIT) Third Progress Report for Wilson Middle School and the Seventh Progress Reports for Marshall Elementary and Mann Middle Schools.

This action is a requirement of the Public Schools Accountability Act (PSAA) of 1999 for schools participating in the Immediate Intervention/Underperforming Schools Program (II/USP) and High Priority Schools Grant Program (HPSGP). The PSAA established the II/USP for schools in deciles one through five on the Academic Performance Index (API) that fail to meet their API growth targets. Participating schools that do not meet acceptable progress criteria are identified as state-monitored.

The State Board of Education requires the district to enter into a contract with a SAIT provider on behalf of a school. The SAIT provider is responsible for assessing the degree to which the school is implementing the nine Essential Program Components (EPCs) and provides the Report of Findings and Correction Action Plan.

The board approved a contract with Perry and Associates to provide SAIT teams for Marshall Elementary and Mann Middle Schools on December 12, 2006 and Wilson Middle School on December 11, 2007. The board reviewed the Corrective Action Plan for Marshall and Mann on February 12, 2007 and for Wilson on March 1, 2008.

The SAIT provider is required to assess a school’s progress three times per year, and to produce three monitoring reports annually that inform the local board, district, school, community and CDE of a school’s progress towards implementing the correction action and targeted benchmarks.
Wilson Middle has fully achieved 17 of its 32 benchmarks and substantially achieved 15.

Marshall Elementary has fully achieved 16 of its 44 benchmarks, substantially achieved 10 and partially achieved 18.

Mann Middle has fully achieved 17 of its 53 benchmarks, substantially achieved 32, and partially achieved 4.

There is no cost associated with this item.

Recommendation: Approval of License and Services Agreement No. 89-300-14 with Oracle USA, Inc. for the purchase of software licenses for the PeopleSoft Business Intelligence (BI), eSettlements, eSupplier Connection and Supplier Contract Management modules; and for the purchase of technical support for these modules from May 29, 2009 through June 30, 2010 at a total cost of $1,493,250.78. This cost represents a 74 percent cost reduction when compared to published license and support pricing for these modules; and

Approval of License and Services Agreement No. 89-301-14 with Oracle USA, Inc. for the purchase of Assisted Services, as and when required, at a cost not to exceed $24,150.92.

Budget implications include the following: an annual increase in our Oracle software maintenance agreement in the amount of $313,561 and a onetime implementation cost associated with bringing up the data warehouse and BI tools as well as the new procurement modules. The onetime implementation costs are estimated to be $700,000 and will be paid for by state school facilities funds. Pending approval we will proceed with an RFP for these specific services.

Background Information On November 12, 2002, the board approved a contract to purchase the PeopleSoft Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) software system to modernize the district’s Human Capital Management (Human Resources) and Financials systems. This system was implemented and became operational in the fall 2003. Since that time, both PeopleSoft Systems have been upgraded from version 8.4 to 8.9., and PeopleSoft has been acquired by Oracle.

The district now realizes that it must build upon the initial investment in 2002 by incorporating additional Business Intelligence (BI) system functionality into the PeopleSoft system that will convert the district’s available data into actionable information for board members, district and school administrators, teachers and parents. Building a data-driven culture focused on continuous improvement and educational excellence will then enable individualized instruction and improved accountability, transparency and efficiency. In order to deliver meaningful information, the district must have the software tools to query information in disparate key operational systems, and have the flexibility to present it in a format that is appropriate for the role of the user.

The district also realizes that it must build upon this initial investment by incorporating additional functionality into its Financials system to improve productivity and make transformational advances in both its Procurement and Accounts Payable units. These modules will streamline the vendor payment process; provide a secure gateway for vendors to self-service their company's information about its purchase orders, invoices and payments; and provide a collaborative tool for the preparation of bids and requests for proposals (RFPs), thereby reducing the time required to complete competitive solicitations that are required in order to submit agreements on the board's agendas for approval.

The Assisted Services agreement provides the district's Chief Information and Technology Officer with specialized resources that are sometimes required from Oracle to support the utilization and operation of the current and additional PeopleSoft modules. These resources provide advanced customer support on-site when needed to solve critical issues affecting the system's operation.

The Oracle/PeopleSoft Business Intelligence (BI) system functionality provides:

  • Access to summary data tied to SDUSD key performance indicators with the ability to drill down to details such as the individual student, teacher, or learning module;
  • Access to historical data for trending and longitudinal analysis;
  • Identification and tracking of leading indicators that will enable the district and its schools to take a preventative approach;
  • The ability to view data in a variety of easy to read graphical formats including charts, graphs, gauges and stop light reporting;
  • Analytical power to go beyond top/bottom performers and identify what is working and which students are at risk for failure;
  • Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) aligned to the district’s vision and goals that measure not just instructional outcomes but the district’s performance against other critical goals such as financial efficiency, employee professional development and retention of highly qualified teachers;
  • The ability to establish thresholds on these KPIs and be notified in real-time when these thresholds are triggered, initiating immediate remedial action;
  • Personalized dashboards that push critical information to all types of users in a format that is meaningful to them while allowing power users to conduct their own queries and analysis;
  • An extensible, logical view of all the district’s data sources, including PeopleSoft, Encore, Zangle, Food Service, Transportation and the current Cognos system;
  • A pre-built data warehouse for PeopleSoft Human Capital Management and Financials data;
  • An extensible application that is pre-built for the PeopleSoft source ERP system and that can be used to extract data from any of the district’s other applications into the Business Intelligence data warehouse;
  • Secure, role-based access to information, ensuring that appropriate information is delivered to each group of recipients of BI output.

The Oracle/PeopleSoft eSettlements module functionality provides:
  • An electronic invoice presentation and payment (EIPP) solution, enabling the district to optimize the invoice payment process with electronic invoices and payments, matching, online approval and dispute resolution, and e-mail notifications.
  • The ability for the district to take advantage of early payment discounts offered by vendors.
  • A process to streamline the invoice payment process thereby improving vendor relations and reducing the number of disruptive inquiries concerning the status of invoice payments.

The Oracle/PeopleSoft eSupplier Connection module functionality provides:
  • A vendor self-service web application via secure web-based connectivity to provide the district’s vendors with the ability to view and modify their own business information (company address, phone, and contact data) at their convenience, to change or add office locations and change or add information about the individuals in their organization that handle various business relationships with the district.
  • A vendor with the ability to review its company’s purchase orders, receipt activity, invoices and payments on-line. A company may also download its company’s purchase orders.

The agreements provide that the selected firms conduct employment practices and offer opportunities to local, small, emerging business enterprise contractors, consultants, and suppliers in a manner that will maximize participation and treat all persons and firms on the basis of merit and experience without regard to race, creed, color, religion, sex, sexual orientation or preference, national origin, citizenship, age, height, weight, marital status, medical condition, physical or mental handicap, disability, or veteran status, within the limits imposed by law.

Recent concerns have arisen with respect to employment of EBE firms on district projects with respect to the agreements because of the insurance requirements contained therein. Specifically, both the prime firm and the contracted subconsultant must provide proof of insurance as follows: (a) Commercial General Liability: $5 million; (b) Automobile Liability: $2 million; (c) Professional Liability: $10 million. (d) Worker’s Compensation Insurance: Statutory; and (e) Employer’s Liability: $2 million. This insurance requirement has essentially restricted the EBE firms from subcontracting with the three primes because of the cost of the coverage.

To resolve the concern, district staff recommends amending the three agreements to reduce the rates for EBE firms. This reduction will have the dual effect of affording small firms better opportunities to work on district projects, and will align more generally with industry insurance practices within San Diego County. The proposed amendments will specify that in order to qualify as an emerging business enterprise, the firm must meet the requirements enumerated by California Government Code §14837, California Public Contract Code §10115 or California Military and Veterans Code §999. The insurance requirements for non-EBE firms will remain in place.

The district’s Insurance and Risk Management Department recommends that the insurance requirements incorporated in the agreements remain unchanged for the following reasons: (1) firms responding to the original solicitation were aware of the insurance requirements, and the proposed amendments will provide an exception to those conditions; (2) the district has always encouraged small business enterprises on all of its projects as a districtwide policy, and this policy has not changed; (3) insurance limits are based on the scope of work and liability exposure to the district, and not upon the size of the company doing the job; and (4) reduction of the required insurance limits will expose the district to higher risks on potential jobs.

The district’s Facilities Planning and Construction Department, however, believes the proposed amendments are in the best interests of the district because (1) increased small firm participation on district projects will result in an expanded work force and good will within the local community; (2) improved small firm participation will comport with the board’s recent request that small businesses be afforded opportunities to work on district projects as much as possible; and (3) the insurance rates will be more in alignment with industry practices in San Diego County. Specifically, the insurance requirements incorporated in the agreements are based upon multiple tasks and disciplines, and liability insurance coverage for individual professional services within San Diego County is generally less for most of the named categories.

The amendments will result in no additional cost to the district.

Recommendation:

Authorization of Seventh Amendment to Agreement with First Student Inc. for an additional expenditure not to exceed $175,000.00 for the term August, 15, 2008 through August 14, 2009. The total value of the Agreement is now $4,675,000.00 for the term August, 15, 2008 through August 14, 2009.

Budget Implications:

When unbudgeted programs are created or budgeted reductions from programmatic cuts are canceled, there is a negative impact on funding. Staff believes these requested funds will not be needed and will make every effort to avoid their use. Maximum impact on budget will be $175,000, if all funds authorized in this item are used.

Background Information:

On July 27, 2004, the board approved an Agreement with First Student, Inc. to provide home-to-school transportation services utilizing various size buses. On May 27, 2008, the board approved a Sixth Amendment for the term of August 15, 2008 through August 14, 2009, and having a total value of $4,500,000 for this term.

The current Sixth Amendment was budgeted with an anticipated reduction in ridership and associated programs during the 2008/2009 school year. However, unbudgeted service requirements such as new magnet programs with transportation, (e.g. Crown Point and Millennial Tech,) changes in previous bell time adjustments, and increased Special Education transportation have necessitated the usage of this contract to provide the new unbudgeted and required transportation services.

There is currently an $800,000 balance in the First Student contract for the remainder of the contract term (through August 14, 2009) once payments through March 31, 2009 have been made. This amount should be sufficient to cover all remaining costs based on a rigorous analysis of current and near-term needs conducted by the Transportation, Finance and Portolan teams. This analysis is coupled with a focused effort to reduce the reliance on First Student for the remainder of the school year. The Transportation Services Department will only utilize First Student buses through June 15, 2009, a 49-day service period. Only district buses will be used for year-round and extended-year (summer school) transportation after that date.

While the analysis and supporting operational decisions indicate that there are sufficient funds for the remainder of the term of the Sixth Amendment, staff believes that it is prudent to seek board approval for an incremental increase in the funds authorization of the current contract in the sum of $175,000 as a financial buffer. This amount will accommodate unforeseen “fact of life” service requirements resulting from unforeseen bus breakdowns, maintenance challenges, and emergent education program needs.

The 2009-10 proposed Transportation Services budget includes a reduction in the use of contracted busing services by approximately 40 percent. It is the intent of staff to continue the process begun several years ago to insource all transportation services. Use of contract buses has been reduced from 96 to 43 in three years. Staff envisions this reduction will continue and is developing a strategy in collaboration with The Portolan Group.

Impacts:

If no action is taken there may not be adequate authorization to cover the costs of utilizing First Student through June 15, 2009. The district does not have adequate buses or staff to make up this potential shortfall. Students would not be able to be transported, or an item for payment “after the fact” would have to be presented to the board for ratification. Staff realizes that the board does not favor the ratification procedure; in that spirit, this request is made.
