BACKGROUND: Through 2007, San Diego Unified offered a Summer School Program to a percentage of general education students in Grades 1-8 who performed below and significantly below grade level based on teacher judgment, instructional level, and assessment data; general education students in Grades 9-12 that needed to make up core courses to graduate.

 

The summer school budget was based on an estimate of the cost of operating the planned program and an estimate of the revenue that could be generated through student attendance.  Summer school funds came to the district as a reimbursement after summer school was completed. 

 

In 2008, the state changed its funding structure for supplemental hourly programs and no longer “reimbursed” districts for summer school based on hourly student attendance.  Districts began receiving Tier III supplemental hourly program funding based on their 2007-08 hourly earnings, less approximately 20 percent. 

 

Since 2009, when the state suspended all restrictions on Tier III funding and allowed districts to use the funds for any educational program or purpose, funds that had in previous years been earmarked for summer school have been absorbed into the district’s general operating fund to help balance the budget. 

 

In 2009 and 2010, summer school was offered to general education students in Grades 9-12 for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.  The general education program was paid for using a combination of Title I and American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) categorical funding. Transportation was no longer provided for general education students. 

 

In 2011, summer school was offered only to Grade 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy

 

In 2012, summer school was offered only to Grade 11 and 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.

 

In 2013, summer school was offered only to Grades 9-12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.

 

In 2014, summer school was offered only to Grades 9-12 general education students for credit recovery. The general education program was paid for out of Title 1 Funds.

 

Changes which led us to not offer summer school to students at Grades 1, 3, and 8:

 

In 2013-14, changes to the state assessment system with the passage of AB484 and the transition to the Common Core State Standards, created a situation where CST and district benchmark data required to make an informed promotion/retention determination were not available. Coupled with the limited funds available for a summer program, summer school at the retention Grades of 1, 3, and 8 was not offered during the 2014 summer school and will not be offered during the 2015 summer school. The office of Leadership and Learning will meet with secondary principals and head counselors to ensure they are identifying those students who are at risk and working to provide supports they will need to be successful in the high school setting.

It is proposed that a 2015 Summer School Program be offered to general education students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects). We estimate approximately 5,000 students will attend summer school. In 2014, we had approximately 4,101 students attend summer school. In 2013, we had approximately 4,259 students attend summer school. Transportation will not be provided.

 

A proposed, one-day bridging program would be also be offered for incoming Grade 9 students.  The program would be offered at every high school to help students transition from middle to senior high school.  Approximately 7,217 students entering Grade 9 in fall 2015 will be eligible.  Transportation will not be provided.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in support of the full implementation of AB 32, the California Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006, and California's Cap and Trade Program.

 

BACKGROUND: The State of California is the 12th largest emitter of greenhouse gases in the world.

 

In 2006, Assembly Bill 32 (AB 32) the Global Warming Solutions Act was adopted by the California Legislature which requires California to reduce its greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to 1990 levels by 2020 – a reduction of approximately 15 percent below expected under a “business as usual” scenario.

 

GHG emissions and climate change are primarily federal, state, and international issues that impact us all; positive change starts with accurate information and education.

 

Alongside AB 32, California’s Cap and Trade Program took effect. This program applies to large electric power plants and large industrial plants and enforces their obligation to reduce their GHG emissions.

 

The Cap and Trade funds could be invested to support local communities that are affected by pollution and poor air quality, and investments could be made for transit infrastructure and renewable energy.

 

AB 32 implementation is important to San Diego Unified because there are many direct and indirect benefits for the San Diego community where students live and go to school.

 

The San Diego Unified Sustainability Advisory Committee is exploring a district climate action plan. The concept of Cap and Trade aligns with the climate action plan goal of reducing GHG emissions.

BACKGROUND: With the passage of Proposition Z on November 6, 2012, San Diego Unified is required by state law to establish an Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee (ICOC) to monitor expenditures of voter approved funds. At the time, the district had already established an ICOC to monitor expenditures of Proposition S, a bond measure approved by voters on November 4, 2008.

 
To fulfill the district’s desire to concurrently implement projects under Propositions S and Z, on February 12, 2013, the board adopted Amended and Restated Bylaws (“Bylaws”) which establish a single Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee to monitor bond expenditures for both Propositions S and Z. In addition to consolidating oversight, the Bylaws also modified the member nomination process to provide greater representation of San Diego Unified citizens to ensure that the perspectives of parents, community members, business, and labor organizations were represented.
 
Under the terms of the bylaws, organizations were identified to provide nominations to the ICOC. If an organization failed to nominate a representative, the district may request nominees from similar organizations as determined by the board. Two organizations, the American Institute of Architects and the Construction Financial Management Association, were identified to nominate representatives.  Despite repeated requests from staff, and the organizations efforts to do so, neither organization has ever nominated a candidate for ICOC membership.  As a result of these ongoing vacancies, ICOC membership has never achieved the 13 members described in the Bylaws as full membership.
 
On December 2, 2014, the president of the ICOC brought to the board's attention the chronic vacancies due to the failure of these organizations to nominate candidates.  He suggested that the board find a solution to this, including the possibility of eliminating the two positions.  Accordingly, the board requested that staff bring a recommendation to resolve the vacancies.  Staff's recommendation is to reduce the number of ICOC members to eleven.  Nominees from these organizations are not required to fulfill compliance with the legal obligations for ICOC representation. Since ICOC membership has never reached full membership as described in the bylaws, staff believes that reduction of the number of ICOC members would in no way affect the committee's ability to fulfill its duties.

3. REVISED 2-9-15: Resolution in the Matter of Support to Remove the Annual Testing Requirement from the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) and Make Other Modifications as Congress Considers Reauthorization of ESEA (No Child Left Behind) (Beiser/Foster)

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in the matter of support to remove the annual testing requirement from the Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) and make other modifications as Congress considers reauthorization of ESEA (No Child Left Behind).

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA), currently known as the “No Child Left Behind Act of 2001," was due for reauthorization in 2007, and the U.S. Congress has not reached a bipartisan agreement that will ensure passage to streamline existing federal requirements and allow states and local educational agencies to develop and implement policies that will best support students.

 

There are several significant aspects of ESEA that should be amended during the Act’s reauthorization, including the elimination of sanctions and unintended consequences; granting states and local educational agencies greater local flexibility; the elimination of federally mandated, annual standardized testing; and maintaining provisions of ESEA that support its original intent of supporting students with the greatest needs.

 

The nation's future, social well-being and economic competitiveness relies on a high-quality public education system that prepares all students for college, careers, citizenship and lifelong learning.

 

The over-reliance on high-stakes standardized testing in state and federal accountability systems is undermining educational quality and equity in U.S. public schools by hampering educators' efforts to focus on the broad range of learning experiences that promote the innovation, creativity, problem solving, collaboration, communication, critical thinking, and deep subject-matter knowledge that will allow students to contribute and thrive in a democracy and an increasingly global society and economy.

 

It is widely recognized that high-stakes standardized testing is an inadequate and often unreliable measure of both student learning and educator effectiveness, and the over-reliance on standardized testing has caused considerable collateral damage in many schools, including narrowing the curriculum, teaching to the test, reducing student’s love of learning, pushing students out of school, driving teachers out of the profession, and undermining school climate.

 

The district Vision 2020 long-term strategic plan, Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood, supports and provides for quality teaching, access to broad and challenging curriculum for all students, closing the achievement gap with high expectations for all, and has implemented is committed to using multiple formative measures of success that go beyond standardized achievement tests.

 

The ESEA Discussion Draft freezes funding for reauthorized programs for Fiscal Year 2016 through Fiscal Year 2021, eroding the investment of federal funding for public education that would result in reductions in services to student subgroups that require additional investments and support systems, including low-income, English learners, and students of color.

 

The ESEA Discussion Draft repeals the long-standing Title I Maintenance of Effort (MOE) and the Title IX General Provisions MOE requirement, and without them, state and local education funding could be lowered by states with no consequences to the state’s ongoing receipt of federal aid.

 

The board calls on the U.S. Congress and the Obama Administration to overhaul the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, currently known as the "No Child Left Behind Act," eliminate the federally-mandated, annual testing requirement in each of Grades 3 through 9, and at least once in Grades 9 through 12; promote multiple forms of evidence of student learning and school quality in accountability; and not mandate any fixed role for the use of student test scores in evaluating educators.

 

The board calls on the U.S. Congress to remove the funding freeze for reauthorized ESEA programs that would severely cut services over the next six years, and urges the passage of a modernized version of ESEA that is fully supported by federal investments in Title I, which has been woefully underfunded for decades.

 

The board calls on the U.S. Congress to reinstate the current Maintenance of Effort requirements in ESEA to protect the integrity and benefits of federal ESEA programs.

 

The board supports a ESEA reauthorization bill that provides states and local educational agencies with additional flexibility to design their own accountability systems, including how states identify schools that are under-performing and determine appropriate interventions or technical assistance to support student growth and achievement, and support the use of multiple measures and growth models of academic achievement that reflect a well-rounded education necessary for success in the 21st century.

 

The board supports a ESEA reauthorization bill that provides school districts the flexibility and resources needed to respond to the educational challenges in local communities, and provides greater local flexibility in the use of ESEA funding for Titles I, II and III as states and school districts are in the best position to make spending decisions to facilitate local innovation and student achievement, without placing undue burdens on districts that would adversely impact effective governance.

 

The board supports an ESEA reauthorization bill that eliminates the inflexible sanctions and prescriptive actions that currently result in more schools being identified as Program Improvement if one or more student subgroup misses Annual Yearly Progress, as without the sanctions, districts would have more flexibility to use Title I funds to develop and/or implement programs and services that have evidence of improving student outcomes and advancing academic progress of all student subgroups.

BACKGROUND:  The Civic Center Act (Education Code section 38130 et seq) authorizes school district governing boards to grant the use of facilities or grounds as a civic center, for specified purposes, upon terms it deems proper.

 

In 2014, the California State Board of Education (SBE) adopted regulations implementing Senate Bill (SB) 1404, amendments to the Civic Center Act. The regulations establish a consistent method by which school districts will calculate fees for the use of school facilities or grounds by any outside entities. These calculations represent the maximum amount a school district is authorized to charge, which does not preclude a school district from electing to charge less.

 

Currently, Civic Center Act use is granted for San Diego Unified sites on a fee basis according to four different categories (free use, direct cost recovery, fair rental value, or commercial basis). Fees are updated annually and presented to the board for approval.

 

The proposed comprehensive fee study will:

  • Review and revise the methodology for Civic Center use fees in accordance with the new state regulations.
  • Evaluate Civic Center use fees in the context of Vision 2020 goals for schools serving as neighborhood centers. 
  • Streamline, where possible, the fee structure so it is easier to understand and accessible for community users.

  • Assure that the district is charging appropriate fees for new types of district-owned facilities, including recently built artificial turf fields and performing arts theaters.

 

The study findings will support the development of the 2015-16 Civic Center rate schedule that will be brought to the board for approval later this spring.

 

California Government Code section 53060 allows a school district to contract with and employ any persons for the furnishing of special services if such persons are specially trained, experienced, and competent to perform the special services required.  NBS Government Finance Group has met the requirements imposed by the California Government Code and is recommended for contract award.

 

District staff obtained proposals from two firms specializing in this type of study, and recommends NBS Government Finance Group based on the following factors: 1) experience and technical expertise preparing fee justification reports; 2) understanding of public agencies; and 3) price for services provided.
