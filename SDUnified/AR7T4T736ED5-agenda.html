RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on students with disabilities, which are included in LCAP Goals 1-5.

 

  • LCAP Goal 1: Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All
  • LCAP Goal 2: Access to Broad and Challenging Curriculum
  • LCAP Goal 3: Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning
  • LCAP Goal 4: Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture
  • LCAP Goal 5: Family and Community Engagement

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:

 

  • 1.1.a.6.The achievement of students with disabilities will be monitored to inform the development of effective academic, attendance and behavior support plans. Professional development focusing on individualized instruction opportunities to meet unique learning and social/emotional and behavioral needs will be provided to ensure improved student outcomes.
  • 1.2.a.2. Least restrictive environment data from the California Special Education Management Information System (CASEMIS) will be used to determine access and opportunity levels for students with disabilities to participate in educational services along with non-disabled peers.
  • 1.3.a.6. Graduation rates and dropout prevention will be emphasized for students with disabilities, as informed by State Performance Plan Indicators for Special Education.
  • 1.3.a.7. Increased training and support will be provided on the development of Transition Plans for students with disabilities, and linking the plans to student educational programs and schedules.
  • 1.3.a.8. Children and Youth in Transition (CYT) staff will be centralized to support multiple schools in monitoring student progress toward graduation and access to advanced pathways, with emphasis on students with disabilities, and African American and Latino students.
  • 1.3.a.10. Area superintendents, principals, the Office of Secondary Schools, secondary counselors, in collaboration with the Special Education Division and the Office of Language Acquisition (OLA) will partner to ensure that appropriate structures, supports and resources are in place for all students to access rigorous UC 'a-g' coursework, support and interventions, stay on track to graduate and are prepared for college, career and community. Well-articulated pathways for Grades 6-12 will be developed and designed to meet the continuum of needs for students to graduate ready for college, career and community.
  • 1.3.a.13. The Office of Language Acquisition (OLA) will continue to monitor student progress toward graduation and access to advanced pathways with an emphasis on English Learners (EL), students with disabilities, youth in transition and African-American and Latino students.  The GradNation Steering Committee will continue to monitor progress and follow up with identified students in the Grade 9 cohort to ensure a successful trajectory toward graduation.
  • 1.5.a.1. A general education core course credit recovery Summer School program will be offered in 2017, and will include increased opportunities for students with disabilities to participate in summer programs based on individual student transition plans, graduation plans, and needs identified in Individual Education Plans (IEP).
  • 1.7.a.3.f. Providing professional development and training in administrative support and leadership for special education; quality instruction at all grade levels; accommodations and modifications that lead to student success and access to instruction; high quality Individual Eduction Plan (IEP) development.
  • 1.7.a.7./2.6.1. Providing quality professional development opportunities for both certificated and classified staff to build capacity in providing high quality instructional opportunities for students with disabilities and administrator support training focused on understanding and leading high quality IEPs and translating those IEPs into high quality instruction for students with disabilities.  Central office resource teachers and administrators will continue their work in support of the coaching and learning cycles at school sites to provide targeted feedback on best practices for students with disabilities to teachers, administrators, and Professional Learning Communities (PLC).
  • 1.7.a.8. Ongoing collaboration with the Office of Secondary Schools to increase graduation rates for students with disabilities.
  • 2.1.4. The Special Education Division will continue to make improvements and refinements to the moderate/severe program to ensure meaningful graduation for students with disabilities.  
  • 2.6.2. The district will continue to review the most appropriate course options, particularly for secondary students with disabilities, to improve graduation rates.
  • 3.4.4. Professional development and capacity building will focus on utilizing the priority standards, proficiency scales, and best practices in planning and delivering instruction for English Learners (EL), African American, Latino, and students with disabilities.
  • 3.5.2. Common Core Cluster Support Teachers (CCCSTs), Special Education project resource teachers, and paraeducators will collaborate and work alongside principals, teachers, and school staff to build capacity around priority standards, proficiency scales and student-centered practices, and state Common Core, Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS), and other content areas.
  • 3.5.3. Special Education central office resource teachers and administrators will continue to work with area superintendents on learning cycles, with emphasis on students with disabilities.
  • 3.5.5. The Office of Language Acquisition (OLA), in collaboration with the Special Education Division, will provide supports and training that includes utilizing the priority standards and proficiency scales for setting learning and language targets for students for special education teachers, paraeducators, and support staff that teach students with special needs, including students with disabilities, English Learners (EL) and Gifted and Talented (GATE) students throughout the school year.
  • 3.5.6. The Special Education Division will continue its emphasis on providing quality professional development opportunities for both certificated and classified staff to build their capacity for providing high quality instructional opportunities for students with disabilities.  Paraprofessional training will continue to focus on building capacity both both academic and behavioral support.
  • 4.1.a.3. The district will take immediate focused actions to reduce the disproportionality of disciplinary actions experienced by students with disabilities.  Suspensions for students with disabilities will not exceed 7.8 per 100 students with Individual Education Plans (IEP). Supports to meet this goal include: delivering intensive professional development to parents and staff focused on the policies and practices of student discipline; examining the data collection process associated with suspensions and expulsions; documenting, addressing and reducing suspensions; and eliminating informal early dismissal practices. Sites should develop positive behavioral systems.  Provide training to all staff in PBIS. Establish procedures to require the review of behavior supports/behavior plans at the first suspension and on an ongoing basis.  Explicitly teach school-wide behavior expectations and provide differentiated modalities for student access and participation in restorative practices/PBIS.  Ensure that behavior is carefully considered at each IEP. Facilitate student voice in the behavior planning process (restorative practices, reinforcement inventory, IEP team discussion as appropriate, etc.). Help parents develop an understanding of their rights and responsibilities relating to IEPs nd manifestation determination.  Share with parents the policies and practices of student discipline on a given campus and information about community-based resources and programs.
  • 4.1.a.6. The Nursing and Wellness and Special Education staffs will participate in restorative practices and professional development.
  • 5.1.a.1. The Family and Community Engagement (FACE) Team will work oin conjunction with the area superintendents and the Special Education Division to facilitate and provide tools, resources, and training needed to establish and implement effective school and district level committees, including Site Governance Teams (SGT) and the Community Advisory Committee (CAC) for Special Education.

 

BACKGROUND: At regular, public board meetings Superintendent Marten provides reports that include the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report, cluster and advisory groups, employee groups, and members of the public have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation.  The complete LCAP document is available on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2018-19 Single Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) and Categorical Budget Summaries for 176 schools.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

 

BACKGROUND: As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle.

 

The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.

 

Additionally, the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs.

 

Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment. The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2018-19 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 176 schools.

 

A copy of each 2018-19 SPSA, which includes the budget summaries and required signed Assurances page is available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, special education status, expansion of grade levels, or other significant revision.

Staff recommends the board grant the requested revisions to the charter of The Learning Choice Academy to remove the out of district sites located in La Mesa and Chula Vista from the charter, effective July 1, 2018.

 

The Learning Choice Academy (“LCA”) began operating in 2004, and currently serves Grades K-12 at the following four sites:

  • 9950 Scripps Lake Drive, San Diego, 92131 (in district)

  • 27919 Jefferson Avenue, Temecula, 92590 (adjacent county)

  • 4215 Spring Street, La Mesa, 91941 (out of district)

  • 881 Kuhn Drive, Chula Vista, 91914 (out of district)

 

On January 18, 2017, the California Supreme Court declined to review a decision issued on October 17, 2016, by the Third District Court of Appeals in the Anderson Union High School District v. Shasta Secondary Home School (“Anderson”) case. The appellate ruling held that current statutes do not permit a nonclassroom-based charter school to locate resource centers outside of the geographic boundaries of the authorizing school district but within the same county.

 

On March 11, 2014, prior to the Anderson ruling, the district renewed LCA’s charter petition that included the four sites identified above. In the Affirmations and Assurances section of the current charter petition, LCA indicated that the charter school “shall comply with any jurisdictional limitations to locations of its facilities. [Ref. Education Code Sections 47605 and 47605.1]”

 

In response to the Anderson ruling, on March 9, 2017, the State, in an effort to provide a transition period for affected charter schools, provided a waiver term through June 30, 2018, for schools to get their sites compliant. We did not submit a waiver on behalf of LCA, but they were afforded this time period to get their sites compliant.

 

On July 10, 2015, the district sent a letter to LCA notifying it of the need to transition all of its sites located outside of the district’s boundaries into compliance with the Charter Schools Act. The Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) is an essential element of California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF). It is a three-year, district-level plan that is updated annually. The 2018-19 LCAP is the second year of the three-year plan. BACKGROUND: In accordance with California Education Code Sections 46600 to 46608 and Section 48204, school districts may establish Interdistrict Attendance Agreements which provide for an exchange of students from one district to another district. An Interdistrict Attendance Agreement allows students to enroll in school districts other than their resident district when there are childcare, employment, residential, academic program or other factors which affect a student's enrollment at the school.

 

On April 29, 2014, the board approved 49 five-year Interdistrict Attendance Agreements with several School Districts and Lakeside Union School District was part of the approved participating districts.

 

This recommendation approves a four-year (2019-2023) Interdistrict Attendance Agreement with Lakeside Union School District.
