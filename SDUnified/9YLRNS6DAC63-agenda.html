1. Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood - Report on Closing the Achievement Gap (LCAP Goal 1)
Meeting
Apr 12, 2016 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board on the implementation of Vision 2020 Quality Indicator: Closing the Achievement Gap (LCAP Goal 1).

 

Vision 2020, Quality Indicator Destination: Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language, or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate the significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring that every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gaps goals are implemented.  All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly or indirectly, to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.  

 

LCAP GOAL 1:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.  

 

Actions and Services:

  • 1.1.a. - Schools use formative assessments to develop learning plans, set goals with students, monitor progress, and evaluate the effectiveness of the plan at the end of the year.
  • 1.2.a. - Develop and implement a well-articulated pathway from pre-school through Grade 12, including primary language instruction (e.g., biliteracy and dual language instructional programs).
  • 1.2.c. - Developmental Reading Assessment (DRA) will be administered to all TK-3 students to monitor the progress of reading development and provide differentiated support as needed.
  • 1.2.d. - TK-3 students will receive small group instruction, in the language(s) of instruction, to ensure that students are proficient in reading by Grade 3.
  • 1.2.e. - Provide targeted intervention and support for at-risk learners, including support in the language(s) of instruction and/or primary language.
  • 1.2.f. - Provide professional development for teachers directed at student-centered teaching, intervention, and support.

BACKGROUND:  Each month, Superintendent Marten provides a report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report each month, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report, provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation and suggested next steps.  The complete LCAP document is available at www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

1. Resolution to Support the Children's Education and Health Care Protection Act (CEHCP) (Beiser/Barrera)
Meeting
Apr 12, 2016 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in support of the Children's Education and Health Care Protection Act (CEHCP). The board supports the passage of CEHCP because it provides needed revenue to public schools and will specifically benefit this district for the reasons described below.

 

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education is committed to making policy and financial decisions that enable the school district to provide quality educational programs and services to the school-age children of the community.

 

During the recent recession, the state cut more than $56 billion from education, healthcare, and other critical services; and public schools statewide experienced unprecedented funding reductions and apportionment deferrals.

 

California public schools rank 42nd out of 50 states in per-pupil spending; California public schools are the most crowded in the nation; and California provides fewer guidance counselors and librarians per student than any other state in the nation1; and many of California’s schoolchildren come from low-income families that lack access to the health care services needed to keep those children in school and learning.

 

Passage of Proposition 30 in 2012, helped increase state revenues, providing a significant boost to California’s K-12 school spending and sending funds directly to school districts through the Education Protection Account; and the revenues from Proposition 30 will decline after 2016, and will disappear completely after 2018, the last year its provisions will be in effect.

 

The Children’s Education and Health Care Protection Act (CEHCP) would extend Proposition 30’s temporary income tax rates for an additional 12 years, to continue funding the Education Protection Account and boost spending on health care for low-income families.

 

Unless Californians extend Propositions 30’s temporary tax increases, the state’s schools most likely will receive relatively fewer dollars in the years ahead, raising the prospect that California’s school spending will fall even further behind the nation.

 

If California’s voters approve the CEHCP, it could mean approximately one-half of the estimated $8 billion to $11 billion in revenue each year to California public schools.

 

The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports the Children’s Education and Health Care Protection Act and urges the Legislature to work with the public education community to identify stable, long-term, adequate funding solutions for public schools.

3. Board Discussion on Proposed Revisions to District Policy and Procedures to Allow Fireworks on School District Property (Evans/Beiser)
Meeting

Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Board members Evans and Beiser will discuss and ask the board to take action to direct the superintendent to revise district policy and procedures to allow for fireworks on school district property.  

 

BACKGROUND: Vision 2020 calls for schools to be closely aligned with their neighborhoods and to meet the needs of that community.  One example of alignment with the community and meeting the needs of the local community is Fourth of July celebrations.

 

For many years, the Mira Mesa and Scripps Ranch communities have enjoyed Fourth of July fireworks at the Mira Mesa High School stadium.  Historically, the fireworks have been launched from the upper field at Mira Mesa High School.  Current district administrative procedures prohibit fireworks on school grounds.  Staff recently became aware that the Mira Mesa upper field is not a joint use field, as previously believed, and therefore, informed the community that July 2016, would be the last year that fireworks would be permitted. 

 

Based on input from many stakeholders and the community advising that an alternative location was not available, staff has recently begun drafting proposed revisions to the relevant district policy and procedures to allow the fireworks event and similar events in the future.  

 

This board agenda item calls for the staff to bring back to the board a revised policy that would allow these community celebrations with fireworks to continue every year.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  Point Loma Nazarene University will provide a Human Trafficking (HT) Peer-to-Peer Education Program to raise human trafficking awareness, learn recruitment tactics and understand the impact of human trafficking in public high schools in an effort to make high schools safer for students.  A pilot of the HT Peer-to-Peer program will be implemented at San Diego High School and once the program has been refined, it will be taken to other high schools that are yet to be identified.  The HT program will educate participants (students, staff, parents, community members, etc) on the growing epidemic of human trafficking and its effect on today’s youth, schools and neighborhood communities. 

 

The underground commercial sex economy (UCSE) represents an estimated $810 million in annual revenue.  Between 8,830 to 11,773 sex trafficking victims/survivors are estimated per year in San Diego County.  The average age of entry into the commercial sexual exploitation of children (CSEC) is 15 years of age.  Recruitment into CSEC is happening at San Diego County high schools.  Sex trafficking is San Diego's second largest underground economy after drug trafficking. 

 

The San Diego Unified School District has taken a proactive approach to begin educating staff about the ever-growing concerns and impact of recruitment on district schools.  Human trafficking trainings and community film screenings have provided district staff the opportunity to learn detailed information, risk factors and recruitment strategies used by sex facilitators in an effort to safeguard district students.

BACKGROUND:  On December 1, 2015, the board authorized the Chief of Facilities Planning and Construction to carry out turf field and parking lot improvements at Pacific Beach Middle School in compliance with Proposition S and Proposition Z (Proposed Project). Accordingly, staff retained Westberg + White, Inc. from the district’s list of prequalified architectural firms and developed a site plan and construction documents that have been reviewed and approved by the Division of the State Architect. 

 

The Proposed Project encompasses a four-acre area on Pacific Beach Middle School, including a decomposed granite field and an asphalt parking lot. The Proposed Project would result in the redevelopment of the site with a regulation-size soccer field and a parking lot with a student loading area. The field would be synthetic turf and have an all-weather track around its perimeter. The parking lot would include 136 stalls, including 36 new parking stalls. The Proposed Project would also include new landscaping, drainage, and security improvements, including parking lot and pathway lighting. All improvements would comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111 (b), the district must competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more.  The district advertised the Proposed Project in accordance with the Public Contract Code and bids were opened on March 4, 2016.  The district received bids from Byrom-Davey, Inc., Straight Line General Contractors, Inc., and USS Cal Builders, Inc.  USS Cal Builders, Inc. is the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.  Additionally, consistent with the May 10, 2011, board-approved resolution in support of service-disabled, veterans-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE), USS Cal Builders, Inc. submitted a 3.1 percent DVBE participation in its bid.  The construction contract is effective as of April 15, 2016 and continues until the Proposed Project is complete. 

 

Environmental Review

 

The Proposed Project is subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code section 21000 et seq. ICF International is on the district’s list of prequalified environmental consulting firms and was retained to prepare environmental documentation for the Proposed Project. Based on the findings of an Initial Study, staff determined that the Proposed Project is exempt from further environmental analysis under three categorical exemption classes as set forth below and as further described in the attached Notice of Exemption (NOE) and Initial Study:

 

15301. Existing Facilities (Class 1)

 

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency‘s determination. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. 

 

The Proposed Project would improve the existing field and parking facilities at Pacific Beach Middle School and would continue to serve the school’s current uses and programs. While the parking lot would be expanded by 36 stalls, operation of the school would not increase. The parking stalls would provide additional on-site spaces for school staff and visitors. The Proposed Project does not include field lighting; similar to the existing conditions, all field uses would discontinue at sunset. 

 

15304. Minor Alterations to Land (Class 4)

 

Class 4 consists of minor public or private alterations in the condition of land, water, and/or vegetation which do not involve removal of healthy, mature, scenic trees except for forestry or agricultural purposes, including grading on land with a slope of less than 10 percent and minor trenching and backfilling where the surface is restored.

 

Construction of the Proposed Project would require the removal of existing improvements and debris, including an asphalt parking area, decomposed gravel, and ornamental vegetation. No mature, scenic trees exist on the project site. Project implementation would require minor grading and trenching of the relatively flat site.  

 

15314. Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14)

 

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption.

 

The Proposed Project would not add new classrooms or other facilities on the existing campus and would therefore not exceed the existing capacity of that school site.

 

Categorical exemptions may be not be relied upon if the application of the exemption triggers any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2. Based on the Initial Study, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemptions. Approval of this item authorizes and directs staff to file, with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, a Notice of Exemption in substantial conformance with the attached Notice of Exemption and awards the construction contract to USS Cal Builders.
