FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: In December 2021, the American Heart Association announced its 2021 Workplace Health Achievement Index. The San Diego Unified School District achieved national Gold Level recognition for taking significant steps to build a culture of health and well-being for its workforce. The American Heart Association defines best practices for employers to use to build a culture of health and well-being for their employees in the workplace. The American Heart Association’s Workplace Health Achievement Index measures the extent to which the company has implemented those workplace health best practices.  Organizations recognized at the Gold level have achieved an Index score of 128 - 151 out of a maximum 217 points.

 

San Diego Unified is one of more than 450 organizations nationwide that completed the 2021 Index assessment, evaluating the time period of July 31, 2020 to June 30, 2021. Of the organizations that completed the Index assessment, nearly all earned gold (43%), silver (31%) or bronze (19%) status. 

 

The American Heart Association, a global force for longer, healthier lives for all, created the Index with its CEO Roundtable, a leadership collaborative of more than 45 members from some of America’s largest companies. The Index is a web-based scorecard that evaluates the overall quality and comprehensiveness of a company's workplace health program through a combination of best practices and a snapshot of a company's aggregate employee heart health.   Additional information is available at www.heart.org/WHSrecognitions.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2021-22 School Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) and Categorical Budget Summaries for 160 schools.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Each school's School Site Council (SSC) worked with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education received various training regarding specific plan requirements. Specifically, the DAC received information and provided feedback on the timeline and components of developing the SPSA at their October 20, 2021, meeting.   

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the State holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. As part of the annual planning cycle, the SPSAs must be reviewed and updated.  SPSAs may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act (Public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.

 

Additionally, staff in the Strategic Planning for Student Achievement Department provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, gain approval by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the Strategic Planning for Student Achievement Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment. The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2021-22 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 160 schools.

 

Area superintendents review site plans during visits to schools and discuss progress with principals as determined by the measurable outcomes. In addition, Strategic Planning for Student Achievement Department resource teachers monitor progress made, and work with area superintendents and principals to evaluate the plans. Electronic copies of each 2021-22 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required, signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item; hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) on Compensatory Education discussed the Title I Ranking process and allocations at the December 15, 2021, and January 19, 2022, meetings. 

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). Title I, which is one of several programs under ESSA, provides funding to schools to be used to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of students who qualify for Title I funding who reside within each district’s boundary. The federal census is used to identify these children, ages 5 through 17, who are living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The proposal of the 2022-23 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2021-22 LCFF survey percentage of 40 percent or higher; also including one school extended by wavier at 33%. The per student amount was $558.75 per eligible student and we continue to use the formula to decrease the per student amount by $5.50 for every 1 percent drop in percentage count. This report represents a Title I base allocation to sites of $18.6 million.

 

The total 2022-23 Title I entitlement is approximately $38,000,000.00.

3. Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing February 2022, as National Black History Month

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of recognizing February 2022 as National Black History Month and recognize the tremendous contributions made by Black Americans to the development of our culture, communities, and nation; and our ongoing commitment to fostering an anti-racist, anti-biased and restorative district for all students. Black lives matter.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Black Americans helped develop our nation in countless ways, some recognized, some unrecognized and unrecorded. The origin of Black History Month began in 1915, half a century after the Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery in the United States, and grew out of “Negro History Week,” the creation of noted historian Carter G. Woodson and other prominent Black Americans who established a month-long celebration to reflect on the history, teachings, contributions, and achievements of Black Americans. In 1976, Black History Month was officially established as a month-long celebration to reflect on the history, teachings, contributions and achievements of Black Americans and is a national tradition established in response to the inadequate and often biased depiction of Black history and communities in history books and schools’ curricula. Since the beginning of American history, people of Black descent have fought and died for the advancement of the essence and substance of American democracy; an ideal from which they have frequently been excluded. Black history reflects an unbreakable spirit of perseverance and pride in its struggles and triumphs in the face of racism, bias, prejudice, and oppression to equally share in the opportunities of a nation founded upon the principles of freedom and liberty for all people. The history and contributions of Black citizens have consistently been overlooked, misinterpreted, and undervalued in the curriculum of public education institutions prior to the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and still today. We recognize that “matter” is the minimum for Black students—that state education data reflects the egregious disparities in experience, treatment, opportunity, access, visibility, and academic success. We are committed to addressing these disparities through our Black Youth Call to Action, focusing on staff diversity, ethnic studies, grading practices, and student discipline and will be held accountable to progress by the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee on Racial Justice and Equity. Black History Month will offer excellent opportunities to focus the energy, attention, commitment, and action of the entire community on closing the health, experience, opportunity, and achievement gaps for Black students and families with a critical examination by our staff of our progress toward and execution of San Diego Unified School District’s Black Youth Call to Action. Black History Month is a vehicle to promote anti-biased, anti-racist, restorative, and culturally sustaining practices on every school campus and district office. Black history curriculum and materials are available to all San Diego Unified educators through our Ethnic Studies curriculum and Youth Advocacy programs such as Black Student Unions. We are committed to Ethnic Studies for ALL students and the ongoing professional learning opportunities for all district staff to engage in critical self-awareness and anti-biased/anti-racist leadership; and we are encouraging educators, students and families to learn about the heritage and achievements of Black Americans. Each year, in honor of Black History Month, San Diego Unified School District’s central office will fly the Black Lives Matter flag. The flag signifies our commitment to creating a just, equitable, and empathetic world. It is a symbol of inclusion, especially for our students of color. The flag is a reminder that history, including Black history, is not just something of the past. History is constantly being made, and this flag and this movement are a part of it. Together, we demonstrate our commitment to a quality educational experience that is equally accessible to all students, free from racist and biased practices. Not only during the month of February, but throughout the year, it is important that we, as a district, seek to understand how to thoughtfully and holistically address Black American disparities in experience, quality of educational and social interactions, and outcomes as well as each of our roles in dismantling racist, biased, and oppressive systems and practices in order to move towards greater equity in our educational system and the greater community.

BACKGROUND: When San Diego High School became a small school complex in 2004, there were initially six small schools supported by the Bill Gates Foundation. In 2009, Gates's funding expired. Between the years of 2011-2013, the six schools were closed and reorganized into three small schools. In 2019, the need to provide opportunities for equity and access for all students across the complex was identified, including access to programs such as the International Baccalaureate, Pathways, including California Partnership Academies, and California Career and Technical Education (i.e., Engineering, Medical, Journalism, Finance, Culinary, Computer Science). School demographic data illustrate that the majority of our students with disabilities and English learners were enrolled in only one of the small schools. Data also indicated that opportunities for advanced coursework existed primarily at one of the other small schools. This reorganization will provide more opportunities and equitable access for all students to programs and pathways while the site will be able to maximize resources.
