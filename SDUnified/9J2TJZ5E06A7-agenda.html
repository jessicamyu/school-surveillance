BACKGROUND: General Information--Program Improvement (PI) Status: Under the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001, a Title I school that, for two consecutive years, does not meet or exceed a series of annual academic performance goals known as Adequate Yearly Progress (AYP) is designated Program Improvement. Such schools must provide specified types of services and/or interventions during each year that they are identified as PI. A school exits PI if it makes AYP for two consecutive years.

 

District Schools in PI Year 4, 2013–14--School Restructuring: A school that enters its fourth consecutive year in PI status is required to develop a plan to restructure that will be implemented the following year, in this case in 2014–15. The restructuring plan must describe in detail the changes in governance, instructional practice, and staffing that will be required to improve students’ academic achievement and enable the school to meet its AYP goals. District schools submitting a PI Year 4 restructuring plan are: Euclid, Fay, Hamilton, Johnson, Lafayette, Spreckels, and Webster elementary schools; Bethune K-8 School; Challenger and Dana middle schools; Point Loma and San Diego-SciTech high schools. BACKGROUND: At the request of the board, the Superintendent established an ad hoc i21Now Committee to develop a high-level framework of vision, opportunities, and recommendations that would build on the strengths of the nationally recognized i21 program and support Vision 2020, long-term plan for today's learner and tomorrow's worker and global citizen.  The i21Now initiative represents the next phase of funding under Propositions S and Z, which support repairs, renovations, classroom technologies, safety and security upgrades, and more.  The committee was led by Chair Matt Spathas.

 

To facilitate a robust discussion, the task force was made up of over 80 stakeholders drawn from business, education, community, government, and San Diego Unified, including teachers, principals, students, and central office staff (see attached committee roster).  Six subcommittees were assigned to formulate ideas and develop an unconstrained vision of next generation learning environments in the areas of Curriculum and Professional Development, Facilities, Digital Policy and Tools, Infrastructure, Finance and Broadband.  Each subcommittee was co-chaired by an San Diego Unified staff member and a representative from the community, business, government, or education.  Over a 90-day period, the full task force met three times and each subcommittee met four times, with over 1,000 hours contributed by the dedicated and passionate committee members.
 

The i21 Now Committee collaborated on a wide range of new ideas for the next phase of i21 and made high-level policy recommendations for further consideration by the Board of Education.  The recommendations support an efficient, cost-effective program based on the core principles of equity, access, and student achievement, with the goal of creating next generation learning environments that engage, empower, and educate students and prepare them for the 21st century global community. These recommendations include:

 

Curriculum and Professional Development

  • Create learner-centered environments that support collaboration and critical thinking.
  • Incorporate reputable online resources and real-time data to differentiate instruction and engage students with real-world content.
  • Implement competency-based learning and problem-solving-based assessment, aligned with Common Core standards.
  • Foster digital literacy and parental/community involvement.
  • Build scalable leader and teacher professional development models.

Facilities

  • Create safe, open, and welcoming learning spaces that respect neighborhood culture, with group tables, quiet corners, and large gathering spaces that facilitate different instructional modes, private learning, informal gatherings, and community experiences.
  • Develop new design guidelines for both new school buildings and the redesign of existing schools.
  • Incorporate sustainable strategies that create lifelong sustainable advocates.
  • Support teacher collaboration and professional development.
  • Build a student-centered environment that ensures student ownership and choice, while fostering collaboration and interaction among both students and teachers.

Digital Tools and Policy

  • Provide equity of access to all students with individual devices and 24/7 connectivity.
  • Standardize on a limited number of device form factors and platforms to reduce costs.
  • Evaluate a blended model of district-supplied and student-owned devices.
  • Re-evaluate all district policies regarding privacy, new materials, AUP (Acceptable Use Policy), and compliance with CIPA (Children’s Internet Protection Act).

Infrastructure

  • Create a robust, flexible, sustainable infrastructure to enable anytime/anyplace learning and extend the learning environment from school to home and beyond.
  • Support upgraded wireless, wired, and 1:1 environments, plus building systems and VOIP (Voice over Internet Protocol), while ensuring accessibility with digital dashboards and portals.
  • Provide access to both cloud-based and locally available content and applications, and ensure physical and virtual security.

Finance

  • Provide policy makers and administrators with robust indicators of every transformative project’s key implementation factors (KIF) aligned to district goals.
  • Pursue new funding sources and repurposing current funding by moving expenditures away from textbooks and structured classrooms toward virtual learning, digital content and personalized learning.
  • Inform policy with data-driven decision making and informational analytics, supported by ongoing independent review of prioritization and funding metrics.

Broadband

  • Provide students with mobile access to broadband connectivity anytime/anyplace, while leveraging resources and partnerships to drive down costs.
  • Ensure sustainable funding to provide access at home and beyond for all students.
  • Create Broadband Task Force (district, business, and government) to explore equitable access for all.

The district requires waste and recyclables collection on an ongoing basis.  These waste and recycling services are necessary to maintain clean and healthy facilities, while providing recycling and other waste diversion services that will save the district money on disposal costs and contribute to the state’s 75 percent waste reduction goal by 2020.

In the fall of 2013, at the direction of the board, the Superintendent established the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee to discuss utility and operational costs and to solicit innovative ideas that could contain or reduce costs as well as reduce the district’s environmental footprint.  As part of this effort, the committee’s November 2013 meeting was dedicated to waste and recycling management.  Subsequently, over the next few months the committee provided substantial input and guidance on the development of the waste and recycling RFP released in early 2014.

 

On February 26, 2014, the district received three proposals in response to the district's RFP from the following vendors: EDCO Disposal Corporation, Republic Services, and Waste Management.  A five-member Evaluation Committee was formed and charged with finding the best proposed Waste and Recyclables Collection and Management Services solution(s) for the district and recommend the vendor with the best overall proposal.  The Committee included representation from various positions/roles across the district including central office staff, Physical Plant Operations management, and waste and recycling technical staff.  The committee also included one member from the City of San Diego Environmental Services Department.

 

All proposers were required to submit a Baseline Service Fixed Price.  Republic Services was the only proposer to submit an alternate pricing option, which was allowable under this RFP.  Their option, or Service Improvement Plan, was based on using six cubic yard trash and recyclables collection dumpsters at certain sites, instead of the standard three cubic yard dumpsters. This is a valid consideration for reducing truck traffic to school sites and decreasing costs; however, there is no guarantee that the number of larger dumpsters proposed could actually be located at the sites listed in the alternate pricing proposal without further review and site approval.  In order to give consideration to the effort put into this option, Strategic Sourcing and Contracts completed a cost analysis using pricing provided by the other two vendors for three and six cubic yard dumpsters at the level proposed by Republic Services.  In this scenario, Waste Management had the lower pricing.

 

The committee's evaluation of the written proposals determined that all three proposers should be further evaluated utilizing an oral interview.  The interviews consisted of a series of questions that were developed in order to evaluate, in further detail, the proposer’s experience, implementation plan, customer service approach, education and outreach commitment, waste reduction plans, reporting, website development, incentives, and pricing.  The proposer with the highest overall scoring of both the written proposals and the oral interviews was EDCO Disposal Corporation. 

 

All of the proposers had a history of performing the value-added services described above.  Each proposer was evaluated as part of the evaluation committee's review.

 

EDCO Disposal Corporation will be providing the district with containers to collect waste and recyclable materials, and provide haul-away service/recycling service as applicable.  This new contract provides the district with an opportunity to create a partnership with a waste and recycling company, rather than a strict service agreement.  In a side-by-side comparison of costs from our current contract pricing there is an annual savings of just over $30,000.

 

In addition, this new contract provides significant value-added services that will assist the district in reducing overall costs, educating staff and students, and providing reporting tools to facilitate program planning.  Under the new contract, EDCO will:

 

1) Provide $5,000 annually as an incentive to reward the best recycling schools in the district (1st, 2nd, 3rd, and honorable mentions).

 

2) Create a dedicated web-page (on their existing company website) for district schools and sites to access information on recycling competitions, school waste and recycling rates (by site), and other information we deem important/appropriate.  Sites would have a specific code to access the website.

 

3) Create a dedicated Environmental Times newsletter specific to our schools to be distributed twice per year (content to be approved by the district).

 

4)  Conduct waste and recyclable composition studies to provide information and  detailed breakdowns of the materials in each stream, as well as waste quantities (tonnage) to determine generation and diversion rates.

 

5)  Conduct up to six waste composition analysis annually for specific 40-yard roll-off container loads to identify material that can be diverted from disposal and assist in educating the sites on diversion alternatives/strategies.

 

6)  Assist student groups in conducting on-site waste audits in order to provide visual feedback on the types and amounts of waste disposed by students at their school and to tailor specific diversion programs for the school.

 

7)  Provide a quarterly report that would overview the district’s diversion efforts, including program participation and compliance, quantity estimates for all waste streams, and projected results looking forward.

 

8)  Exchange all existing trash and recycling bins with new bins prior to the start of the Agreement.

 

9)  Visit all sites within the first three months of the contract to assess service needs and levels (right-size service) and identity potential to increase recyclables diversion by site.

 

10) Adjust service levels, based on right-sizing evaluations with the goal to decrease collection of non-recyclables by 15 percent within the first three months of the contract. On an annual basis this is equal to a $138,000 cost savings.

 

11) Instruct drivers to conduct bin-fullness surveys (visual survey to determine ¼, ½, ¾, or full status of each bin) when servicing district sites to continually provide feedback on appropriate service levels or trends.

 

12) Provide a detailed follow-up plan for each site to increase diversion levels.

 

13) Prepare an initial education plan and meet with the district to review the plan shortly after contract award.

 

14) Actively seek qualified environmental education organizations to assist and reach out to student clubs and organization.
