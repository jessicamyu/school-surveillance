RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution Recognizing February 11 - 15, 2019 as Gun Violence Prevention Week.

 

BACKGROUND: On February 14, 2018, a gunman opened fire at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School in Parkland, Florida, killing 17 students and staff members and injuring 17 others, making it the deadliest shooting at a high school in US history. This tragedy has been a catalyst for change in the national conversation on gun violence prevention, and students across the country have used their voices and their social media skills to galvanize the youth of our country to speak out against gun violence. Firearm deaths are now the third leading cause of death among children, and over 80% of teen suicides are completed using a firearm from the home. Family fire is a shooting involving an improperly-stored or misused firearm found in the home, including intentional and unintentional shootings and suicide. Students, parents and educators recognize the importance of practicing responsible gun ownership and safety and if guns are in homes, safety begins with keeping all guns and ammunition secured where children cannot access them. California law holds a person criminally liable for keeping a loaded firearm where he or she knows or reasonably should know a child is likely to gain access to it. The one year anniversary of the Parkland, Florida tragedy falls during the week of February 11 - 15, 2019. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes the second week of February 2019 as Gun Violence Prevention Week, and calls upon schools throughout the district to promote the End Family Fire campaign including sending to all parents the Asking Saves Kids (ASK) communication to determine if there is an unlocked gun where their child visits or plays, participating in gun safety awareness such as through creating posters, and participating in a San Diego town hall with teens from around the country to discuss gun violence prevention.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution in Support of Establishing a College-Going Culture and Promoting Student Success through the California College Promise.

 

BACKGROUND: The California College Promise, established by Chapter 735, Statues of 2017 (Assembly Bill 19), represented a critical step toward making college more affordable and accessible. Assembly Bill 19 authorizes funding to be used by California Community Colleges to waive fees for first-time, full-time students during their first academic year, while creating a framework for California Community Colleges to maximize students’ access to available financial aid, adopt evidence-based student success strategies, and implement partnerships with school district to increase student awareness regarding college opportunities. Assembly Bill 2, as introduced on December 3, 2018, would authorize a California Community College to use California College Promise funding to waive fees for two academic years. The Governor’s preliminary 2019-20 budget proposal extends the California College Promise to waive enrollment fees for first-time, full-time students for a second academic year. Based on 2017-18 data, approximately 28,000 first-time, full-time students are projected to be eligible for a second year of free tuition. An important component of the California College Promise is to provide students information about financial aid and services available to support their success. To assist community colleges with these efforts, the proposal includes funding for the Chancellor’s Office to expand outreach around the College Promise. This initiative will focus on developing resources that can be used to promote student access and success, using technology to better reach students as they are making critical decisions and providing professional development to community college practitioners. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports the Governor’s efforts to make Community College again free in the State of California.

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and approve the San Diego Unified School District’s initial proposal to negotiate with all bargaining units in accordance with section 3457 of the California Government Code and section 1031 of the bylaws of the Board of Education.


The provisions of California Education Code 3457 require that public school employers and the exclusive representatives present proposals which relate to matters within the scope of the representation at a public meeting of the public school employer prior to commencing negotiations.


This constitutes the initial proposal of the district for the purposes of negotiating a reduction in work year. The district hereby proposes to negotiate a reduction in work year which is necessary for the district to maintain fiscal responsibility while fulfilling its obligation and commitment to serving students.


The district looks forward to meaningful, objective, and reality-based collaborative negotiations with each of the bargaining units.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Fiscal impact is not known at this time. The full monetary impact will be known at the conclusion of bargaining when agreements are reached with each of the bargaining units. The reduced work year budget savings that the district relied on over the last two fiscal years has been equal to approximately $14.4 million per fiscal year and sunsets on June 30, 2019. In order to balance the budget, the district needs to negotiate a budget savings and is seeking an extension of the reduced work year in lieu of the elimination of services which will directly impact student programs.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Unknown until the completion of bargaining.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Due to the district's financial shortfall and the need to balance the 2019-20 budget, the district has identified as a solution a reduction in work year for all district employees.  For several years the district has been grappling with a variety of challenges, including but not limited to, declining enrollment, pension contribution increases, labor market pressures, and mandates from the state and federal governments. Stability can be achieved, and it requires efficiently using available resources in order to focus on student achievement. 

A reduced work year is a solution to the projected deficit in the 2019-20 budget and will allow the district to meet its mandates and requirements, as well as the goals of Vision 2020 without reducing services that would directly impact students.  This is a recommended solution considering approximately 95 percent of the district’s unrestricted budget is dedicated to staff, including salary and benefit costs.
 

 

California continues to underfund our public education system. The teachers’ strike in Los Angeles has increased public awareness of the extent to which our schools are suffering from a lack of resources. In San Diego, our district and our Board of Education has often spoken publicly about the need for adequate state funding. We continue to press for a broader state conversation on adequacy and tax reform, and we acknowledge recent comments by Governor Newsom of his intent to pursue this conversation.

 

Even as we press for additional action at the state level, San Diego Unified must meet our obligation to pass a balanced budget for the coming school year. The challenge is too big for the district to accomplish alone. For that reason, San Diego Unified is committed to working with our bargaining units to find solutions to the budget challenge. We recognize at the outset that neither the district nor the bargaining units have a monopoly on good ideas, and the best solutions come through common-sense dialogue and collaboration.

 

As educators, administrators, and staff we share a common commitment to provide our students with a world-class education and we will work with all our bargaining units to make sure San Diego Unified continues to attract and retain the best employees in the nation.


The district noticed all bargaining units on January 18, 2019, of its intent to bargain a reduced work year.

CEQA ANALYSIS: The Proposed Project can be found exempt from environmental review pursuant to CEQA Guidelines Section 15061(b)(3). The activity is covered by the general rule which exempts activities that can be seen with certainty to have no possibility for causing a significant effect on the environment. The installation of a new ADA ramp in an area where existing concrete walkway and entry currently exists would not create any significant environmental impacts. Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines 15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.

BACKGROUND:  The District's Physical Plant Operations (PPO) Department requires the rental of heavy, medium and light equipment, and various tool (hereinafter referred to as Equipment) on an ongoing, as needed basis in order to repair and maintain all school sites and all administrative facilities.  The newer equipment provided by HERC and United Rental produces less greenhouse gas emissions due to HERC and United Rental business practice of replacing their equipment within 36 to 48 months (depending on usage trends) contributing to the district's Sustainability Action Plan (SAP). San Diego Unifieds Sustainability Action Plans, Dream Big Solutions for a Sustainable Future were developed to help reduce the districts environmental footprint with a goal towards net zero waste.

 

Under Public Contract Code section 20118, the governing board has determined that it is in the best interests of the District to utilize this piggyback contract to purchase supplies. The statute provides that the District may purchase personal property from contracts already competitively bid by another public agency. 

 

On July 8, 2014, the Board received the final report from the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee. Included in this report as a Dream Big" idea was a recommended action to join or create larger purchasing cooperatives. A purchasing cooperative establishes various contracts which aggregate the total purchases from many government entities resulting in lower costs to governmental entities who then utilize these contracts.
