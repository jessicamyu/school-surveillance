BACKGROUND:  San Diego County is a "System of Care" county. The System of Care is based on Child and Adolescent Service System Program (CASSP) System of Care principles and the Wraparound Initiative of the State of California.

According to the Policy Leadership Cadre for Mental Health in Schools, May 2001, there is a need to create a cohesive framework of mental health services for students and families with mental health needs. This framework should weave school-owned resources and community resources together and connect in major ways to the mission of schools. Mental health services in schools need to be viewed as essential to addressing barriers to learning and not as an agenda separate from the schools' instructional mission. Traditional referral and treatment of students with mental health needs through schools has been difficult for families to access and more integration between mental health services and schools is necessary to monitor the quality of treatment, particularly in relationship to expected educational outcomes.

San Diego Unified School District has recognized the need for the screening and identification of students who have unmet mental health needs that impair their educational performance and the need to provide and coordinate quality mental health services at their school sites. Through the district's contracts with the County of San Diego, Health and Human Services Agency, the district has established a Mental Health Resource Center to provide and/or integrate the mental health services necessary to give students the optimum opportunity to achieve. The MHRC coordinates the services of community mental health providers with district mental health support services to provide a continuum of mental health services to district students and their families.

In accordance with this Agreement, Charmi Patel Rao, M.D., will provide medication, assessment, and treatment services to individual students, interdisciplinary team consultation to individual programs, and in-services and staff consultation to other district schools as assigned. Overall programmatic consultation will be provided to the Program Manager of the Mental Health Resource Center as needed.

Students may exhibit disabilities such as autism or other developmental and/or physical disabilities, psychological, emotional and/or behavioral disturbance; or may require a residential treatment environment. The NPA programs provide educational support services including tutoring, individual, group and family counseling, and staff training.  NPA services are provided in home, office, or school settings.

 

The rates for CDE-certified nonpublic schools and agencies are negotiated by the San Diego County Office of Education (COE) and the Special Education Local Plan Area (SELPA) representatives. 

 

In addition to rate negotiation, a "master contract" is developed and approved by the SELPA representatives for use by each school district. The master contract is then distributed to the 42 school districts for approval by their respective boards.  Because the SELPA representatives often finalize its master contract after the fiscal year-end, and because the California Department of Education (CDE) certifies nonpublic schools and nonpublic agencies for a calendar year (January through December) rather than for a fiscal or school year (July through June), the district submits master contracts for school board approval twice yearlyâ€”once within the first quarter of the fiscal year for services provided from July through December; and once within the first quarter of the calendar year for services provided from January through June after expiration of the previous year's CDE certifications and re-approval for the new calendar year. Because of timing issues, these contracts are often submitted to the school board as ratifications.

 

Nonpublic school placements and nonpublic agency educational support services are dictated by each student's IEP, due process or mediation agreement. In addition, students may be placed in an NPS by County Mental Health or other County agencies; or nonpublic students may move into district boundaries requiring the district to provide special education student services which includes NPS placement or NPA services if mandated by the student's IEP.  Therefore, student enrollment counts can change or new nonpublic schools and agencies may be identified at any time requiring ratification of additional NPS/NPA agreements.

The district continued to make payments under these agreements since that time.

 

The SELPA representatives began continuous negotiations with the nonpublic schools and nonpublic agencies and ultimately, the COE Superintendent requested intervention by the Division of Administrative Hearings in order to facilitate finalizing of an agreement with both the nonpublic schools and the nonpublic agencies. On March 11, 2011, a tentative agreement was reached. In June 2011, the 2010-11 master contract, the 2011-2012 master contract, and rate sheets were distributed to school districts by the COE along with a letter directing each school board to approve individual agreements with each nonpublic school and agency where students are placed.

District staff further recommend that the board adopt the draft Resolution of the Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District Revoking the Charter of Promise Charter School and Making Written Findings In Support of Revocation.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

The Notice to Remedy was delivered to Promise Charter School on April 13, 2011, and provided notice to the charter school of its obligation to refute or remedy the violations identified therein by May 20, 2011. Promise Charter School responded to the Notice to Remedy on May 20, 2011. 

 

After thorough review of Promise Charter School’s response to the Notice to Remedy and all supporting documents submitted therewith, the district concluded that the charter school failed to refute or remedy all of the violations set forth in the Notice to Remedy. 

 

At the June 7, 2011 board meeting, the board reviewed the staff reports and supporting information, and the draft Notice of Intent to Revoke setting forth the grounds, facts, and evidence upon which the revocation proceedings were based, as well as a schedule for the proceedings, and approved the issuance of the Notice of Intent to Revoke and Notice of Facts in Support of Revocation (Ed. Code, section 47607(e)) to Promise Charter School.  Promise was provided with the Notice of Intent to Revoke and all supporting evidence on June 8, 2011. In conformity with Education Code section 47607(e), the public hearing was to take place at the June 28, 2011 board meeting; the board was to issue a final decision on the revocation at its meeting scheduled for July 5, 2011.

 

Shortly after the Notice of Intent to Revoke was issued by the board, Promise Charter School requested for the district stay revocation proceedings to allow the parties to participate in mediation, in an effort to avoid revocation of the Promise Charter School charter.  Based on execution of a mediation agreement between Promise Charter School and the district regarding the stay of revocation proceedings, the board approved postponing the public hearing and revocation decision to allow the parties to participate in mediation. 

 

The parties agreed to a Memorandum of Understanding Regarding Conditions for Suspending Revocation Proceedings (MOU) during mediation, which was not approved by the Promise Charter School Board.  As a result, the revocation proceedings were reinstated and on July 19, 2011, the public hearing was held in conformity with Education Code section 47607(e), in the normal course of business, on the issue of whether evidence exists to revoke the Promise Charter School charter.  At the time of the public hearing, the board had before it the Notice to Remedy, Promise’s Response thereto, the Notice of Intent to Revoke, Promise’s Opposition thereto, the district’s Reply, as well as all documentation and supporting information offered by the district and Promise Charter School. During the public hearing, the board received information and comment from district staff, legal counsel, Promise Charter School, Promise Charter School legal counsel, and the public on the issue of whether evidence exists to revoke the Promise Charter School charter.

 

Education Code section 47607(e) provides that not later than 30 days of the public hearing, the chartering authority shall issue a final decision to revoke or decline to revoke the charter. The chartering authority shall not revoke a charter, unless it makes written factual findings supported by substantial evidence, specific to the charter school, that support its findings.

 

District staff submits that, as set forth in the draft Resolution, grounds exist to revoke the Promise Charter School charter and asks the board to make the final decision to revoke the charter and adopt the Resolution as its written factual findings supported by substantial evidence, specific to Promise Charter School, in accordance with section 47607(e).

RECOMMENDATION:  Staff is requesting board approval to begin a shortened version of the process outlined in Administrative Procedure 3113 to consider consolidating, reconfiguring or closing district schools. Because of the continuing financial crisis impacting the district's budget, it will be necessary to shorten the timeline identified in the procedure. Staff intends to initiate a community consultation process on the issue, resulting in recommendations to the board before the end of the calendar year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: As the district developed solutions to its 2011-12 budget deficit, one of the items that the board requested staff to begin examining was the potential to close or reconfigure and consolidate schools for the 2012-13 school year.  Earlier this year, following board direction, a staff working group (membership list attached) has discussed the issues surrounding closure and consolidation and has examined information on enrollment change, operating cost, capacity and other variables pertinent to identifying closure or consolidation candidates.

 

Administrative Procedure 3113 (School Utilization/Reconfiguration/Closure Planning: Staff and Community Process - attached), approved by the board at its July 27, 2010 meeting, maps out a process over a period of approximately nine months, starting in July with an initial summary of enrollment and capacity, proceeding through the fall as a community-based committee studies data and holds public meetings, and ending in April with the board taking action on recommendations from the community committee. 

 

Given the need to have an earlier decision from the board in order to program the effects of any school closure/reconfiguration/consolidation plans into the 2012-13 budget development process, plus allowing affected families and school communities the ability to know as far as possible in advance what alternative options are available, staff is requesting to initiate a more compressed timeline than laid out in the Procedure. 

 

The Deputy Superintendent Business, is requesting that in September, as the new 2011-12 school enrollments are stabilizing, a report will be presented to the board that will include recommendations of clusters where it appears that school closure/consolidation/reconfiguration can occur, and at that time, as discussed in the procedure, that the board direct staff to begin a dialogue with cluster communities to develop specific recommendations.  It is expected that a final report incorporating those recommendations would be presented to the board (first and second reading) by December 2011.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In 2002, the Board of Education made an investment to replace the legacy applications with Oracle’s PeopleSoft Enterprise Resource Planning system and the Zangle Student Information System (SIS). Over time other primary systems were replaced including a transportation system, PPO work order system, and in progress is a new Special Education IEP tracking and Response to Intervention system.

 

Current challenges include:

  • The district currently does not have easy drill down access to our data across the core student, HR, Finance and other business systems
  • The version of Oracle PeopleSoft that the district is currently running on is nearing its end of supportable life and will require significant upgrades starting in 2012
  • The district’s current SIS provider (C-Innovation – Zangle) has gone out of business and the district now needs to consider a replacement system
  • Most Non-Instructional computers at school sites and central offices are outdated, out of warranty and unfixable as they fail.

Information Technology Support Services (ITSS) recommended solutions to address these challenges are as follows:

 

1.  Oracle Business Intelligence Build-Out:  It is recommended that the district ‘build out’ the Oracle Business Intelligence Enterprise Edition (OBIEE) platform via rapid deployment of Board of Education and Superintendent priority reports/dashboards providing real-time key metrics. Additionally, after the system is built out, fund the upgrade of the Oracle BI system from 10gR3 to 11gR1.

 

2.  PeopleSoft Upgrades:  It is recommended that the district stay on the PeopleSoft suite of business applications and to upgrade the applications as new releases become available. To maintain an Oracle supported platform and ensure that core business systems are up to date and current through 2017 will require approving and funding a multi-year upgrade of the PeopleSoft Human Capital Management, Finance, and Supply Chain Management systems from version 8.9 to 9.1/9.2. This upgrade also accounts for the integration with Primavera and Contract Manager that will assist FPC in managing financial aspects of Proposition S.

 

As part of the upgrade, ITSS is recommending the approval and funding of one Enterprise Business Software Coordinator type position to manage the complex PeopleSoft upgrade and related tasks.

 

3. Zangle Replacement:  It is recommended that the district replace the current, non-supported SIS (Zangle) to provide a modern and supported platform for student records to be maintained and reported from. In May of 2011, the district put out an RFI to allow staff to better understand what is available today, specifically looking for features such as an online student registration module, an informative parent portal, and comprehensive school site reporting tools.  In July a diverse team of stakeholders will review the top responders and assist in developing an RFP that included all of the essential features identified by the team.

 

As part of the replacement project, ITSS is recommending the approval and funding of one Enterprise SIS Software Coordinator type position to manage the complex SIS replacement and related tasks.

 

4.  Non-instructional Computer Replacement:  It is recommended that the district replace the outdated and out of warranty DWA/Non-Instructional computers and provide a structure to establish a budgeted four year replacement schedule past year five. This strategy is geared to align with both warranty duration (four years) and the maximum useful life of computers procured today. An ITSS coordinated deployment will guarantee that school sites and departments have current and warrantied computers thereby improving staff efficiency and minimizing downtime due to computer hardware failure, lack of parts availability, incompatibility, or general performance (slow).

 

Funding-The initial system purchase in 2002 and upgrades to other districtwide systems have traditionally been funded through the State Schools Facilities Fund (SSFF).  Since this is such a large project and due to the current budget situation we are recommending that SSFF be considered as a funding source for this critical project.
