RECOMMENDATION: This is a First Reading of the proposed 2020-21 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district. The report includes 2020-21 Title I Eligibility Ranking and school participation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.00.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.00.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) on Compensatory Education discussed the Title I Ranking process and report on October 16, 2019, November 20, 2019, and December 18, 2019, with a vote taken at the December meeting to approve the report as presented.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). Title I, one of several programs under ESSA, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the district and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of "children in poverty" residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The proposed 2020-21 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2019-20 meal eligibility percentage of 40 percent or higher. Four schools' percentage rose above 40 percent from last year and will be included in the Title I Program for 2020-21: Tierrasanta and Hage elementary schools, DePortola Middle School, and University City High School. Seven additional schools fell below 40 percent from last year and are recommended to be grandfathered for one year: Gage and Ericson elementary schools, Pacific Beach and Standley middle schools, Henry High School, San Diego Metropolitan, Regional, Career and Technical High School, and iHigh Virtual Academy. The total 2020-21 Title I entitlement is approximately $38,000,000.00.

Proposed 2020 Summer School Program

 

It is proposed that a 2020 Summer School program be offered to general education students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects).

 

A proposed six-week program will be offered at an estimated nine high school sites, to be located districtwide, for students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects). Historical data indicates that approximately 5,000 students in Grades 9 through 12 receive F grades in core subjects at the first semester, which renders them eligible for the program. Proposed program dates are June 15 16, 2020 – July 24 25, 2020. Transportation will not be provided.

 

Proposed 2020 Extended School Year (ESY) Program for Students with Disabilities

 

The California Code of Regulations Section 3043(f) requires that Extended School Year (ESY) services be provided for each student with disabilities whose unique needs require special education and related services in excess of the regular academic year. Such students have disabilities which are likely to continue indefinitely or for a prolonged period, and interruption of the student's educational programming may cause regression, when coupled with limited recoupment capacity, rendering it impossible or unlikely that the student will attain the level of self-sufficiency and independence that would otherwise be expected in view of his or her disabling condition. The lack of clear evidence of such factors may not be used to deny participation in an ESY program if the student’s Individual Education Program (IEP) team determines the need for such a program and includes ESY in the IEP. The proposed program for high school students with disabilities is June 15 16, 2020 – July 24 25, 2020; elementary and middle school students on traditional schedules, June 15 16, 2020 – July 10, 2020; and elementary and middle school students on year-round schedules, July 27 23, 2020 – August 21 17, 2020. Transportation services will be provided.

 

Proposed 2020 Summer School/Extended School Year (ESY) Sites

 

It is proposed that summer programs for both general education students and students with disabilities be held at districtwide sites. Tentative high school sites are: Clairemont, Garfield, Hoover, iHigh, Lincoln, San Diego SCPA, Serra and Twain. Due to major construction projects in the district, all final summer school and ESY host sites have not yet been selected. Final sites will be determined after consultation with Physical Plant Operations (PPO) and Facilities, Planning and Construction (FPC) to ensure no conflicts, and in collaboration with Integrated Youth Services, the Area Superintendents, and the Transportation Department, taking into consideration actual geographic patterns of students eligible for ESY.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  On December 16, 2019, the City of San Diego and representatives from a regional coalition announced San Diego’s signing of the Mayor’s Monarch Pledge. The Pledge is a nationwide effort by the National Wildlife Federation to support struggling monarch butterfly populations. In the last 20 years, monarch populations have fallen by 90%, putting the species in danger of disappearing. By signing the Mayor’s Monarch Pledge, the City of San Diego is committed to creating habitat for the monarch butterfly and pollinators, and to educating citizens about how they can make a difference at home and in their communities. The plight of the monarch butterfly is being incorporated into San Diego Unified’s Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts and Math (STEAM) curriculum beginning with Kindergarteners who learn about the needs of living things by observing plants and animals directly and through books and media to discover patterns in what they need to survive, distinguishing between plants and animals based on those needs, and describing how organisms meet their needs using resources from their surroundings. The biodiversity in landscapes second grade curriculum allows students to investigate the needs of plants and engineer models that mimic their pollination and seed dispersal structures and includes examples of complex relationships and interdependence such as monarch butterfly dependence on native milkweed and how humans are impacting environments. We want our students to actively apply their learning to make a difference in their communities, and the plight of the monarch butterfly is a great local phenomenon that allows students to design and actively contribute solutions. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports the efforts of the regional partnership that includes the City of San Diego, San Diego Zoo Global, San Diego Gas and Electric Company, and the Resource Conservation District of Greater San Diego, a group dedicated to reversing the steep losses in pollinator populations in creating demonstration gardens and increasing the amount of native milkweed and flowering plants in San Diego to restore monarch friendly habitats and ensure that children can continue to enjoy monarch butterflies for generations to come.

BACKGROUND: As of October 6, 2009, eligible charter schools qualify for Multiyear Facilities License Agreements (MFLAs) on district properties. On that date, the board approved offering multiyear terms for the location of charter schools meeting certain criteria: demonstrated financial stability; have been through the renewal process at least once; were ‘conversion charter schools’ from previously district-run schools; or charter school is not sharing a site with a district-run school or use. On December 10, 2019, the board approved expanding the multiyear criteria to include, at the board's discretion, those charters that are sharing a site with a district-run school or use, where construction of a new classroom building through a board-approved charter school project has reached substantial completion. The district-run school on the shared site shall not be projected over the MFLA term to use the spaces to be allocated to the charter.

 

San Diego Global Vision Academy has previously been through the renewal process. It has operated at the Adams Elementary School, located at 4672 - 35th Street, San Diego, California 92116, since July 1, 2011. A new two-story classroom building has been completed as a charter school project approved by the board on July 7, 2015. It has been completed and is currently occupied by San Diego Global Vision Academy under a single-year Facilities License Agreement. The district-run school sharing the site, Adams Elementary, recently completed a whole site modernization that provides sufficient facilities for its stable enrollment; therefore, it is not projected over the term of this MFLA to use the spaces designated for the charter.  Thus, San Diego Global Vision Academy meets the eligibility criteria for an MFLA.  The approval of this MFLA is contingent upon board approval of the charter renewal of the same term.

 

CEQA ANALYSIS: Approval of the MFLA is considered a project under CEQA and is subject to the requirements of the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et. seq., as amended). However, it is exempt from further environmental review under the following CEQA categorical exemption class:

 

Existing Facilities (Class 1) - Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency‘s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

 

The Proposed Project includes the execution of a new MFLA which would not result in expansion of use beyond existing conditions. SDGVA has operated as a charter school on the Adams Elementary School site since July 1, 2011. Furthermore, approval of the MFLA would not result in an increase in student enrollment, capacity, or require the construction or provision of additional classroom facilities not already planned and approved by the board. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.

  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.

  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

 

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of an existing elementary school in a suburban community and would not extend into any offsite areas that would impact a sensitive environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no projects proposed on the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Completion of the Adams Elementary School Whole Site Modernization Project would be completed prior to the commencement of the proposed MFLA. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  3. Significant Effect. There is no reasonable possibility that approval of the MFLA would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. A portion of the site is currently used and occupied by SDGVA and approval of the MFLA would result in operations consistent with existing use. Therefore, this exemption does not apply to the project.

  4. Scenic Highways. The project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. There are no district facilities located on a hazardous waste site that are included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  6. Historical Resources. Approval of the MFLA would not require demolition of structures or excavation and impacts to historic, prehistoric, or paleontological resources would not occur. Therefore, this exemption does not apply to the project.

Approval of this item approves the MFLA with San Diego Global Vision Academy and directs staff to file a Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.

BACKGROUND: San Diego County Office of Education (SDCOE) will provide principal learning network meetings, collective learning walks, and individual principal coaching at Chollas/Mead, Encanto, Johnson, and Webster elementary schools, and a final report during the 2019-20 academic school year.

 

In collaboration with SDCOE, district principals will participate in the following:

  • Principal Learning Network Meetings. The Consultant/Professional shall conduct eight monthly, three-hour Principal Learning Network meetings between September 2019 and June 2020. These meetings will focus on building principals' teaching and learning leadership capacity around a group-identified early literacy problem of practice within a specific K-3 grade level.
     
  • Collective learning walks. Walks shall occur during each network meeting, with each principal participant hosting an instructional learning walk one to two times during the 2019-20 school year. The purpose of these learning walks is not to evaluate teachers but to assist principals in developing their leadership skills in supporting strong early literacy classroom instruction. By using the Five Dimensions (5d) Framework on Teaching and Learning, principal participants will build capacity around documented descriptive evidence and targeted teacher feedback to improve the implementation of teaching and learning best practices in their schools. The 5d tool was created for educational leaders to guide principals to provide teachers feedback on quality instruction. Collective leadership next steps will be determined as a result of consistent monitoring of improvement data. Data shall be determined using the 5d Framework as a guide.
     
  • Individual Principal Coaching. Twice monthly one-on-one individual coaching sessions shall be conducted for three of the participating site-based leaders, including Chollas/Mead, Encanto, and Webster elementary schools. Connection to the network meetings will help guide content and leadership goal setting. The 5d Framework for Teaching and Learning will be used as a lens to guide instructional improvement as measured by data collected during classroom walkthroughs. Individual goal setting will be conducted with each principal participant and data on goal achievement will be monitored and revised through a Plan-Do-Study-Act continuous improvement model. Artifacts will be collected as a means to monitor and measure improvement aims.
