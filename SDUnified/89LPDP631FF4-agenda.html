RECOMMENDATION:  Approve the 2010-11 Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA) and Categorical Budget Summary for La Jolla High School.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:   None.

 

BACKGROUND:  District practice has been for sites to develop their Single Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) during the spring preceding the year of implementation.  Problems last spring with the newly adopted Site-Based Budgeting (SBB) tool that includes a function to develop SPSAs, required delaying SPSA development until fall 2010.

 

As a result of SB 374, the state holds each school accountable for developing and implementing a SPSA. The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

Additionally, Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their smart goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

Prior to implementation, the local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA.


A copy of the SPSA, budget summary, and required, signed assurances page are available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

6. Proposed Amendment to Long-Term Title I Planning (Evans)

RECOMMENDATIONS:

  1. The study period and community input for the long term Title 1 allocation plan be extended to November 30, 2011;
  2. In order to receive broad and adequate community input on these programs, a decision on the threshold level be postponed until after receipt of all input; and
  3. Any phase-in planning for the 75 percent threshold level be put on hold.

BACKGROUND:  At the December 14, 2010 Board of Education meeting, the board voted to keep the Title I threshold at 40 percent Free Reduced Lunch (FRL) on a sliding scale from 99 to 40 percent.  The board called for a review of the results achieved through Title I funding in the district.  The board asked for broad community input in this process, which was further clarified at the January 11, 2011 meeting.  However, the board also voted to direct staff to begin developing a process to gradually change the threshold from 40 to 75 percent over five years before gaining community input.

 

A board workshop on Title I funding was held on April 12, 2011.  Staff outlined rules and procedures for use of Title I funds.  Title I funding is a community based program with decisions on school use of funds approved by a School Site Council.  This occurs at all Title I schools throughout the district.  At schools with 75 percent FRL or greater the funds must be awarded to schools at all grade levels according to their rank.  For the 40 to 75 percent FRL schools, the funds may be disbursed by rank or by grade level groupings, according to the district’s priorities.  No information was presented at the workshop to suggest that Title I funds are being used any more or any less effectively at 75 to 99 percent schools versus 40 to 75 percent schools.  However, the funds are weighted for 2011-12 on a sliding scale ranging from $425 per student for a 99 percent FRL school down to $71 per student for a 40 percent FRL school.  Thus, the vast majority of funds already go to schools with 75 percent or greater FRL. These funds provide essential academic support for the economically disadvantaged students at the school which they attend.  In next year’s allocation for elementary schools, the schools at or above 75 percent FRL will receive $10.4 million collectively and those in the 40 to 75 percent range will receive $2.0 million.  High schools such as Morse and Mira Mesa, a majority of whose students are FRL, would receive no Title I funds when this transition is made, while next year they will receive $423,000 and $205,000 collectively for key academic assistance for their neediest students.

 

There are potential changes that could allow more Title I funds to be used at school sites. The district may apply for a waiver against the requirement that supplemental education services (SES) funds be awarded to outside contractors who are not accountable to the district for results.  Instead, these funds would be used by the district at Program Improvement schools.  Set asides for transportation and administration can also be reviewed for increased funding to school sites.

 

The board has asked for community and stakeholder input.  However, the change from 40 to 75 percent as the threshold was decided without community input.  This potential change needs to be discussed at the school site and cluster levels.
