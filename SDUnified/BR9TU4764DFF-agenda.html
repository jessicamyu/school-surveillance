2. Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing April 22, 2021, as Earth Day in the San Diego Unified School District (Patterson/Bazzo)
Meeting

Type

Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing April 22, 2021, as Earth Day in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Earth Day has inspired millions of Americans from across the country and has served as a foundation for US environmental movements. Growing out of the first Earth Day, Earth Day Network (EDN) works with over 22,000 partners in 192 countries to broaden, diversify, and mobilize the environmental movement. EDN’s Educators' Network, used by over 30,000 teachers and administrators nationwide, provides innovative tools and resources to promote civic participation and to develop a sense of environmental responsibility among students of all ages. Green schools conserve energy, save water, save money, and create a more successful environment for students. In 2013, San Diego Unified formed the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee (ESAC) which developed many recommendations to conserve energy and water, green schools, and preserve the environment. San Diego Unified has implemented many of the ESAC recommendations and has established Garden to Café Programs at many schools; over half of district schools have native and vegetable gardens and Green Teams focusing on recycling and conservation projects at their sites; over 200 district teachers have aquaponic systems in their classrooms, teaching students important environmental principles. San Diego Unified offers environmental literacy programs at multiple high schools through biology, environmental science, and community college based environmental programs. San Diego Unified is working to combat climate change by retrofitting its bus fleet to electric vehicles. San Diego School Unified District currently has a climate action plan that reviews the ways the district can make a positive impact on climate change and how they can become an environmental leader in San Diego County. San Diego Unified students living in communities of color and low-income communities, including City Heights, Barrio Logan, and South East San Diego, are disproportionately affected by climate change and environmental impacts. The San Diego Unified School District has shown support of student leadership in fighting for a green future. In 2014, the San Diego Board of Education acknowledged the threat climate change poses to youth’s health and adopted a resolution in support of the Parents Teachers Associations initiative to tackle climate change. In 2021, San Diego Unified passed a resolution urging Governor Newsom to cease issuing new oil permits and to develop a more comprehensive plan for phasing out fracking in California. In 2015, the San Diego School Unified District created a plan to transition to 100% renewable energy by 2035 in order to mitigate the effects of climate change.

BACKGROUND:  SBCS Corporation is the lead agency for the San Diego Promise Neighborhood (SDPN) Grant to provide community services at San Diego High School and Perkins K-8 School.

 

This Agreement will provide services for students and families in the San Diego High School and Perkins K-8 school communities. Services may include housing assistance, independent living skills, employment readiness, financial literacy services, computer literacy courses, mental health counseling, alcohol and drug prevention services, domestic violence counseling, child abuse intervention, juvenile crime prevention, therapeutic, educational programming, and meals with nutritional support.

 

A "Promise Neighborhood" is a community of opportunity that allows children to learn, grow, and succeed. Funded by the U.S. Department of Education's Office of Innovation and Improvement, the initiative builds on successful efforts already underway. It brings together schools, community organizations, local businesses, and other community members in a local neighborhood. This grant is inspired by the Harlem Children's Zone; Promise Neighborhoods wrap children in high-quality, coordinated health, social, community, and educational support from cradle-to-college-to-care. San Diego High School and Perkins K-8 School were selected based on the location of the schools to the Promise Neighborhood catchment area where services are being offered and the highest need based on the data at the time of the grant application.

BACKGROUND: On July 27, 2004, the board approved a lease between San Diego Unified and Appleseed San Diego (Appleseed) for a portion of the former Marcy Elementary School site. The lease was amended on April 21, 2005, June 22, 2007, July 1, 2010 and August 1, 2021 (Attachment 2). With one available five-year option remaining on the lease, Appleseed could remain at the site until June 30, 2030.

 

Due to the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent reduced student enrollment guidelines in San Diego County, Appleseed requested continuation of abatement of the monthly rent. San Diego Unified (District) received financial statements and bank records from Appleseed that demonstrate a substantial decrease in income caused by COVID-19. This information shows Appleseed received a Pay Check Protection (PPP) loan in May 2020 which allowed them to continue payment of rent to the District through July 2020, although not open for students.

 

In an effort to assist Appleseed with continuing to provide services to their students and maintain this site as a revenue-generating facility for the district, staff recommends 50 percent abatement of monthly rent March 2021 through August 2021.  Appleseed expects student enrollment to reach a sustainable level in September 2021 when more people are vaccinated against COVID-19.  Monthly rent will resume at its current rate of $69,448.68 in September 2021 unless student enrollment restrictions remain in San Diego, in which case another amendment may be submitted for board consideration.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The proposed amendment to the existing lease for abatement of monthly rent does not constitute a "Project" per California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines section 15378, because the proposed amendment would not result in physical changes to the existing school site or result in changes that would expand operations beyond existing use, and would not result in a direct physical change in the environment, or a reasonably foreseeable indirect physical change in the environment.  Furthermore, the proposed activity is covered by the common sense exemption, consistent with CEQA Guidelines section 15061 (b) (3), which states that CEQA applies only to projects that have the potential for causing a significant effect on the environment. Where it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that the activity in question may have a significant effect on the environment, the activity is not subject to CEQA. Therefore, the proposed amendment to the existing lease is not subject to CEQA.

BACKGROUND: San Diego Unified (District) staff proposes replacing existing roofing material at four district school sites.  Following is a description of the project at each site.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: Each project is subject to CEQA. However, they are exempt from further environmental analysis under a Class 1, Existing Facilities, categorical exemption. “Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency's determination,” (CEQA Guidelines §15301).  The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. These projects would result in the repair and replacement of existing facilities at each of the four school sites and would not change existing on-site uses and operations at each site.  Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for each project.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

CEQA Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites and would not extend into off-site areas where sensitive or critical habitat is located. Replacement of existing roofing and mechanical equipment is consistent with existing uses. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed on the school sites or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction duration of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. There is no reasonable possibility that the proposed activity would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. All of the proposed improvements would occur on existing buildings within existing operational school sites. No work is proposed on the exterior of the existing structures or offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the Proposed Project is not located on a site where hazardous materials or hazardous waste have been identified, nor is it located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because grading and trenching into native soils is not proposed. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

Approval of this item approves each project as described and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk (Attachment 1).
