RECOMMENDATION:  Approve Agreement with Discover Financial Services to accept grant funding for revision and production of a personal financial literacy curriculum for 12th grade regular and honors Economics courses, and professional development for 50 Economics teachers (PS-15-0501-76).  The funding will be used during the 2015 summer to revise and produce the curriculum for use during the 2015-16 and 2016-17 school years.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  Revenue in the amount of $161,533.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  A portion of the funding will be used to provide professional development to 50 Economics teachers.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  In October 2005, the district formed the Personal Financial Literacy Task Force (PFLTF) in response to board and staff concerns about the ability of graduating students to manage their own personal finances.  The Task Force was comprised of representatives from the District Relations Department, Office of College, Career and Technical Education, school administrators, 12th grade Economics teachers, the presiding bankruptcy judge for San Diego County, community representatives and local financial institutions (US Bank, California Coast Credit Union, and Chase).  Because California is one of the few states that neither requires financial literacy for graduation nor explicitly includes it in state standards, the Task Force determined that a prudent course of action was to identify a current personal financial literacy curriculum that could be implemented in our high schools.  After reviewing financial literacy materials being used across the country, no single curriculum resonated with the Task Force. As a result, the Task Force elected to develop an “in‐house” curriculum.  Teachers from Kearny High School of International Business and Hoover High School wrote, field‐tested, and revised lessons on financial literacy based on teacher feedback.  The final product of this endeavor was the ten‐day Financial Literacy…The Game of Life curriculum.  On April 22, 2008, the board endorsed the Interdivisional Curriculum Committee’s (ICC) recommendation that all district seniors receive personal financial literacy instruction in the Principles of Economics courses and unanimously approved the Financial Literacy. . .The Game of Life curriculum.   Initial costs to develop and print the curriculum were offset with a grant from the United Way. The curriculum has been continuously implemented since fall 2008.  The funds being provided through Discover Financial Services will allow the district to revise and update and produce the curriculum and provide professional development on the updated curriculum to 50 Economics teachers.

BACKGROUND:  The district and Qualcomm desire to enter into this partnership agreement beginning with the 2014-15 school year to engage student interest in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math (STEM) careers, promote K-12 education-industry collaboration, encourage the flow of knowledge and resources between the district and local businesses, and to enhance educational experiences that develop students' ambition, enthusiasm, and creativity.  A pilot program was tested in spring 2014, with students from Roosevelt Middle School. At the end of each academic year, both parties will evaluate the relationship and develop further plans based on these evaluations.

 

Through this partnership, Qualcomm will:

  1. Provide learning opportunities for district students and teachers through its Thinkabit Lab (also referred to as the STEM Lab).  Thinkabit is the combination of a hands-on lab, makerspace, and classroom for students in Grades 6, 7, and 8, to learn about careers, as well as participate in engineering experiments to promote the importance of STEM skills and opportunities for all.  Thinkabit has two main working areas.  One area, the QWOW! room, provides students with exposure to the World of Work, allowing students to learn about various careers at Qualcomm.  The activities performed in this area combine brief lectures with team interactions.  In the second area, students experience the Thinkabit lab and makerspace environment under the leadership of a Qualcomm engineer to create, manipulate, collaborate and present their electronic creations. Students learn how to create personalized robotic representations using coding, laptops, Arduino boards and servo motors. They also become familiar with circuitry and Ohlm’s Law through manipulation of multimeters, batteries, resisters, LEDs, and breadboards.  Students are limited only by their imagination and time.  Students work together and are encouraged to innovate and execute by collaborative means that represent 21st century skills. In 2014-15, Qualcomm will host 35 visits of up to 34 students and teachers for a total 1,190 students exposed the Thinkabit experience.  
  1. Make Thinkabit STEM Lab space available to San Diego Unified for meetings and teacher professional development.
  1. Provide education pathway guidance for students seeking potential future internships in Qualcomm or other similar companies.

District 'a-g' Implementation History:  In an effort to ensure that all San Diego Unified students have the opportunity to participate in college and be prepared for their choice of career, the Board of Education, with the support of community members, adopted a resolution in June 2009 that a steering committee be formed to develop recommendations and a plan for implementation of the 'a-g' course sequence for all students.  In 2009-10, the Education Trust West performed an Educational Opportunity Audit and analysis of Class of 2009 transcript data with regard to 'a-g' readiness, met with principals, teachers and students at five focus high schools for input, and conducted a Community Conversation with parents and community members to gather input on preparing all students for college and career readiness.  In 2010-11, the 'a-g' Task Force began developing implementation plans based on the audit results. The Board of Education approved the Task Force recommendations to:  align district graduation subject requirements with UC/CSU 'a-g' subject requirements, require new graduation requirements to be implemented with the Class of 2016, and encourage extra support for students earning D and F grades.  Since 2011-12, district staff have been laying the 'a-g' implementation groundwork to ensure success for the the Class of 2016. Implementation has included assisting sites in developing master schedules that include 'a-g' courses; working with high school principals, counselors and registrars on the 'a-g' course sequences; providing additional supports for students; developing four-year plans with 8th grade students to ensure they will be on track to graduate; and establishing a High School Resources Office to coordinate implementation efforts.  

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 4, Broad and Challenging Curriculum, Destination:  All students have equitable access to a broad and challenging curriculum, aligned to California/Common Core State Standards, that engages them in inquiry, critical thinking, and creativity and provides them with a strong foundation in the academic disciplines, broad exposure to the liberal arts and opportunities to pursue individual interests.  Teachers are prepared to provide a broad and challenging curriculum.

 

LCAP Goal:  Students have equitable access to rigorous, well-rounded standards-aligned curricula that assures readiness for a full range of post-graduation options.

 

LCAP Actions and Services - Year 1:

  1. Review, update, and monitor the district's course of study to fully align with the Common Core and English Language Development (ELD) State Standards, the Next Generation Science Standards, and UC/CSU 'a-g' requirements.
  2. Provide for cohesive, coherent, and well-articulated programs and course pathways within and across grade levels.
  3. Master schedules guarantee access to 'a-g' courses leading to graduation and college/career readiness for all high school students, student groups and provide for a growing number of students to have access to advanced learning courses (e.g., Advanced Placement, International Baccalaureate, Honors)
  4. School staff support students and families to plan and monitor courses to prepare them for graduation and post-graduation opportunities (e.g., 4-6 Year Plan, Naviance)
  5. Provide for cohesive and coherent enhanced/extended learning opportunities including:  Linked Learning, Career and Technical Education, Gifted and Talented Education, Advanced Placement, International Baccalaureate, Biliteracy and Dual Language Programs, Visual and Performing Arts, Advancement Via Individual Determination, Science Technology Engineering Arts Math, and outdoor learning opportunities.
  6. Develop and refine Common Core aligned curriculum maps, tools, and guides including access to quality digital tools and reputable online resources in service of effective teaching and learning for diverse groups.
  7. Identify and provide standards-aligned instructional materials and resources.
  8. Provide increased access to school libraries.
  9. All levels of leadership and staff develop and implement curriculum, and educational collections and resources that provide programs and services that are inclusive of the needs of all students in the community they serve.

Background:  Each month, Superintendent Marten provides an LCAP Implementation Report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting Year 1 LCAP actions, services, and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's LCAP Implementation Report each month, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report, and provide input and feedback on current implementation and suggested next steps.

BACKGROUND:  The Teacher Pipeline Task Force (TPTF), a joint undertaking of San Diego Unified and the San Diego Education Association (SDEA), involved a range of school-district, union, and community stakeholders in developing a collaborative plan of action for cultivating a highly skilled, effective teacher workforce that draws from district graduates and reflects the demographic diversity of the district’s student population.  The parameters for its work were defined in a December 2012, resolution of the district Board of Education, which called for the establishment of an ongoing partnership between San Diego Unified and SDEA focused on developing comprehensive strategies for:  1) Recruiting, supporting, and retaining district students to enter the teaching profession, and 2) Increasing teacher diversity to reflect that of the student population. 

 

Following several initial planning meetings during the 2012-13 and 2013-14 school years, the TPTF convened formally on January 16, 2014, with the goal of presenting a report of findings and recommendations to the board during the 2014-15 school year.   Subsequent meetings were held in February, March, April, June, July, and September.  The work occurred in multiple phases: Establishing the Case for Change, Information Gathering, Information Analysis and Planning, and Approval and Presentation. Decision-making occurred by consensus.  The report includes the Task Force's recommendations on Teacher Acquisition, Teacher Workforce Development, Teacher Workforce Retention, Teacher Pipeline Evaluation, and Ongoing Collaboration.

3. Review of Process for Adjusting Classes in First Weeks of School (Evans/Foster)
Meeting
Oct 28, 2014 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

Background:  It is usually unsettling for students and parents and teachers to move students from one classroom to another after having already attending one class for a few weeks up to several weeks.  In 2014-15, there appeared to be much less turmoil in this process compared to previous years, but there were still limited schools that had a difficult adjustment.  School enrollment can be projected, but it is never 100 percent accurate, as parents are free to move and sign up students at a public school with no advance notice. Traditionally, enrollment was not considered stabilized until the fourth Friday, and at that point final decisions were made about a school’s allocation. Sometimes in better budget times it was possible to hire more teachers for the schools that were understaffed and to leave those teachers at schools that exceeded the allocation to maintain stability from the first day of school.  Also, combination classes are sometimes formed when the enrollment in each grade is uneven and to prevent classes from being too large.

 

Action requested: Direct the Superintendent to produce the following:

  • A narrative report on what worked this year and lessons learned; an explanation of how the process works, including guidelines for exceptions
  • A review of how enrollment projections are made and how the gradual move towards increased neighborhood attendance may complicate future projections
  • A review of the pros and cons of making the adjustments based on enrollment at second Friday vs at a later date
  • Budget item: What would be the cost of leaving staff in place at overstaffed schools and hiring new staff for understaffed schools (using this year as an example)?
  • Budget item two: What would be the cost of having no combination classes throughout the district regardless of a school’s enrollment?

4. Development of Board/Superintendent Plan to Define Adequacy of Funding for San Diego Unified (Evans/Barrera)
Meeting
Oct 28, 2014 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

Background: With the severe budget cuts in recent years during the economic crisis it is widely recognized that public school funding in California is woefully inadequate.  The California Constitution codifies the intent that per pupil expenditures should equal or exceed the average of the top ten states. California currently ranks 50th among the states in per pupil funding.  However, beyond such comparisons there is a need for the legislature and the public to understand what would be achieved with increased funding.  We need to develop an operational definition of adequate funding.  We currently operate on a system that requires us to plead each year for additional funding, but there is no long term plan in place to achieve the funding called for in the Constitution and by the voters who passed Proposition 98.

 

Action:  For the San Diego Unified Board of Education and the Superintendent to collaborate on the development a long term advocacy plan for adequate funding, which would include, but not be limited to, the following:

  1. Development of a high level estimate of the funds that would be needed in the budget to fully implement the goals of the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP) and the larger goals of San Diego Unified’s Vision 2020.  Examples would include the cost of full implementation of class size reduction, scaling up high school programs for a full range of college and career preparation options for all students, additional support for English Language Learners, a broad range of professional development for new and veteran teachers, expansion of arts programs, etc.  Further development and enhancement of these programs is based upon the community input accumulated during development of the San Diego Unified LCAP.   This is consistent with the goals of LCFF to provide accountability by showing the taxpayers what we plan to achieve with their tax dollars.
  1. Seek cooperation from other school districts in various coalitions to develop their own estimates of the cost of implementing their plans to improve student achievement. This could potentially lead up to the calculation of a base grant amount that would be needed by districts across the state. This could be used as a more concrete definition of funding adequacy.
  1. Advocate for legislative hearings in early 2015, to begin considering a long term plan that defines adequacy of funding, as well as the means of generating those revenues needed for public education, which could include consideration of significant tax reform.
  1. This long term work needs to begin prior to the budget planning season, which begins with the development of the district's First Interim Financial Report in December. The development of this plan will also allow us to show a concrete “funding gap” between what is needed for the 2015-16 budget and what is proposed by the state.

RECOMMENDATION: The superintendent will discuss her plan to bring forward 2015-16 and 2016-17 instructional calendars and a multi-year plan for transitioning district schools to a common calendar, as a first reading. This item is for information only. An action item will be presented for consideration at the November 18, 2014 meeting, in conjunction with the Calendar Committee's annual recommendations.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The cost for converting schools to a traditional calendar is attached.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  If any changes are made to the instructional calendars, employee collective bargaining contracts require that the district notify bargaining units of any change to the instructional calendar, and bargain those changes if requested by the organizations. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Staff has provided regular informational updates on the development of the multi-year plan to the Calendar Committee, which is engaged in the development and recommendation of proposed instructional calendars for 2015-16 and 2016-17.

 

BACKGROUND: The district currently has a total of 54 year-round calendar schools and 118 traditional calendar schools. Both calendars have 180 days of academic instruction. Consistent with Vision 2020 goals of strengthening neighborhood schools within each of the 16 comprehensive high school clusters for greater community cohesion, transitioning district schools to a common calendar offers a variety of positive benefits and supports for families. In addition, the transition supports stabilizing enrollment as attendance data shows that absences can increase at year-round schools during the summer months (as early as May) and some schools, particularly those who serve military families, can lose enrollment.

 

On June 18, 2013, the board directed staff to conduct an in-depth review of moving the district to a common calendar for 2014-15 or beyond. At the January 14, 2014 board meeting, the superintendent provided an update on instructional calendar planning for 2014-15 and beyond. The board unanimously approved converting four year-round schools (Ericson, Mason, and Walker elementary schools in the Mira Mesa cluster and Sequoia Elementary School in the Madison cluster) to the traditional calendar as part of comprehensive cluster master planning completed in spring 2014, and directed staff to return with a proposal to transition the remaining year-round schools using rationale including schools serving military families and cluster or pathway alignment.

 

Multi-Year Calendar Plan

 

Based on board direction, staff has developed a proposal that moves district schools to a common calendar in phases. The intent of this approach is to allow ample time for on-going communication and planning with families and schools about the transition and to incorporate the common calendar effort into multi-year budget planning.

 

The first year of the proposed multi-year plan transitions 14 year-round schools (includes the four schools already approved through board action) to the traditional calendar for the 2015-16 school year. The schools are proposed as follows:
