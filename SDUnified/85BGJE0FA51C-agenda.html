RECOMMENDATION: Approve Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with the California Partnership for Achieving Student Success (Cal-PASS) and it’s participating school districts, community college districts, colleges and universities to share data, effective May 25, 2010 through June 30, 2015.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. All 110 California’s community colleges, most universities and two-thirds of the state’s school districts belong to Cal-PASS data sharing consortium. It is free to schools and colleges. The $4 million a year program is funded by the State of California through the California Community Colleges System Office and by foundations working to repair ruptures in the educational pipeline and help more students succeed in college.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: By joining the data-sharing network, we agree to make available to partners in our region at least five years of data, including grades, attendance and demographic information. This work will be performed by the Integrated Technology Support Services (ITSS) Department and will not require new, increases in FTEs, or additional salaried district positions. This item complies with the hiring and spending freeze business rules implemented for 2009-10.

 

BACKGROUND: Cal-PASS is the California Partnership for Achieving Student Success, a voluntary, collaboration in which California Schools, colleges and universities share student data and bring together teams of educators to mine that information for insights to reshape curricula and help more students enroll in college.

 

The database includes years of student grades as well as California’s Standardized Testing and Reporting (STAR) results in English language arts, mathematics, science and history/social science, and the California High School Exit Exam (CAHSEE). It can follow students’ results as they move from middle to high school to college universities and into the workforce.

 

Cal-PASS complies fully with the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act ( FERPA). Access to the database is password-protected and tightly restricted. All members must sign and abide by strict confidentiality safeguards. The student information is encrypted and made anonymous. Cal-PASS tools allow members to follow cohorts from middle school through college, but student names, Social Security numbers and other identifiers are not disclosed.

 

Cal-Pass will provide us with critical information about the success of our students as they move to the next educational level. Once armed with this transition information and the specifics about what is expected of our students, we can improve our teaching by better aligning our curricula.

It addresses Response to Intervention (RTI) for both academics and behavior by providing a tiered, problem solving structure for schools. This is supported by research indicating behavior issues as one of the main reasons "at risk" students drop out.

On November 18, 2008, the Board of Education approved a Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) Pilot Proposal for 15 volunteer Title I schools. The PBIS strategy is not a packaged program, but a behavior-based system approach that assists a school staff, with feedback from students and families, to develop an effective school wide behavioral support and discipline plan.
