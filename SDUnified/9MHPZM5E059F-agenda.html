RECOMMENDATION: Approve proclamation recognizing the Honorable Peter Chacon for his work advancing biliteracy.

 

BACKGROUND: Peter Chacon was born in Phoenix, Arizona on June 10, 1925; and as the son of a commander in Pancho Villa’s revolutionary army, Peter Chacon inherited his father’s passion and determination to fight for the rights of those who could not defend themselves.

 

After a brief but eventful tour of duty in the United States Armed Forces during World War II, Chacon used his G.I. Bill to attend college earning a bachelor’s degree in education and a master’s degree in school administration.

 

As a teacher in the late 1950’s, Chacon was struck by the inequality in the education system when it came to Spanish-speaking students and vowed to make the necessary changes to serve bilingual students.

 

In an effort to bring the Latino community together as one voice, Chacon founded and served as the first Chairman and Executive Director of the Chicano Federation in 1968.

 

In 1969 while serving as principal of Sherman Elementary School in San Diego, Chacon was recruited by a group of minority activists to run for the 79th Assembly District seat; and as the first Latino elected to the Assembly from San Diego, Chacon spent his 22-year career addressing inequalities in bilingual education.

 

Pete Chacon’s accomplishments while serving in the State Assembly include the 1976 approval of the Chacon-Moscone Bilingual-Bicultural Education Act which created transitional bilingual education programs in public schools.

 

The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes Peter Chacon and honors his work in bilingual education.

RECOMMENDATION: Trustees Evans and Foster will request the board take action to direct the Superintendent to develop the 2015-16 district budget without staff layoffs to maintain the stability of our classrooms.

 

BACKGROUND: Each year the district must make its final budget decisions by June.  However, before the state approves its final budget in June, the district must make its decision regarding how many teachers it can afford by March 15.  March 15 is the date by which teachers must receive temporary layoff notices.

 

Laying off teachers is very destabilizing to schools and disrupts staff cohesion. Before the budget is even adopted the district must issue a notice of intent to lay off a teacher by the March 15 deadline.  Sometimes it is determined by May or June that those layoffs are not necessary, but in the meantime there is unnecessary anguish and uncertainty on the part of teachers, students and parents.  In some cases teachers have already made plans for other employment before waiting for the final notice and the district can lose a valuable team member.

 

The Superintendent has released a plan for the 2015-16 budget.  While LCFF (Local Control Funding Formula) funding has increased, so have expenses, including STRS, PERS, and other items.  Despite the budget limitations, the Superintendent has pledged to reduce class size, increase counselor support, and to provide extra funding for English Language Learners, early learning programs, and high school transformation.  While we laud this proposal and support these priorities, we have asked the Superintendent to come back to the board in the next few weeks with more specific calculations as to how these priorities will be covered with the funds available.

 

The Superintendent has recognized the selling of real properties for operational expenses beyond those previously earmarked is no longer an option.  As the Superintendent looks for millions of dollars of budget solutions to meet the above priorities, this proposal makes clear to the Superintendent that the board will not consider staff layoffs and is committed to maintaining the stability of district classrooms.

BACKGROUND: On May 7, 1997, the federal government implemented the Telecommunications Act of 1996.  This program (referred to as E-rate) is designed to ensure that eligible schools have access to modern telecommunication services, which includes installation and/or replacement of technology infrastructure, Local Area Network (LAN) electronic components, and wireless systems.  Under the program, successful awardees receive grants that work similar to a coupon, enabling purchases of eligible technology equipment at discounts of up to 90 cents for every dollar in value.

 

The district has utilized the E-rate program in conjunction with capital bond proceeds to improve school and classroom technology infrastructure since 2004.  During the Proposition MM capital program, the district received E-rate grants totaling $13.8 million.  Since the passage of Proposition S and implementation of the district's i21 technology program, the district has received E-rate grants of $39.9 million (February 10, 2009), and $7.7 million (December 13, 2011).  Since the passage of Proposition Z, the district has received $43.5 million (June 18, 2013).  In total, over $104 million in E-rate grants have been used to leverage capital bond proceeds in advancing the district's instructional technology infrastructure.

 

On July 8, 2014, the board approved the “i21 Now Committee Final Report.”  The report recommended a “robust, flexible sustainable infrastructure to support wireless, wired and VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol)."  The attached E-rate Implementation Plan contains the proposed strategy to implement this initiative.  The plan proposes utilizing this funding opportunity to replace and/or install technology infrastructure and components in support of i21 classroom connectivity at all district schools located on public property.  E-rate and bond funds will also be used install VoIP at the remaining 86 traditional district schools that do not currently have this technology.

 

In consideration of the district's historical participation in the E-rate program, the adopted i21 Now Committee's report, and the E-rate Implementation plan, staff recommends approval to apply for an estimated $12.6 million E-rate grant for Funding Year 2015.  If successful, the grant award will be combined with funding from Proposition S and Z bond sales to implement planned technology upgrades.  Approval will allow all district schools to have access to high capacity wired and wireless technology infrastructure, including VoIP. 

 

The schools will remain in operation while construction work is being performed. Construction phasing and other strategies will be employed to minimize disruption so that teaching and learning are not compromised.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On September 2, 2014, a district electrician responded to a power outage at the football stadium at La Jolla High School.  It was determined that the affected power source was the high voltage (4,160 volts) portion of the circuit.  District electricians generally do not work on circuits over 600 volts.  This was the first week of school and the due to the immediate need for power to support scheduled stadium events and the safety of students at those events, Southland Electric, Inc. was contacted.  On September 3, 2014, Southland Electric workers were dispatched to troubleshoot the site.  They found a blown fuse in the 4160 switch, feeding the stadium lighting system.  Further testing confirmed that the cause of the fault originated from failed feeder wire insulation in an underground steel conduit run.  In addition, the power failure had taken down the transformer that fed the affected stadium lights before the fuse had blown.  Southland Electric then sourced and ordered a replacement transformer.  Since the transformer was not due to arrive for approximately three weeks, they were tasked to hook up a temporary generator to power the lighting as needed in the interim.  On September 22, 2014, Southland Electric received and installed the replacement transformer and replaced the failed portion of the feeder cabling.  The system was then tested, re-energized, and the stadium lighting was back on the permanent power source.

 

California Public Contract Code, section 20113(a), states, in part, that, in an emergency when any repairs, alterations, work, or improvement is necessary to any facility of public schools to permit the continuance of existing school classes, or to avoid danger to life or property, the board may, by unanimous vote, with the approval of the county superintendent of schools, do either of the following:

  1. Make a contract in writing or otherwise on behalf of the district for the performance of labor and furnishing of materials or supplies for the purpose without advertising for or inviting bids.
