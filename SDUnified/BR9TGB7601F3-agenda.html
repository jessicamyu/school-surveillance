RECOMMENDATION: Approve the revised proposal of the 2021-22 Title I Eligibility Ranking Report. The report includes 2021-22 Title I Eligibility Ranking and school participation.
 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $18,600,000.00.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.00.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) on Compensatory Education discussed the Title I Ranking process on November 18, 2020, and December 16, 2020, and reviewed the attached Title I Ranking Report with the DAC Executive Board on February 3, 2021.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). Title I, which is one of several programs under ESSA, provides funding to schools to be used to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of students who qualify for Title I funding who reside within each district’s boundary. The federal census is used to identify these children, ages 5 through 17, who are living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement. 

 

The revised proposal of the 2021-22 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2020-21 LCFF percentage of 37 percent or higher. This report includes allocations that take the Board of Education’s goal to hold schools harmless due to the difficulty in collecting information for students in poverty counts. The allocation will provide funding that ensures schools are not adversely impacted due to incomplete data collection.

Using the recently released guidance from the Federal Education Department (received January 29, 2021) and the extension of the CALPADS deadline (received January 28, 2021), staff used the following data to calculate the revised Title I allocations for 2021/22:

For schools that had an increase in their 20-21 percentage of students in poverty, the higher 20-21 percentage was used. There are 54 eligible schools that fell in this category. For schools that had a decrease in their 20/21 percentage of students in poverty, their 19/20 percentage, the higher percentage of the two was used.The flexibility to use 2019-20 data when 2020-21 accurate data is not available, due to the difficulty in collecting the LCFF forms, is unique to this year. 

We allocated to the 37% level. This benefited 6 schools that received Title I funding this year. These schools are: Gage Elementary (38%), Ericson Elementary (38%), Standley Middle School (39%), PB Middle School (37%), SD Metro Career & Tech (39%) and Henry High School (38%). According to current State guidelines schools allocated below the 40% poverty level will be required to be in a Targeted Assistance Program. However, the State also has a process to waive this requirement, therefore allowing these schools to continue their school wide programs. We will ask CDE for this waiver upon approval of the Board of Education.

The per student amount was increased from $437 to $502 per eligible student. We continue to use the formula to decrease the per student amount by $5.50 for every 1% drop in percentage count. The per student increase in allocations resulted in a total Title I allocations of $18.6 million, an increase of $3.1 million from the current year total of $15.5 million.

The total 2021-22 Title I entitlement is approximately $38,000,000.

2. Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing February 14-20, 2021, as Gun Violence Prevention Week

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing February 14-20, 2021, as Gun Violence Prevention Week, directing the Superintendent and staff to update Facts for Parents and the district website to include information about parents’ and guardians’ legal duties to safely store or disable firearms, craft and disseminate an appropriate letter to parents and guardians that explains the importance of safe gun storage and the legal obligations to protect minors from negligent gun storage, to be included in annual registration materials, and continue to work with local law enforcement agencies, health agencies, businesses, firearms owners and non-profit and advocacy organizations to increase efforts to inform parents and guardians of their duty to safely lock or securely disable their firearms.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Firearm-related deaths in the United States are the second leading cause of injury-related deaths in children between the ages of 1 and 17. Having a firearm in the home that is not safely locked or securely disabled increases firearm related injuries, deaths, and crimes, including teen suicide, creating significant local and national public health impacts. Safely locking up or disabling firearms when they are not in the owner’s immediate control is highly effective in reducing gun violence and has been found to be the single most effective gun-safety measure enacted by states and municipalities. Suicide attempts often are impulsive acts, frequently occurring within five minutes of their decision to attempt suicide, and secured firearms impede the opportunity for impulsive suicide attempts, giving suicidal individuals time to contact family, friends, and health-care professionals, and giving others time to react. Educating gun owners is a critical component of gun safety, in that an estimated 4.6 million children in the United States live in a home with unsecured firearms, and 73% of children ages 9 and under-reported knowing the location of their parent’s firearms, and 36% of those children admitted that they had handled the firearms, including children whose parents had reported their children did not know the location of their firearms. Nationally 80% of school shooters obtained their weapons from the homes of family and friends where they were not securely stored.

 

In 2019, San Diego City Attorney Mara W. Elliott introduced and the San Diego City Council enacted a Safe Storage of Firearms Ordinance that took effect September 12, 2019. The City of San Diego’s Safe Storage of Firearms law requires all residents to safely lock or store firearms inside their residences, unless the weapon is being carried on the body or in the immediate control of its owner or other authorized user. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education has joined educators and public officials nationwide seeking action on what they declared a “crisis of gun violence in our country’’ in a resolution adopted Feb. 27, 2018. Students, parents and educators recognized the importance of practicing responsible gun ownership and safety, beginning by keeping all guns and ammunition secured where children cannot access them, in a resolution adopted January 22, 2019. The San Diego Unified Board of Education have called on fellow “elected officials to commit to closing gaps in the current patchwork of regulation, including those gaps that enable felons, people convicted of domestic violence, children, those found to be a danger to themselves or others, and other prohibited persons to access firearms, and those that allow the trafficking of illegal guns." The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education now seeks to inform parents and guardians of the importance of safe storage in reducing accidental shootings of children and other firearm-related injuries and deaths. The rising threat of gun violence has struck at the heart of the cherished shared ideal of providing a future for our youth through education, stretching from tragedies like San Diego’s own Cleveland Elementary School shooting in 1993 and terrible attacks on innocents that followed including school shootings at Columbine High School in 1999, the Sandy Hook Elementary massacre in 2012, and the Parkland, Florida, mass shooting in February 2018. There have been more than 227 school shootings nationwide – including five in San Diego County -- shattering communities and leaving emotional scars.

RECOMMENDATION: Determine that the Joint Use Agreements with the City of San Diego at Cabrillo Elementary, Farb Middle, Hage Elementary, Pacific Beach Middle and School for Creative and Performing Arts are Exempt from Further Environmental Review Pursuant to California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines Section 15323 – Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings; and Approve new Joint Use Agreements (Agreements) with San Diego Unified and the City of San Diego (City) for the construction, operation, and maintenance of natural grass playfields, parking and recreation facilities for a 25-year term at the following sites:

 

  • Cabrillo Elementary (SV21-0636-99, Attachment 1)
  • Farb Middle (SV21-0637-99, Attachment 2)
  • Hage Elementary (SV21-0638-99, Attachment 3)
  • Pacific Beach Middle (SV21-0639-99, Attachment 4)

BACKGROUND: These Agreements replace expired agreements to continue joint use at the following sites:

  1. Cabrillo Elementary: 1.63 acre joint use facility with 1.22 acres of natural grass playfield, ball infield area, backstop and bleachers. This new agreement replaces expired agreement executed on January 6, 1982.
  2. Farb Middle: 4.65 acre joint use facility with 2.75 acres of natural grass playfield, ball infield area, backstops and bleachers, decomposed granite walking track, parking lot, asphalt and concrete hardcourts and drinking fountains. This new agreement replaces expired agreement executed on January 15, 1992.
  3. Hage Elementary: 3.4 acre joint use facility with 2.90 acres of natural grass playfield, ball infield area, backstops and bleachers, asphalt parking area and a concrete access drive. This new agreement replaces expired agreement executed on March 24, 1993.
  4. Pacific Beach Middle: 2.6 acre joint use facility with 2.15 acres of natural grass playfield, ball infield area, backstop and bleachers, sports field lighting and drinking fountain. This new agreement replaces expired agreement executed on July 25, 1991.
  5. School of Creative and Performing Arts: 7.8 acre joint use facility with 6.5 acres of natural grass playfield, ball infield area, backstops and bleachers, sports field lighting and an asphalt parking area. This new agreement replaces expired agreement executed on January 7, 1980.

The City will continue to operate and maintain the joint use fields for the contract term, while the District will continue to maintain landscaped areas outside of the joint use area and all perimeter fencing.

 

Usage of each joint use area is as follows, with any modifications being mutually agreed upon by District and City staff to accommodate each site’s specific needs:

  • Each day school is in session, the District has exclusive use of the area 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day until 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • Each day school is in session, the City will have use of the area up to 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day and 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • City shall have exclusive use of the area on holidays, weekends and other days when school is not in session.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: Approval of the proposed joint use agreements with the City of San Diego at Cabrillo Elementary School, Farb Middle School, Hage Elementary School, Pacific Beach Middle School, and the School of Creative and Performing Arts (Proposed Project) are subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), however, they are exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Section 15323 (Class 23) – Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings.

 

Class 23 consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose. Past history is defined as a similar kind of activity that has been occurring for at least three years and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323).

 

The Proposed Project includes the approval of new joint use agreements to replace existing, expired agreements, to support recreational uses by students and residents. These schools have operated as joint use facilities since the 1980s and 1990s. Therefore, the application of a Class 23 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may have an impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not applicable.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites and existing joint use areas. The Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project sites, when combined with operation of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Operation of the joint use areas is consistent with existing use and would not contribute to a cumulatively considerable impact. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes approval of joint use agreements for existing recreational uses. No construction activity is proposed on- or off-site that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the existing school sites and joint use areas, or surrounding area. Operation of the joint use areas would be consistent with existing use. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project sites are not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at the project site is anticipated. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

Approval of this item approves the Proposed Project as described, authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption (Attachment 6) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and approves the above mentioned Joint Use Agreements between the San Diego Unified and the City of San Diego.

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount: $25,818.68, for a new Contract not to exceed amount of $4,150,290.68. Budget String: 5553-06701-00-5456-6000-0000-67001-0000.

BACKGROUND: SELF is a Joint Powers Authority created under Title 1, Division 7, Chapter 5, Article 1, of the CA Government Code. SELF is a member-owned, statewide partnership of public educational agencies providing quality pooled programs for excess coverage. The district has self-insured retention of $350,000.00 and purchases excess insurance for costs and liabilities of $350,000.00 to $5 million from CSAC.  The district currently purchases excess liability insurance coverage for costs and liabilities in excess of $5 million from SELF.  

 

On January 1, 2020, Assembly Bill 218 officially became law. This state bill extends the statute of limitations for survivors of child abuse in California, while also adding special provisions so that all survivors may have an opportunity to pursue compensation. With Assembly Bill, 218 in-law SELF had to do an assessment for all its members based on ADA.  The premium increase is $503,174.10.  

 

The significant increase in premium and an additional assessment of $4.61 per student based on ADA for the years the district was a member of SELF reflects the insurance market's response to high dollar verdicts and settlements across the state CA schools, including traumatic brain injuries, molestation, and abuse cases.  Additionally, AB 218 eliminated the previous statute of limitations for recovery of damages in abuse cases, including repressed memory sexual abuse cases. This unpredictability has led to insurers demanding higher premiums, and/or higher retentions and in some cases, abandonment of the CA market. The SELF board accordingly raised its coverage rates to ensure sufficient funding to anticipate future losses.
