On May 15, 2018, the Board of Education adopted a Resolution in Support of Healthy Start times for All High School Students, with implementation by Fall 2020. There is overwhelming scientific evidence on the benefits of Healthy Start Times for the two-thirds of teenagers that are sleep deprived, including improvements in academic performance, behavior, and physical and emotional health. Numerous scientific organizations including the American Medical Association, American Association of Pediatrics, the Centers for Disease Control, the National Sleep Foundation and the American Psychological Association, have defined Healthy Start Times for secondary students as no earlier than 8:30 a.m. Studies have shown that in school districts that have already implemented Healthy Start Times the students have significantly increased their sleep time.

The pilot will be analyzed and full implementation planning will take place in Fall-Winter 2019-2020. The Fall 2020 schedule for all high schools' healthy start times will be finalized in February 2020.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution Recognizing May 22, 2019, as Harvey Milk Day in San Diego Unified.

 

BACKGROUND: Harvey Milk was born May 22, 1930, in Woodmere, New York.

 

Harvey Milk served in the United States Navy during the Korean War as a diving officer aboard the submarine rescue ship USS Kittiwake and was later transferred to Naval Station, San Diego to serve as a diving instructor with the rank of Lieutenant.

 

Harvey Milk was a tireless advocate of fairness for all Americans, both as a candidate for office and as a San Francisco Supervisor.

 

In 1977, Harvey Milk was elected to the San Francisco Board of Supervisors, becoming the first openly gay man to be elected to public office in California. This milestone achievement gave hope to millions of gays and lesbians across the country that a day would come when they could live their lives openly and honestly without fear of discrimination.

 

As a Supervisor, Harvey Milk worked with others to secure passage of San Francisco’s landmark Gay Rights Ordinance. This ordinance extended employment protections to gays and lesbians in San Francisco, and it became a model for anti-discrimination legislation throughout California and the nation.

 

In 1978, Harvey Milk traversed the state to campaign against Proposition 6, which was known as the Briggs Initiative. Had it passed, Proposition 6 would have required California school districts to fire openly gay and lesbian teachers solely because of their sexual orientation. The proposition was defeated in the November 1978 election in part because Harvey Milk successfully appealed to Californians’ basic sense of fairness.

 

Although Harvey Milk’s life was cut short, he was a hero and role model, and his legacy of hope, tolerance, and equality lives on.

RECOMMENDATION: :  Authorize a $.25 increase in school lunch and breakfast meals, in the 2019-2020 school year, and an additional $0.25 increase in school lunch and breakfast meals in the 2021-2022 school year.  Direct the Food Services Department to continue to annually review meal prices, costs, and make appropriate recommendations to the board.  The lunch price increase is necessary due to Title 7, Code of Federal Regulations (7 CFR), Section 210.14 (e), pricing paid lunches.  School Food Authorities are required to annually determine the paid lunch price to ensure funding for meals for disadvantaged students is not inappropriately subsidizing meals for students able to pay for full-price meals.  The price increases will impact students who purchase full-price meals only. Students who qualify for free or reduced-price meals will not be affected by the recommended meal price increases.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: a $.25 increase for paid meals equates to approximately $339,435 in additional revenue annually.  The district is required to follow the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) regulation regarding Paid Lunch Equity. The Food Services Department is financially self-sufficient and, historically, non-dependent on the district General Fund.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The Food Services Department is proposing two $.25 increases in school lunch and breakfast meals in order to comply with federal regulations.  If the price increases are not implemented the district will be out of compliance with federal regulations and be required to transfer  $ 940,696 from the general fund to the food service program over the next three years. The price increase will impact students who purchase full-price meals only. This will result in an elementary lunch meal price of $2.50 and a secondary meal price of $3.00 in 2019-2020 and in 2021-2022 elementary lunch would be $2.75 and secondary lunch would be $3.25.  Breakfast meal prices will increase to $1.25 in 2019-2020 and $1.50 in 2021-2022 for all grades.  Students who qualify for free or reduced-price meals will not be affected by the meal price increases.

 

Currently, over 130,000 school meals are served per day; on average 8,674 are paid lunch and breakfast meals. The last time lunch meal prices increased for students with a paid eligibility was May 2016 (effective for the 2016/17 school year).  The price for a paid breakfast meal has not increased since th 2008/09 school year. 

 

In addition, the district is required to follow the USDA regulation regarding Paid Lunch Equity. The USDA sets benchmarks yearly as to what a district's average paid lunch price should be. These requirements are to prevent school district food service departments from using federal reimbursement for free and reduced price meals to subsidize the cost of a paid meal. The $.25 increase will cover increased costs and will not deter students and families from eating school meals. The paid meal increase of $.25 will result in an annual cost of $45.00 ($90.00 if consuming two meals per day) in a school year, per student.

However, documented research indicates that healthy foods counteract obesity and diabetes. Research has further indicated that well-nourished students have longer attention spans and improved study habits, supporting the mission of San Diego Unified.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  This Agreement with Big Brothers Big Sisters (BBBS) of San Diego County provides mentoring support to military children whose parents may be deployed or may be facing increased stress or difficulties due to their military lifestyle.

 

Big Brothers Big Sisters shall provide two 1:1 mentoring programs for students selected at district selected elementary and middle school sites. The first BBBS Mentoring Program called, “Operation BIGS Partnership Program” provides 1:1 mentoring experiences with elementary students while playing games and connecting on topics that aid the students’ personal development, and meets for one hour per week.  The second BBBS Mentoring Program called, “Beyond School Walls” provides an afterschool 1:1 mentoring experience for middle school students that allows them to connect on topics with adult volunteers at identified corporations in San Diego. This “Beyond School Walls” Program supports the students’ personal and leadership development, and meets for two hours once per month for approximately 9-10 sessions per year.  Both programs support the student’s social and emotional needs, as well as, encourages leadership development.

 

As the nation's largest donor and volunteer supported mentoring network, BBBS strives to make meaningful, monitored matches between adult volunteers and students between the ages of 7-18.  Before being matched with students, BBBS staff will recruit, enroll, and train the adult volunteers.  The volunteers will also be screened according to both BBBS and district requirements.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.

  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway.   5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.   4. Scenic Highways. Thereafter, on September 14, 2015, the district publicly advertised Request for Qualifications (RFQ) No. PS-16-0433-02 for the prequalification of firms to participate in subsequent Request for Proposals (RFP) for the Design-Build Delivery of Solar Power Generation Systems. The four prequalified firms are: Borrego Solar Systems, Inc. (Borrego), ENGIE Services U.S., Inc. The eighth site from Round 1, Penn Elementary School, was not assigned to a firm on the aforementioned Master Agreements and was reserved for a firm that ultimately decided not to enter into an agreement with the district. Thus, on December 13, 2016, the board approved assignment of Penn Elementary School site to ENGIE for completion of the solar project under the terms of the agreement (PS-17-0512-02).

 

On September 14, 2017, the subsequent RFP (phase two) No. PS-18-0281-06 for Design-Build Solar power generation at 10 sites was issued to the four prequalified firms.

 

Three of the four prequalified firms submitted their RFP proposals (ENGIE, SunPower, Rosendin) by the closing date of October 23, 2017, and were scored by a qualified evaluation committee on the basis of best value. Eight categories were scored for a possible total of 100 points for each vendor. The committee considered (1) Overall Costs, (2) Overall Responsiveness to the Proposal, (3) Technical Expertise, (4) Component Quality, (5) Technical Proposal, (6) Management Plan, (7) Qualifications of Subcontractors, (8) Operations and Maintenance and Performance Guarantee.  The members of the evaluation committee consisted of Bernize Gil, Financial Planning and Development Analyst; Mike Clark, Project Manager; Joel Hermosillo, Project Manager; Darin Vey, Manager (TOC), Safety, Training, and Environmental Compliance; and Francisco Campuzano, Senior Project Manager. All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. The Proposed Project would be designed to ensure that all existing ADA accessible paths of travel are maintained or reconfigured as prescribed by code. Electrical transmission lines would be installed in an underground conduit and would extend from the combiner to the main electrical service in one of the existing school buildings. All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

Once complete, the solar systems at the aforementioned 10 phase two schools are estimated to produce approximately 3.8178 million kilowatt-hours (3,817,800 kWh) electricity each year that will offset CO2 greenhouse gas emissions and utility costs (approximately $775,767.00 annually based on current utility rates).

On February 8, 2019, the district received two proposals in response to the district's RFP#SV20-0001-19 from the following vendors: EDCO Disposal Corporation and Allied Waste Systems, Inc., (DBA) Republic Services of San Diego. A qualified three-member evaluation committee comprised of central office staff, physical plant operations management, and waste and recycling technical staff was charged with finding the best-proposed waste and recyclables collection and management service solution(s) for the district.  

 

All proposers were required to submit comprehensive waste and recycling diversion plans, customer references, and a baseline service fixed-price proposal.
