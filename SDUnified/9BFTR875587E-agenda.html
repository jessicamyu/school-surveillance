RECOMMENDATION: Superintendent Marten will provide a status report on the 2014-15 instructional calendar.  This item is for information only.  The superintendent will discuss her plan to bring forward 2014-15 instructional calendars for board consideration and action later in the month which recommend no change to the current traditional and year round school schedules.

 

On June 18, 2013, the board directed staff to conduct an in-depth review of moving the district to a modified common calendar for 2014-15 or beyond, and also directed staff in the Government Relations Department to pursue potential funding from the Department of Defense, relating to military student family deployments and a common calendar. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: If the instructional calendars were to change to a common modified calendar, the district's Finance Division estimates a one-time cost of $12 million to move all school-based employees to a July 1-June 30 fiscal year. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  If any changes are made to the instructional calendars, employee collective bargaining contracts require that the district notify the San Diego Teachers Association, Administrators Association of San Diego, California School Employees Association, and Police Officers Association of any change to the instructional calendar, and bargain those changes if requested by the organizations. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Any change to the calendar configurations will require substantial community engagement and information, including enough planning time for families and community organizations that provide programs for students.

 

The board directed the Calendar Committee to prepare a recommendation for a common instructional calendar beginning with the 2014-15 school year or beyond and further directed that spring break coincide with the community college calendar, which starts around March 31.  The School District Calendar Committee met eight times between March and October 2013, to develop a common instructional calendar recommendation to the board (attached).  The District Calendar Committee continues to recommend that it would be desirable and cost efficient to have all district schools on one common calendar, preferably one that starts the last week in August to better align with neighboring school districts in the county. Currently, 151 schools (includes special education community day schools and all high schools) are on a traditional calendar and 54 schools (includes four middle schools and 49 elementary schools) are on a single track year-round calendar.

 

San Diego Unified implemented a year-round calendar for some schools in the late 80s-early 90s as an instructional strategy to meet the needs of educationally disadvantaged students and to more efficiently utilize overcrowded school facilities.  In response to an increase in student absences, with a corresponding drop in average daily attendance revenue, in year-round schools, the district put all schools on a common start date, the day after Labor Day in September 2000.  Intersession offerings at year-round schools were substantially reduced or eliminated by budget cuts in 2007 and 2008.  Limited intersession and summer school programs are now offered at select schools.

RECOMMENDATION: This is a First Reading of the proposed 2014–15 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district. This report presents historical information as well as feedback from the District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education for board consideration in determining the 2014-15 Title I eligibility ranking and school participation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The DAC had preliminary discussion of the Title I ranking student counts on November 20, 2013, and reviewed the proposed site allocations at its meeting on December 18, 2013.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources. To that end, although the 2014-15 Title I district allocation is anticipated to be 11 percent less than in 2013-14, the proposed plan allocates the same overall amount to schools in 2014-15, that they collectively received in 2013-14.

 

The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). The DAC, at its November 20, 2013 meeting, was provided a preliminary report showing student counts on which funding allocations would be based (actual funding amounts were not available). At the December 18, 2013 meeting, the DAC discussed a report that reflected the status quo—both in method and in total Title I funding allocated to sites. An action at the meeting established a subcommittee to review the district's Title I spending plan. The subcommittee met in early January to develop recommendations for the board and for the DAC general body. The DAC will again review the Title I Ranking method at its January 15, 2014 meeting.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the 2013-14 Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA) and Categorical Budget Summary for Logan Elementary.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

 

BACKGROUND: As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA).

 

Additionally, Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their SMART goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

The local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2013–14 SPSA and categorical budget summary for Logan Elementary. All other 176 schools' 2013-14 SPSAs were approved by the board on December 10, 2013.

A copy of the SPSA, which includes the budget summary and required, signed assurances page, is available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

BACKGROUND: On August 12, 2013, Governor Jerry Brown signed Assembly Bill 1266 into law.  AB 1266 provides transgender students have a right to access sex-segregated facilities and activities consistent with their gender identity.  The bill amends California Education Code section 221.5 to include, at subdivision (f):

 

A pupil shall be permitted to participate in sex-segregated school programs and activities, including athletic teams and competitions, and use facilities consistent with his or her gender identity, irrespective of the gender listed on the pupil’s records.

 

California is the first state to enact legislation explicitly allowing students to access sex-segregated facilities and activities according to their gender identity.  Sex-segregated facilities and activities can include any program or location designate as “males only” or “females only.”  For school districts, these often include locker rooms, restrooms, health classes or athletic teams.

 

Education Code section 200 et seq. already prohibits gender identity discrimination against students in educational programs and activities.  AB 1266 clarifies that transgender students may access sex-segregated facilities in a manner consistent with their gender identity.

 

As an initial step in updating district procedures and practices consistent with AB 1266, revisions to Board Policies are necessary to include references to gender, gender identity and gender expression so that they are consistent with Education Code section 200 et seq.
