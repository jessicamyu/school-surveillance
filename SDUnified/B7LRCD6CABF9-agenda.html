RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution recognizing October 2019, as National Bullying Prevention Month in San Diego Unified and use this opportunity to highlight our ongoing commitment to promote an improved school environment, enforce positive interactions between students, and raise awareness for teachers if a student is being bullied.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The National Bullying Prevention Month campaign was founded in 2006.

 

San Diego Unified School District Vision 2020 calls for all schools to be safe, inclusive neighborhood learning centers, and each quality neighborhood school to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.”

 

School district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing updated and revised protocols and procedures, participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved.

 

San Diego Unified has partnered with the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and their No Place for Hate program which provides participating schools with anti-bias and anti-bullying resources to help create sustainable change that leads to an improved school environment.

 

San Diego Unified has implemented programming to support schools in building positive school climates and a culture of upstanders through "Ally Action,” bullying prevention efforts,  and student-led capacity building for peers as well as school site staff.

 

The San Diego Unified Council of ASB Presidents fully supports October as National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within the month and throughout the year. Students’ participation in anti-bullying activities provide opportunities for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and find solace in the knowledge that others have been in similar situations.
  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying which people may experience including, but not limited to, cyber, verbal, physical, and exclusion; on the basis of actual or perceived race, religion, national origin, disability, sexuality, gender identity and/or socio-economic status.
  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus and the consequences of these actions.
  • Support students to come up with solutions to stop bullying and enforce positive interactions between students.
  • Engage teachers and parents in raising awareness if a student is being bullied.
  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying.
  • Help empower students in the recognition of the role everyone plays in addressing and reducing bullying with the goal to eradicate bullying on all school campuses.

San Diego Unified School District offers lesson plans, video resources, and print resources through the Digital Cultural Awareness Center at culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com and information about Ally Action Programming, bullying prevention, and bullying intervention at youthadvocacy.us.

 

All elementary, middle, and high schools shall coordinate anti-bullying activities at the beginning of each new school year to be held in the month of October, during a week or days of their choosing.

BACKGROUND: In 1988, Filipino American History Month was first proclaimed by the 103rd Congress of the United States through House Resolution 155.

 

The Filipino American community is the second largest Asian American group in the United States with a population of approximately 3,100,000 people and over 5,900 students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

Filipino Americans have contributed greatly to the fine arts, music, dance, literature, business, journalism, education, science, technology, government, politics, fashion, health care, the armed forces, and many other fields that enrich the landscape of the country.

 

Efforts should continue to promote Filipino American history and culture, because the roles of Filipino Americans and other people of color have been overlooked in the writing, teaching, and learning of United States history.

 

In September 1988, Bell Junior High School was the largest junior high school west of the Mississippi, and had the largest concentration of Filipino students in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

In 1989, the Filipino community rallied to advocate the offering of Filipino language classes at Morse High School and Bell Junior High School.

 

Filipino language classes are now offered at Challenger Middle School and Mira Mesa High School as well.

 

Filipino Heritage Month is a time of reflection and remembrance, and provides an opportunity for all people to learn and appreciate Filipino Americans’ historic contributions.

BACKGROUND:  There has been a tremendous amount of media coverage about the ethnic studies graduation requirement. The San Diego Unified School District believes in the value of ethnic studies, because we believe all students have the right to see themselves reflected in the curriculum. We strongly support the implementation of a statewide ethnic studies curriculum in California. BACKGROUND: As with all large children-serving institutions, the district must always challenge itself to develop the most effective strategies and structures possible to protect every student from all forms of child abuse, including sexual abuse and harassment. Protecting district students from sexual harassment and abuse requires the district to work effectively with a variety of public agencies and community-based organizations.   • Reviewing the district's external relationships with other agencies involved in the protection of children from sexual harassment and abuse, and comparing those efforts to the aforementioned practices.
     

  • Recommending to the board necessary actions to create a comprehensive multi-agency cooperation protocol for the district regarding the reporting, response, and prevention of sexual harassment and abuse of district students.

District and City staff have negotiated the new lease with the following provisions:

  • 99-year lease term to commence upon full execution of the lease.
  • Execution of this new lease will terminate the 1974 lease.
  • Lease consideration to be through an exchange of two District-owned properties known as Camp Elliott #3 (APN 373-030-03, 11.73 acres) and an unused portion of Logan-Memorial Preparatory Campus (APN 545-591-01, 0.42 acres) currently utilized by City for the Logan Memorial Pool and Recreation Center. District shall convey its fee title interest in these properties to the City after the Board approves the Resolution of Intent to Exchange District Owned Property as Consideration for Lease with City of San Diego For San Diego High School Property and full execution of this Lease.

CEQA ANALYSIS: Approval of a new lease for a 99-year term and the exchange of fee title for the Camp Elliott #3 property and a portion of Logan-Memorial Preparatory Campus is considered a project under CEQA and is subject to the requirements of the Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000, et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et seq., as amended). However, the Proposed Project is exempt from further environmental analysis consistent with CEQA Guidelines Section 15301 – Existing Facilities (Class 1). Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the Proposed Project involves negligible or no expansion of use.

 

The proposed 99-year lease replaces the existing lease between the District and City for use of the San Diego High School property by the District to operate San Diego High School. The proposed lease would facilitate the continued operation of San Diego High School and would not result in the expansion of use of the High School. Additionally, the exchange of fee title for the Camp Elliott #3 property and a portion of Logan-Memorial Preparatory Campus from the District to the City would not result in a change in land use, or the expansion of use, when compared with existing conditions. Therefore, it can be determined with certainty that there is no possibility that the new 99-year lease and the exchange of property would result in a significant environmental effect, and the application of a Class 1 exemption is appropriate.
