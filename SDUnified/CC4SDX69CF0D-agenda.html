RECOMMENDATION: School Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2022. The 16 plans for SDUSD's CSI sites are included in this board item.  Also, three additional non-CSI sites being reviewed during the Federal Programs Monitoring (FPM) Review are also included so that the reviewers will have access to the most recent, Board approved plan during the review scheduled for December 5-16, 2022.  We recommend approval of the 19 plans.  The remainder of the district's site plans will be included on the January 24, 2023, Board of Education Agenda.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) received information on the timeline and components of developing the SPSA at their October 20, 2021, meeting.  In addition, site principals received training regarding specific plan requirements on April 21,2022 and September 7, 2022. They then consulted with their school's educational partners through the School Site Council to develop these plans. In addition to these trainings, principals were provided one-on-one support by the Strategic Planning for Student Achievement Department (SPSA Dept) beginning shortly after the training occurred, and this support continued through the submission of the plans.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. SPSAs are created under this part may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeed Act (public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.

 

The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.

Additionally, SPSA Dept staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, approved by their SSCs, and submitted the plan to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the SPSA Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements. SPSA staff also ensured that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment. The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA annually.

Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires state educational agencies to determine school eligibility for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI). Local educational agencies (LEAs) with schools that meet the criteria for CSI must partner with stakeholders to locally develop and implement a plan to improve student outcomes. The county office of education (COE) also plays a role in providing technical assistance and support to LEAs within its county that serve schools that meet the criteria for CSI. CSI schools are identified by the state education agency as one of the lowest-performing schools in the state based mostly on students’ academic performance using the California Dashboard. Also, any high school with a graduation rate of 66 percent or less is a CSI school.

Board approved SPSAs for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2022. This report brings forward for board approval the 16 2022-23 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for the 16 identified CSI schools, and three additional non-CSI sites being reviewed during the Federal Programs Monitoring (FPM) Review are also included so that the reviewers will have access to the most recent plan during the review scheduled for December 5-16, 2022. Electronic copies of each 2022-23 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item, and hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103. SPSAs from all remaining district schools will be presented to the board on January 24, 2023.

BACKGROUND: On August 3, 1990, George H. W. Bush, President of the United States, declared the month of November as National Native American Heritage Month. The President authorized and called upon federal, state, and local governments, groups and organizations, and the people of the United States, to observe November with appropriate programs, ceremonies, and activities. The contributions of Native Americans to this nations government, history, and culture are as numerous and varied as the tribes themselves. San Diego County has 18 different Native American Indian reservations, more than any other county in the United States. Approximately 20,000 Native Americans live in San Diego County, most coming from four local tribesKumeyaay/Diegueo, Luiseo, Cupeo, and Cahuilla. The San Diego Unified School District, through its Ethnic Studies program and the Family Engagement Department, as well as through all offices and school sites, promotes equity for all students and will continue to provide tutoring, career guidance, mental health support, cultural awareness, leadership development, and cultural enrichment programs for Native American students. The commemorative month provides a platform for Native Americans to share their culture, traditions, music, crafts, dance, and ways and concepts of life. The Indian Education Program and the Native American Indian community have made and continue to provide significant and important contributions to the community and the nation as a whole.
