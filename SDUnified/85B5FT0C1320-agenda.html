RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution supporting the Local Control of Local Classrooms Funding Act and urge all registered voters in the community to sign the petition in order for the initiative to qualify for placement on the statewide ballot in November 2010.

FISCAL IMPACT: None at this time. If the Local Control of Local Classrooms Funding Act initiative is placed on the statewide ballot in November 2010, and is approved by the voters, the California Constitution would be amended so that parcel tax measures for local school districts would be approved by 55 percent of the qualified electors. If a parcel tax were approved by the local San Diego Unified community, the school district would benefit from the revenue generated.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: Reductions in the State of California General Fund revenue have led to significantly reduced school funding. The State General Fund revenue is unstable and erratic resulting in unpredictable funding levels for school districts. If a local parcel tax measure is approved by the voters, the revenue from the parcel tax can provide a stable, predictable source of school revenue. Placement of the Local Control of Local Classrooms Funding Act on the November 2010 ballot, and subsequent approval of the initiative in November 2010, would allow local school districts to pass a parcel tax measure with support by 55 percent of the voters. The local community could directly support their schools with local resources to fund important educational programs and initiatives including, but not limited to, attracting and retaining qualified teachers, retaining smaller class sizes, supporting strong core academic programs, and maintaining art, music, physical education, athletics, and student support programs.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the 2010-2011Title I Eligibility Ranking for the San Diego Unified School District. This report presents four scenarios for the 2010-11 Title I eligibility ranking and school participation.

FISCAL IMPACT: Approximately $23,050,000.00, Title I Entitlement.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: $24,000,000.00

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001, and signed by President Bush on January 8, 2002, for implementation on July 1, 2002. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty.

Title I includes legislation that requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The Federal Census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty that are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

The educational and administrative leadership of the district are challenged with designing a plan that meets strict legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources.

Key factors that affect the projected reduction in school allocations are:
  • Projected addition of 25 schools to Program Improvement status
  • Student enrollment; enrollment declined by 617 students from 133,097 in 2008-09 to 132,480 in the 2009-10 school year
  • Free and Reduced lunch students increased over prior year by 1.4 percent from 58.53 percent in 2008-09 to 59.96 percent in 2009-10
  • Impact of entering district Program Improvement

The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required bvy Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education members discussed several Title I Ranking scenarios at the meeting on November 18, 2009, and voted on these scenarios at the December 16, 2009 meeting.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the Johnson Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) Magnet reconfiguration from K-6 to K-5.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Possible loss of two leave replacement teachers and one permanent teacher.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

BACKGROUND: Johnson Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) Magnet emphasizes engineering, math, and science in a rich, inquiry-oriented learning environment that empowers all students to become academically literate individuals who understand how science, engineering, and mathematics work together to affect the world around them. Students are taught through 21st century technology advancements with critical thinking and problem-solving skills in all core curricular areas. As a NASA Explorer school, the program includes space exploration in core curriculum areas and laboratory studies.

Johnson fifth grade students interested in pursuing sixth grade course work in a middle school setting already choose to leave at the end of fifth grade. A large number of Johnson students continue at Johnson and promote at sixth grade. Students at both fifth and sixth grade make a variety of school choices at the end of their Johnson career. Johnson’s neighborhood middle school is Mann Middle School. When applications are received by February 15, Johnson students receive continuity priority application status to attend Millennial Tech Middle (MTM), a dedicated STEM magnet. Johnson students also choose other magnet, Voluntary Enrollment Exchange Program (VEEP), School Choice and Program Improvement School Choice (PISC) options and neighboring charters.

Capacity at Millennial Tech Middle is limited. There are a total of 180 to 200 seats per grade level for at total capacity of up to 600 students. Millennial Tech Middle is successfully recruiting students for the sixth grade class. Very few students attending MTM leave during their middle school career, therefore very few (5 to 20) seats, are available at seventh grade for incoming students. Johnson parents respect the success of MTM and want to be sure that all Johnson students interested in continuing their education with an emphasis in STEM have that opportunity.

At this time, Johnson fifth grade students interested in continuing at Johnson for sixth grade receive continuity priority application status for seventh grade seats at MTM when are made following School Choice application deadlines. Available seats at MTM may not allow all students who apply with continuity to be offered enrollment at seventh grade. Johnson has approximately 60 sixth grade students each year and classroom space at Millennial is limited.

The STEM magnet schools’ articulation plan includes Johnson STEM Magnet (K-5) and Webster Science and Research Magnet (K-6) to Millennial Tech Middle School, a dedicated magnet with continuity priority application (6-8), to a series of high school options that includes Lincoln Center for Science and Engineering (9-12). The Lincoln High School Cluster has embraced the STEM education opportunities. Although not a magnet school, students who live in the Lincoln boundary may attend the Lincoln Center for Science and Engineering at ninth grade.

Following board approval, the Enrollment Options and Choice office and Johnson Elementary School staffs will contact each of the fifth grade families to determine school preferences for Grade 6. The families have been provided an extension of the regular School Choice deadline and the office will work to assist the families with their school preferences. We have also contacted and extended the deadline to sixth grade families because of the limited number of seats available at MTM.

Staff and Community Engagement: Johnson staff expressed concern regarding the limited number of seats available for their students at MTM. They voted as a group to support the reconfiguration to K-5. Three community meetings were held to hear comments regarding the configuration. The majority of parents and community members agreed that the reconfiguration will benefit Johnson students. Parents were informed that if this change occurs there will be promotion exercises for both fifth and sixth grades this year.

Facilities Implications: At present, 68 sixth grade students are projected to attend Johnson in 2010-11. If the school is reconfigured to Grades K-5, it is likely that a large number of Johnson students may elect to apply to attend MTM, which enrolls a sixth grade class of 180-200 students. Johnson STEM fifth grade students have continuity priority to attend MTM STEM magnet and are selected first when they apply by the School Choice deadline of February 15. The district middle school that serves the Johnson area is Mann. Johnson plans to continue to recruit primary grade students to maintain school capacity at current enrollment.

Transportation is offered to residents of the Johnson attendance boundary who live more than 2.5 miles from Mann and have a 15 student ridership. This transportation is due to the Gompers conversion to a charter school, therefore Mann became the district neighborhood middle school. Students from the Johnson area are also selecting to attend Pacific Beach and Lewis middle schools through the district’s VEEP program, and also neighboring charter schools such as Gompers Charter Middle School, Keiller Leadership Academy, and O’Farrell Community School.

BACKGROUND: One of the largest physical assets within district’s physical plant is asphalt pavement. The system includes playgrounds, parking lots, driveways, walkways, drainage swales and ramps totaling more than 21 million square feet. The current replacement value of the system exceeds $100 million. This physical asset must be maintained to minimize the cost of ownership. Asphalt deteriorates from exposure to weather (heat, rain, etc.) mechanical wear (such as dirt and grit, vehicle and foot traffic) and environmental impacts (rodents, fluids from vehicles, underlying soil failures). This maintenance plan addresses these conditions and extends the asset service life, maximizing return on investment in a cost effective manner.

Maintenance Strategy
Physical Plant Operations (PPO) maintains and repairs the district’s asphalt systems utilizing in-house and contract resources. Primary aspects of the asphalt maintenance program include: planned maintenance (PM) and reactive maintenance (site-generated repair orders). Planned maintenance (PM) is the most predictable and cost-effective method of maintaining a usable and appropriate surface for students and staff. PPO supports a four-year maintenance cycle that includes some combination of asphalt repair, slurry seal and line striping for every district asset. Reactive maintenance consists mainly of small, isolated asphalt repairs and safety concerns such as potholes and cracks. Each year the planned maintenance work of PPO is evaluated for updates and improvements. After analysis of the previous year’s operations and other variables, specific crews will be assigned to seal coat work and the non-routine asphalt work. With this separation, a seal coat crew of three to four professional district staff and seal coat equipment can re-seal 25 percent of the district each year, visiting every district site in a four year cycle. The cost of sealing the asphalt is approximately 4 percent of the cost of removal and replacement and is by far the most economical way to preserve and maximize the life cycle of this district asset. There will be in-house crews working to prepare and repair damaged areas prior to seal coat at each of the sites as required. Ramona Paving will be contracted to accomplish projects where the district does not have current or foreseeable capacity. For fiscal year 2009-10, the value of the work to be accomplished by contracting is projected to be $1.7 million (see Attachment A). By separating the project work into seal coat and repair, to better utilize in-house capacity, we are projecting a need for only $961,000 of contract asphalt repair work in fiscal year 2010-11 (Attachment B).

Workload
California Public Contracting Code 20114 requires the district to contract projects that exceed 750 labor hours or $21,000 in material costs (Force Account). IDIQ contracts provide predictable delivery and costs for projects. This contract will only be utilized after the in-house forces are at capacity or the scope of the project exceeds force account requirements. The Asphalt Service Schedule (Attachment B) represents 5.2 million square feet of asphalt to be serviced within the next year, at a cost of $2.3 million. This schedule fully utilizes PPO crews, executing $1.3 million of asphalt maintenance.

Summary
This contract was publicly bid and awarded to Ramona Paving and Construction Corp. Inc., on April 14, 2009, providing asphalt repairs, paving and seal coating at various district sites on an as-needed basis (IDIQ). Contract duration is one year, with option to renew for an additional two, one-year terms (for a total of three years) with the initial term completion of April 14, 2010. This contract is for work to be performed on an as-needed basis, with individual projects or tasks ranging anywhere from $10,000 to $250,000 in value. PPO uses this contract to complete projects that it cannot perform in-house due to size (exceeding the district’s force account) or scheduling conflicts.

Ramona Paving and Construction Corp, Inc. is an established contractor, is qualified to perform this work, and continues to provide quality services to the district.

Per Public Contract Code section 20111(b) a school district is required to competitively bid a public works project valued at $15,000 or more. Per Public Contract Code Section 20114, the district must contract this work because it exceeds the force account limitations for performing the work with in-house forces.

Since the district desires to continue levying school developer fees, there is a need to obtain a new justification report.

To provide the necessary analysis and report, the district requested proposals from three firms to perform the analysis and justification report. Two firms responded with their proposals and a third declined the invitation to propose. Of the two proposing firms, Schoolhouse Services is the best qualified in this specialized area and the cost for the services is less than the proposal received from the second firm.
