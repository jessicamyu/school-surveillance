4. Resolution Calling for Full and Fair Funding of California's Public Schools (Beiser/McQuary)
Meeting
Jan 23, 2018 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of calling for full and fair funding of California's public schools at the national average or higher by the year 2020, and at a level that is equal to or above the average of the top 10 states nationally by 2025 and to maintain, at a minimum, this level of funding until otherwise decreed.

 

BACKGROUND: California has the sixth largest economy in the world, and the largest Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of any state in the nation.

 

Despite California’s leadership in the global economy, the state falls in the nation’s bottom quintile on nearly every measure of public K-12 school funding and school staffing.

 

California ranks 45th nationally in the percentage of taxable income spent on education, 41st in per-pupil funding, 45th in pupil–teacher ratios and 48th in pupil–staff ratios.

 

K-12 school funding has not substantially increased, on an inflation-adjusted basis, for more than a decade.

 

Under the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF), state funding for K-12 schools has only this year recently returned to levels predating the Great Recession of 2007.

 

The modest revenue increases since the implementation of LCFF have been eroded by rapidly increasing costs for health care, pensions, transportation and utilities.

 

58 percent of California’s public school students are eligible for free and reduced-price lunch — 13 percent above the national average — and 23 percent of California students are English learners, more than twice the national average.

 

California’s investment in public schools is out of alignment with its wealth, its ambi­tions, its demographics and the demands of a 21st-century education.

 

In 2007, a bipartisan group of California leaders commissioned a report titled Getting Down to Facts, which stated it would take an additional $17 billion annually to meet the State Board of Education achievement targets for K-12 schools.

 

In 2016, a California School Boards Association (CSBA) report, California’s Challenge: Adequately Funding Education in the 21st Century, updated the Getting Down to Facts data and deter­mined that, adjusting for inflation, an additional $22 billion to $40 billion annually would be required to provide all public school students with access to a high-quality education.

 

California funds schools at roughly $1,961 per student less than the national average, which translates to approximately $3,462 per student when adjusted for California being a high-cost state.

 

California trails the average of the top 10 states by almost $7,000 in per-pupil funding.

 

In Robles-Wong v. State of California, a group of plaintiffs led by CSBA argued that California’s school funding system violated Article IX of the State Constitution by denying all students access to an education that prepares them for economic security and full participation in our democratic institutions.

 

The California Supreme Court declined to hear the case by a 4-3 margin, prompting Justice Goodwin H. Liu to write: “It is regrettable that this court, having recognized education as a fundamental right in a landmark decision 45 years ago Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584], should now decline to address the substantive meaning of that right.”

 

In order to prepare our students for participation in a democratic society and an increasingly competitive, technology-driven global economy, California must fund schools at a level sufficient to support student success.

 

Despite its vast wealth, California has consistently underfunded public education while widening its scope, adding new requirements and raising standards without providing appropriate resources to prepare all students for college, career and civic life.

 

If California is to close opportunity and achievement gaps and create a public school system that offers consistently high levels of education, the State must provide schools with the resources to meet the needs of their specific populations.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2018-19 Title I Eligibility Ranking - Scenario 1 ($5.50 decrement). This report presents 2018-19 Title I Eligibility Ranking and school participation. There are two schools, Ericson Elementary and Patrick Henry High School, that no longer meet the forty percent eligibility threshold. Staff recommend these two schools be grandfathered for the 2018-19 school year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.00.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.00

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) discussed the Title I Ranking at the November 15, 2017, December 13, 2017, and January 17, 2018 meetings. At the January 17, 2018 meeting, a vote was taken and approved with 58 schools represented to recommend Scenario 3 ($4.50 decrement). 

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). Title I, one of several programs under ESSA, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the district and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of "children in poverty" residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The proposed 2018-19 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2017-18 meal eligibility percentage of 40 percent or higher and the two additional schools recommended to be grandfathered. There are also 14 schools which were grandfathered last year and are no longer eligible to be grandfathered. These schools include Barnard Elementary, Doyle Elementary, Green Elementary, Hage Elementary, Hickman Elementary, Loma Portal Elementary, McKinley Elementary, Salk Elementary, Correia Middle, Dana Middle, Pershing Middle, Standley Middle, Point Loma High and University City High Schools. The total 2017-18 Title I Entitlement is $38,541,900.00.

BACKGROUND: The Commercial Street project began with the 2002 Board's direction to District staff to create a plan for development of the former Maintenance and Operations Center at Commercial Street and Irving Avenue ("Commercial Street Property") which would provide income to the District and transform an unused and environmentally compromised site into an asset to the community.

 

On July 19, 2012, the District entered into a Ground Lease with COMM22 Family Housing, LP (SV-15-0709-06); subsequently, two amendments to this Ground Lease were approved by the board on November 18, 2014 and May 24, 2016.

The San Diego Housing Commission and the City of San Diego have reviewed and approved the Lease Riders.

 

The Lease Riders do not change the Ground Lease terms, nor do they impose any obligation on the District for payment of the loan or require an encumbrance to be recorded against the property. The execution of these TCAC Lease Riders carry out existing obligations of the District in accordance with the previously approved Ground Leases.
