BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue our improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting district wide, we are implementing a new process streamlining our Independent Contractor Agreements. Our goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

BACKGROUND:  During the 2008-09 school year, the district began working with Mid-continent Research for Education and Learning (McREL) to develop a new principal job performance evaluation process and rubric.  McREL worked with key district staff to define evaluation component standards, identified and convened an oversight task force, developed a rubric for evaluating site leaders, pilot-tested the evaluation processes and materials, revised materials based on the pilot test results, field tested the revised materials, conducted a validation study based on the field test and prepared final revisions of the evaluation instruments for consideration and adoption by the school board.


During the 2009-10 school year, the district contracted with McREL to provide professional development for administrators in the Balanced Leadership model to further administrators' acquisition of knowledge and skill development related to leadership, purposeful community, magnitude of change and focus of change. This training was provided to administrators in alignment with the Tentative Agreement (TA) with the certificated unit of the Administrators Association of San Diego (AASD), and was adopted by the school board on November 10, 2009. The TA outlined the principal evaluation process using the McREL evaluation instrument developed during the 2008-09 school year, and it included the provision to create a system to support the satisfactory performance of all principals.


A delay in implementation of the McREL evaluation tool occurred during the 2010-11 and 2011-12 school years, during which time the district engaged in negotiations with AASD over certain contract provisions.  With the school board's adoption of the Tentative Agreement with the AASD Certificated Unit on September 25, 2012, implementation of the McREL evaluation tool began with the 2012-13 school year.  During the delay period, some principals that had originally been trained in 2009-10, had changed school sites, left the district, or retired. Training was provided on September 25-27, 2012, as a refresher to those administrators that had participated in the 2009-10 training, to new administrators who had since been appointed, and to Area Superintendents who are responsible for carrying out the McREL evaluations.

RECOMMENDATION:  The purpose of this agenda item is to provide a quick overview of the Governor's assertions regarding the 2013-14 State Budget.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  $120 million

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  To be determined.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The Governor's Budget Proposals for 2013-14 reflect the fact that the state economy is no longer shrinking and that Proposition 30 has taken the pressure off the state to make even larger budget reductions.  The Governor proposes higher funding for K-12 education, community colleges, and higher education.  He also uses a very aggressive longer term economic forecast to predict dramatic future growth in education funding.

 

The Governor clearly intends to increase per-student funding for the first time in more than five years.  But the proposal for a new distribution system makes it difficult to assess how the new dollars will be applied.  The Weighted Student Formula of last year is gone and reprised as the Local Control Funding Formula.  In addition to a name change, the new distribution system benefits from improvements driven by both the vitriolic reception afforded last year's plan as well as the positive outreach of the Administration to identify and solve problems.

 

Finally, this budget proposes the most dramatic change in school financing since the implementation of Senate Bill (SB) 90 and the implementation of revenue limits in 1972.  The plan is complex and embryonic, and many details are still emerging.  The Governor's proposals have not yet been through a single legislative committee, so there is no way to accurately forecast its effect.  The Governor's plan is accompanied by an optimistic estimate of the resources that will be devoted to public education in the future.  His plan succeeds if and only if those resources materialize.

 

As a result, staff offers a reminder that the Governor's Budget marks the beginning of the budget process - not the end.
