A Strategic Process for Creating Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood:  Quality School Indicator 4 - Access to a Broad and Challenging Curriculum:

 

Access to a broad and challenging curriculum may be best-defined as the knowledge; skills and dispositions students need to be fully prepared to enter college as well as a career of their choice. Career choices may include long-term goals of advanced college degrees or entering the workforce during and immediately after high school. San Diego Unified must prepare ALL students to be successful in taking advantage of multiple opportunities after high school.

 

Currently, there is disparity among San Diego Unified schools in terms of college preparatory courses offered and available to students. While all San Diego Unified middle and high schools offer all courses required for eligibility to the University of California (UC) and California State University (CSU) systems, better known as UC “a-g” subject area courses, the variety and number of sections of those course offerings differ significantly from school to school. Further, as the requirements for high school graduation, college entrance and career readiness increase, students will have less choice in the courses available in their areas of interest.

 

The district must be committed to address and resolve these issues to ensure that all students have access to challenging courses and courses that meet their interests.

 

Area 3 would like to recognize Dr. Matt Fallon, Principal at Wangenheim Middle School for the outstanding work he has accomplished in strengthening the science program and preparing students for a future in the STEM world of work.

  • Wangenheim has made an instructional shift to an inquiry based model for science instruction. The scientific process is embedded in each lesson/unit. Students are more successful in building the high order thinking skills of analysis, synthesis, and writing using evidence.
     
  • Working with the Rigorous Design team at the district this year, the science department has worked more specially with learning the new standards and beginning to implement them into the current curriculum.
     
  • Eighth graders enrolled in Advanced Science always participate in the Science Fair, but this year we doubled the enrollment in Advanced Science and provided additional supports for students who might not have succeeded in previous years.
     
  • The department developed and implemented the Science Olympiad with the seventh grade students, each teacher chose a specific Olympiad challenge, recruited students and worked with them after school for several months in preparation.  At the competition, we had a great first year experience and the teachers came back excited and full of ideas for the next year.
     
  • We hosted our first Family Science Night with participation from many community groups including Steven Birch Aquarium, San Diego Astronomers Association, and CalPirg.  The evening was a success and will now be an annual event for Wangenheim.
     
  • Using the three-year STEM grant to participate in the SeaPerch project designed by MIT. It is an innovative underwater robotics program that equips teachers and students with the resources they need to build an underwater Remotely Operated Vehicle (ROV).  Throughout this project, students will learn engineering concepts, problem solving, teamwork, and technical applications.  Students will build models and compete in various events, both locally and nationally.
     
  • Outside of the Science Department we have the Rubik's Cube Club which is advised by two math teachers.  One of the math teachers along with a social studies teacher also advise the Academic League which is indirectly supportive of engagement with science.

BACKGROUND: The district does not have sufficient staff available to provide occupational therapy services to district students with identified or suspected special needs as specified in their Individualized Education Program (IEP) and as mandated under state and federal special education laws.  Therefore, in order to establish a sufficient pool of qualified providers of occupational therapy services to meet the needs of these students, and in order to stimulate competition amongst providers of occupational therapy providers for the district's occupational therapy business, the district released a Request for Proposals (RFP) for school-based occupational therapy services.  The district received 36 proposals from 360 Degree Customer Services, Advanced Medical Personnel, Advantage on Call Therapy, AlphaVista Services, Ardor Health, Care Stat Medical Services, Coast to Coast Therapy, Cross Country Staffing, EBS Healthcare Services, Invo Healthcare, Jackson Therapy, Learn-It Systems, MTX Therapy, myTherapyCompany, Pioneer Healthcare, Prime Healthcare, Pro Care Therapy, Progressus Therapy, ProvidaStaff, Soliant Healthcare, StaffRehab, Staffing Options and Solutions, Sunbelt Staffing, Supplemental Healthcare, Therapists Unlimited, and Therastaff.  Proposals from Mediscan and KIDS Therapy & Associates were also received after the proposal due date and time and, therefore, were not considered for evaluation.

 

An Evaluation Committee evaluated all proposals received in accordance with the criteria set forth in the RFP, including each proposer's understanding of the needs of the district and its ability to perform the scope of work in accordance with the requirements and specifications in the RFP, and each proposer's qualifications, experience, expertise, references and price proposal.  The Evaluation Committee ranked the twenty-six proposals received and recommends that agreements with Advantage on Call Therapy, Coast to Coast Therapy, EBS Healthcare, myTherapyCompany, Pioneer Healthcare Services, Progressus Therapy and Therapists Unlimited, a Division of CareerStaff Unlimited Inc. be recommended for approval to provide the required occupational therapy services.  Prices from each proposer are fixed for the entire two-year term of the respective proposer's agreement.

 

California Government Code 53060 grants the district the authority to contract with and employ persons for the furnishing to the district of special services if such persons are specially trained, experienced and competent to perform the special services required.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve panel removal agreement with UPC Solar SDS, LLC to authorize the removal of solar panels and related equipment at four district sites and terminate existing solar purchase power agreements for those four district sites.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  $1,350 for district maintenance costs.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  $1,000 for Solar Systems Management.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  Small increase in maintenance work orders.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  This current panel removal agreement is necessary to address the identified fire hazards and other defects in the solar panels installed at Ericson, Standley, Tierrasanta, and Walker. 

 

In 2005-07, the district entered into various power purchase agreements (PPA’s) at 28 sites that allowed it to receive sustainable power from the solar panels, but also received new roofs on the buildings during the solar system installation. The fundamental benefit of these agreements was that the new roofs that would belong to the district upon the termination or expiration of the 20-year agreements.

 

In February 2012, Solar Integrated Technology (SIT), the original solar provider, filed for bankruptcy.  It was then discovered that the solar panels were defective and may pose serious fire hazards.

 

On June 28, 2012, the board approved the removal of the failed solar panels on 24 district sites.

 

Ericson, Standley, Tierrasanta and Walker are the remaining four sites of the total 28 sites that have the same defective panels installed.  These systems are owned and maintained by UPC Solar SDS, LLC. Under this panel removal agreement, UPC Solar SDS, LLC, will bear all the costs associated with the removal of the solar panels, and the roofs received during the solar system installation will become property of the district. There will be a warranty for one year on the areas where the solar was removed.  This ensures that the roofs are watertight in these areas.

 

Unlike the other 24 PPAs for the previous solar systems, the PPAs for these four sites included   a prepayment for electricity.  The prepayment offset the reduction in incentives and offset the cost of roofing in the areas where solar was not installed.  As a result, the district has a high quality roof installed at these four sites for approximately 1/3 of the cost of a lesser quality district standard roof.  Return of this prepayment directly is unlikely since, UPC does not have reserves or assets to reimburse the district.  As covered in the removal agreement, in the unlikely event UPC receives payment from the bankruptcy court from the claim it submitted and after deducting for its costs, UPC will reimburse the district.

 

The district’s goals of sustainability and energy independence continue to move forward. This event is a technical issue with the solar system with safety concerns taking precedence. The system shut down will not affect power to district sites; it will simply revert back to San Diego Gas & Electric providing 100 percent of the power rather than a portion being provided by solar. district staff continues to coordinate these activities and future actions will be planned to minimize impacts to school operations. As noted above, upon the removal of the solar panels and related equipment, the district will be able to keep the quality roof at approximately one-third the cost of a district standard roof.    

 

The board's approval of this removal contract effectively ends the four purchase power agreements and provides the district with a contractual means for the solar equipment to be removed at UPC's expense, while ensuring the high quality roofs remains intact and water tight.

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District jointly awards two Adult Education high school diplomas through the district’s Adult School and the Continuing Education (Adult Education) Division of the San Diego Community College District.  San Diego Unified's Adult School offers the young adult learner (18 to 19.11 years of age) a last chance to earn a high school diploma by way of two diploma options, known as Option 1 and Option 2.  Hundreds of district students earn these diplomas each year in lieu of becoming  high school drop-outs. Students, with counselor guidance, select the option that best meets their needs.

 

Option 1 diploma mirrors current district requirements for a secondary high school diploma, with the exception of the physical education requirement. Option 2 diploma model enables the district’s most at-risk students a last chance to graduate by completing 4 additional semester credits above state high school diploma requirements, passage of the California High School Exit Exam, a 2.0 minimum G.P.A., demonstrated computer literacy, and the completion of the Senior Exhibition requirement. This board item pertains only to the Adult Education Joint Option 2 High School Diploma, which was originally approved by the board on August 30, 1994, with revisions to the requirements approved by the Board on May 13, 2008 and May 29, 2012.

 

On April 22, 1991, a joint meeting of the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education and the San Diego Community College Board of Trustees directed that a joint committee of faculty, staff, and administration be created to develop an alternative adult education high school diploma.  In February 1992, the committee began to meet on an ongoing basis and delineated its recommendations for the alternative high school diploma on May 17, 1994. Following is a summary of the committee’s findings:

 

The district’s Adult School student population is unique and distinct from the student population served in the traditional high school setting. The majority of these students have experienced failure in school, and the Adult School is their last chance to earn a high school diploma. Further, the time demands of the multiplicity of roles young adult students have already assumed require an interdisciplinary curriculum focused on standards as opposed to credits. The recommended standards for the Option 2 diploma preserve the quality and integrity of the high school diploma while making the diploma obtainable for this special student population. The Option 2 diploma will enable students to transition to the workplace and/or the vocational and academic programs of the community college.  The proposed diploma standards are a blending of high-school level courses and non-course requirements, college courses, and standardized testing.

 

The committee recommended that the board approve the alternative standards for the Adult Education Joint Option 2 High School Diploma through the authority delegated to governing boards as set forth in Education Code Section 51255.3, subsection (2)(b):

 

      The governing board….shall adopt alternative means for pupils to

      complete the prescribed course of study which may include practical

      demonstration, supervised work experience, or other outside school

      experience, vocational classes in high schools, courses offered by

      regional occupational centers or programs, interdisciplinary study,

      independent study and credit earned at a postsecondary institution.

 

On August 30, 1994, the board approved the alternative 22 semester credit Adult Education Joint Option 2 diploma.

 

In March 2008, the aforementioned committee reviewed the Option 2 diploma requirements.  The committee agreed that the diploma has, over the years, enabled thousands of adult students to obtain the high school diploma and has helped these students successfully transition to the workplace and/or to the community college for further vocational or academic study.  Further, in order to sustain the quality and integrity of this diploma, the committee recommended that all non-course requirements remain the same but that the course credit requirement be increased from 22 semester credit to 26 semester credits. This change was in alignment with Education Code requirements.

 

On May 13, 2008, the board approved the recommended credit requirement change to the Adult Education Option 2 Joint High School Diploma by increasing credit requirements from 22 semester credits to 26 semester credits.

 

On May 29, 2012, the board approved further recommended credit requirement changes (effective for the class of 2013) to the Adult Education Option 2 Joint High School Diploma by authorizing the completion of one year of a Career Technical Education course as an option for students in lieu of one year of Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language, as authorized by Assembly Bill 1330.  

 

At present, this report brings forward a proposed change to the current Option 2 diploma requirements to no longer permit the utilization of GED test scores to waive two course credits in Language Arts, and/or, Mathematics, and/or Science, and/or Social Science for this diploma option. This change is necessary as per GED state guidelines for the new GED test which will be implemented on January 1, 2014.

As part of the passage of Proposition Z, the voters approved the construction of aquatic and recreational facilities "swimming pools" (page 106, Proposition Z).

 

The intent of this proposal, is for San Diego Unified to fund the construction of the pools, and the YMCA (or other appropriate organization) would operate and maintain the facilities. 

 

Earlier this spring, YMCA leadership and staff met with Superintendent Kowba, Trustee Barnett, and facilities staff.  Subsequent meetings resulted in identifying approximately 10 sites, of which about five (one in each board member sub-district) would be the highest priority.

 

This proposal is an ideal example of a win win partnership for the taxpayers, communities, and children of San Diego Unified.

 

We respectfully request approval.

RECOMMENDATION: Direct staff to negotiate and finalize a contract with Sedona Pacific Corporation to provide real estate planning and consulting services (PS-13-1047-64).  The term of this Agreement is June 1, 2013 through December 31, 2013.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  Not to exceed amount of $105,000.00. Budget string: 5498-04003-00-5100-8200-0000-4003-0000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The district seeks assistance from a qualified real estate consultant to plan and manage its real estate sales and future development efforts.  The district requires planning support from a group that specializes in the same, as opposed to brokers and/or developers.  The district is looking for independent planning assistance wherein the consultant receives no benefit from its advice or guidance.  The district is seeking to 1) complete the sales of the properties as directed by the board and 2) create RFP(s) for the development of all district properties in a manner that optimizes revenue from the same.  We anticipate this is an effort that will continue for years to come as the district optimizes its utilization of all available properties for income-generating purposes rather than further sales.  We are seeking support in the first six months of that effort.  Sedona Pacific has emerged as the company most suited to provide this service.  They specialize in real estate planning.  Even with the eventual hire of a new director, we will need the kind of support that understands the differentiated needs of development opportunities, especially with a strong background in San Diego real estate development.   Sedona Pacific and the district have agreed on pricing and the general scope of the services to be provided.  There simply remains some terms and conditions of the contract to be worked out in the near-term future.  Staff is requesting the board approve going forward with the plan, with some specific terms and conditions yet to be negotiated.

RECOMMENDATION: Ratify the change of date, time, and location for the bid opening and Auction for the Sale of Porterfield as deemed necessary by district staff to ensure maximum return on the sale of the property.  ITEM REQUIRES 4 VOTES.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  $37,800,000.00 appraised value, which is the minimum bid amount, less the escalating annual income of $1,937,340.00.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The Advisory Committee on the Utilization of Excess School Property recommended the continued ground leases of Porterfield, as set forth in the annual Property Management Plan Exhibit C (attached), approved by the board on May 29, 2012. 

 

BACKGROUND:   At the May 14, 2013 closed session, the board gave the Deputy Superintendent Business authority to delay the bid opening and auction of Porterfield for up to thirty days if deemed in the best interest of the district.  On February 26, 2013,  under Item I.3., the board approved the Resolution of Intent to Sell Porterfield on bid opening date of June 3, 2013, at 9:00 a.m. in Annex 9, Testing Room.  

 

If delayed, district staff will proceed to 1) re-advertise the property for sale, with a new date, time, and location; 2) select the bidder with the highest bid and then conduct an auction for the same to arrive at the offer most beneficial to the district; 3) return to the board for approval of the sale of the property to the selected bidder.

 

This action amends the board direction to staff from a timeframe "up to thirty days" to "as deemed necessary by district staff to ensure maximum return on the sale of the property."

 

Approval of this agenda item will authorize the Deputy Superintendent Business, or his designee, to execute any documents in connection herewith.
