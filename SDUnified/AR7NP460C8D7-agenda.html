RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and approve a Resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula funds and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials.  The board is required to adopt a resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable. 

 

Current Williams legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12.  The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core, lab equipment for science laboratory courses.  All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual resolution of sufficiency.  Administrative Circulars 4, 5, and 6, dated August 15, 2017, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”  In September 2017, instructional materials inventory data and current PowerSchool enrollment for September 01, 2017, was compiled and entered into the “Instructional Materials Sufficiency Form” and provided to each elementary school by the Instructional Materials Office.  Sites were directed to verify their instructional materials inventory and take action to remedy any insufficiencies by ordering from the Instructional Materials Office.  Principals confirmed instructional materials sufficiency and returned the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 15, 2017. Secondary school principals, after completing their inventories and taking action to remedy any insufficiencies, also returned the “Principal Verification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency." 

 

Twenty-five elementary schools, five K-8 schools, seven middle schools, and six senior high schools were visited by a team from San Diego County Office of Education.  All 43 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials.  Additionally, all six senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

1. Proposal to Request Staff to Review Later School Start Times for Secondary Schools (Evans/Whitehurst-Payne)
Meeting
Oct 10, 2017 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Board Trustees John Lee Evans and Sharon Whitehurst-Payne will discuss secondary start times, and propose that the Board direct the Superintendent to begin an investigation on the possibility of a later start time, 8:30 a.m. or later, for secondary schools, beginning with high schools.  This investigation will include a review by the District Wellness Office on the research related to later start times.  The District will develop a procedure for school communities to petition for a later start time and approval would depend upon the financial feasibility.

 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  Extensive research studies that support a later school start time for secondary students, and provide evidence of the benefits on student health, safety and achievement include the American Medical Association (AMA),  http://news.cision.com/american-medical-association/r/ama-supports-delayed-school-start-times-to-improve-adolescent-wellness,c2029027; Centers for Disease Control (CDC), https://www.cdc.gov/media/releases/2015/p0806-school-sleep.html; and the American Academy of Pediatrics, http://pediatrics.aappublications.org/content/pediatrics/early/2014/08/19/peds.2014-1697.full.pdf. The later start times for secondary students is also supported by the National PTA, https://www.pta.org/advocacy/content.cfm?ItemNumber=4782&navItemNumber=5192.  

 

SB 328, which would have mandated that no high school in California start earlier than 8:30 a.m. was recently approved by the State Senate. The bill did not garner enough votes in the Assembly to pass, but it likely will be revisited by the Legislature in the future. School start times is currently a decision for local districts.  In the spirit of local control any potential change needs to be considered by each local school community involving all stakeholders including educators and parents, as well as students. 

 

Currently one of the District’s comprehensive high schools (Kearny High School) has had a later start time (8:45 a.m.) since 2002.

 

Any start time change proposal is subject to financial considerations, as budget priorities have already been established in the Vision 2020 LCAP.  Transportation scheduling is a crucial factor, as a change to one school’s bell time can affect routes and scheduling for other schools across the District.

 

Any potential change to the start time at a school depends upon four factors:

  1. Consensus on a change by each school community that involves input from all stakeholders, including, but not limited to School Governance Council, Wellness Council, employees and their representatives, parents and students
  2. Review of the impact of changes to elementary or middle schools
  3. Analysis by district staff to determine that the change is revenue neutral and can be accomplished with existing resources
  4. Sufficient notice to the local school community in the year prior to any change

BACKGROUND: This Permit allows Chevron to access a portion of Henry High School to conduct monthly and groundwater monitoring and samples of monitoring wells located in the grass field commonly used for baseball. These wells, and others in the area, are related to an investigation of a gasoline release at a former gasoline station located across the street at 7121 Park Ridge Blvd. The wells are sampled monthly and laboratory analytical data from this monitoring are presented in reports submitted to the County of San Diego Department of Environmental Health and distributed to other cognizant parties, including the district. Chevron will only access the site during non-school hours.

 

Environmental Review:

The proposed project is under CEQA, Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The proposed project, however, is exempt from expanded environmental review under the following Categorical Exemption Class:

 

Information Collection (Class 6) - Class 6 consists of basic data collection, research, experimental management, and resource evaluation activities which do not result in a serious or major disturbance to an environmental resource. These may be strictly for information gathering purposes, or as part of a study leading to an action which a public agency has not yet approved, adopted, or funded. (CEQA Guidelines §15306).

 

The purpose of the proposed project is to allow access to district property monthly, for groundwater monitoring at monitoring wells located in the parking area at Henry High. No new construction, including construction of monitoring wells, is required. Because the proposed project would permit Chevron to collect data from existing monitoring wells, the application of a Class 6 exemption is appropriate.

 

Approval of this item approves the project as described and authorizes and directs staff to file an NOE (Attachment 2) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk. Chevron staff has indicated they would assist district staff in filing of the NOE, including payment of the $50.00 County Recorder/Clerk filing fee.
