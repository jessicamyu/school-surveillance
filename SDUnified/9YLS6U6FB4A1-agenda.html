RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in support of SB 1041 (Hueso) Energy: Electric and Gas Rates: Public and Secondary Schools. The board supports SB 1041, and urges the state’s Public Utilities Commission to take into account the fixed budget and responsibility of the district to meet the educational needs of children and require SDG&E to establish a fair and reasonable rate structure for the schools it services

 

BACKGROUND: Increases in energy prices in San Diego County continue to have a negative impact on the San Diego Unified budget and the ability to offer programs that will improve our schools and help educate children.

 

The California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) ensures that consumers have safe, reliable utility service at reasonable rates.

 

On November 14, 2014, San Diego Gas and Electric (SDG&E) filed a General Rate Case application with CPUC to request an increase in revenue for calendar years 2016-2018.

 

San Diego county school districts experienced, on average, a 39 percent increase in electricity costs last year with approximately 33 percent attributable to rate increases. This resulted in an increased cost, due solely to rate increases, of $3.8 million for San Diego Unified.

 

SB 1041 (Hueso) Energy: Electric and Gas Rates: Public Elementary and Secondary Schools, would require the state’s energy utilities to develop and implement a rate for electric service specific to K-12 public schools to reduce the amount of money public schools spend on electricity freeing up more resources to educate our children.

 

SB 1041 aims to keep schools from spending their limited funds on high electricity bills and putting the money in the classrooms where it can benefit our students.

 

The County Office of Education spearheaded the San Diego county schools to defend recent year and future years of rate shock to hit county schools and as a result 39 of 42 school districts, and the San Diego County Office of Education, have joined a school energy coalition to file as formal interveners in SDG&E’s current Phase II General Rate Case.

 

On September 15, 2015, the San Diego Unified school board approved a resolution in support of joining the San Diego Schools Coalition for Electricity Cost Reduction and pursuing efforts that will lower energy costs for the future and support investment in clean sustainable energy.

BACKGROUND: Pursuant to Administrative Procedure 3300, Naming of Schools and School Facilities, this is a request to approve the renaming of Kearny High School of International Business to Kearny High School of College Connections. The School Names Committee has reviewed the supporting documentation required for the request and concurs with the renaming.

 

The School of International Business (SIB) at the Kearny High Educational Complex opened in 2004, as part of a high school redesign effort. Thereafter, the Fast Track Early Enrollment Program between Kearny High and Mesa College greatly expanded. Due in part to this expansion, the core mission of SIB has evolved into a college prep pathway. 

 

The current electives being offered, which include AVID in Grades 9-12, fit under a broader umbrella of college preparation leading to multiple dual enrollment courses while still enrolled in high school. SIB students now routinely graduate having earned multiple college credits through three different programs with Mesa College: Fast Track, Accelerated College Program and Legacy. The proposed new name - School of College Connections - more accurately reflects an essential pathway and will clearly communicate the focus of the school to perspective students and families.

 

From September 2015 through January 2016, formal meetings with various stakeholder groups to discuss the name change were held including the SIB staff, School Site Council, Kearny Alumni Association, SIB students, and the Kearny Cluster members. After completing the meetings, consensus was reached to rename the school to the Kearny High School of College Connections.

 

The request along with supporting documentation (attached) was submitted to the district School Names Committee. After review, the majority of the committee concurs with the renaming.

BACKGROUND: In summer 2015, a national movement to remove Confederate flags and symbols was sparked after what authorities said was a racially-motivated massacre in which nine people were shot and killed inside a Charleston, South Carolina church. The image of the alleged shooter posing next to the Confederate flag convinced some that the flag's reputation for white supremacy and racial oppression had trumped its symbolism of Southern heritage and ancestral pride. Subsequently, proposals were made recommending that schools named after Robert E. Lee, a Confederate general, be renamed.

 

In California, proposed legislation would have required such schools to be renamed effective with the 2017-18 school year.  Governor Brown vetoed the legislation.  In his veto message, he said:  “I am returning Senate Bill 539 without my signature. This bill would prohibit the naming of any school, park, building, or other public property after certain persons associated with the Confederate States of America. Recently we saw a national movement to remove the confederate flag from state capitols in the South – a long overdue action. This bill, however, strikes me as different and an issue quintessentially for local decision makers. As far as we know, only two schools, and a street in Stockton would be affected by this law. Existing local processes provide for the naming or re-naming of public facilities, and in several cases local residents have voiced their opposition and have succeeded in re-naming schools and other public property. Local governments are laboratories of democracy which, under most circumstances, are quite capable of deciding for themselves which of their buildings and parks should be named, and after whom.”

 

Previous to the proposal being introduced, San Diego Unified heard concerns voiced by members of the community that it may not be appropriate to have a school named after Robert E. Lee.  The district saw this as an opportunity to have a larger community dialogue with students, staff, and families about the school name and look at the history and research surrounding Lee, in order to make a collectively informed decision about changing the name or retaining it. 

 

Robert E. Lee Elementary opened in San Diego Unified in January 1959, in the Paradise Hills neighborhood. The name Robert E. Lee was chosen for what district historical data indicates was Lee's record as an "American soldier and educator." The school is very diverse: 75.6 percent Hispanic, 6.6 percent Filipino, 6.4 percent Multi-racial, 5.5 percent African American, 2.5 percent Caucasian, 1.9 percent Pacific Islanders, and 0.3 percent Asian.  

 

Two community forums were scheduled to offer an opportunity for Lee students, families, staff, alumni, neighbors, and other community members to come together to learn about the district’s policy and procedure for school and facility naming. The naming procedure is a prescriptive, thoughtful ,and inclusive process that takes time to assure sufficient and appropriate community input to be reviewed by the district’s school names committee, which is carefully made up to include all stakeholders – including community members, principals, bargaining unit representatives, and district staff. 

 

October 23, 2015: The first of two community meetings was held at the school where information was shared, district staff offered to conduct a community survey. This will allow the district time to engage everyone in the community, be able to come back and report on the results, and identify next steps for renaming (if clearly recommended) to the School Names Committee and then the board.  The survey was available to the public through Friday, November 20, and results were shared at the forum on December 2. The survey was available for two voting communities.  One included current staff, families, and students of Lee Elementary who used a password-protected website to participate.  An identical survey without a password was available to the wider public (including residents within the San Diego Unified boundary). See Survey results.

 

December 2, 2015: The second community forum was held at the school. After reviewing the results of the survey, which did not reflect a two/thirds vote for either choice: to rename or not rename, the decision was made to move the renaming process to the School Names Committee. Due to winter break, the committee would be scheduled to meet to review the documentation, survey results, and other community input at the end of January or February, 2016. The committee was to develop a recommendation to the superintendent and the board. The board makes the final decision.

 

February 25, 2016: The Schools Names Committee met and the members unanimously agree to recommend that Robert E. Lee Elementary should be renamed and that the name should not include the name Lee in any form. The committee recommended that the school should take the lead in choosing the new name in a process that included all students (K-5), staff, parents, and families. The superintendent supported the committee's recommendation.

 

March 14 through April 13, 2016: Principal Silvia Martinez and her Instructional Leader Team (ILT) developed a curriculum-based and democratic process to pick a new name for the school. All teachers, students, and classrooms participated in the process. The process starting with a discussion about the school’s vision statement and what a name represents. Students studied historical figures. After the lessons, each classroom proposed a new name for the school. On April 13, the Electoral College, otherwise known as the ILT, met. After analyzing and discussing the name recommendations, the ILT came up with the top two choices: Pacific View Leadership Elementary and Amelia Earhart Elementary.

 

April 14, 2016: Two voting assemblies were held for all grade levels. Two students presented the arguments for the two choices: Pacific View Leadership Elementary and Amelia Earhart Elementary.

 

April 19, 2016: Voting was held in the school’s auditorium. Lee students, staff, parents, and family members participated in the vote. When the votes were counted, Pacific View Leadership Elementary had the most votes.

 

The new name and supporting documents were provided to the School Names Committee who unanimously concurred with the renaming of Robert E. Lee Elementary to Pacific View Leadership Elementary.

 

Physical Plant Operations estimates it will cost up to $9,000 to change the school's name on the exterior locations. This is based on not knowing what the new name would be, but assumes it would be of reasonable length, etc. (includes changing the name on the marquee and the aluminum letters mounted on the building).

Trustees Whitehurst-Payne and Evans will move that the board take action to direct the superintendent to return to the board with a plan to provide that all students take at least one community college or other college course.  The community college or other college level course should include International Baccalaureate (IB), Advanced Placement (AP) courses, Career Technical Education (CTE) pathway course, or participation in an internship or apprenticeship program.  We believe it is important to begin accelerating these offerings in high schools between now and the year 2020.

We believe that we should continue to increase the number of opportunities available for all students, especially those that will actually go on to a community college.  We believe the community college connection is ideal because it covers technical careers as well as preparation for a four-year university.  We believe and understand that this is not a one size fits all proposal and this should NOT be a mandated graduation requirement. It is about making opportunities available and accessible to all students.

BACKGROUND:

 

Proposed Project

The Project includes the multi-phase construction and operation of upgraded athletic facilities and whole site modernization at Point Loma High School (Project) in the City of San Diego. The improvements are identified in Propositions Z and S and meet the goals and objectives of Vision 2020, and the 2008 Long Range Facilities Master Plan. The proposed improvements are summarized below; a detailed description of the Project is provided in the Final EIR.

 

Phase 1 – Athletic Facilities Upgrades (Construction slated in summer 2016 for nine months)

  • Install four 20 ft tall grandstand light poles on the home side bleachers (max height of 45 ft above grade)
  • Install four 70 ft on the inside track, marked and lighted in accordance with FAA regulations
  • Remove two speaker poles on the home side of the field and replace with four 29 ft poles
  • Construct one-story, 616 sf electrical substation with signal panel enclosure

Phase 2 – Whole Site Modernization (Construction slated in summer 2018 for 24 months)

  • Demolish Building 800 containing a library and 11 classrooms
  • Construct a new three-story building, approximately 37,000 square feet in size, containing 20 classrooms and a library/media center
  • Renovate Buildings 200 and 300
  • Construct external security enclosures and new architectural facades
  • Construct/improve bus turnout
  • Demolish auto shop and reconfigure/expand adjoining parking lot

Phase 3 – Athletic Facilities Upgrades (Construction projected in summer 2020 for 9 months)

  • Construct one-story, 125 sf ticket booth on northwest end of field
  • Construct 500-seat visitor side aluminum bleachers
  • Construct 140 sf press box at home side grandstand, marked and lighted in accordance with FAA regulations (max height of 34 ft above grade)
  • Construct elevator with maximum height of 40 ft above grade
  • Install three 20 ft speaker poles on the visitor side 
  • Construct one-story, 392 sf storage building
  • Construct one-story visitor restroom and concession building
  • Install two 30 ft tall bleacher light poles  
  • Replace PA system

Phase 4 – Whole Site Modernization (Construction projected in summer 2020 for 24 months)

  • Demolish Building 400 containing 13 classrooms
  • Construct new cafeteria/food services building
  • Construct custodial supply building
  • Modify service drive between new cafeteria and boys’ locker building
  • Provide basketball courts
  • Demolish classroom wing of Building 600 containing 7 classrooms
  • Construct elevator and stair access to the upper floor of Building 600
  • Construct new academic landscape/hardscape (including passive and active use areas)
  • Renovate gymnasium/boys’ locker room/ROTC Building
  • Renovate girls’ locker/gymnasium
  • Renovate auditorium
  • Renovate administration facilities

The proposed improvements would comply with the California Building Code and improve compliance with the American with Disabilities Act (ADA).

 

The Project would not substantially change the operations of the campus. The school calendar would remain consistent with the district calendar for a traditional high school program, and all school facilities would remain available for public use as required by the Civic Center Act (Administrative Procedure 9205). Furthermore, the whole site modernization component of the Project would result in a net loss of 13 classrooms, and the capacity of the high school would not increase. During construction of Phase 2, no temporary classroom facilities would be required; displaced seats caused by the demolition of Building 800 would be accommodated in underutilized classrooms in Buildings 400 and 600. Up to five portable facilities, however, may be installed in the auto shop area for use as a temporary library.

 

Operation of the football field would increase due to the addition of lights and visitor side bleachers. With the addition of the field lights, the football field would operate in the evenings for athletic practices and athletic events.  However, some of those uses are not new but rather are uses that would have occurred during the afternoon.  Although the Final EIR provides a conservative analysis of the maximum operation of the athletic facilities upgrades (e.g., daily operation of the 2,500-seat bleachers with the field lights turning off at 11:00 pm) in order to capture potential community use of the facility, as required by the Civic Center Act, the actual operation of the field would be limited to the standing Point Loma High School Field Use Policy, as adopted by the board on July 29, 2014. 

 

Environmental Process

 

The district has complied with the requirements of CEQA (Public Resources Code Section 21000 et seq.) and CEQA Guidelines (California Administrative Code Section 15000 et seq.) for the Project. The district issued several Notices of Preparation (NOP) to prepare a Draft EIR: May 3, 2013, for the athletic facilities upgrades improvements component of the Project (i.e., Phases 1 and 3), October 30, 2015 and again on November 13, 2015, to include the whole site modernization component of the Project (i.e., Phases 2 and 4). A Notice of Availability of the Draft EIR was published in the San Diego Union-Tribune and La Prensa and filed at the County of San Diego and California State Clearinghouse. Copies of the Draft EIR were sent to the State Clearinghouse, state and local agencies, and community entities and organizations. The Draft EIR was made available at the following locations:

  • Online at www.sandiegounified.org/node/1286

Environmental Impacts

 

The district received 71 comment letters on the Draft EIR, from the following entities:

  • Three agency letters from the Native American Heritage Commission, Governor’s Office of Planning and Research, State Clearinghouse and Planning Unit, and Department of Toxic Substances Control, and
     
  • Four community and regional entities, including City of San Diego Council District 2, Peninsula Community Planning Board, Pro Point Loma Community Group, and Taxpayers for Accountable School Bond Spending, and
     
  • Sixty-four letters from concerned individuals.

The comment letters raised several issues of concern regarding the Project and its potential effect on the surrounding community including (but not limited to):

  • Significant and unavoidable effects on the community from the stadium lights, noise, and traffic
  • Effects on parking from the use of the stadium
  • Civic center uses of the field and the frequency, size, and potential community impact from such events
  • The overall capacity of the stadium.

Responses to all comments received on the Draft EIR are included in the Final EIR (Attachment B), and the Draft EIR was updated to clarify the impact analysis therein. None of the revisions made have changed the conclusions of the Draft EIR or are considered significant new information. As updated, the Final EIR concludes that the Project would result in significant and unavoidable impacts to on the issues of field light trespass, operational noise from nighttime use of the field, and increased traffic from the addition of 500 spectator bleacher seats. All other environmental topic areas studied, per Appendix G of the CEQA Guidelines, were found to have no impact or a less-than-significant impact on the environment. Feasible mitigation measures have been identified to mitigate potentially significant impacts related to paleontological resources, unstable soils, potential hazardous soils onsite, and traffic impacts. They have been included in the Mitigation Monitoring and Reporting Program (Attachment C).   

 

Written Findings (Attachment D) have been prepared for each of the significant effects. Additionally, because the Project would result in significant and unavoidable environmental effects that cannot be avoided or lessened, the proposed approval of the Project would require the board to adopt a Statement of Overriding Considerations (Attachment D), which requires the board to balance the benefits of the Project against its unavoidable environmental effects. Should the board determine that the benefits of the Project outweigh the unavoidable adverse environmental effects, these effects may be considered “acceptable,” and the Project may be approved, at which time the board may direct staff to file the Notice of Determination (Attachment E).

BACKGROUND: The Food Services Department is proposing $0.25 increase in school lunch meals in order to maintain its current programs, due to increased costs and U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) regulations. The price increase will impact students who purchase full-price meals only.  This will result in an elementary lunch meal price of $2.25 and a secondary meal price of $2.75.  Students who qualify for free and reduced-meal prices will not be affected by the meal price increases. 

 

Currently, over 130,000 school meals are served per day; 14,000 are paid lunch meals.  The last time lunch meal prices increased for students with a paid eligibility was June 20, 2008.  The current $0.25 increase is due to rising costs, such as labor and food expenses.

The $0.25 increase will cover increased costs and will not deter students and families from eating school lunch. The paid lunch increase of $0.25 will result in an annual cost of $45.00 in a school year, per student.  

 

Currently, the paid lunch revenue is comprised of the federal subsidy of $0.37 per meal in addition to the $2.00 or $2.50 paid by elementary and secondary students respectively.

 

Costs continue to increase substantially, particularly in the following areas:

  • 2015-16 Wage/Salary 5 percent increase to take effect to the Cafeteria Fund in 2016-17 school years.
  • PERS contribution rates are rising significantly until year 2020.
  • Food costs have increased due to the implementation of the Healthy Hunger Free Kids Act enacted in 2010.

BACKGROUND:

Proposed Project — The Proposed Project is the installation and operation of a wireless-controlled marquee sign at Doyle Elementary School.  The sign would be double-pole mounted with a single-sided, 5 foot by 9 foot monochrome light-emitting diode (LED) display.  The height of the sign would be 7 feet 11 inches tall. The outdoor display would use a single color of LED to create text and graphics.  The sign would have an automatic photo sensor that would light the display at 100 percent during day and reduce to 30 percent in the evening.  The text could be displayed using a variety of display options like sparkle, flash, blink, and scroll to create a message; however, the district will pre-program it to not blink or flash and automatically turn off from 11:00 p.m. to 6:00 a.m., which is consistent with City of San Diego Municipal Code, Chapter 14, General Regulations.  Construction of the proposed improvements would commence in the spring 2016.  The existing signs would not be removed.

 

The proposed project is a 2015-17 Proposition Z school site discretionary project, under the December 1, 2015, board-approved revised bond program plan.  The project will be implemented through an Indefinite Delivery Indefinite Quantity (IDIQ) contract.  

 

Environmental Review — The proposed project is subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code section 21000 et seq.  ICF International, who is on the district’s list of prequalified environmental consulting firms, was retained to prepare environmental documentation for the proposed project.  Based on a review of the proposed project, staff determined that the proposed project is exempt from further environmental analysis under three categorical exemption classes as set forth below:

 

Minor Alterations to Land (Class 4) —  Class 4 consists of minor public or private alterations in the condition of land, water, and/or vegetation which do not involve removal of healthy, mature, scenic trees except for forestry or agricultural purposes, including minor trenching and back-filling where the surface is restored (CEQA Guidelines Section 15304[f]).

 

Construction of the proposed project would require minor electrical trenching and back-filling where the surface will be restored for the installation of the new marquee sign.  All work would occur entirely within the developed campus and would not require the removal of any healthy, mature, scenic trees.    

 

Accessory Structures (Class 11) —  Class 11 consists of construction, or placement of minor structures accessory to (appurtenant to) existing commercial, industrial, or institutional facilities, including on premise signs (CEQA Guidelines Section 15311[a]).

 

The proposed marquee sign would be located within the premises of the existing school campus.

 

Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14) —  Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less (CEQA Guidelines Section 15314).  

 

The proposed project would serve the existing student population and not add new classrooms or other facilities on the existing campuses.  Therefore, the proposed project would not exceed the existing capacity of that school site.

 

Categorical exemptions may be not be relied upon if the application of the exemption triggers any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2.  Based on a review of the proposed project, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemptions. Approval of this item authorizes and directs staff to file a Notice of Exemption in substantial conformance with the Notice of Exemption Attachment with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. BACKGROUND: On December 13, 2011, the board approved $15,500,000.00 to implement the Five-Year Enterprise Software Plan which included replacing the Zangle Student Information System.  To support this plan, the board approved Agreement SW-12-0001-13, on May 15, 2012, with NCS Pearson, Inc. to provide PowerSchool as the successor Student Information System.  On May 10, 2016, the board approved a change from NCS Pearson, Inc. to the newly formed PowerSchool Group, LLC.  The terms, conditions, and pricing of the original agreement with NCS Pearson, Inc. remain in full force and effect.

 

The 2016-17 school year will be the fourth, full year that San Diego Unified has run PowerSchool as its primary Student Information System.  During this time there have been a number of improvements made by both the vendor and the district's Information Technology Support Services staff.  This First Amendment with PowerSchool Group, LLC will provide the following enhancements: 

 

1. On-line Enrollment Options System for the Neighborhood Schools and Enrollment Options Office. The new on-line solution will replace a 20+ year-old legacy system that is developed on software tools no longer supported and that is not integrated with PowerSchool.  The new system will be integrated with PowerSchool and built based on district-specific requirements to provide parents with an easy to use on-line application, as well as numerous built-in efficiencies currently missing from the legacy system.  The system will better support both the Neighborhood Schools and Enrollment Options Office and school site staff that process the 15,000+ Choice applications annually.

 

2. On-line Extended Day Application/Enrollment System for the PrimeTime Program Office.  The new on-line solution will replace the paper application process that the program office processes in excess of 18,000 applications annually.  The new system will be integrated with PowerSchool, will automate most of the manual processes staff currently perform, and provide more real-time system generated updates to parents as to the status of their students’ extended day application.

 

3. PowerSchool services to manage 24/7/365 system monitoring, support response, and upgrades. As the district continues to build new functionality and automation into PowerSchool, support to manage the servers and student data also increases.  This added service will provide district staff with the additional access to PowerSchool support staff needed during non-peak hours when most system maintenance/support is performed.

 

As part of the long-term strategy to more tightly integrate operational and instructional systems with the SIS, the two new on-line systems (Enrollment Options and Extended Day) will utilize the same look/feel that parents have become familiar with when enrolling their students using the on-line, New Student Enrollment System implemented at the end of the 2014-15 school year.  Staff will also use the same on-line administrative system tools giving them a much-reduced learning curve as these systems are deployed, as well as providing for employee efficiency and increased productivity. The consistency for both parents and staff will be big improvements in making the application and enrollment process much more user-friendly using current technologies.
