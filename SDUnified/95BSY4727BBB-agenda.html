BACKGROUND: On November 19, 2013, the board had a first reading of the CCSS Proposed Spending Plan.

 

California, along with 45 additional states, has adopted the Common Core State Standards (CCSS). California Assembly Bill 86 provides one-time funds to local educational agencies to support activities required to implement the CCSS. These new standards are required to be in place for the 2014-15 school year. This new funding is targeted to CCSS implementation and must be used to support the following:

  • Professional Development to build the capacity of teachers, leaders, and support staff involved in the direct instruction of students that is aligned to the CCSS.
  • Instructional materials and supplemental materials aligned to Common Core State Standards.
  • Technology support needed to implement CCSS, including computer-based student assessments.

Agreement with Pearson Education, Inc.  Adoption of the Common Core State Standards (CCSS) requires changes to current courses and creation of new courses.  The Smarter Balanced Assessment to be given in the eleventh grade is an integrated assessment that requires students to use algebra, geometry, trigonometry, and statistics.  In examining the CCSS and the expectations of the Smarter Balanced Assessment, district teacher leaders agree that teaching the content in the traditional sequence (algebra, geometry, and intermediate algebra) will not adequately prepare students for success.  Integrated Mathematics I, II, and III 1-2 are a series of three courses that incorporate the CCSS.

 

The Interdivisional Curriculum Committee (ICC) voted to pilot these courses districtwide beginning in 2014-15, and the board approved the ICC course recommendations on May 14, 2013.  In order for these courses to meet the district’s “a-g” requirements, UC “a-g” approval must be granted for the courses, including the adopted instructional materials. 

 

The board approved these instructional materials, as recommended by the ICC, on September 3, 2013, allowing staff to begin the process for obtaining UC “a-g” approval.

 

All legal requirements have been met in accordance with California Education Code sections 60002, 60040-60048, and Administrative Procedures 4000, 4050, and 4500.  The Instructional Materials Office worked closely with the mathematics program manager and staff to facilitate a committee evaluation of instructional materials meeting the description and requirements of the courses.  Publishers were provided course descriptions and asked to submit applicable instructional materials for committee review.

 

The adoption committee was composed of classroom teachers, parents, site administrators, and central office staff, including Integrated Technology Support Services and the Office of Accountability.  While evaluating instructional materials to be used by students in regular education classrooms, the adoption committee also considered the unique needs of special student populations. 

 

The adoption committee reviewed the submitted instructional materials and requested publisher presentations for each submission meeting the criteria for the course.  Following presentations, committee members continued with independent evaluation of the publisher programs.  A final committee meeting was held and the committee reached consensus with their recommendation to the school board to adopt the instructional material identified in this report.

 

The committee participants, meeting dates, evaluation criteria member rubrics, evaluation summary, and recommended instructional materials are documented in an Instructional Materials Adoption Packet (IMAP) completed by the adoption committee chairperson.  The completed IMAPS and the recommended instructional materials are available for review in the Instructional Materials Office upon request.

BACKGROUND:  Under the federal Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) legislation, San Diego Unified must have a Parent Involvement Policy that is developed in collaboration with parents and reviewed every year. The expansion of the current Parent Involvement Policy has to be broadened from Title I sites to be all-inclusive. The policy guidelines establish the LEA’s expectations for parent involvement, and describes how it will:

  • provide the coordination, technical assistance, and other support necessary to assist participating schools in planning and implementing effective parent involvement activities to improve student academic achievement;
  • build the schools’ and parents’ capacity for strong parental involvement;
  • coordinate and integrate parental involvement strategies;
  • conduct, with the involvement of parents, an annual evaluation of the content and effectiveness of the parental involvement policy; and
  • involve parents in the activities of the schools.

The revisions to the guidelines, generally, include: 1) inclusion of all schools, not just Title I; 2) removal of specific district department names so that the guidelines will remain current even with changes to district organizational structure; and 3) inclusion of parent input into the development of the Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP).

BACKGROUND: As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA).

 

Additionally, Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their SMART goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

The local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2013–14 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 177 schools.

A copy of each SPSA which includes the budget summary, and required, signed assurances page is available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.
