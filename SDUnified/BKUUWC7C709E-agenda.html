3. Resolution in Support of Updating the History and Social Science Framework (Beiser/Barrera)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of Updating the History and Social Science Framework and that the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education requests that the State Board of Education update the History and Social Science Framework to include a history of racism and oppression and a history of labor unions.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes that the lack of a concise history of racism and oppression and a history of labor unions in California’s History and Social Science Framework results in an incomplete education for students. The history and social science framework promotes the development of the habits and skills of good citizens, along with lessons on government, voter education, civic responsibility, and the founding documents of the United States, such as the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution. A concise history and social science education includes an examination in detail of the legacy of the important events, people, laws, and court decisions that have shaped the history of this country, and which incorporates the history of racism and oppression and of labor unions. Recent events in American society underscore the importance of updating the history and social science framework and shifting towards a substantive history that incorporates a concise and comprehensive understanding of the institutions and systems that contribute towards racism, oppression, and the role of labor unions. For example, the murder of George Floyd while in police custody on Memorial Day, 2020, has laid bare the persistence of racism within American society and the 400 years of racial inequality have also been exposed by the different ways the Covid-19 pandemic has impacted communities of color, resulting in higher rates of infection and higher morbidity rates. The current history and social science framework ignores the history of labor unions and ignores their role in winning social protections such as child labor laws, social security, and Medicare, and implicitly and explicitly represents labor organizing and disputes as inherently violent.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Facilities, Planning and Construction has determined that many major school construction projects will take place within the next two years. Due to the size, complexity, and timelines of these critical projects, it is desirable to use lease-leaseback, which is a delivery method based on best value, for the construction of these projects.  This is the fourth group of evaluation procedures for qualifications and proposals of lease-leaseback contractors since the district began using this delivery method several years ago.  On September 12, 2017, July 24, 2018, and June 18, 2019, the board approved evaluation procedures for various construction projects using the lease-leaseback delivery method.

 

Pursuant to Education Code section 17406, school districts must award lease-leaseback contracts based on a competitive solicitation process to the proposer providing the best value to the district.

 

Before awarding a lease-leaseback contract, the governing board of the school district must adopt and publish required procedures, guidelines, and criteria for evaluating the qualifications and subsequent proposals of prospective lease-leaseback contractors ("Evaluation Procedures") which ensure that the best value selections by the district are conducted in a fair and impartial manner. Toward that end, district staff has developed Evaluation Procedures, an exemplar of which is attached to the Resolution as Exhibit A.  District staff also developed Criteria and Scoring, exemplars of which are attached to the Resolution as Exhibits B and C.

 

Through a Request for Qualifications, the district will request a Statement of Qualifications from prospective contractors, and from that pool of qualified contractors, the district will subsequently issue Request for Proposal for certain District Projects. Upon successful negotiations, district staff will bring the lease-leaseback contracts for specific projects to the board for approval.

BACKGROUND: On May 7, 1997, the federal government implemented the Telecommunications Act of 1996. This Program (often referred to as E-rate) was designed to ensure that eligible schools have access to modern telecommunication services, which includes replacing infrastructure and LAN electronic components. Subsequently, district staff filed the necessary paperwork to enroll in the E-rate Program and take advantage of the various discounts.

 

In accordance with the Agreement provisions, the services were to be furnished in accordance with the California Multiple Award Schedule (CMAS) and California Public Contract Code sections 10299, et seq., and 12100.7, et seq.

 

On February 10, 2009, the district entered into an Agreement with AT&T in order to qualify for the available funds and provide the technology services required under the Proposition S bond measure. The Agreement specified a maximum consideration of $13,200,000.00 and initial three-year term of February 10, 2009, through February 9, 2012, with an option to extend up to two years at district’s discretion. The Agreement also stated that the contract could be renewed at the end of the five-year term for an additional negotiated term.

 

In November 2009, the district was awarded $40 million in E-rate funds to provide technology upgrades at 76 district schools that qualified under the E-rate Program. The First Amendment was approved by the board on July 12, 2011, to incorporate a fourth CMAS contract to the Agreement which allowed the district flexibility in purchasing Cisco products. There was no cost associated with the First Amendment.

 

In the fall of 2011, the district was awarded $7.7 million in E-rate funds for an additional 11 district schools that qualified under the E-rate Program. A Second Amendment was approved by the board on December 13, 2011, to extend the Agreement through February 9, 2014, increase the consideration by $3 million, and update the contract provisions to current standards.

 

On November 6, 2012, Proposition Z was approved by the voters of San Diego, which provided the district with an opportunity to leverage Proposition Z funds to qualify for and accept an additional $52.3 million in E-rate funds for specified projects. On June 18, 2013, the board authorized the use of $19.2 million from Proposition Z bonds sales for these projects.

 

In the spring of 2013, the district was awarded $43.5 million in E-rate funds for 88 district schools to receive technology upgrades. Thereafter, a Third Amendment was approved by the board on December 10, 2013, to extend the Agreement an additional three years with an option to extend up to two years in order to take advantage of the additional E-rate funds available, increase the consideration of the Agreement by $12 million, and update the contract provisions to current standards.

 

In the winter of 2015, the district was awarded $11 million in E-rate funds for technology upgrades at 176 district schools. A Fourth Amendment was approved by the board on January 24, 2017, to extend the Agreement through February 9, 2019, and increase the funds by an additional $25 million and update various administrative provisions.

 

On May 29, 2018, the board approved the Fifth Amendment to extend the Agreement an additional two years through February 9, 2021, which will allow the district to take advantage of the E-rate funds available. The consideration of the Agreement needed to be increased by an additional $20 million and various administrative provisions, including the CMAS contracts, needed to be updated to support the planned technology work to bring all district schools up to the same standard. Many district schools have received more than one E-rate funded technology upgrades.

 

This Sixth Amendment will allow Pacific Bell Telephone Company dba AT&T DataComm (AT&T) to continue providing electronic components and pathways for Local Area Network (LAN) upgrades at selected district schools. Various administrative provisions of the Agreement need to be updated, including the CMAS contracts utilized, to support the planned technology work to bring all district schools up to the same standard. The short term extension will allow the district to fix critical components in its wireless infrastructure and continue to work on its Emergency Communication System (ECS). The district is also using the existing contract to add external wireless for school reopening, in view of Covid-19. Additionally, the district needs to start the process of improving the integrity of our internal wireless systems early in 2021. Extending its existing contracts will ensure continuity should a delay occur in completing the full procurement process for the new contracts. FPC and IT cannot afford the impact of losing the ability to contract this critical work. The scope is to obtain the services of qualified integrators (including pre-design, procurement, warehousing, asset management, burn-in, testing, component, addressing, commissioning, replacement and warranty repair services) to install technology at district’s schools (WAN/LAN components, wireless, VoIP/ECS and routers, core and edge switches refresh replacement).

 

Construction phasing and other strategies are employed to minimize disruption of operations.

BACKGROUND: This project consists of furnishing and installing shade shelters and some play structures on playground areas at nine district sites. Other scopes of work include concrete and asphalt patching. The nine sites are Clay Elementary, Dingeman Elementary, EB Scripps Elementary, Fay Elementary, Hardy Elementary, Hickman Elementary, Jefferson Elementary, Jonas Salk Elementary, and Tierrasanta Elementary Schools.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111(b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000.00 or more. The notice calling for bids was published once a week for two successive weeks in accordance with Public Contract Code section 20112. Bids were due and opened on October 9, 2020. The district received a total of three bids from Byrom-Davey, Inc., R.E. Schultz Construction, Inc., and Team C Construction. After evaluating the bids, staff determined R.E. The recommended contractor, R.E. Schultz Construction, Inc., submitted a four percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Multi-Site Shade Shelter Project, which includes the installation of shade shelters within playground areas at Clay, Fay, Jefferson, Tierrasanta, Hardy, Dingeman, EB Scripps, Hickman, and Jonas Salk Elementary Schools (Proposed Project), is subject to CEQA. However, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15303 - New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures (Class 3) and Section 15311 Accessory Structures (Class 11). As described above, the Proposed Project consists of the installation of shade shelters at nine school sites. This is consistent with a Class 3 Exemption which allows for limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures and consistent with a Class 11 Exemption which allows for the placement of minor structures accessory to existing institutional facilities. Therefore, the application of a Class 3 and Class 11 Categorical Exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified Environmental Impact Report.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Analysis: The following provides analysis of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites. Construction of proposed shade shelter installations would serve existing schools and would be consistent with existing operations at each of the school sites. Implementation of the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the timing of the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Other projects may be planned for each of the identified school sites; however, they would not overlap with the construction of the proposed shade shelter projects. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations at each site would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. No work associated with the Proposed Project is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. There are no designated scenic highways that are visible from each of the project sites. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, there are no district school facilities located on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures and the proposed shade shelter installations would be mounted within existing paved playground areas, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at each of the nine district sites is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking because grading and trenching into fossil bearing soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.

Approval of this item approves the Shade Shelter Projects at Clay, Fay, Jefferson, Tierrasanta, Hardy, Dingeman, EB Scripps, Hickman, and Jonas Salk Elementary Schools as described, authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and approves the agreement with R.E. Schultz Construction, Inc., to furnish and install shade shelters at nine district sites.

BACKGROUND: San Diego Unified (District), as the lead agency under CEQA, has prepared the following CEQA analysis to evaluate the potential environmental consequences associated with solar photovoltaic (PV) installations at five district schools (Proposed Project), including Clairemont High School (HS), Mission Bay HS, Point Loma HS, Standley Middle School (MS), and University City HS. Specifically, the Proposed Project involves the installation of a fixed-tilt canopy system at each of the above-mentioned school sites, located within existing paved and/or disturbed areas. Attachment 1 shows the site plan for each project.

 

In alignment with the recommendations of the district’s Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee, solar photovoltaic (PV) system installation projects have been initiated to create an energy efficient and renewable power source, delivering a significant cost savings to the district.

 

The Proposed Project involves the installation of solar PV canopy systems and associated battery energy storage systems (BESS) on existing school campuses. The solar PV canopy systems would consist of PV arrays mounted onto canopy structures in located entirely within existing parking lots. All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school properties. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

 

New lighting may be installed under the proposed canopies to provide a similar amount and quality of lighting as the existing lighting on the campus. All new lighting would be down-shielded to prevent any light spillover offsite. Lighting would be turned off consistent with district Administrative Regulation (AR) 3511, which requires all interior and exterior lighting to be turned off when students and staff leave for the day.

 

Construction is anticipated to begin for each site when school is not in session and span over an estimated duration of approximately six months, beginning in the summer 2021. All construction areas and staging areas would be fenced off and isolated from the schools. The heaviest construction activity would occur when school is not in session to avoid operational disruptions.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Proposed Project is subject to CEQA; however it is statutorily exempt from further environmental review pursuant to California Public Resources Code Section 21080.35, which provides the following statutory exemption that would be applicable to the installation of any solar photovoltaic arrays within existing parking lots:

 

  1. Except as provided in subdivision (d), this division does not apply to the installation of a solar energy system on the roof of an existing building or at an existing parking lot.
  2. For the purposes of this section, the following terms mean the following:
  1. “Existing parking lot” means an area designated and used for parking of vehicles as of the time of the application for the solar energy system and for at least the previous two years.
  2. “Solar energy system” includes all associated equipment. Associated equipment consists of parts and materials that enable the generation and use of solar electricity or solar-heated water, including any monitoring and control, safety, conversion, and emergency responder equipment necessary to connect to the customer’s electrical service or plumbing and any equipment, as well as any equipment necessary to connect the energy generated to the electrical grid, whether that connection is onsite or on an adjacent parcel of the building and separated only by an improved right-of-way. “Associated equipment” does not include a substation.
  1. (1) Associated equipment shall be located on the same parcel of the building, except that associated equipment necessary to connect the energy generated to the electrical grid may be located immediately adjacent to the parcel of the building or immediately adjacent to the parcel of the building and separated only by an improved right-of-way.
  1. Associated equipment shall not occupy more than 500 square feet of ground surface and the site of the associated equipment shall not contain plants protected by the Native Plant Protection Act (Chapter 10 (commencing with Section 1900) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code).
  1. This section does not apply if the associated equipment would otherwise require one of the following:
  1. An individual federal permit pursuant to Section 401 or 404 of the federal Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Sec. 1341 or 1344) or waste discharge requirements pursuant to the Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act (Division 7 (commencing with Section 13000) of the Water Code).
  2. An individual take permit for species protected under the federal Endangered Species Act of 1973 (16 U.S.C. Sec. 1531 et seq.) or the California Endangered Species Act (Chapter 1.5 (commencing with Section 2050) of Division 3 of the Fish and Game Code).
  3. A streambed alteration permit pursuant to Chapter 6 (commencing with Section 1600) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code.
  1. This section does not apply if the installation of a solar energy system at an existing parking lot involves either of the following:
  1. The removal of a tree required to be planted, maintained, or protected pursuant to local, state, or federal requirements, unless the tree dies and there is no requirement to replace the tree.
  2. The removal of a native tree over 25 years old.
  1. This section does not apply to any transmission or distribution facility or connection.

The Proposed Project is consistent with California Public Resources Code Section 21080.35 because:

  1. The proposed Solar PV installations at Clairemont HS, Mission Bay HS, Point Loma HS, Standley MS, and University City HS would be constructed entirely within existing parking lots at least two years old (all schools were operational in their current configuration on or before 1981);
  2. All associated ancillary equipment, specifically the BESS, would be located on the same parcel as the solar PV installations and would not occupy more than 500 square feet of ground surface at each site;
  3. The proposed Solar PV installations would not be constructed in an area with plants protected by the Native Plant Protection Act of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code. There are no such areas on any district school site;
  4. The proposed Solar PV installations would not be constructed in an area requiring an individual federal permit pursuant to Section 401 or 404 of the federal Clean Water Act;
  5. The proposed Solar PV installations would not be constructed in an area requiring an individual take permit for species protected under the federal Endangered Species Act of 1973;
  6. The proposed Solar PV installations would not be constructed in an area requiring a streambed alteration permit pursuant to Chapter 6 (commencing with Section 1600) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code;
  7. The proposed Solar PV installations would not require the removal of trees protected by a tree preservation ordinance or native trees over 25 years old. All project sites are on existing schools within existing parking lots and no native or open space areas exist on any of the Proposed Project sites. Trees may require removal, but the district does not have a tree preservation ordinance and no native trees would be removed; and
  8. The Project does not include the construction of a transmission or distribution facility or connection.

Statutory exemptions are projects specifically excluded from CEQA consideration as defined by the State Legislature. These exemptions are delineated in PRC §21080 et seq. A statutory exemption applies to any given project that falls under its definition, regardless of the project’s potential impacts to the environment. However, it is important to note that any CEQA exemption applies only to CEQA and not, of course, to any other state, local or federal laws that may be applicable to a proposed project.

 

The Proposed Solar PV installation at Clairemont High School would occur within 1,000 feet of a historic burned waste site located underneath the existing main parking area adjacent to Ute Drive. This area has been classified as a potential public safety hazard by the Department of Toxic Substance Control (DTSC) if certain measures are not implemented prior to ground disturbing activities.

 

On December 8, 2016, the DTSC, acting as lead agency under CEQA, prepared and filed a Notice of Exemption (NOE) for a Removal Action Workplan (RAW) for the installation of new hardscape at existing slope and planter in front parking lot of Clairemont High School adjacent to Ute Drive (SCH#: 2016128066) (Attachment 2). The purpose of the hardscape was to cap an exposed portion of the known burned waste footprint to prevent contact and inhalation of existing hazardous waste by school staff, students, and construction workers, as well as the general public.

 

Project approval by DTSC also required the following measures to be implemented prior to, during, and after construction: (1) implementation of institutional controls described in an Operations and Maintenance (O&M) Agreement, which establish procedures to maintain the engineering cover/cap to ensure future activities at the site do not result in an exposure that would be considered an unacceptable risk to human health; (2) procedures for communications between district staff and regulatory agencies; (3) construction monitoring; and (4) preparation and recordation of a Land Use Covenant (LUC). Completion of the hardscape at existing slope and planter occurred at the end of March, 2020.

 

Consistent with the previously approved RAW, construction of the Proposed Project at Clairemont High School would require notification to the DTSC and the City of San Diego Solid Waste Local Enforcement Agency (LEA) prior to any construction activity on or within 1,000 feet of the known waste footprint and to provide monitoring during construction consistent with the Community Health and Safety Plan (Attachment 3) prepared for Clairemont High School.

 

Because construction of the Proposed Project at Clairemont High School would be required to comply with the existing approved conditions outlined in the RAW and associated documentation, impacts associated with an existing hazardous materials condition would not be exacerbated by the construction of the Proposed Project. While the Solar PV project at Clairemont HS is statutorily exempt from CEQA, project construction would still require implementation of the previously approved measures identified above.

 

Approval of this item approves the Solar PV Projects at Clairemont HS, Mission Bay HS, Point Loma HS, Standley MS, and University City HS as described and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption (Attachment 4) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.

BACKGROUND: San Diego Unified (District), as the lead agency under CEQA, has prepared the attached Initial Study for the Photovoltaic System Installation Projects to evaluate the potential environmental consequences associated with solar photovoltaic (PV) installations at seven district schools (Proposed Project), including Cherokee Point Elementary School (ES), Clark Middle School (MS), Doyle ES, Encanto ES, Farb MS, Fay ES, and Jerabek ES. Specifically, the Proposed Project involves the installation of a fixed-tilt canopy system at each of the above-mentioned school sites, located within existing paved and/or disturbed areas.

 

In alignment with the recommendations of the district’s Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee, solar photovoltaic (PV) system installation projects have been initiated to create an energy efficient and renewable power source, delivering a significant cost savings to the district.

 

The Project involves the installation of solar PV canopy systems and associated battery energy storage systems (BESS) on existing school campuses. The solar PV canopy systems would consist of PV arrays mounted onto canopy structures in existing disturbed areas within the campus, such as parking lots and paved playgrounds. All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school properties. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

 

New lighting may be installed under the proposed canopies to provide a similar amount and quality of lighting as the existing lighting on the campus. All new lighting would be down-shielded to prevent any light spillover offsite. Lighting would be turned off consistent with district Administrative Regulation (AR) 3511, which requires all interior and exterior lighting to be turned off when students and staff leave for the day.

 

Construction is anticipated to begin for each site when school is not in session and span over an estimated duration of approximately six months, beginning in the summer 2021. All construction areas and staging areas would be fenced off and isolated from the schools. The heaviest construction activity would occur when school is not in session to avoid operational disruptions.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Proposed Project is subject to CEQA; however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15303 - New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures (Class 3) and Section 15311 Accessory Structures (Class 11). As described above, the Proposed Project consists of solar PV installations at seven school sites. This is consistent with a Class 3 Exemption which allows for limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures, including electric utility extensions; and consistent with a Class 11 Exemption which allows for the placement of minor structures accessory to existing institutional facilities. Therefore, the application of a Class 3 and Class 11 Categorical Exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified Environmental Impact Report.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Analysis: The following provides analysis of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project. A more detailed analysis is provided in the Attached Solar Photovoltaic Installations Project Initial Study (Attachment 1).

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites. Construction of solar PV installations would serve existing schools and would be consistent with existing operations at each of the school sites. Implementation of the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the timing of the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Other projects are planned for each of the identified school sites; however, they would not overlap with the construction of the proposed solar PV projects. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations at each site would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. No work associated with the Proposed Project is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas. On October 22, 2020, Environmental Science Associates Biologist Adrienne Lee completed a survey for active nests, the presence of sensitive habitat, or protected tree and plant species for each of the project sites where trees and/or vegetation may be removed or trimmed. No active nests, sensitive, or protected habitat was identified at any of the project sites. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. There are no designated scenic highways that are visible from each of the project sites. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, there are no district school facilities located on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures and the solar PV installations would be mounted within parking lots or existing paved areas, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at each of the seven district sites is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking because grading and trenching into fossil bearing soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.
