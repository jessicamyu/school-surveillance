RECOMMENDATION: Approve the plans for Lincoln High School.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $64,500 is needed for the 2013-14 school year in order for the Lincoln High School Middle College to launch successfully.  Staff is needed to begin development of the program, start the recruitment process of students, develop the internship program, and initiate the work with the Educational Cultural Complex (ECC).  For the 2014-15 school year, $660,000 is needed to develop, refine, and implement the Lincoln High Middle College.  Budget string:

3637-00000-00-XXX-2490-0000-01000-0000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On December 10, 2013, the Board of Education directed the Superintendent to begin the process of developing the plans for Lincoln High School that will create conditions to provide additional educational and program opportunities for students in a middle college program.  The Lincoln High School Middle College will have a S.T.E.A.M (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts and Math) focus.  The Middle College will place an emphasis on “hands on” project based learning, with an advisory model utilizing coordinators, internships for students, student exhibitions and dual enrollment with San Diego Unified and the community college providing students the opportunity to earn college credit. The district will formalize a partnership with the San Diego Community College District (SDCCD) with support from Big Picture Learning (BPL) for the middle college. The purpose of the Lincoln Middle College is to provide students with a positive college experience earning college credit with a supportive learning environment ensuring success.  Students attending the Middle College will experience challenging common core aligned curriculum on relevant topics and issues related to real world situations. Lincoln High School Middle College will contribute to the San Diego community and have the potential to serve as a national middle college high school where students will have the potential to earn college credits with the experience of internships.  Middle college high schools retain more students in school, engage them fully in productive learning that prepare students for college success, careers, and positive life experiences.

 

The Lincoln High School Middle College design will emphasize the following:

  • Fostering an innovative learning environment through professional development and distributed leadership, enabling administrators and teachers to respond to the challenges of preparing the underprepared (closing the achievement gap)  and advancing the well prepared through mastery, competency, and project-based learning.
     
  • Provide the faculty with state-of-the art professional development and time for developing curriculum modules/Common Core Standards and planning instruction.
     
  • Infuse design and the arts into all aspects of the curriculum as a means of nurturing creativity and innovations and creating competent problem-solvers.
     
  • Meeting diverse students where they are academically when they enter high school, enabling all to master high school work and engage in complex real-world questions through a STEAM lens.
     
  • As students demonstrate key skills, academic course content, and rigor, they can enroll in college level courses and earn multiple years of college credit for free.
     
  • Provide academic, social supports and interventions (academic labs in English/math) that will help students succeed in a challenging course of study.
     
  • The school provides supports to ease the difficult transition between high school and college: students begin taking college courses as early as tenth grade.
     
  • Provide opportunities for students to obtain, in addition to a high school diploma, multiple forms of certifications and credentials in their career interests through industry-certified programs, where available.
     
  • Assess student performance using multiple measures, stressing exhibitions, simulations, and other performances that demonstrate skill and understanding.
     
  • Interdisciplinary projects aligned with Common Core Standards will drive the high school level curriculum.
     
  • Partnerships with local business, academic, and service communities are highly visible in curriculum choices (content, context) and in students’ opportunities for internships and research.
     
  • Ensure that all parents have numerous and varied opportunities to contribute to and monitor their child’s learning program and engage in school activities.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve proclamation recognizing Cabrillo Elementary School's Sister School Grundschule of Neuhaus an der Oste.

 

BACKGROUND: Louis Rose was born in Neuhaus an der Oste in 1807 and arrived in San Diego in 1850. Rose served his community as a member of the Board of Education, County Board of Supervisors, and Grand Jury. In 1869, Rose built the village of Roseville alongside San Diego Bay in the belief that San Diego could become a great seaport. Shortly after his death, Roseville Elementary School opened and Rose’s surviving daughter, Henrietta, was named as its first teacher.

 

As Roseville continued to grow it was incorporated into the Point Loma neighborhood and eventually Roseville Elementary was replaced by Cabrillo Elementary School.

 

On March 29, 2011, the San Diego Unified Board of Education approved the Sister School Relationship between Cabrillo Elementary and the Grundschule of Neuhaus an der Oste to, “develop the ability to successfully use their knowledge of diverse cultures to think and act globally and become thoughtful, responsible and successful global citizens.”

 

In October 2012, principal Doris Henningson and teacher Dorothee Fetz of the Grundschule of Neuhaus an der Oste visited Cabrillo Elementary in an exchange program. Cabrillo Elementary Principal Nestor Suarez has designated faculty member Skye Oluwa to visit Neuhaus an der Oste in an exchange visit. The Louis Rose Society for the Preservation of Jewish History has underwritten both exchange visits.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve restoration of remaining furlough days for SDEA, AASD-Certificated, Confidentials, Non-Represented Management, and Board Trustees.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The total estimated fiscal impact is $31.2 million.  These are current budgeted costs based on preliminary 2014-15 budgeted FTE. Final dollar impact will be dependent on actual employees in 2014-15.  Dollar amounts reflected below include variable benefit costs (statutories).

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: Yes. As a result of the concession agreements and imposed deferrals, the district realized savings in 2012-13 and 2013-14, from deferred salary increases and continued furloughs.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  In the 2012-13 school year, the district academic calendar included 175 of the previous 180 instructional days, which equates to five furlough days for all Certificated, Confidential and Non-Represented employees.  During the 2013-14 school year, the district restored four of the five instructional days, which increased student instructional days to 179. Similarly,  the same number of days were restored for SDEA, but Confidentials, and Non-Represented managers only had 3 of the 4 days restored (consistent with restoration terms). For 2014-15, the board approved restoring the instructional calendar back to 180 instructional days. The increased instructional calendar necessitates a restoration of furlough days for impacted employees. Calendars reflecting the increase to 180 days of student attendance for both traditional and year-round schools are currently being created and will be posted online as soon as they have been completed.

 

The board has previously committed to the principle that each employee group, including those not represented by an employee organization, should contribute equally to addressing the district’s fiscal circumstances. During 2012, the district asked all employees, through their bargaining units or individually, to take salary concessions to address fiscal challenges. SDEA and AASD-Certificated units agreed to defer previously negotiated salary schedule increases and to continue furlough days as a joint effort to meet the district’s financial obligations, contingent upon their restoration should new revenue be identified. While some of the deferrals were restored in 2013-14, as noted above, the remaining deferred salary and furlough days expire on June 30, 2014.

 

The board also imposed salary concessions and furlough days equivalent to what SDEA and AASD-Certificated units agreed to upon Confidential and Non-Represented employees. The board has remained committed to restoring salary concessions to all employees upon similar restoration of SDEA and AASD-Certificated units. To implement the remaining deferrals for all bargaining units, the district has agreed to the restorations as set forth below effective July 1, 2014:

 

SDEA: Consistent with the Tentative Agreement restoration schedule, implement the remaining deferred 5 percent salary increase on the salary schedule effective July 1, 2014. Increased instructional calendar necessary to eliminate the remaining furlough day, for the 2014-15 school year. Total fiscal impact of restoration is $29.6 million.

 

AASD-Certificated: Consistent with the Tentative Agreement restoration schedule, implement remaining deferred 2 percent salary increase and increase to the principal calendar 10 workdays and the Vice Principal by three days work year calendars ranging from two to ten workdays, mitigating any furlough days effective July 1, 2014.Total fiscal impact of restoration is $0.9 million.

 

Confidential Employees: The increase in instructional calendar will result in two additional work days for the 2014-15 school year. Total fiscal impact of restoration is $25,000.

 

Non-Represented Employees: Consistent with restoration of the remainder of imposed deferrals, implement a 5 percent increase on salary schedule. Similarly, the increase in instructional calendar will result in two additional work days for the 2014-15 school year. Total fiscal impact of restoration is $0.6 million.

 

Board Members: The increase in instructional calendar days will result in the equivalent of two additional workdays for the 2014-15 school year. Total fiscal impact of restoration is $1,000.

BACKGROUND: These multiple agreements continue the establishment of the Navy Junior Reserve Officers Training Corps Units at Henry, Point Loma, and Serra high schools.

 

The United States Junior Reserve Officer Training Corps (JROTC) came into being with the passage of the National Defense Act of 1916.  Under the provisions of the Act, high schools were authorized the loan of federal military equipment and the assignment of active duty military personnel as instructors.  In 1964, the Vitalization Act opened JROTC up to the other services and replaced active duty instructors with retirees who worked for and were cost shared by the schools.


Title 10 of the U.S. Code declares that "the purpose of Junior Reserve Officers' Training Corp, is to instill in students in United States secondary educational institutions the value of citizenship, service to the United States, personal responsibility, and a sense of accomplishment."

 

The JROTC Program has changed greatly over the years.  Once looked upon primarily as a source of enlisted recruits and officer candidates, it became a citizenship program devoted to the moral, physical and educational uplift of American youth.  Although the program retained its military structure and the resultant ability to infuse in its student cadets a sense of discipline and order, it shed most of its early military content.


The study of ethics, citizenship, communication, leadership, life skills and other subjects designed to prepare young men and women to take their place in adult society, evolved as the core of the program.  More recently, an improved student centered curriculum focusing on character building and civic responsibility Is being presented in every JROTC classroom.


JROTC is a continuing success story. From a modest beginning of 6 schools in 1916, JROTC has expanded to over 3,200 high schools within every state in the nation, as well as American schools overseas. Cadet enrollment has grown to 281,000 cadets with 4,000 professional instructors in the classrooms.  Comprised solely of Army, Navy, Air Force and Marine retirees, the JROTC instructors serve as mentors developing the outstanding young citizens of our country.

BACKGROUND:  A proposed revision of the rate schedules has been prepared and is attached.

 

Under the Civic Center Act (Education Code section 38130 et seq.), the governing board of any school district may grant the use of facilities or grounds upon terms it deems proper so long as the “civic center use” is subordinate to, and does not interfere with, the instructional program or other public school purposes.

 

Civic Center use may be on a free use, direct cost recovery, fair rental value or commercial basis.  Currently, community-based free user groups that have been formed with volunteers to advise public agencies (i.e., City of San Diego) may, upon request, be exempt from the permit processing fee and insurance requirements.  On May 28, 2013, the board also approved exempting the Parent Teacher Associations from the permit processing fee.

 

Senate Bill (SB) 1404 (2012) which expands, until January 1, 2020, the definition of direct costs that a school district governing board may charge for the use of school facilities or grounds pursuant to Education Code (EC) Section 38134 (the Civic Center Act). These regulations are proposed, as required by EC Section 38134(h), to be used by a school district in determining the proportionate share and the specific allowable costs that a school district may include as direct costs for the use of its school facilities or grounds.

 

Per this guidance from the state, staff is proposing that the 2014-15 rate increase is tied to CPI instead of COLA. The CPI is calculated based upon changes in prices paid by urban consumers for representative goods and services, which is a more accurate measure of the district's direct costs (i.e. utilities, cleaning supplies). The CPI increase is 2.2 percent for the 2014-15 year and the rate would be rounded to the nearest half dollar.

 

SB 1404 regulations establish a consistent method by which school districts will calculate fees for the use of school facilities or grounds by outside entities. District staff is working to determine the direct cost proportionate share and the specific allowable costs.  The calculated fees will represent the maximum amount a school district is authorized to charge, which does not preclude a school district from electing to charge less, or to assess no fee at all. Staff will return to the board when this analysis is complete.

 

This item is a part of the district's real estate utilization strategy, in support of Vision 2020 goals for schools to serve as neighborhood centers. A key component of the real estate utilization strategy is to build capacity in real estate department services to generate new ongoing revenues.
