4. Resolution in Support of SB 1058 (Hueso) Digital Divide Relief Plan (Beiser/McQuary)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of SB 1058, the Digital Divide Relief Plan, which will ensure that all Californians, especially vulnerable populations, have reliable access to the telecommunications resources necessary to obtain essential services during emergencies, such as distance learning for TK-12 students, and establish requirements for reliable telecommunications that will better prepare Californians for ongoing and future disasters and emergencies. The San Diego Unified School District’s successful effort to establish and maintain equity in distance learning by distributing more than 50,000 devices and ensuring connectivity to all students regardless of where they live or family income, enabled more than 96% of our students to participate in distance learning with their teachers from the safety of their homes during the COVID-19 pandemic.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The board recognizes that the lack of broadband internet accessibility during the current COVID-19 pandemic has limited many students’ ability to access essential educational services such as distance learning. For some students this inaccessibility stems from the absence of broadband infrastructure in their areas, and even in areas where infrastructure and providers exist, the cost of internet plans may render broadband inaccessible.  Traditionally, students without broadband at home may have been able to access internet services at public facilities that are unavailable during the current pandemic. While several internet service providers are offering low-cost or no-cost internet plans for qualified low-income Californians, the eligibility and terms of service for these plans are not clear and consistent, and many Californians who have been unable to enroll in public assistance programs are ineligible for these plans despite having lost all household income. In addition to lack of broadband availability and affordability, approximately 250,000 of California’s most vulnerable residents are facing the possibility of higher, unaffordable basic home phone service due to reductions in federal subsidies for the Lifeline Universal Telephone Service. The digital divide may prevent many of these households from obtaining other forms of telecommunications.

Given a reduction in the minimum child days of enrollment from 364,077.9 to 354,484.0, and combined with an increase in the rate per child per day from $49.85 to $50.70, the revised grant amount is $17,972,350.00, a reduction of $176,932.00.
