RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution in the Matter of Calling for a Moratorium on Charter School Expansion and Strengthening of Transparency and Oversight in Governance Practices.

 

BACKGROUND: Since the passage of the 1992 California Charter Schools Act, the number of publicly-funded, and privately-operated charter schools has grown rapidly statewide.

 

In 2018, researchers from Columbia University and the University of Florida found that charter schools nationwide are less likely to respond to enrollment requests from students with significant special needs.


A May 2018 a study by In the Public Interest found that charter schools led to a combined net fiscal shortfall of over $142 million annually for three California school districts, including over $66 million annually for San Diego Unified.

 

In August 2018, an evaluation of 43 LCAPs for charter schools in five districts, found charter school engagement, transparency, and accountability woefully lacking to such a degree that it was sometimes impossible to determine how charter schools were spending millions of dollars.

 

Current law requires district campuses to accommodate co-locations of charter schools, resulting in shortages of resources and space and increasing tension and conflict within school communities.

 

Local school boards are the best determiners of how local education dollars should be spent to benefit local students, including the appropriate number and type of schools in their district, yet are prohibited under current law from considering such factors when deciding whether or not to approve a charter school petition.

 

The California State Board of Education and the County Boards of Education currently may authorize charter schools within San Diego Unified boundaries over the objections of democratically elected School Board Members.

 

The NAACP, the Journey for Justice Alliance, the Movement for Black Lives, the Los Angeles Unified School District, the West Contra Costa Unified School District, the Anaheim Union High School District, the City of Richmond, the City of Huntington Park, and other organizations have called for and/or passed a moratorium on charter school expansion and for the strengthening of oversight in governance and practice of charter schools.

The Community Coalition of University Heights (CCUH) was honored by the Congress of History at its 54th Annual History Conference with a Special Project Award for the Coalition’s contributions to the preservation of the historic Teachers Training Annex 1 and the original Alice Birney Kindergarten Annex 2.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: In 2015 the Advisory Committee on Utilization of Excess School Property submitted a property management plan to the Board of Education (Board) in which it recommended that the former Forward Elementary School site continue being leased.

BACKGROUND: In 1984, the Board of Education declared the site of the former Forward Elementary School, located at 6460 Boulder Lake Avenue San Diego, CA 92119, as surplus property.).  The property’s current lessee has provided excellent tenancy, grounds improvements, and consistent on-time rent payments.  San Diego Unified (District) staff would like to engage in direct negotiations with the existing tenant of the property to enter into a 20-year lease. Currently the site is leased to Specialized Education of California, Inc who runs the Springall Academy and Sierra Academy of San Diego on the project site. Both schools have a combined enrollment of approximately 45 students. No physical or operational changes to the existing site are proposed as part of the new 20-year lease.

 

Education Code 33050 allows the District to apply for a waiver from the requirements of Education Code 17455 (and related provisions governing the sale or lease of surplus property) from the SBE.  Under the general waiver authority of Education Code 33050-33053, the particular Education Code or California Code of Regulations sections to be waived are: 17455, 17466, 17468, 17469, 17472, 17473, 17474, 17475 (code sections).

 

Obtaining a waiver of certain language within these code sections allows the District to negotiate with and potentially lease the property to the existing tenant.  Direct negotiation brings substantial financial savings to the District, including but not limited to, elimination of potential substantial re-tenanting costs, gaps in rental income and broker commissions.  The waiver provides the District flexibility in managing its real property all while ensuring a public process whereby the agreed upon terms of the new lease with the existing tenant are shared openly.

 

In accordance with SBE requirements, bargaining units were informed via letter dated April 12, 2019, of the District’s intent to consider applying for a waiver of Education Code requirements related to surplus property.  To follow are bargaining units responses with respect to proposed action:

 

  • AASD (Certificated and Classified) – Neutral (Attachment 2)
  • OSS – Support (Attachment 3)
  • OTBS – Support (Attachment 3)
  • PARA – Support (Attachment 3)
  • POA – Neutral (Attachment 4)
  • SDEA - Neutral (Attachment 5)

 

CEQA ANALYSIS: The proposed new 20-year lease of the former Forward Elementary School site (Proposed Project) is considered a project under CEQA and is subject to the requirements of the Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000, et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et seq., as amended). However, the Proposed Project is exempt from further environmental analysis under the Common Sense Exemption, Section 15061(b)(3), which states that an “…activity is covered by the common sense exemption that CEQA applies only to projects which have the potential for causing a significant effect on the environment. Where it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that the activity in question may have a significant effect on the environment, the activity is not subject to CEQA.”

 

As described above, the Proposed Project would not result in any physical or operational changes, specifically changes to enrollment or capacity, of the existing site. Therefore, it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment.

BACKGROUND: The Human Resource Services Division launched a Position Description Review Project in the Fall of 2015, as part of the district’s obligation to maintain the classification plan and in response to requests from the bargaining units and management team to develop a process to regularly review positions. The draft position descriptions were prepared using the data collected from employees through an online survey tool as well as management feedback. The goal of this system-wide review of classified and classified management position descriptions was to ensure that position descriptions were more than a list of tasks and duties. A well-written position description gives the reader an understanding of the basic and essential functions as well as the requisite knowledge, skills, and abilities required to perform successfully in the position. By updating position descriptions, we are able to clearly define the roles and interactions, and enhance the efficiency of the organization.

The Committee’s role is to accept, review, and make recommendations to the Superintendent for requests for classification review. The committee consists of one representative from each classified bargaining unit and an equal number of district representatives Reclassification Committee.

 

The classified bargaining units received the first set of draft job descriptions on April 7, 2017, the next set on September 28, 2017, and the parties have been in ongoing negotiations since that time. Approved position descriptions are brought before the Board of Education for approval  on an ongoing basis as the parties reach agreement.

CEQA ANALYSIS: The Proposed Project can be found exempt from environmental review pursuant to CEQA Guidelines section 15061(b)(3). The activity is covered by the general rule which exempts activities that can be seen with certainty to have no possibility for causing a significant effect on the environment. The installation of HVAC controls at existing developed school sites would not create any significant environmental impacts.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites. Installation of HVAC controls is consistent with existing uses at each of the existing school sites. Generally the surrounding land uses at each site consist of residential housing and the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations at each site would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the installation HVAC controls at eleven school sites. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about each of the school sites or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, none of the project sites are located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures and the HVAC controls would replace existing control units, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at each of the eleven sites is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archaeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because grading and trenching into non-native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.

Section 15301: Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination.

 

Section 15311: Class 11 consists of construction, or placement of minor structures accessory to (appurtenant to) existing commercial, industrial, or institutional facilities.

 

Section 15314: Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25% or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption.

 

As described above, the Proposed Project includes the repair, maintenance, and minor alterations to an existing public school facility that involves negligible expansion of use. The Proposed Project would also include the construction of minor structures accessory to (appurtenant to) an existing institutional (educational) facility. The Proposed Project would not result in the addition of 10 or more classrooms or result in a 25-percent increase in student capacity. Therefore, the application of the above mentioned categorical exemptions are appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

a) Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.

b) Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

c) Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

d) Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.

e) Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

f) Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

a) Location. Construction and operation of the Proposed Project would be contained to the existing Carson Elementary School campus. Land use designations adjacent to the Proposed Project site are comprised of residential uses. The Proposed Project site and adjacent areas are heavily disturbed and not considered as environmentally sensitive. Additionally, existing site use will not change as a result of the Proposed Project. The Proposed Project will not impact environmentally sensitive areas or an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. This exception does not apply to the Proposed Project.

b) Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed on the school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project. This exception does not apply to the Proposed Project.

c) Significant Effect. The Proposed Project is not considered as an unusual circumstance as these activities fulfill the demands of the existing school and include standard maintenance of building facilities. The WSM alterations and improvements of the existing building are necessary to meet current standards for public health and safety. Such activities are not considered as an unusual circumstance as these activities do not include specifications that would be considered out of the ordinary. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

d) Scenic Highways. There are several designated and eligible state scenic highways throughout San Diego County; however, the nearest scenic highway is SR-52, between Santo Road and Mast Boulevard, and is located approximately 6.2 miles to the northeast and is too far to provide views of the project site. The Proposed Project will not involve activities resulting in directly, or indirectly impacting an officially designated highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway as the activities associated with the Proposed Project will occur within the existing school campus.

d) Scenic highways deemed eligible or officially designated are not located in the immediate vicinity of Carson Elementary School. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

e) Hazardous Waste Sites. The Proposed Project will occur within an existing, operational school. A review of federal and state standard and supplemental databases indicated that the Proposed Project site is not located within any identified hazardous material site pursuant to Government Code section 65962.5. A historical resources evaluation (HRE) of Carson Elementary School was conducted by a historian/architectural historian on October 10, 2018 (See Attachment 2 – Initial Study Checklist). The HRE applied California Register of Historical Resources (CRHR) significance criteria in accordance with section 15064.5(a)(2)-(3) of the State CEQA Guidelines and the criteria outlined in section 5024.1 of the PRC. The HRE concluded that Carson Elementary School is eligible for listing in the CRHR under Criteria 1 and 3. Under CRHR Criterion 1, the school is an important representation of the historically significant event of Linda Vista’s development by the federal government. Under CRHR Criterion 3, it is significant as an excellent example of a Moderne-style 1940s school that features prominently visible bas reliefs created by the renowned artist, Donal Hord. Carson Elementary School qualifies as a historical resource as defined in section 15064.5 of the State CEQA Guidelines. However, the Proposed Project design elements would minimize the potential visual disruptiveness of HVAC installation, and the combination of HVAC installation, window and door replacement at the Auditorium lobby, and new perimeter fencing would not cause a substantial adverse change in the historical and architectural significance of the school. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

CEQA ANALYSIS: The Proposed Project can be found exempt from environmental review pursuant to CEQA Guidelines section 15061(b)(3). The activity is covered by the general rule which exempts activities that can be seen with certainty to have no possibility for causing a significant effect on the environment. The removal and replacement of matting and surfacing at existing developed school sites would not create any significant environmental impacts.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

 

a) Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.

b) Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

c) Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

d) Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.

e) Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

f) Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

 

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

 

a) Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites. Installation of playground matting is consistent with existing uses at each of the existing school sites. Generally the surrounding land uses at each site consist of residential housing and the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

b) Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations at each site would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

c) Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the installation playground matting at nine school sites on existing playgrounds. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about each of the school sites or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.

d) Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

e) Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, none of the school sites are included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

f) Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at each of the nine sites is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archaeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because grading and trenching into non-native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.
