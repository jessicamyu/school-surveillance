Recommendation: The Superintendent is recommending closure of North Park Elementary School at the end of the 2008-09 school year in order to relocate the Alternative Learning for Behavior and Attitude (ALBA) program to the North Park site, beginning with the ALBA High School in 2009-10 and ALBA Middle School in 2010-11. ALBA High School, serves 40-50 ninth through twelfth grade students during the school year, is located to the east of Mann Middle School, and consists of ten portable buildings which are between 41 and 50 years old. The structures have reached the end of their life cycle, and are in poor condition. ALBA Middle School, serves 35-70 sixth through eighth grade students during the school year, is located to the north east of Marston Middle School, and consists of nine portable buildings ranging in age from 15 to 58 years old. The Superintendent is also recommending a waiver of the district’s applicable Administrative Procedure 3113 and supporting policies, to expedite the closure of North Park Elementary School and the relocation of the ALBA program to that site.

This item has been changed from First Reading to a proposed Action item on May 26: 2009-10 Use of North Park Elementary School Site and Relocation of the Alternative Learning for Behavior and Attitude (ALBA) Program, First Reading Action

Fiscal Implication: The cost of these modifications and moves is being estimated, but will be funded through Proposition S, which included resources for the upgrade of ALBA. Proposition S includes the construction of a new classroom building for ALBA at the Twain site in Linda Vista, at a cost of approximately $6.8 million (see Attachment 4). Utilizing the North Park site instead, with minor modifications, will result in significant savings to Proposition S that can be redirected to other projects.

Consolidation of the two ALBA campuses to North Park will generate savings in utilities, custodial, maintenance, and landscaping services. The best estimate is that this will be less than $100K after the first year off-set stemming from the demolition of the old portables.

Background: North Park Elementary is one of six schools recommended for closure by the board and superintendent-appointed Small Schools Committee in January 2009. The school’s enrollment has fallen by 105 students in five years, and stood at 123 K-5 students in fall 2008. The school is a little over one block to the north of Jefferson Elementary and four blocks to the south of Garfield Elementary. Both Garfield and Jefferson have also lost enrollment over the last five years and have the capacity to accommodate students currently attending North Park.

The closure of North Park Elementary School and the relocation of the ALBA program to the North Park site will:
  • Permit ALBA to move out of substandard facilities to a stand-alone site, in compliance with State Education Code.
  • Give ALBA students the ability to travel to their new school location using public transportation along the El Cajon Boulevard and University Avenue corridors.
  • Allow better utilization of the district’s facilities, achieving higher capacities at different campuses.
  • Result in savings to the Proposition S program, which can be redirected to other projects. Currently, the ALBA Program will have a $6.8M classroom constructed at Linda Vista site of Twain High School under the auspices of Prop S.
  • Result in savings in utilities custodial, maintenance, and landscaping services that would be mitigated in the first year due to the proposed demolition of the substandard portable classrooms at the current ALBA sites. Subsequently, there will be recurring facilities savings of less than $100K for the two sites combined.
The superintendent is also recommending a waiver of the district’s applicable Administrative Procedure 3113 and supporting policies, to expedite the closure of North Park Elementary School and the relocation of the ALBA program to that site.

In accordance with the provisions of Education Code Section 46392, the superintendent recommends approval of submission of a “Request for Allowance of Attendance Because of Emergency Conditions” (Form J-13A and Affidavit of Governing Board Members) to the California Department of Education (CDE) for a substitution of two days of attendance from May 4 through May 5, 2009, during which two schools were closed as a consequence of the H1N1 Flu Virus.

The CDE permits districts to claim ADA and instructional time credits for days on which schools are forced to close. Approval of the request allows the school agency to receive credit for the lost ADA for the days of the closure as for the missed instructional time. Once the credit is approved, the district is no longer required to make up the days, and receives the ADA and instructional minutes as though the emergency did not occur.

Furthermore, pursuant to this provision, the district may claim loss in ADA for the days of the school year related to the emergency. When the Governor declared a State of Emergency in San Diego County, any decrease in ADA was deemed to be material, and the usual threshold of a minimum decrease of 10 percent does not need to be met. The claim for a material decrease may be filed at a later date once the district has determined there is a decrease in ADA related to the H1N1 Flu Virus.

The J-13A, along with the board’s affidavit will be submitted to the County Office of Education and subsequently forwarded to the CDE together with those of the other county school districts.

This request, if approved, will maintain the district’s ADA funding at the level that otherwise would have been received if the H1N1 Flu Virus closures had not occurred.

First reading of corrective action plans for ten district schools in Program Improvement (PI) Year 3 status and restructuring proposals for three district schools in PI Year 4 status. The target board date for the adoption of these documents for implementation in 2009–10 is June 9, 2009.

General Information: Program Improvement (PI) Status: Under the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 (NCLB), a Title I school that, for two consecutive years, does not meet or exceed a series of annual academic performance goals known as Adequate Yearly Progress (AYP) is designated PI. Such schools must provide specified types of services and/or interventions during each year that they are identified as PI. A school is eligible to exit PI if it makes AYP for two consecutive years.

District Schools in PI Year 3, 2009-10: Corrective Action: A school that enters its third consecutive year in PI status must be identified for corrective action. Corrective action is a significant intervention in a school that is designed to remedy the school's persistent inability to make adequate yearly progress toward all students’ becoming proficient in English language arts and mathematics.

District schools in PI Year 3 status: Carson, Edison, Horton, and Rosa Parks elementary schools; Language Academy; Lewis, Pershing, and Wangenheim middle schools; San Diego Communications, Investigations in a Multicultural Atmosphere (CIMA) and San Diego Media, Visual, and Performing Arts (MVPA) high schools. The school corrective action plans were developed by school teams in collaboration with the Office of Federal and Special Programs.
Fiscal implications: Existing district Title I funds (approximately $20,000 per school) were used to support the costs of web-based reading interventions and related professional development; and "push-in" teachers.
District Schools in PI Year 4, 2009–10: School Restructuring: A school that enters its fourth consecutive year in PI status is required to prepare a plan to restructure that will be implemented the following year, in this case in 2009–10. The restructuring plan must describe in detail the changes in governance, instructional practice and staffing that will be required to improve students’ academic achievement and enable the school to meet its AYP goals.

District schools in PI Year 4 status: Encanto Elementary School; Creative, Performing and Media Arts (CPMA) Middle School; and Clairemont High School. The restructuring proposals were developed by School Program Improvement Leadership Teams composed of teachers, administrators, classified staff and parents, in collaboration with the Office of Federal and Special Programs.

Fiscal implications: Each school received regular Title I funding based on their school's ranking. In addition, each PI 4 school received $170 per student per board action on February 17, 2009.

RECOMMENDATION: Use of available State School Facilities Funds for elementary classroom technology upgrades in the amount estimated to be $9,200,000 $9,000,000.

State School Facilities funding is awarded to local educational agencies throughout California for the construction, modernization, and emergency repair of schools. This request is to authorize the expenditure of available State School Facilities funds to purchase and install five network appliances in all district elementary classrooms. Many district elementary classrooms have outdated equipment and are greatly in need of new technology tools. The purchase of new classroom technology utilizing funding identified for this purpose will provide students equal access to software-based interventions in mathematics and language arts, and teachers with ready access to on-line assessments and student data. In addition, this districtwide initiative will serve to enhance teaching in the classroom, provide more opportunities for interactive learning, and promote individual instruction to better meet the needs of all students.

Background
The San Diego Unified School District has a state-approved Educational Technology Strategic Plan that provides a roadmap for the district to improve student academic achievement through the use of technology. The goals of the Educational Technology Strategic Plan include integrating technology into instruction, making classroom management more efficient, enhancing communication with parents and the community, preparing teachers to use technology as an integral tool to enhance and support their teaching, and providing the needed technology infrastructure.

One objective of the plan is to provide all students with adequate access to computing resources to meet their learning needs. To address this objective, the district has developed a long-term strategy designed to help prepare students to learn, live and work in the 21st century by providing them access to software tools and learning resources.

Title II, Part D of the federal No Child Left Behind Act (NCLB) established a primary federal goal for technology in education: To improve student academic achievement through the use of technology. The goal of this component of NCLB is to “Assist every student in crossing the digital divide by ensuring that every student is technologically literate by the time the student finishes the eighth grade, regardless of the student’s race, ethnicity, gender, family income, geographic location, or disability.”

Why Students Learn with Technology
The U.S. Department of Education conducted an online survey of 210,000 K-12 students (NetDay, 2003) to obtain students’ perspectives on the use of technology in education. They found that today’s students are very technology-savvy, feel strongly about the positive value of technology and rely upon technology as an essential and preferred component of every aspect of their lives. Students are not just using technology differently today but are approaching their lives and their daily activities differently because of the technology. As students get older, their use of technology becomes more sophisticated, but, comparatively, the younger students are on a fast track to becoming greater technology users and advocates. Students asked for more opportunities to use computers for online access to information, school assignments, and tutoring.

Technology can enable the development of critical thinking skills when students use technology presentation and communication tools to present, publish, and share results of projects. Students acquire some of the prerequisite skills for workforce preparedness when they learn to use applications used in the world of work such as word processors, spreadsheets, computer-aided drawing, website development programs and the Internet. Students become independent, critical thinkers when they use the Internet to research topics, share information, and complete a collaborative project.

SDUSD Digital Divide: Elementary vs. Secondary
Each year, the California Department of Education requires school sites to complete the State Technology Survey (STS) and the Ed Tech Profile (ETP). The STS is an equipment survey, which reports the number of computers by school site. According to 2006-2007 data, 77 percent of district computers reported is now greater than four years old with many being more than ten years old. Further disaggregation of the data shows that the ratio of students to computers (less than four years) at elementary sites is approximately 12-to-1. The Ed Tech Profile is a technology proficiency assessment that each district teacher updates annually. The ETP provides important information about teachers’ use of technology for teaching and learning. The table below summarizes 2006 data in key proficiencies that demonstrate the digital divided that currently exist between elementary and secondary classrooms. Teachers at the secondary level report a significantly higher level of technology integration into student learning.


Proposed Initiative
The passage of the Prop S bond measure will provide 400 million dollars over the next 15 years for the upgrading of 7,000 classrooms with new integrated technology. As a result, many school sites may have to wait up to several years before classroom upgrades occur.

District staff proposes an Initiative to close the digital divide at elementary sites and bridge the gap until Prop. S technology upgrades are completed at all sites. Utilizing State Facilities funding and potential external funding sources, the district would systematically provide 5-7 computer workstations in every upper grade elementary classroom by December 2009. Through this Initiative, student increased access to an internet connected computer will provide students “just-in-time” access to online education software and resources.

The Initiative will 1) address the lack of computing resources in elementary classrooms, 2) begin to change instructional delivery in ways that will prepare students to learn, live and work in the 21st century; (3) provide a scalable and sustainable bridging solution to provide students and teachers with access to affordable technology tools; and 4) prepare students to meet the NCLB mandate to be technologically proficient by the completion of grade 8.

Low Cost Sustainable Computing Alternative
The proposed initiative includes the use of network appliances in place of traditional computers. These appliances are a proven technology that provides the same processing power at a fraction of the cost and easy to maintain and support. These internet connected network Appliances have no hard drive with all the processing is done from servers located in our data center. From an [information technology] IT perspective, software only needs to be loaded on the servers, not on every machine. Also, since these network appliances have no moving parts, they require little maintenance and have a usable life span of 7 to 10 years, freeing up technicians to manage their servers and networks from a single location. Network appliances cost approximately $385, or half the price of a conventional computer, and one-third the price of a laptop.
