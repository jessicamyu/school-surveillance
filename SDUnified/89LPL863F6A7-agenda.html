On November 30, 2010, the district publicly advertised Request for Qualifications (RFQ) No. PS-11-0577-02 for competitive proposals for the desired work.  The RFQ was advertised in the San Diego Daily Transcript and Union-Tribune, posted on DemandStar to 250 vendors, and forwarded to more than 69 firms in the Facilities Outreach database and community.

The Company Nurse Program targets the reduction of Workers Compensation claims, frequency, and costs reduction while ensuring that injured employees receive immediate medical advice and referrals to appropriate medical and healthcare providers within the districts contracted Medical Provider Network.  Services were billed directly to the claims file at a rate of $150.00 per claim and billing was coordinated with York Insurance Service Group, Inc. - California on behalf of the district. Only triaged calls resulting in referrals for medical treatment constitute a claim and result in a billing to the district.  Expected benefits from the Company Nurse Program included:

  • Expedited medical referral process for employees;
  • Timely and accurate reporting of occupational injuries/illnesses, including critical reporting to OSHA of serious    incidents; and
  • Reduction of reported number of industrial claims and costs and increases in return to work, resulting in a 20-30% decrease in overall Workers Compensation claims costs in the first year of service.

BACKGROUND: In September 2010, Governor Schwarzenegger signed the Kindergarten Readiness Act.  SB 1381 changed California’s kindergarten entry date from December 2 to September 1 so children enter kindergarten at age 5.  The legislation will phase in the new age requirement by moving the cutoff date one month a year for three years, beginning in 2012.  It also established a new transitional kindergarten, the first year of a two-year kindergarten program of those children between September and December.

 

To help the youngest kindergartners be successful, the two-year kindergarten program is intended for children whose birthdays fall between July 1 and December 2. Transition kindergarten will serve as a bridge for children who need the gift of time, essential to gaining confidence in their social and academic lives.  Research demonstrates that children who attend high-quality early learning programs are 11 percent less likely to be placed in Special Education.  Transitional kindergarten could potentially reduce the number of children receiving special education services in Grades K-12.  Additionally, children who attend high-quality, early learning programs are less likely to drop out of high school or be held back a grade, and are more likely to score better on reading and math tests.

 

Many districts throughout California have already implemented transition kindergarten programs.  Los Angeles Unified is in their first year of a transition kindergarten program.  Districts in Poway, Fresno, Palo Alto, and Orange City have also implemented pilot programs for two-year kindergarten programs.

 

There is a need in the district for transitional kindergarten.  Currently, there are three elementary schools in the district that offer a Primary Enrichment Program (PEP), Scripps E.B., Hickman, and Marvin.  This program offers a developmental kindergarten class designed for children who would benefit from two years of a kindergarten program.

 

Transition kindergarten will be available on a voluntary basis. Prospective pilot sites include:  Baker, Balboa, Carver, Chollas/Mead, Dingeman, Freese, Gage, Horton, Lindbergh/Schweitzer, Normal Heights, Rosa Parks, Porter, Ross, Silver Gate, Sunset View, Tierrasanta, and Washington elementary schools.

BACKGROUND:  At the May 10 special meeting, the board received a report from consultants Jones Lang Lasalle, who were hired by the board to analyze the district’s 27 properties that have been declared excess by the Advisory Committee on the Utilization of Excess School Property (ACUESP).  Following the presentation, the board directed staff to prepare an outline for a potential study of all of the district’s property.

The second phase’s duration is dependent on how many properties merit deeper study as a result of the first phase.  These properties would be assessed in terms of such factors as their highest and best use, zoning issues, value range, timing and risk assessment.  A final strategic plan on the disposition of those properties, including impacts such as moving costs, renovations, etc, would be the final product to be presented for the board’s consideration.
