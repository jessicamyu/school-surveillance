RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution in Support of Senate Constitutional Amendment (SCA) 5, which would allow school district voters to approve a school district parcel tax with 55% voter approval, rather than a two-thirds vote requirement. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  Despite state funding increases in the past six years, many school districts, such as San Diego USD, are struggling to maintain quality educational services as we face increasing cost and enrollment barriers.  School districts across the state are grappling with growing fiscal challenges such as rising pension obligations and costs to administer Special Education programs.  For many districts, the growth for these costs is outpacing the amounts we receive in discretionary spending from the state.

 

Parcel taxes are one of the few ways local school districts are able to raise revenue to supplement the funds we rely on from the state.  However, parcel taxes remain inaccessible to many communities because of the two-thirds voter threshold.  More than 80% of the 26 local school parcel tax measures that failed in California from 2012-2017, would have passed if a supermajority vote of 55% was required for approval, instead of a two-thirds vote.  The 55% vote threshold for school parcel tax revenues would be consistent with the 55% voter requirement for school facilities bonds that the voters approved in 2000, via Proposition 39.

 

Additionally, SCA 5 builds in transparency requirements to hold school districts accountable for the parcel tax revenues granted by their local communities.  SCA 5 requires school districts to identify how the funds will be used, and it also requires an annual independent audit of the parcel tax proceeds collected and how they are expended.

BACKGROUND: The project generally consists of removing and replacing main electrical switchgear at De Portola Middle School, 11010 Clairemont Mesa Blvd, San Diego, CA 92124.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111(b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000.00 or more. The Proposed Project can be found exempt from environmental review pursuant to CEQA Guidelines section 15061(b)(3). The activity is covered by the general rule which exempts activities that can be seen with certainty to have no possibility for causing a significant effect on the environment. The removal and replacement of main electrical switchgear, where switchgear currently exists, would not create any new significant environmental impacts as described below.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.

  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.

  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of an existing elementary school in a suburban community. The site has been developed as a school since 1986 and all areas on the school are either paved or disturbed, and therefore are not environmentally sensitive. Surrounding land uses consist of residential housing and open space. However, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed on the school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the installation replacement electrical switch gear. The project site and surrounding neighborhood are located within a suburbanized area, and no work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.

  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the Proposed Project is not located on a site where hazardous materials or hazardous waste have been identified, nor is it located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. The project site is located within a Formerly Used Defense Site (FUDS) boundary where the potential to discover Unexploded Ordinance (UXO) exists when grading or excavating. However, the Proposed Project would not require excavation or grading and impacts related to UXOs are less than significant. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  6. Historical Resources. De Portola Middle School was constructed in 1986 and implementation of the Proposed Project would not cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource as defined in Section 15064.5 of the State CEQA Guidelines, and this exception does not apply to the project.
