2. REVISED 6-9-14: San Diego Unified Pledge as Part of the Council of Great City Schools Efforts Regarding African American, Hispanic, and Males of Color
Meeting
Jun 10, 2014 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve San Diego Unified pledge as part of the Council of Great City Schools regarding African American, Hispanic, and males of color.

 

BACKGROUND:  The Council of Great City Schools is joining hands with the Obama Administration and 55 other urban school districts in committing to improving the outcomes for males of color.  San Diego Unified joins with school district colleagues across the country and pledges to address these important issues.

 

The district's Pledge document will be added to this agenda item on Monday, June 9, 2014.

 

Whereas, some 32 percent of the nation’s African American males and some 39 percent of the nation’s Hispanic males are enrolled in school in one of the Great City School systems; and

 

Whereas, the academic achievement of males of color in the nation’s urban school systems and nationally is below any acceptable rate for these young people to be successful in college and careers; and

 

Whereas, disproportionate numbers of males of color drop out of urban school and often have low attendance rates, and

 

Whereas, the nation’s Great City Schools have an obligation to teach all students under their aegis to the highest academic standards and prepare them for successful participation in the nation

 

Be It Therefore Resolved that, the San Diego Unified School District pledges to ensure that its pre-school efforts better serve males of color and their academic and social development, as part of the Harvard Study, and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will adopt and implement elementary and middle school efforts to increase the pipeline of males of color who are succeeding academically and socially in urban schools and who are on track to succeed in high school, as can be observed in the long-term Vision 2020, Common Core implementation and Restorative Practices and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will keep data and establish protocols that will allow it to monitor the progress of males of color and other students in schools and appropriately intervene at the earliest warning signs, as it is currently developing an “Early Warning System;” and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will adopt and implement promising and proven approaches to reducing absenteeism, especially chronic absenteeism, among males of color, as indicated in the board adopted Blueprint to Accelerate the Achievement of African American and African Students; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will continue to develop initiatives and regularly report on progress in retaining males of color in school and reducing disproportionate suspension and expulsion rates, through its work with the Blueprint Task Force and the Latino Advisory Committee; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will develop initiatives and regularly report on progress in increasing the numbers of males of color and other students participating in advanced placement and honors courses and gifted and talented programs, including the work of the Blueprint Task Force and the Latino Advisory Committee; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will strongly encourage colleges of education to adopt curriculum that addresses the academic, cultural, and social needs of Males of Color, and that the district will maintain data on how these teachers do with males of color, as reflected in the work of the Teacher Pipeline Task Force and the San Diego UNIFIED through Diversity Committee; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will develop initiatives and regularly report on progress in increasing the numbers of males of color and other students who complete the FASFA; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will work to reduce as appropriate the disproportionate numbers of males of color in special education courses through its work with the Special Education Community Advisory Committee (CAC), the Blueprint Task Force, the Latino Advisory Committee; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will work to transform high schools with persistently low graduation rates among males of color and others and to provide literacy and engagement initiatives with parents; and

 

That the San Diego Unified School District will continue to lead and engage in a broader discussion and examination of how issues of race, language, and culture affect the work of the district, examples of this can be seen in the Martin-Zimmerman dialogues the district organized, and ongoing collaborations with the Anti-Defamation League and the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP).

5. REVISED 6-10-14: Resolution to Improve Education While Living Within Our Means: Continuation of the Three-Year Budget/School Stabilization Plan (Evans/Foster)
Meeting
Jun 10, 2014 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Trustees Evans and Foster submit for board approval resolution to improve education while living within our means.

 

BACKGROUND: The passage of Proposition 30 stopped the hemorrhaging of state budget cuts to education; the current plan increases funding so slowly that we will not return to the 2007 level of funding until 2020.

 

The new Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) addresses the issue of equity of school funding across the state, it does not address the adequacy of funding, since the base funding for all schools across the state is woefully inadequate, as indicated by the fact that we rank between 46th and 49th among states on per pupil funding.

 

In July 2013, upon adoption of a Three-Year Budget Plan (2013-14, 2014-15, 2015-16), we acknowledged that we would need a period of three years to stabilize the budget to simply meet current operations, staffing levels, and class size, meaning there is no “extra” money.

 

The district adopted a School Stabilization Fund with funds from property sales to allow us to bridge the gap in the three years that it would require for income to catch up with current expenses.

 

It is not sustainable to continue selling properties that generate annual revenue to the district, nor is it reasonable to ask for further concessions from district employees who have already helped stabilize schools in the past few years through their sacrifice.

 

The estimated budget shortfall for the 2014-15 school year is $97 $106.4 million and $69 $63.7 million for 2015-16.

 

In 2014, the board offered an early retirement incentive to teachers and other certificated staff to reduce ongoing payroll expenses as a better option than issuing layoff notices, which would have created instability for students, particularly in high poverty schools.

 

The district cannot afford to hire additional staff to replace all of the retiring teachers under the current state funding situation; and 

 

The Three-Year Budget Plan requires us to stabilize the schools over the next three years, so that in the following year, 2016-17, we can then begin to invest new revenue in improving education; and

 

The Superintendent must develop a budget based upon the current inadequate state funding of schools and still continue ongoing improvement of educational outcomes while living within our means.

 

The San Diego Unified Board of Education reiterates its intention for the Superintendent to present the board the general budget plan for 2015-16, at the same time the 2014-15 budget is presented for approval, as well as information demonstrating that the adjustments of these two budget years will allow the district in 2016-17, to fully meet its current obligations and begin to improve financially.

 

The Superintendent will develop a budget for both the 2014-15 and 2015-16 school years that places the highest priority on student needs in the classroom; and

 

Following the adoption of the 2014-15 school budget and the Three-Year Budget Plan, the Superintendent and the Board of Education enter into a joint advocacy plan to address the inadequacy of public education funding and call for an investment that will return California to being a public education leader for the nation.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve Declaration of Need for fully Qualified Educators, effective July 1, 2014 through June 30, 2015, submitted to the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CCTC) by county offices of education, public school districts, state-wide agencies, or non-public schools/agencies.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CCTC) requires that under Education Code Section 44300, local boards of education annually adopt a Declaration of Need for Fully Qualified Educators and an authorizing resolution in the form of a motion, prior to employing teachers on emergency permits. The Declaration requires the district estimate the number, type, and subject of emergency permits that may be needed for the ensuing school year and to provide information about recruitment activities that have been conducted in the effort to employ or train fully credentialed teachers.

 

The district utilizes emergency permits in designated subject fields such as special education. Emergency permits are also utilized to broaden the teaching authorizations of teachers who are fully certified in another subject to meet the specific instructional need for a school or program. School districts, county offices of education, and charter schools (LEAs) will receive funds from the EPA based on their proportionate share of the statewide revenue limit amount, which includes charter school general purpose funding.
