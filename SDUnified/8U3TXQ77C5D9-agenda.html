BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue our improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting districtwide, we are implementing a new process streamlining our Independent Contractor Agreements. Our goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

BACKGROUND: Established in 1969, ROP is a state-funded program designed to prepare individuals 16 years of age or older to qualify for: entry-level employment opportunities, career advancement, and/or entry into advanced education programs in community colleges or four-year colleges.  State funding granted to district by ROP is overseen by the district Office of College, Career and Technical Education (CCTE). This structure and office has been implemented to ensure a confluence of efforts in the development of innovative programs and courses of study. Each sequence of courses will have high quality, standards and competencies-based curriculum that lead to a mastery of skills and a continued passion for life-long learning.  The integration of core academics, career technical content and career exploration are presented in a personalized, supportive and caring environment of high expectations for all students. These programs prepare students for the challenges and opportunities of the 21st century. To ensure a successful transition from the critical years of childhood to young adulthood, CCTE equips students with the skills they need to become responsible citizens, contributing members of society and gainfully employed.

 

Over the years, district staff members have implemented ROP promotion and recruitment practices that have resulted in annual enrollment increases of eleventh and twelfth grade students.  The total percentage of high school to adult student enrollments has steadily increased over the past 15 years.  High school enrollment has increased from a low of 37 percent in the 1992-93 school year to virtually 100 percent in the 2012-13 school year.  Therefore, the district is in compliance with the passage of AB 2448 which required that adult students comprise no more than 10 percent of ROP enrollment by July 1, 2010. District recruitment efforts will continue to be directed toward eleventh and twelfth grade students through the enrollment priority procedures and recruitment plan activities presented in this report.

BACKGROUND:  This MOU establishes the district's participation with the California Center for College and Career (ConnectEd) in order to implement the SB 1070 and AB 790 approach to Small Learning Communities/Career Academies, also known as Linked Learning, with other participating districts in a five-district consortium model.  The five districts in the consortium are San Diego Unified, Carlsbad Unified, Escondido Union, Oceanside Unified, and Grossmont Union.  The term of this MOU spans a planning phase through the remainder of the 2012-13 school year, and then an implementation phase during the 2013-18 school years.  Initial funding to be provided the district in the amount of $75,000 from ConnectEd will support the initial planning period.  ConnectEd is responsible for securing ongoing funding for the implementation phase from philanthropic sources, foundations, corporations, and/or appropriate grants.

 

Small Learning Communities/Career Academies (SLCs) is a research-supported, high school reform structure to accelerate student achievement and to close the achievement gap.  Facilitated and led by teachers, the structure is characterized by (a) high levels of personalization for student needs; (b) integrated career theme and academics to improve relevance of content matter and Project-Based Learning; (c) a series of work-based learning opportunities; (d) targeted and expedient student support services; (e) common planning time for teachers; and (f) purity of classes within the SLCs structure. 

 

The concept and practice of Small Learning Communities (SCLs) began to take shape as a teacher-led high school redesign model in the mid-1960s in Chicago. It was noted that for many students, contact and attention from a small but consistent group of instructors over a longer period of time resulted in higher student performance. This relationship provided for a more personalized and trusting learning environment for students and teachers. Public schools administering the SLC concepts and practices continued to grow as a teacher-led initiative through the 1970s. Research on schools that had SLCs found students performing at higher levels on standardized tests, were more likely to attend school and graduate, had fewer behavioral incidents, and had higher college attendance rates. A few of the published research experts in the field of Small Learning Communities include Dr. Theodore Seizer, Dr. Mary Jalongo, Dr. Joel Westheimer, Dr. Larry Cuban, Dr. George Wood, and Dr. Deborah Meier. Recent research conducted on behalf of the California Department of Education support previous research findings on SLCs.

 

The supporting research for SLCs led the U.S. Department of Education to create incentive grants for high school redesign efforts.  San Diego Unified School District received a federal incentive grant for SLCs in 2003. After the restricted funding sources lapsed, district SLCs oversight and support offices were closed. Nearly all SLCs are currently supported by state funds titled California Partnership Academies (CPA) which have defined SLC parameters within California State Education Code. Because all district SLCs utilize a career theme, a request was made by the California Department of Education in 2010, to assign the oversight and support for SLCs to the leadership and support structures of the Office of College, Career & Technical Education (CCTE).

 

There are currently SLCs at the following district school sites: Clairemont, Crawford, Henry, Hoover, Lincoln, Madison, University City high schools and Kearny and San Diego complexes. High school sites that choose to engage in SLCs contact the Office of College, Career & Technical Education (CCTE) to support and lead SLC development and implementation. Once established an SLC continues to be supported by CCTE.

 

The California Center for College and Career (ConnectEd) began to work with the district in 2008 on a grant to expand opportunities for students to engage in a Pathways concept that closely mirrored the district’s ongoing work in meaningful, real world based, Project-Based Learning and SLCs. During the 2008-09 school years, ConnectEd worked with CCTE on a series of business and community forums to discuss the topic. This series of meetings found mutually beneficial synergistic connections in the collaboration. In the spring of 2009, ConnectEd awarded a continuation grant to the district for the purpose of certifying two programs in their newly formed “Linked Learning” rubric. In 2010, the district became the first district in the United States to have a program certified and the first district to have two programs certified. The connections between ConnectEd and CCTE have continued with ongoing mutual collaboration on SLCs, Project-Based Learning, and curriculum development.

 

Each of the other participating school districts have approved this non-binding MOU.  The collaboration of the five districts is intended to accelerate opportunities for Linked Learning in the region as outlined in SB 1070, signed into law this year, and AB 790. The MOU is a starting point for a discussion with schools with CCTE currently implementing the CPA rubric with SLCs, which is the basis of the Linked Learning rubric for Pathways. The collaboration made possible by this MOU will support and enhance current SLCs and allow participating sites the opportunity and framework for expansion if they so choose to do so. The MOU is open to all district schools, including charters, which wish to participate. The terms of student access outlined in the MOU are met within the existing structure of SLCs as implemented by CCTE.

The district seeks to recruit new teachers who are trained to lead students to academic achievement and to equip new teachers with ongoing professional development and support to further develop and sustain their professional practice.  Each year, the district has a need to recruit new teachers to fill open budgeted positions in key subject areas and to fill positions in high need schools.  Under this agreement, TFA would recruit up to 25 teachers per year who would only be hired should the district have unfilled budget positions.

RECOMMENDATION:  Budget discussion and direction in preparation for 2012-13 First Interim Financial Report.

 

BACKGROUND:  As mid-year approaches it is now necessary to focus our effort on the First Interim Report and initiate the 2013-14 Budget Planning Process.  Staff is beginning to work on the First Interim Report and it is scheduled to be presented to the board in mid-December 2012.  The purpose of this agenda item is to provide updated information to the board and create an opportunity for discussion with the board regarding budget planning.

The following topics will be addressed:

  • Contrast Between Adopted Budget and Provision of Prop. 30
  • State Implementation of Proposition. 30
  • SD County Office of Education Guidance for First Interim
  • Cash Flow
  • Budget Solution for 2013-14
  • Next Steps and Timeline
  • LAO State Financial Outlook

Contrast Between Adopted Budget and Provision of Proposition. 30: Prop. 30 does not provide additional revenue, it only provides Level Funding.  The funding per ADA should be approximately the same as the amount received in 2011-12.  It should be noted that this level of funding per student is approximately the same that the district received prior to 2006-07. While Prop. 30 does not eliminate the need for future budget reductions, it will reduce the amount required.

 

The 2012-13 Adopted Budget was based on the assumption that Prop. 30 would fail as recommended by San Diego County Office of Education (SDCOE).  This means the budget revenue limit per ADA was $441 less for 2012-13 than 2011-12.  The total loss was $47,205,522 (107,042 ADA.)

 

The term Level-Funded means the loss of revenue as reflected above would not occur, therefore, the revenue in the Adopted Budget would rise by the same amount. These revenues would then need to cover savings lost by not implementing the 14 furlough days and the 1 percent salary increase for those at the top steps of the salary schedule per the TA agreements.

 

Prop 30 will also provide the buy-back of some deferrals therefore benefitting the district’s cash flow.  It should also be noted that the Prop. 30 increase to the Base Revenue Limit for 2012-13 will carry forward on multi-year projections for 2013-14 and 2014-15. 

 

State Implementation of Prop. 30: The state has not yet issued a directive regarding implementation of Prop. 30.  This will be forthcoming in the next days and weeks.  Staff is hopeful this clarifying information might be available for incorporation in the First Interim Report.  When details are finalized they will be communicated from the Department of Finance through the California Department of Education and the SDCOE to all school districts.

 

SD County Office of Education Guidance for First Interim: “Most district 2012-13 budgets and Multi-year Projections (MYP’s) were submitted assuming the tax initiatives would fail and the “trigger reductions” would be implemented.  With the passage of Prop 30, the amount of reductions identified in the board resolution may be reduced.  If the district still needs to make reductions but has the required reserves, they can resubmit the resolution with a reduced dollar amount and the timeline and plan will no longer be required.

 

If the district does not have their statutorily required reserves, a revised resolution will need to be submitted along with the timeline and plan previously requested with the original budget submission.”

 

Source: “2012-13 First Interim Reports” San Diego County Office of Education

 

Cash Flow: Governor Brown’s initial proposal for the Prop. 30 tax initiative included using a portion of the proceeds to “buy back” deferrals.  While this does not provide additional revenue, it does begin to pay back school districts for current year income which is now being paid in the subsequent year.

Cash flow has become significantly impacted by the high level of state income deferred.  The district is very near its maximum TRANs borrowing level for 2012-13 and was required to set aside $100 million in Alternate Liquidity to assure timely repayment of the $240 million TRANs.  Due to the district being so near the maximum TRANs borrowing limit, the buyback of deferrals through Prop 30 is critical for cash flow purposes.  Some districts, even though projecting a positive fund balance, need to make budget reductions just to meet Cash Flow requirements.

 

Budget Solution for 2013-14: While Prop. 30 Implementation Guidelines have not yet been established by the state, it is very clear that the additional revenue provided will not eliminate the need to make further budget reductions.

 

When the board approved the Tentative Agreement with SDEA, the Budget Gap for 2013-14 was projected at $80 million even if Prop. 30 passed.  That projected shortfall will be refined through the First Interim Report.  When the First Interim is submitted to the SDCOE it must include a definitive Budget Solutions for 2013-14 that are actionable.

RECOMMENDATION:  Information only.  This item is an update to the board on the board-identified focus areas, Operational Expectation (OE) OE-4 Democratic Engagement of Stakeholders, Sections 4.1 and 4.6 and OE-6, Financial Planning, Sections 6.1, 6.2, and 6.3.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

The board has identified five Operational Expectations as focus areas for 2012.  The board's focus areas are included at the following sections:

 

OE-2, Instructional Program (Monitoring report provided at item F.2.)

Section 2.2 provides,

Base instruction on academic standards that meet or exceed state and/or nationally-recognized model standards.

 

Section 2.4 provides,

Effectively measure each student’s progress based upon individual growth toward achieving or exceeding the standards, and assure that students are able to self-monitor their own progress.

 

OE-4, Democratic Engagement of Stakeholders

The OE-4 Monitoring Report including Evidence of Compliance for 2012 will be presented to the board on December 11, 2012.  The board previously monitored this OE on November 29, 2011.

 

Section 4.1 provides,

Assure the timely flow of information, appropriate input, and strategic, twoway dialog between the district and the community that informs, shapes, and builds understanding and support for district and school efforts.

 

STATUS UPDATE: The board added this section as a focus area in March 2012.  The Superintendent and staff continue to work toward increased communications internally and externally on major issues and programs. 

 

The Superintendent continues to reach out to the leadership of the district advisory groups to increase communications and provide additional opportunity for feedback and suggestions.  The Superintendent has directed responsible staff who work with advisory groups to be sensitive to more advanced communications on key issues. 

 

On August 23, 2012, the Superintendent held his Back-to-School event for principals and department managers.  This annual event provided an opportunity to communicate with district leadership the goals for the new school year, and highlight and promote communications initiatives.  The targeted communications efforts related to the November ballot measures were successful with the passage of Proposition Z.

 

Section 4.6 provides,

Develop a cluster management strategy that will be supportive of democratic clusters in the improvement of schools.  Management will demonstrate responsiveness to each unique community.  Management will encourage development of lead principals chosen by the cluster to work in close cooperation with the community leaders.  Clusters will be held accountable for proving democratic process.  Cluster administrative support leaders will facilitate and support, not dictate from the top.

 

STATUS UPDATE: In 2012-13, the Academic team continues to work toward three primary districtwide goals - College and Career Readiness, Professional Learning Communities, and Community and Parent Engagement.  Community and parent engagement has flourished over the past two years, with the implementation of the Cluster Model in 2010.  Monthly meetings are held in each cluster, which include community leaders, Area Superintendents, principals, and parents.  The goal of the Cluster Model is to provide both a forum for input as well as provide a venue for communication of district and site goals.  In 2012-13, the clusters will begin using uniform templates designed to solicit input and feedback.  Information gathered from these templates will help staff identify common themes and needs.  The Cluster Model has also proven to be a valuable forum for communication on issues related to educational focus and resource allocations.

 

OE-5, Personnel Administration (Monitoring report provided at item I.3.)

The OE-5, Monitoring Report including Evidence of Compliance was previously approved by the board on November 29, 2011.

 

Section 5.16 provides,

The superintendent will maintain accurate information on employee positions and overall statistics and have the ability to effect staffing changes on short notice due to accurate, real time numbers and information; and

 

Section 5.17 provides,

The superintendent will have the Human Resources staff work with the Deputy Superintendent Academics and Deputy Superintendent Business to develop recruitment and retention plans for their respective areas that set high standards, develop internships, residency-type programs, and other means to develop new staff, work towards having staff match the diversity of the students and effective means to retain and professionally develop all employees.

 

OE-6, Financial Planning

 

The board approved the monitoring report for OE-6 on September 11, 2012. 

 

Section 6.1 provides,

The superintendent will develop a budget that is in a format understandable to the board and the community and presented in a manner that allows the board to understand the relationship between the budget and the Goals for Student Achievement priorities and any Operational Expectations goals for the year.

 

STATUS UPDATE: The board approved the 2012-13 balanced budget on June 28, 2012, which included financial projections for the two out-years. Prior to adoption, many budget workshops were held to update the Board and community on the status of the 2012-13 budget. The Finance Team provided financial information to help the board identify resource alignment options and additional budget reductions /solutions to help balance the budget. Regular and consistent presentations were communicated to the board and to the public, key information on the state of the economy and on the current state of the district’s financial condition.

 

Finance presented the First Reading on the proposed budget on June 22, 2012. A detailed Standardized Account Code Structure (SACS) Report was made available to the board and to the public prior to the final adoption of the budget. Additional information provided included a detailed list of budget assumptions, both revenues and expenditures; detailed budgets by major object code and program code; various budget scenarios based on variable levels of state funding; cash flow analysis, and budget reduction options for 2013-14.

 

Finance and IT Teams have been collaborating on enhancing the transparency and usability of web-based budget tools.  Site budget information is now available on Oracle Reporting and Business Intelligence Technology (ORBIT’s) General Ledger Dashboard. Dashboard reports provide easy-to read visual, color coded charts, graphs and tabular data.

 

The Finance Team and Human Resources meet regularly with Position Control to review the current status of district hiring requests to validate for budget availability, potential attrition and retirement savings against the budget.

 

Section 6.2 provides,

The superintendent will develop a budget that accurately describes revenues and expenditures.

 

STATUS UPDATE: The district’s 2012-13 adopted budget was prepared according to the state required Standardized Account Code Structure (SACS) format and budget assumptions required by the San Diego County Office of Education. We also have incorporated new Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB 54) guidelines and requirements, pursuant to board action on June 28, 2012, within SACS.

 

The Finance and IT Teams are focusing on enhancing the transparency and accessibility of web-based reporting for schools. Site budget information is now available on Oracle Reporting and Business Intelligence Technology (ORBIT’s) General Ledger Dashboard. Dashboard reports provide easy-to read visual, color coded charts, graphs and tabular data.  The web-based tool provides users access to detailed financial results for departments and resources.

 

Section 6.3 provides,

The superintendent will develop a budget that at a minimum, shows the amount budgeted and spent in each budget category for the most recently completed fiscal year, the amount budgeted and percentage spent to day for each category for the current fiscal year and the amount budgeted for the next fiscal year.

 

STATUS UPDATE: As a result of staffing reductions in the central office, there will not be a District Budget Book published for the 2012-13 Adopted Budget. Instead, the Finance and IT Teams will focus collaboratively on enhancing the transparency and usability of web-based budget tools. Finance recommends removing this indicator from OE-6.

 

The 2012-13 budget approved by the board on June 28, 2012, includes comparisons of the current year (estimated actuals) to the proposed budget and related variances. Additional documents provided include a detailed SACS report, list of budget assumptions used for both revenues and expenditures; detailed budgets by major object code and program code and 2011-12, and 2012-13 cash flow analysis. Multi-year projections included the current year (2012-13) and two out-years (2013-14 and 2014-15).

 

Since August 2010, the Finance Team has presented monthly financial updates to the board. Based on board feedback, in the last year we have tailored those reports to be streamlined, high level, and informative. Data now include: Summary of General Fund Unrestricted and Restricted Remaining Balances (spend rates); Total Expenditures to Date and Expenditure Comparison to Prior Year; Expenditure Rates by Division; Cash Flow Analysis, and a Financial Horizon list that provides any new requirements being monitored by the district.

The OE-7 Monitoring Report including Evidence of Compliance was provided to the board on January 24, 2012.
