2. Revise District Graduation Requirements to Eliminate Current Senior Exhibition; BUDGETED: YES, GENERAL FUND UNRESTRICTED
Meeting
Jul 30, 2013 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
Yes
Dollar Amount

Budgeted
Yes
Budget Source
General Fund Unrestricted Savings

RECOMMENDATION: Revise district graduation requirements to remove the current Senior Exhibition as a requirement for graduation, effective with the Class of 2014.  Senior exhibition is not a state graduation requirement, nor is it required for UC 'a-g' eligibility. Staff will continue to work with senior high principals and review and monitor graduation requirements to ensure student graduation readiness in alignment with a current A-G requirements,  the expectations of the Common Core State Standards, and 21st century skills. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Savings to the district of $400,000 (general fund unrestricted) in 2013-14.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: In 2012-13, the district provided central funding to sites in the amount of $56 per student to defray costs related to senior exhibition ($400,000 total).  In prior years, sites funded senior exhibition through their site budgets.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  Senior exhibitions were created prior to: our current assessment system, A-G requirements, and Common Core State Standards.   They were intended to be an additional means to evaluate high school experience via a portfolio. Though this is a district graduation requirement, there is no district standard or guidance for the content of the Senior Exhibition.  Each high school crafts its own Senior Exhibition as well as how it is implemented.  Some of the current school requirements are similar to a culminating project and include community service work, evidence of success throughout their school career, and work samples.  Current implementation of senior exhibition is based on individual site-developed criteria that is not necessarily in alignment with the expectations of the Common Core State Standards.  

 

District staff will continue to build and shape a cohesive and coherent district system that aligns to the expectations of the Common Core State Standards, A-G requirements, and 21st century skills.

 

Note:  On May 14, 2013, the board approved continuing the senior exhibition requirement, and incorporating it with the computer proficiency requirement.  Should senior exhibition be eliminated as a graduation requirement, students will still be able to complete the computer proficiency requirement through completion of a course of study that incorporates proficiency in computer applications (e.g., computer applications courses or in i21 classrooms) or through satisfactory results on the high school level district test of computer proficiency.   

 

This item is being re-agendized at the request of Trustee Foster.

FISCAL IMPACT: Each school has been allocated regular Title I funding based on the school's ranking, according to the sliding scale distribution approved by the board. In addition, each school will receive supplemental Title I funding for Program Improvement (PI) to help implement their restructuring plans. The schools will receive the following amounts Freese--$8,919; Fulton--$9,678; Golden Hill--$12,471; Linda Vista--$13,013; Oak Park--$13,501; Ross--$7,943; Washington--$7,781; DePortola--$12,660; Crawford--30,933; Kearny CTA--$7,808; Mission Bay--$23,233; San Diego SCPA--$20,848; Serra--$31,854; and Whittier--$949. 


PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: General Information--Program Improvement (PI) Status: Under the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001, a Title I school that, for two consecutive years, does not meet or exceed a series of annual academic performance goals known as Adequate Yearly Progress (AYP) is designated Program Improvement. Such schools must provide specified types of services and/or interventions during each year that they are identified as PI. A school exits PI if it makes AYP for two consecutive years.

 

District Schools in PI Year 4, 2012–13--School Restructuring: A school that enters its fourth consecutive year in PI status is required to develop a plan to restructure that will be implemented the following year, in this case in 2013–14. The restructuring plan must describe in detail the changes in governance, instructional practice, and staffing that will be required to improve students’ academic achievement and enable the school to meet its AYP goals. District schools submitting a PI Year 4 restructuring plan are: Freese, Fulton, Golden Hill, Linda Vista, Oak Park, Ross, and Washington elementary schools; DePortola Middle School; Crawford, Kearny CTA, Mission Bay, San Diego SCPA, and Serra high schools; and Whittier (K-12).

 

The restructuring proposals were developed by School Program Improvement Leadership Teams composed of teachers, administrators, classified staff, and parents, in collaboration with the Office of Accountability.

 

Each school's School Site Council (SSC) was involved in the development and approval of its restructuring plan.

RECOMMENDATION:  Review and uphold Superintendent’s decision denying the protest of school developer (impact) fees of CP III CentrePoint LLC (Carmel Partners) imposed on the development located at 4645 63rd Street.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Unknown.  Carmel Partners seeks a refund of $390,598.40 in fees.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On April 10, 2012, the board adopted the Fourteenth Amendment to Resolution Levying Fees on Development Projects pursuant to Education Code Section 17620.  That statute allows the governing board of any school district to levy fees against new construction within the boundaries of the school district for the purpose of funding construction or reconstruction of school facilities to accommodate new students entering the district as a result of development.   The State Allocation Board sets the per-square-foot Level I school developer fees every two years at its January meeting in even numbered years. The district has been collecting school developer fees (“impact fees”) since 1987. 
 

By letter dated May 21, 2013, Carmel Partners protested payment of “excess” school impact fees.  Carmel Partners demanded that the City of San Diego (the "City") reduce the amount of assessable space subject to the fees and that the district refund $456,921.60 in fees paid.  Carmel Partners claims that the City improperly included corridors, laundry rooms, leasing office, and community recreation areas as assessable space.  The Superintendent denied Carmel Partners’ protest in a June 12, 2013 letter because it appeared Carmel Partners failed to timely file its protest, based on information available at the time.
 

Subsequent to the protest being filed, the City reevaluated the assessable area and revised its calculation of assessable space.  The City determined that the prior calculation included the width of exterior perimeter walls, elevator floor openings and vent shafts.  Once these areas were excluded as assessable space, the City notified the district of the revised assessable square footage.  The district is currently processing a request from Carmel Partners to refund $66,323.20 of the $1,780,954.08 of fees that were paid based on the City’s original calculation.   Thus, the total amount of disputed fees is now $390,598.40.   The board approved a document entitled “Appeal Process to the San Diego School District Fees on Development Projects Pursuant to Government Code Section 17620” on February 3, 1987. The process was revised on October 6, 1987, and September 10, 1996. Pursuant to this process, Carmel Partners submitted a letter on June 21, 2013, requesting that the board review the Superintendent’s decision denying its protest.  (See attached exhibit)
 

The protest is based on a disagreement between the City and the developer as to what constitutes assessable space.  Although the district originally denied the protest as untimely, the district continues to deny the protest, now reduced to $390,598.40, because Carmel Partners has not provided evidence or authority to support a refund.

6. Direction to Superintendent to Initiate Dialogues with Middle and High School Students Concerning the Trayvon Martin Case (Barrera and Foster)
Meeting

Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No

Board Trustees Richard Barrera and Marne Foster will discuss and request board action to direct Superintendent Cindy Marten and staff to work with the district's Race and Human Relations Department to initiate dialogues among middle and high school students across the district concerning the Trayvon Martin case.

 

The San Diego Unified School District is committed to provide a quality education to all students preparing them for success in college and career, and to provide a safe and supportive environment for all students to learn and succeed. The San Diego community is committed to supporting the young people in our schools as they make positive choices that lead to successful futures. The board believes it is in our middle and high school students’ best interests to discuss the Trayvon Martin case. The dialogue with district middle and high school students would provide opportunity for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their identification with Trayvon Martin’s story, including feelings of fear, anger, and skepticism that they will live in a just society as they prepare for the future.
  • Allow students to speak honestly about the world view that prompted George Zimmerman to confront Trayvon Martin.
  • Help students develop perspectives and strategies to channel their feelings about Trayvon Martin into positive work for themselves and the larger community.
  • Allow students to speak honestly about the “Stand Your Ground” laws that could give one person an unfair advantage over another and the pros and cons of their perceptions.
  • Help students develop perspectives and strategies to channel their feelings when confronted by others in an authoritative manner.
  • Students will discuss “The Single Stories” of Trayvon Martin and George Zimmerman and identify other diverse stories and their impact on the American public that speak of the trial.
  • Students will discuss the decisions that George Zimmerman and Trayvon Martin chose to operate out of and how that could have been done differently.

Under the district Quality School indicator that provides for the school to be a neighborhood center, forums should be facilitated to allow adults, staff and parents, and student discussions to occur. The San Diego Board of Education directs Superintendent Cindy Marten and other appropriate staff to work with the Office of Race Human Relations and Advocacy to initiate dialogues among middle and high school students across the district concerning the Trayvon Martin Case and encourages parents and the community to support the work of the district.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $83,400 (for the year 2013) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and for contracts, leases or other documents no greater than $83,400 (for the year 2013) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111.
