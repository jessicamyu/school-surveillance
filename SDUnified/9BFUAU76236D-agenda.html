BACKGROUND: San Diego and Yokohama share America’s first and longest sister city relationship on the west coast, started by Mayor Dail and Mayor Hiranuma in 1955. San Diego and Yokohama established relations under U.S. President Eisenhower’s Sister Cities Program in 1956, with the mission to connect global societies.

 

San Diego and Yokohama have made many goodwill exchanges including the Friendship Bell presented at Shelter Island in 1958, and San Diego’s gift of the Camino Real Mission Bell to Yokohama in 1959, ongoing high school and university student exchanges, official delegation visits, and exchange of zoo animals and library books.

 

San Diego Yokohama Sister City Society was established as a non-profit organization in 1967, and made many funding contributions to the Japanese Friendship Garden in Balboa Park, with the generous donation by their Yokohama counterparts of cherry blossom trees and the gift of “The San Diego Song” sculpture in 1983.

 

San Diego Yokohama Sister City Society is committed to serving Barnard Magnet Elementary School and all schools in the San Diego Unified School District to establish and assist in the Japanese Language and Culture Program which will help the students of the district to be globally competitive in the 21st century.

EDN’s Educators' Network, used by over 30,000 teachers and administrators nationwide, provides innovative tools and resources to promote civic participation and to develop a sense of environmental responsibility among students of all ages. EDN’s Education and Policy Teams organize federal and state legislative campaigns to green school facilities, improve school food, and enhance environmental education and civic engagement.  These include the Healthy Schools Act and No Child Left Inside.

RECOMMENDATION: Board Trustees Kevin Beiser and Richard Barrera submit for board discussion and approval a resolution in support of Educate Our State (EOS), and efforts to place an initiative on the ballot entitled "School Funding, Property Tax Revenue."

 

BACKGROUND: In the recent past years, the state's income tax revenue dropped significantly, and the state "deferred" payments to schools for sometimes many months, with deferrals in 2011-12 reaching $10.4 billion.

 

The reduction in funding to school districts resulting from these deferrals has caused financial difficulties to districts and has meant that some school districts have had to borrow money from financial institutions and/or to reduce instructional days, lay off employees, and/or cut programs.

 

Schools need to be assured of a stable level of funding in order to plan and execute their obligation to provide a quality education to every student.

 

A volunteer group, Educate Our State (EOS), is seeking to place on the ballot an initiative which the Attorney General has titled, "School Funding. Property Tax Revenues."

 

The initiative would restore the schools' rights to their allocated share of property tax revenue, prevent the State Legislature from redirecting those funds in the future, and direct the state to compensate local governments for the actual Vehicle License Fees (VLF) revenues they have lost from the state's general fund, all without raising taxes.

 

EOS currently is collecting signatures to qualify the initiative for voter consideration.

 

San Diego Unified supports the School Funding, Property Tax Revenues Initiative, which would prohibit the Legislature from enacting future legislation which would, 1) require the district to pay, remit, loan, shift, or otherwise transfer, directly or indirectly, ad valorem property tax revenues or tangible personal property tax revenues to, or for the benefit of, the state, any agency of the state, or any local agency; nor require the auditor or controller of any county to effect any such payment, remission, loan, shift, or transfer; or 2) Reduce the allocation of ad valorem property tax revenues to the district other than: (i) for the purpose of fulfilling the obligations of the Economic Recovery Bond Act of 2004, as outlined under the Revenue and Taxation Code of the State of California Section 97.68 as that section read on January 1, 2014; or (ii) for the distribution among local agencies of excess revenues remaining in the Educational Revenue Augmentation Fund after all existing statutory distributions to school entities from that fund have been made.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve purchases less than $84,100 for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment, and non-construction services; purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and contracts, leases or other documents less than $84,100 for the period February 1, 2014 through February 28, 2014.  These transactions are submitted to the Board of Education for review pursuant to Education Code section 17605 and under its delegation of authority to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Each purchase order has sufficient funds from available budgets for the purchase.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: For the period February 1, 2013 through February 28, 2013:  $921,364.20.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $84,100 (for the year 2014) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and for contracts, leases or other documents no greater than $84,100 (for the year 2014) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111. The purchase orders, contracts, leases, or other documents are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders, contracts, leases or other documents. For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 18, 2013, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

 

Thus, the district has met the requirements of Education Code section 17605 with this process of reporting out the specified purchase orders under $84,100 (for the year 2014) that are not associated with any previously-approved board contracts.
