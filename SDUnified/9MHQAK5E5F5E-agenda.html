4. 2014-15 First Annual EDCO Recycling Challenge Awards; BUDGETED: N/A

RECOMMENDATION: Presentation of awards to the EDCO Recycling Challenge Winners:

  • Walker Elementary with 45 percent waste diversion
  • Angier Elementary with 36 percent waste diversion
  • Encanto Elementary with 34 percent waste diversion
  • Dingeman Elementary with 34 percent waste diversion

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The Board of Trustees recognizes that environmental sustainability is necessary for ensuring a quality learning setting for students and community and to control the district’s operating costs with sustainable actions.  As a school district we should provide superior educational opportunities in environmental and sustainable subjects.  For the district to meet this vision and direction certain resources and partners need to be in place.  A new contract with EDCO Disposal Corporation for districtwide waste and recyclables collection services was executed in August 2014, with this in mind.  Included in the contract is implementation of an annual EDCO Recycling Challenge that was initiated during this school year. The goal of the Challenge is to promote recycling and reduce waste in schools to facilitate compliance with state law requiring school districts to divert 75 percent of their waste from landfill disposal by 2020.

 

Representatives from EDCO will present a Recycling and Waste Diversion Certificate of Achievement and monetary award to Walker, Angier, Encanto, and Dingeman elementary schools, the top four schools with the highest waste diversion and recycling efforts.   The winners were determined based on overall waste diversion (i.e. waste vs. recycling service), bin fullness and weight surveys, and level of recycling education and outreach programs implemented on campus through March 31, 2015.  Results were based on per capita student enrollment.

 

The top four schools have an average diversion rate of 37 percent.  In contrast, the average diversion rate for most schools across the district is 14 percent, so this is quite an accomplishment for these schools.  Ten additional schools achieving honorable mention are: Adams, Barnard, Chesterton, Hage, and Hancock elementary schools; Bell, Knox, Wangenheim, and Wilson middle schools; and Madison High School.  These schools have an average diversion rate of 26 percent. 

 

In addition to the EDCO Recycling Challenge, the district partners with EDCO on a number of initiatives to increase waste reduction and recycling participation and awareness in our schools and facilities. EDCO has conducted districtwide and school specific waste composition analyses that help identify recyclables still ending up in the trash and provides opportunities to target these materials for diversion from the waste stream.  In addition, bin fullness surveys conducted by EDCO drivers help schools determine the most cost effective trash and recycling service for their site.  Already 37 sites have “right-sized” services resulting in 25 percent trash service reduction for these schools.  Recycling presentations and assemblies are also part of the education and outreach efforts EDCO provides under contract.

 

As part of a joint project with the City of San Diego, EDCO, and the district, a Model School Sustainability Project has been implemented at Cherokee Point Elementary with the goal to incorporate sustainability education and programs on campus, including programs on recycling, food waste diversion, water and energy conservation, and gardening. 

 

The spring edition of the Environmental Times Newsletter is in production and will highlight Recycling Challenge winners, provide tips and resources for recycling, reuse, and diversion during end of year cleanouts, spotlight a specific recyclable (i.e. pizza boxes) to educate staff and students about what can be recycled in the program, and a recycling game for students.     

 

Congratulations to the winners on their recycling and waste diversion achievements and please recognize their efforts!

 

Please visit http://www.SDUSDrecycles.com to see the district and EDCO recycling website with information on recycling and how schools can prepare for next year’s Recycling Challenge.

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board on the implementation of LCAP/Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 5: Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All, with a focus on middle school.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator 5, Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All, Destination:  Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language, or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring that every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gap goals are implemented.  All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly or indirectly, to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.

 

LCAP Goal:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.

 

LCAP Actions and Services - Year 1:

  1. Schools use multiple assessment measures to gauge student understanding and application of learning.
  2. Schools set and monitor progress goals for all students and student groups.
  3. High schools participate in Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) processes and will develop strategic plans to improve both school climate and student behavior outcomes.
  4. Early learning programs will define, develop, and assure the implementation of a well-articulated pathway from pre-school through Grade 1.
  5. Provide strategic supports at the earliest levels by lowering class size in Grades TK-3; provide targeted intervention and supports for at-risk learners; and, provide professional development for teachers directed at best first teaching, intervention, and support.
  6. Improve outcomes for TK and Kindergarten students with disabilities by providing access to age-appropriate educational opportunities with non-disabled peers.
  7. Counselors and support staff monitor student access to advanced pathways.
  8. Central office staff supports area superintendents, principals, and school staffs to access data and build a culture of data analysis across all schools.
  9. Create and monitor the implementation of appropriate programs and services for students with disabilities.
  10. All middle and high school students have a 6- or 4-year plan, respectively, that clearly articulates the courses students must complete to meet the 'a-g' requirements, including World Language. 
  11. Central office and secondary school staffs work collaboratively to identify and implement best practices that improve outcomes for Grade 9 students at every high school.
  12. Improve the outcome for students with disabilities.  Specifically:  (a) increase the percentage of students who earn high school diplomas, and (b) increase the percentage of students participating in functional curricula, who by the age of 22, transition into the community as contributing members.

BACKGROUND:  Each month, Superintendent Marten provides a District Accountability Report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting Year 1 LCAP actions, services, and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's District Accountability Report each month, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report, and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation and suggested next steps.

4. Resolution in Support of Assembly Bill 1461 to Extend Definition of "Residence" for Voters to Include Place of Business or Public Space for Voting Purposes (Beiser/Barrera)

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve resolution supporting AB 1461 to extend the definition of "residence" for voters to include a place of business or public space for voting purposes.

 

BACKGROUND: For voting purposes, California Elections Code Section 2027 currently defines a person’s residence as their permanent or fixed place of habitation in which the person has the intention of remaining and to which the person has the intention of returning when absent.  The law further provides that residence in a trailer or vehicle, or any public camp or camping ground may constitute a person’s domicile for voting purposes.

 

AB 1461 would extend the definition of residence to include a place of business or public space to also constitute a person’s domicile for voting purposes.

 

California Elections Code Section 14310, provides that all at elections a voter claiming to be properly registered, but whose entitlement to vote cannot be immediately established, is entitled to submit a provisional ballot.  Existing law prohibits rejection of a provisional ballot of a voter who is otherwise entitled to vote because the voter did not cast his/her ballot in the precinct in which he/she is assigned.

 

AB 1461 would extend a protection to any voter claiming to be registered to vote by prohibiting the rejection of a provisional ballot if the address provided on the ballot does not match the address on the voter’s Affidavit of Registration.

 

The revisions to the Elections Code included in AB 1461 would allow greater access for individuals in the San Diego Unified community to participate in the democratic election process and exercise their right to vote.

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent recommends the reconfiguration of San Diego High School effective July 1, 2015, to better serve the needs of students.  It is recommended that the School of Media, Visual and Performing Arts (MVPA) be closed due to continued low enrollment, and the school's pathways be realigned into the remaining schools on the campus.  The School of Business and Leadership will offer computerized graphic design in addition to its specialized career-themed focus.  The School of Science and Technology will incorporate the broadcast journalism pathway.  The School of International Studies will broaden the performing arts (theater and technical theater). This reconfiguration will provide current MVPA students with access to more academic and enrichment opportunities for college and career readiness while allowing them to pursue their artistic talents. 

 

Upon board approval of this recommendation, next steps include:

  • A meeting on May 21, 2015, for MVPA students in the Classes of 2016, 2017, and 2018, and their parents/guardians to provide the opportunity for students to select one of the three remaining small schools on the San Diego High School campus.
  • MVPA students will also have the opportunity to apply to the School of Creative and Performing Arts (SCPA) and San Diego Early/Middle College.  Enrollment in other district high schools will be on a space-available basis.
  • Implementing a communication plan and information on the three remaining small schools for exiting eighth grade students who matriculate to San Diego High School.
  • Neighborhood Schools and Enrollment Options will work with students and their parents/guardians who have already selected MVPA as their Choice school for the 2015-16 school year on alternative enrollment.
  • California Department of Education notification of MVPA closure.     

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Existing MVPA certificated staff will have priority consideration during the post and bid period for positions at the remaining schools on the San Diego High campus.  Classified staff will be reassigned in accordance with collective bargaining contracts.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

CONSULTATION WITH SCHOOL COMMUNITY:  The Area Superintendent and San Diego High School principals held meetings during March and April 2015 with MVPA students, parents/guardians, administrators, certificated and classified staff, as shown on the attached communication timeline.      

 

BACKGROUND: In 2004, the board approved the small school concept to be implemented at three redesign high schools (San Diego High School, Crawford High School and Kearny High School).  San Diego High School was redesigned from one comprehensive campus into six small schools: School of International Studies (IS); Learn, Explore, Achieve, Discover and Serve (LEADS); Commmunication, Investigation in a Multicultural Atmosphere (CIMA); School of Business; Media, Visual and Performing Arts (MVPA); and Science and Technology (SciTech).  In 2011, the board approved the reconfiguration of San Diego High School from the six small schools into four:  School of International Studies (IS); Media Visual and Performing Arts (MVPA); School of Business and Leadership; and Science and Technology (SciTech).  

 

Despite the best efforts of the MVPA staff over the past two years, enrollment at this small school has continued to decline.  As a significantly smaller school within the current configuration, MVPA has been challenged with providing an optimal learning environment that offers a full range of opportunities for students.  Low enrollment has resulted in limited course offerings for students to receive academic enrichment and needed intervention courses to accelerate learning.  The school has been unable to offer full-time teaching assignments, which has resulted in staffing instability. 

 

Under the leadership of the Area Superintendent and San Diego High School principals, the focus will be on a smooth transition for MVPA students and families during the reconfiguration process, and on maintaining a positive learning environment through the end of the school year.  All San Diego High staff will create a welcoming and nurturing environment for the students during the transition.  An open and ongoing dialogue is occurring on the campus for students and their families to discuss any apprehensions, or ask questions about next steps.
