RECOMMENDATION: Adoption of the Mid-City Community Advocacy Network's (CAN) Six School Community Climate Bill of Rights for implementation during the 2017-18 school year in conjunction with the district's restorative practices framework:

 

We, as students, educators and parents of the San Diego Unified School District, as well as other community members, in pursuit of health and safety for all, by affording all students equity, regardless of economic status, race, gender-identity, sexual orientation, immigration status, religion or creed, through the opportunity to access meaningful Restorative Practices and Restorative Justice intervention, put forth this School Climate Bill of Rights.

  1. Students, staff, administrators and parents/guardians have a right to a positive, collaborative, healthy, healing school environment.  Schools should strive to be sanctuaries for students and parents/guardians.
  2. Students and parents/guardians have a right to resolve conflict through Restorative Practices (RP) over traditional punitive disciplinary measures.  Prioritizing RP over traditional methods affords all students the best possible opportunity to succeed academically and socially.  
  3. Students, staff, and administrators have the right to comprehensive training and development in RP.  Training and development are essential for effective RP implementation.
  4. Students, staff and administrators have the right to a school structure that supports RP.  RP can only thrive if administrators and educators are given the time, tools and staff to execute RP rollout.
  5. Students, parents/guardians and educators have the right to have their input, opinions and voices heard and reflected in the decisions and recommendations put forth by a Restorative Practices Advisory Committee.  
  6. Students, parents/guardians, and educators have the right to efficient and transparent implementation of RP programming.  Uniform data keeping and evaluation of RP practices throughout the district are essential to successful program development.

 

BACKGROUND:  Under the guidance of the Mid-City Community Advocacy Network (CAN), the Peace Promotion Momentum Team (PPMT) was created in 2009 by City Heights residents who wanted to foster a safe, fair, restorative and thriving community. Their vision is a safe community established and maintained by engaged and informed residents.  PPMT is comprised largely of City Heights residents as well as members of local organizations that are faith-based, community networking-focused, or service-oriented, and members of other stakeholder groups interested in public safety, community cohesion, community-police relations, education, youth well-being/academic success, and racial justice. Since its onset, PPMT has focused on the application of restorative justice practices in order to address the aforementioned issues and therefore dismantle the school-to-prison pipeline. 

 

In 2013, PPMT conceptualized a restorative justice alternative to juvenile detention and chose the National Conflict Resolution Center (NCRC) to implement its City Heights Restorative Community Conferencing (RCC) Pilot Program, with hopes that such a program will soon be adopted by the County of San Diego and made available to all youth in the region. To ensure that the pilot program would adhere to the original vision of community-led restorative justice, PPMT also established the RCC Steering Committee that is composed of residents and systems partners from the San Diego District Attorney's Office, Public Defender's Office, Department of Probation and San Diego Unified School District Police Department.

 

In 2015, PPMT began its Restorative Pipeline to Success campaign for the implementation of restorative practices at the San Diego Unified School District. It wrote the School Climate Bill of Rights with input from students, teachers, parents/guardians, administrators, school police officers, district staff and many other members of the community.

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and Approve the continuation of early admission at the beginning of the 2017-18 school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a Transitional Kindergarten (TK) program as per flexibility offered to districts by changes to the California Education Code.  At the beginning of the 2015-16 school year eight schools in the Henry Cluster (Benchley-Weinberger, Dailard, Foster, Gage, Green, Hardy, Hearst, and Marvin elementary schools) offered enrollment to children whose fifth birthday fell within the six-month window of September 2 to March 2.

BACKGROUND:  The laws applicable to transitional kindergarten were changed as a result of Assembly Bill (AB) 104, the Education Omnibus Trailer Bill.  The new law, effective July 1, 2015, permits local educational agencies and charter schools the option to grant early admission at the beginning of the school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a TK program. AB 104 amended the California Education Code (EC) to add subsection (B) to EC 48000(c)(3) and states the following:

 

B(i) For the 2015-16 school year and each school year thereafter, a school district or charter school may, at any time during a school year, admit a child to a transitional kindergarten program who will have his or her fifth birthday after December 2, but during the same school year, with the approval of the parent or guardian, subject to the following conditions:

 

(I) the governing board of the school district or the governing body of the charter school determines that the admittance is in the best interest of the child.

(II) The parent or guardian is given information regarding the advantages and disadvantages and any other explanatory information about the effect of this early admittance.

RECOMMENDATION: In alignment with AB 288, conduct a public hearing and first reading of the 2017-18 College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) Agreements with the San Diego Community College District for college classes to be conducted on San Diego Unified School District high school campuses.  AB 288 requires community college and K-12 districts offering college courses on high school campuses to enter into CCAP partnerships that include specific language regarding the targeted number of high school students to be served and the total number of full-time equivalent students (FTES) projected to be claimed by the community college district for those students.

  1. SV-18-0137-99 San Diego City College - for various classes at Crawford, Garfield, Hoover, Lincoln, Morse, and San Diego high schools and the School of Creative and Performing Arts (SCPA) 
  2. SV-18-0138-99 Mesa College Accelerated College Program (ACP) - for Mathematics and Political Science classes at Clairemont, Henry, La Jolla, Madison, Morse, Point Loma and University City high Schools  
  3. SV-18-0139-99 Mesa College - for various classes at Clairemont, Henry, Kearny, La Jolla, Madison, Point Loma, Twain, and University City high schools 
  4. SV-18-0140-99 Miramar College - for various classes at Mira Mesa, Scripps Ranch, Serra and University City high schools

FISCAL IMPACT: None. The San Diego Unified School District is eligible for average daily attendance (ADA) revenue for the periods each day that students are enrolled in San Diego Unified courses, and the San Diego Community College District is eligible for apportionment for the period that students are enrolled in City, Mesa, or Miramar College courses.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. As stated in the agreements, each entity agrees to adhere to its respective collective bargaining agreements and no San Diego Community College District or San Diego Unified School District teachers will be displaced by the implementation of these agreements.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  AB 288 authorizes community college and K-12 districts to enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships with the goal of developing pathways from high school to community college for career technical education or preparation for transfer, improving high school graduation rates, or helping high school students achieve college and career readiness.  Through dual enrollment partnerships, school district and community college districts can create clear pathways of aligned, sequenced coursework that allow students to more easily and successfully transition to for-credit, college-level coursework leading to an associate degree, transfer to the University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) systems, or to a program leading to a career technical education certificate.  The intent of AB 288 is to offer or expand dual enrollment opportunities for students who may not already be college bound or who are underrepresented in higher education. AB 288 was supported by both the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees and the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education.  The 2017-18 CCAP Agreements were presented to the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees for first reading on June 8, 2017, and will be agendized for final reading and adoption on July 20, 2017.  The 2017-18 CCAP Agreements will be brought forward for San Diego Unified School District Board of Education final reading and adoption on July 25, 2017.

BACKGROUND:  Under the Civic Center Act (Education Code §38130 et seq.), organizations, clubs and associations formed for recreational, educational, political, economic, artistic or moral purposes are permitted by state law and district policy to use school buildings and grounds.  Civic Center use must be subordinate to and not interfere with the instructional program or other public school purposes.  Civic center use may be on a free, district cost recovery, fair rental value, or commercial basis.

Pursuant to Administrative Procedures 9205, Civic Center Use of Buildings, Grounds, and Equipment, and 9229, Use of Athletic Fields and Lighted Stadiums at Senior High Schools, the district established policies and procedures for the use of athletic fields and lights stadiums, including prioritization for use, guidelines for times of use and manner of use, and measures to mitigate negative impacts to the community.

 

In accordance with Administrative Procedure 9229, district staff has been working with Hoover High School and community stakeholders to design a site-based field use policy for board consideration which will govern the uses of the stadium facility.  The Hoover High School Athletic Field Use Policy considers the size, location, school needs, available athletic facilities, and addresses the needs and concerns of the surrounding community. 


ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW:  The project is subject to CEQA, Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The project, however, is exempt from expanded environmental review under as further described below and in the attached NOE (Attachment A).

 

Existing Facilities (Class 1)

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

 

The proposed project involves the implementation of a field use policy governing the use of the existing athletic facilities at Hoover High School, and would not result in the change or expansion of any existing previously approved uses. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the proposed project.

 

Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings (Class 23)

Class 23 consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose. Past history is defined as a similar kind of activity that has been occurring for at least three years and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323).

 

The proposed project entails the implementation of a field use policy governing the use of the existing athletic facilities at Hoover High School. Use of these facilities has occurred in a similar manner for several decades, since the school began operation in the 1930s. Implementation of the field use policy would not represent a change in the operation of the athletic facility as it would still operate consistent with existing use. Therefore, there is a reasonable expectation that the future use of the facility would not represent a change in the operation of the facility.

 

As set forth in Attachment A, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemption, and approval of the project would not compromise the environmental quality of the site and surrounding area. Approval of this item approves the Project as described and authorizes and directs staff to file a Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.

BACKGROUND: Actions to accept, budget, and spend income, in excess of that included in the adopted budget, are authorized by Education Code Section 42602. These actions provide the means by which the board of education can access income which becomes available to them after they have adopted a final budget. 21080.35. Statutory Exemption for Solar Energy Systems

The solar power generation portion of the project is statutorily exempt from CEQA, as defined in PRC 21080.35, because the solar energy system would be installed in an existing school parking lot that has been used for parking of vehicles for at least the previous two years.

 

15301. Existing Facilities (Class 1)

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agencys determination (CEQA Guidelines section 15301).

Minor alterations include ADA accessibility improvements and increased seating capacity, as well as a new modular elevator to provide ADA access. The overall increase in seating capacity would represent only a minor increase from the existing seating capacity at the stadium, softball field, and baseball field, and would consist primarily of ADA seating. As such, the increase would represent a negligible expansion of available seating.

 

15302. Replacement or Reconstruction (Class 2)

Class 2 consists of replacement or reconstruction of existing structures and facilities where the new structure will be located on the same site as the structure replaced and will have substantially the same purpose and capacity as the structure replaced (CEQA Guidelines section 15302).

 

The proposed project would replace numerous existing structures on the campus, including the existing scoreboards, PA system, and ticket booth/restroom/concession building at the stadium. The proposed replacement structures would be generally located on the same site as the structures being replaced and would have substantially the same purpose and capacity as the structure replaced. In addition, the proposed project would reconstruct portions of the existing stadium bleachers to include ADA access, increase ADA seating capacity, and accommodate a new modular elevator to provide additional ADA access.

 

15303. New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures (Class 3)

15314. Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14)

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15314).

 

The proposed project would include the installation of various new ADA accessibility improvements and upgrades to the existing athletic facilities within the existing school campus, as well as the installation of a new solar power generation system in the schools southwest parking lot.
