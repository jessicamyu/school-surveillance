BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Vision 2020 plan calls for schools to be “safe, attractive neighborhood learning centers,” and for these same quality neighborhood schools to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.”

 

The San Diego community is committed to supporting the young people in schools as they make positive choices that lead to successful futures; and district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing revised procedures, participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved.

 

The Council of ASB Presidents fully supports National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within that month and throughout the year. The students’ participation in anti-bullying activities would provide the opportunity for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and find solace in the knowledge that others have been in similar situations.
  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying including cyber, verbal, physical, exclusion, and lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, questioning (LGBTQ) bullying.
  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus and the consequences of their actions.
  • Support students to come up with solutions to stop bullying and enforce positive interactions between students.
  • Engage teachers and parents in raising awareness if a student is being bullied.
  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying.
  • Invite the media to further emphasize the importance of taking a stand against bullying.
  • Help empower students in the recognition of the role everyone plays in addressing and reducing bullying with the goal to eradicate bullying on all school campuses; and

All elementary, middle, and high schools shall coordinate anti-bullying activities at the beginning of each new school year in the month of October, during a week or days of their choosing.

 

The Board of Education encourages parents and the community to support the work of the students and staff.

1. Informational Report on District Hot Weather Plan
Meeting
Sep 29, 2015 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type

RECOMMENDATION: Information only.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: No additional staff is needed. Expectations for existing site and central office employees are outlined in Administrative Procedure 4032: Operation of Schools During Extremely Hot Weather, and Administrative Procedure 5003: School or Site Closure/Early Dismissal of Students.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: At the September 15, 2015 board meeting, the board asked the Superintendent to bring back a plan that would equip every classroom and critical space at all schools with air conditioning, as well as provide an update and plan for possible extreme hot weather days in October and beyond.  The Office of Leadership and Learning, Facilities Planning and Construction, Communications Office, and Student Support Services have worked together to identify short term and long term plans for hot weather.

 

Near-Term Strategy for Hot Weather Days

 

The Office of Leadership and Learning sent a Hot Weather Plan template to each principal in order to calibrate where we are in the process of creating optimum learning environments at every site.  Attached please find a copy of the template sent to all principals as well as a spreadsheet indicating their responses for a hot weather plan.   The template provided suggested strategies, but knowing that every site is unique, each school will customize their site plan to best meet their needs during the hot weather.  The template required sites to create indoor instructional strategies and outdoor activity plans (ATTACHMENT A).  Due to the limited time to create the plan, schools are encouraged to revise and modify with their staff as needed.  As of September 15, 2015, 80 percent of schools have submitted their Hot Weather Plan (ATTACHMENT B).  The office of Leadership and Learning will continue to work with the remaining 20 percent of schools to complete their hot weather plans.

 

Facilities Planning and Construction staff have created a near-term strategy to develop air conditioned cool zones to provide relief on hot days at non-air conditioned schools.  District staff will provide and install four temporary/portable air conditioning (AC) units to each of the schools with limited or no air conditioning.  

 

Facilities staff sent a preliminary email to every principal advising them that facilities project managers will be contacting them to identify two rooms as cool zones to be air conditioned.  Facilities project managers will obtain and review the floor plans for each school.  Project managers will coordinate meetings with each principal, walk the campus and review school floor plans with the school principal.  The project manager and principal will identify the rooms on the campus that the principal determines the best places to locate cool zones.  Each school will be supplied up to four temporary/portable AC units for the two rooms that are best suited for AC. 

 

Project managers will assess potential cool zone spaces using the following criteria:

  • Exhausting hot and moist air
  • Electrical power connections
  • Window security, after installation
  • Avoid South facing walls.
  • Room size 400 sq ft per AC unit

The school’s Building Services Supervisor and/or Physical Plant Operations (PPO) staff will install the portable AC units.  The facilities project manager will act as the point of contact and liaison for the principal throughout the installation process.  After installation, the principal can contact the PPO work order desk if equipment problems occur.  School custodians will turn the AC units on in the morning and turn them off at the end of the day. 

 

Communications has created an informational packet for principals that included a checklist, sample message (phone and email) regarding hot weather plans, and a list of schools (created with input from principals) that would be on modified day should a hot weather day be called by the superintendent (Attachment C). A meeting was held last Friday, September 18, 2015, with senior leadership from Operations, Leadership and Learning, and Student Services to make sure the district is ready when the next heat wave arrives.

 

The public website has been updated to reflect the changes. Information can be found at www.sandiegounified.org/hotweather

 

Update on Long-Term Plan for Air Conditioning

 

At its September 15, 2015 meeting, the Board of Education requested that staff develop a plan to air condition all classrooms and critical spaces in San Diego Unified.  In accordance with that request, staff is developing a plan that modifies the June 3, 2015, board-approved, bond project schedule by creating a separate group of air conditioning projects for schools in Climate Zones 1 and 2 without air conditioning.   Staff is currently analyzing the air conditioning project requirements, projected costs, cash flow requirements and schedule.  The completed air conditioning plan will outline the planning, design, regulatory approval, procurement and construction phases of work.  The plan will adjust the bond program schedule expediting air conditioning projects while delaying other projects to maintain alignment with revenue from scheduled bond issuances.   Staff expects to complete the accelerated air conditioning plan for board discussion and consideration on or before November 10, 2015.

BACKGROUND: An election was held for the San Diego Unified School District, County of San Diego, California on November 4, 2008, at which electors of the district approved by more than 55 percent of the votes cast "Proposition S" authorizing the district to issue up to $2.1 billion in general obligation bonds pursuant to California Constitution Article XIII (A), to be used for the specific school facilities projects set forth in Proposition S.

 

Pursuant to Proposition S, the district shall only issue bonds if it projects that the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on the proposed bonds, when combined with the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on presently outstanding bonds of the district in each year, will not exceed the rate levied in 2007-08 (being $66.70 per $100,000 of assessed valuation) to pay debt service on the presently outstanding bonds alone, referring to bonds issued under Propositions MM and S. Proposition Z, which was passed by electors of the district on November 6, 2012, has a separate tax rate.  Due to the tax rate limit, the majority of Proposition S bonds are issued as Capital Appreciation Bonds (CABs) that do not pay current interest, but compound interest and pay it at maturity.

 

Assembly Bill 182 (AB 182), effective January 1, 2014, requires that any time a CAB is issued that the board consider the item at two consecutive meetings.  This is the second of the two meetings; the first was held September 15, 2015.  Other key requirements in AB 182 are a 25-year maximum term of the bonds, prepayment option for any bonds longer than 10 years, and maximum repayment ratio of 4:1 for debt service to principal amount. The bond issue is being structured to be in compliance with AB 182 with a term of 25 years, call feature after year 10, and a projected repayment ratio of less than 4:1 for all series of bonds.

 

As part of the current plan of finance for the Proposition S Bond Program, the district anticipates issuing one or more series of bonds, as needed, every year, until the full authorization has been issued.  The plan proposes the sale of bonds that allow for the compounding of interest.  On September 15, 2015, the bond resolution authorizing issuance of Proposition S was presented as an information item.  It is now brought forward for board action.

  • Medical oversight to ensure a safe and effective PAD program;
  • Maintaining records of training certification;
  • Collecting incident report and data information;
  • Providing an operating procedure manual that covers California Health and Safety Code, Section 1797.196(b)(5), which governs the use of AEDs on K-12 school sites

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve Memorandum of Agreement to continue participating in the California Partnership for Achieving Student Success (Cal-PASS) Plus program (SV-16-0523-14).  The term of this Memorandum of Agreement is September 30, 2015 through June 30, 2020.  

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.  

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  California Partnership for Achieving Student Success (Cal-PASS) Plus is a voluntary collaboration in which California schools, colleges, and universities share student data and bring together teams of educators to mine that information for insights to reshape curricula and help more students enroll in college.  It is a means to continue to fulfill an agreement with the San Diego Community College District (SDCCD) to exchange student data so that SDCCD can better serve graduates and to follow up on their progress at SDCCD.

 

All of California’s community colleges, most universities, and two-thirds of the state’s school districts belong to the Cal-PASS Plus data sharing consortium. It is free to schools and colleges. The program is funded by the State of California through the California Community Colleges System Office and by foundations working to repair ruptures in the educational pipeline and help more students succeed in college.

 

The database includes years of student grades as well as testing and reporting results in English language arts, mathematics, science and history/social science, and the California High School Exit Exam (CAHSEE).  It can follow students’ results as they move from middle to high school to college universities and into the workforce.

 

Cal-PASS Plus complies fully with the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA).  Access to the database is password-protected and tightly restricted.  All members must sign and abide by strict confidentiality safeguards.  The student information is encrypted and made anonymous.  Cal-PASS Plus tools allow members to follow cohorts from middle school through college, but student names, Social Security numbers and other identifiers are not disclosed.

 

Cal-PASS Plus will provide us with critical information about the success of our students as they move to the next educational level.  Armed with this transition information and the specifics about what is expected of our students, we can improve our teaching by better aligning our curricula.

 

By continuing with the data-sharing network, we agree to make available to partners in our region at least five years of data, including grades, attendance and demographic information.  At completion of each school year and certified submission of data to California Longitudinal Pupil Achievement Data System (CALPADS), district staff will extract data from that submission for submission to Cal-PASS Plus for sharing with SDCCD and potentially with other local educational institutions.  This work will be performed by the Integrated Technology Support Services (ITSS) Department and will not require an increase in full-time equivalents (FTEs) or additional salaried district positions.  Only a few hours of annual work is required to facilitate transfer of data specified in the Data Elements Dictionary provided Cal-PASS does not change the dictionary or the current method of extracting all data from data already submitted to the CALPADS.

BACKGROUND: The board has developed a real estate utilization strategy to maximize the use of district-owned sites, including pursuing opportunities to develop properties, building capacity to generate ongoing revenue, and creating partnerships to help maintain real estate assets to a high standard and serve as a community resource.

 

The board guiding principles for the real estate utilization strategy are:

  • Maintain ownership of district property where appropriate
  • Generate ongoing revenue
  • Develop properties as neighborhood centers providing neighborhood services (Vision 2020 goal)
  • Provide for community engagement and input in the development process

In alignment with this strategy, on July 22, 2014, the board adopted a resolution authorizing the release of a Request for Proposals (RFP) for joint occupancy at five, district-owned sites:

 

1. Fremont/Ballard Center, 2375 Congress St., San Diego, CA 92110

2. Scripps Mesa Conference Center, 10380 Spring Canyon Rd., San Diego, CA 92131

3. Transportation Services Center, 4710 Cardin St., San Diego, CA 92111

4. Barnard Asian Pacific Language Academy, 2445 Fogg St., San Diego, CA 92109

5. Revere Center, 6735 Gifford Way, San Diego, CA 92111

 

On August 1, 2014, the RFP was released via Demand Star and the district Real Estate department website. On August 29, 2014, a mandatory pre-submittal conference was held to answer questions and receive initial qualifications statements from interested proposers.

 

From September 2014 to late November 2014, an initial community engagement period took place, during which public meetings were held at each of the RFP sites to gather community input. Attendance at these meetings was mandatory for all interested proposers in order to incorporate community feedback into their RFP response. Proposals were due to the district on February 2, 2015.

 

RFP Evaluation

The District received six proposals for three sites: one proposal for the Scripps Mesa site; three proposals for Revere Center; and two proposals for Fremont/Ballard Center. The District did not receive any proposals for the Barnard Asian Pacific Language Academy or Transportation Services Center sites.

 

The proposals were reviewed and evaluated according to the criteria listed in the RFP by a committee composed of district staff from Facilities Planning and Construction, Special Projects, Real Estate, and Finance Division, and a faculty member from the University of San Diego graduate school of real estate. The committee recommended selecting the proposal from Monarch for the Scripps Mesa site only.

  • Meeting and conference room spaces for district and community use through Civic Center Act rentals
  • Community garden, an amenity requested during initial outreach with cluster and neighborhood members

Multi-family Residential Community

  • 264 units of “Class A” multifamily for-rent housing and resident amenities
  • Of those units, 22 units designated affordable at 50 Percent Area Median Income (AMI)
  • Affirmative marketing of units to district employees

This mix of uses represents both the highest and best use of the land as well as added district and community benefit. The proposal is also consistent with the board-approved guiding principles for the real estate utilization strategy as follows:

Following SBE approval and community outreach meetings, staff will return to the board with a final joint occupancy agreement for approval. If approved, construction is anticipated to begin in summer of 2017.
