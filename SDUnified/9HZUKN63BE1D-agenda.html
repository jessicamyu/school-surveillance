BACKGROUND: On August 3, 1990, the President of the United States, George H. W. Bush declared the month of November as Native American Heritage Month.

 

The contributions of Native American Indians to this nation’s government, history and culture are as numerous and varied as the tribes themselves.

 

The President authorized and requested to call upon federal, state, and local governments, groups and organizations and the people of the United States to observe November with appropriate programs, ceremonies and activities.

 

The San Diego Unified School District, in promoting and ensuring that equity in education is provided for all students of all cultures, has provided tutoring, career guidance, mental health support, cultural awareness, leadership development, and cultural enrichment programs for approximately 500 Native American students since 1976, under the federally-funded Title VII Indian Education Program.

 

The commemorative month aims to provide a platform for Native American Indian’s to share their culture, traditions, music, crafts, dance and ways and concepts of life.

 

The Indian Education Program, in collaboration with local Native American Indian Organizations and the Ballard Parent Center, will acknowledge the month of November by focusing on heritage with Culture Awareness Workshops on Wednesday evenings during this month.

 

The Indian Education Program and the Native American Indian community have made and continue to make significant and important contributions to the community and the nation as a whole.

RECOMMENDATION: Deny the charter petition to establish Meridian International Academy as a new charter school. Staff has reviewed the Meridian charter petition and recommends the board adopt, in support of a denial, the following written factual findings.

  1. As specified below, the Meridian International Academy charter petition presents an unsound educational program for the pupils to be enrolled in the charter school, and the petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition.
  1. The Meridian charter petition describes a 50/50 dual language immersion program for Grades K-2 that abruptly becomes 90/10 for all students in Grades 3-8.  Dual language immersion programs do not end at Grade 2.  A sound, research-based and pedagogically strong dual language program will maintain a minimum of 50 percent in the target language during Grades 3 through 5.  Moreover, the sample schedule for Grades K-2 on page 26 of the Meridian charter petition does not present a clear language allocation plan for a dual language immersion program.  It is unclear what will be taught in English and what will be taught in the target language.  A well-defined language allocation plan is essential.  The sample schedules for Grades 3-4 on page 27 and Grades 5-8 on page 33 indicate 45 and 50 minutes, respectively, for “language enrichment” for all students, which does not align with a dual language immersion program or the charter school’s stated goal of students becoming fluent in two languages.  Thus, the three sample schedules lined up sequentially, provide evidence of an overall language enrichment program, not a dual language immersion program, and consequently will not result in bilingualism/biliteracy for the English dominant students or for students of the target language.  One of the stated Goal 2 outcomes on page 20 of the charter is that all Meridian students will be fluent in a second language prior to entering middle school.  This cannot be accomplished through the current proposal.
  2. Page 19 of the Meridian charter petition states “Goal 1: Meridian will demonstrate mastery of general core subjects.” However, there is no evidence or measures of accountability of the target language even with 50 percent of the time devoted to instruction through the language in Grades K-2.
  3. Page 20 of the Meridian charter petition states, “Goal 2: Meridian will create a learning environment with an International focus that fosters global awareness through the introduction of a language immersion program.”  However, the only “measure” identified for achieving this goal is that “Meridian teachers will have CLAD certification and/or BCLAD (or work toward certification).”  This is not an adequate measure of the goal.
  1. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition.  Based on internal inconsistencies within the charter petition, Meridian petitioners are, in the district staff’s judgment, unfamiliar with the content of the charter petition, and the charter petition does not accurately reflect the petitioners’ financial and operational plans.
  1.   1. Regarding class size, page 238 of the Meridian charter petition refers to a student to teacher ratio of 24:1 for all grades in all years of operation; however, this is inconsistent with page 269 which describes student to teacher ratios of 36:1 and 33:1.
     
  2. Throughout the Meridian charter petition, it appears sections of text has been “copied and pasted” to duplicate previous statements within the same heading for no purpose.  In describing the method for measuring pupil outcomes pursuant to Education Code section 47605(b)(5)(C), the petitioners state the identical “School Action” on both page 63 and 64 of the charter petition that “Annually, EL students will advance at least one level on the CELDT….”  (See also “Program Elements” section on page 19 and page 20, “Teachers will design and implement benchmark assessment used to inform their teaching.”)
     
  3. In order to promote local responsibility and leadership, MPS Board has created a standing (“MSA-San Diego Governance Committee” or “Committee”) vested with decision-making authority over the operations of MSA-San Diego.  MSA-San Diego will be governed by the Committee, a majority of whose members will be elected locally, from the MSA-San Diego community. The MPS Board retains veto authority over items approved by the Committee and the MSA-San Diego Principal, for the purpose of ensuring consistency with the essential elements of all MPS charter schools, and to ensure fiscal solvency and legal compliance throughout MPS. The Committee will be comprised of five (5) members:

  • Two (2) Directors of the MPS Board selected by the MPS Board of Directors.
  • Two (2) parents of currently enrolled MSA-San Diego students elected by the MSA-San Diego parent body.
  • One (1) MSA-San Diego community member elected by the Committee.

Each member of the Committee shall serve for five (5) years and until a successor member has been designated and qualified. Terms are renewable upon mutual consent between the Committee and the member. The MPS Board will ratify the selection of all Committee members, and the MPS Board retains authority to remove Committee members. The Committee will comply with the Brown Act.

The goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

 

Executive Leadership should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

BACKGROUND:  A.  History and Current Status.  The Commercial Street project began with direction to staff from the board in 2002, to create a plan for development of the former Maintenance and Operations Center at Commercial Street and Irving Avenue which would provide income to the district and transform an unused and difficult site into an asset for the community.   

 

The selected developer, COMM 22, LLC, proposed an option to ground lease the property and construct: (1)  affordable senior housing; (2)  affordable family housing; (3)  residential lofts/commercial retail; and, (4)  for-sale town homes.  COMM 22 would renovate the environmentally blighted site, obtain all entitlements and permits, construct the improvements and pay ground lease rent to the district.

 

The original June 21, 2005 Option Agreement granted one option to COMM 22 to develop the entire site, but problems with State and federal financing required that the plan be revised so that the four options could be exercised at different times. 

 

In September 12, 2012, the board approved changes to the original Option Agreement and divided the one option into four separate options that could be exercised at staggered times. Two options were exercised in 2012 (the Senior Housing Option and the Family Housing Option).  Two options remain in the Option Agreement, which expires on September 30, 2015, (Residential Lofts/Retail Space and For Sale Townhomes).

 

In 2013, the City of San Diego approved the Final Map for the property, which was subdivided into seven separate legal lots (Final Map # 15897 approved on February 13, 2013).  Lot 1 is for the residential lofts/commercial project, Lots 2 and 3 are for the affordable senior housing project, Lots 4, 5, and 6 are for the affordable family housing project, and Lot 7 is for the for-sale town homes.  Construction is taking place on Lots 2, 3 (Senior Housing), and 4, 5, 6 (Family Housing).

 

B.  Summary of Proposed Amendments

 

The proposed amendments make administrative, non-substantive changes to memorialize the amount of rent having been paid in full to the district for the Senior Housing Ground Lease and the amount being paid monthly for the Family Housing Ground Lease during the construction period; and the expiration date of the two options left in the Option Agreement.  All three amendments also update the legal descriptions from Final Map #15897.

 

1.  First Amendment to Senior Housing Ground Lease.  The senior housing option was exercised and upon receipt of HUD financing, COMM 22 pre-paid rent to the district for the Senior Housing Ground Lease in the amount of $582,513.00 on October 7, 2013.  The First Amendment to the Affordable Senior Housing Ground Lease records the pre-payment of rent and updates the legal description with the new legal description from the Final Map.

    

2.  First Amendment to Family Housing Ground Lease.  The family housing option was also exercised.  Pursuant to the Ground Lease, COMM 22 was paying the district Infrastructure/Site Clean-Up Rent of $1.00/month and, as of July 1, 2013, pays Construction Rent (50 percent of Base Rent) in the amount of $8,851.00/month.  The First Amendment to the Affordable Family Housing Ground Lease records the change in rent and updates the legal description with the new legal description from the Final Map.

 

3.  Fifth Amendment to Option Agreement.  The Fifth Amendment to the Option Agreement adds language requiring COMM 22 to provide the district with thirty days’ notice prior to exercising its option for the For Sale Townhome project, which will be exercised no later than September 30, 2015 (the same day the last option for the Lofts/Residential Ground Lease expires).  The Fifth Amendment also updates the legal description for the whole property with the new legal description from the Final Map.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve a one block attendance boundary change between Florence and Washington Elementary schools to assist the Museum School with its application to the state for the Charter School Facility grant Program (SB 740).  This block has no inhabitants, so no students are affected by the boundary change.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In order to assist the Museum School with its application to the state for the Charter School Facility Grant Program (SB 740), a boundary change transferring one uninhabited, non-residential block from the Washington Elementary School attendance boundary to the Florence Elementary School attendance boundary is being recommended.  Attachment 1 is a map of the Washington-Florence change.  No students are impacted by these changes. 

 

Per the California Department of Education’s website, "The Charter School Facility Grant Program is a noncompetitive program that provides assistance with facilities rent and lease expenditures for charter schools that meet specific eligibility criteria. The grant program was enacted in 2002 to reimburse charter schools for rental and lease costs in low-income areas. This program is targeted toward schools and communities with high proportions of economically disadvantaged students. Eligible applicants must have at least 70 percent of students enrolled at the charter school who are eligible for free or reduced-price meals or the charter school must be physically located in an elementary school attendance area where at least 70 percent of students enrolled are eligible for free or reduced-price meals. The charter school must also give a preference in admissions to students who reside in the elementary school attendance area.”

 

By placing this uninhabited non-residential block in the Florence Elementary School boundary, the likelihood that the Museum School will need to apply for facilities space from the district through the Proposition 39 process will be reduced if they are successful with their grant application.  This block was transferred from Florence to Washington by action of the board on October 23, 2012.  Museum School has been notified that the district will make no further boundary changes to accommodate its application should the Free and Reduced Lunch status of either school change.
