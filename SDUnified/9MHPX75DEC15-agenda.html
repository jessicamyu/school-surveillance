RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2016-17, 180-day instructional calendars reflected on Attachment 1. The superintendent recommends that for the 2016-17 school year, the first day of school for students should begin August 29, 2016 (pre-Labor Day).

 

At the December 9, 2014, board meeting, the 2015-16 and 2016-17 academic calendars were submitted for approval. The 2015-16 calendar was adopted, to begin on September 8, 2015 (post-Labor Day). The board directed staff to engage the community and solicit feedback regarding the proposed, pre-Labor Day start date for the 2016-17 calendar.

 

District staff have engaged the community to ensure that stakeholders are informed and understand the rationale behind the proposed change. In January 2015, staff developed presentation and feedback protocols, which have been presented to stakeholders including district advisory committees, cluster groups, the San Diego Unified Council of PTAs, Project Ujima, and other stakeholder groups. These presentation outreach activities began in February and continued through May in order to reach all identified groups.  As part of the presentations, participants were asked to provide comments on feedback forms which were collected at the meetings. The presentation included the link to the district webpage www.sandi.net/calendarchange where an online feedback form was posted so participants could submit comments electronically at their convenience. 

 

The presentation and feedback form were provided to sites to post on individual school websites, via the Principals’ Page and the district’s electronic newsletter, Unified Vision. The feedback form was not a formal survey but a mechanism to collect feedback and questions. The feedback form did not collect school or cluster demographics.

 

Of the presentations made directly at community meetings, a total of 265 feedback forms were collected from stakeholder participants.  Of these, 55 percent stated that they favored the recommended pre-Labor Day start date, 34 percent preferred a post-Labor Day start, and 11 percent were undecided or did not provide a preference.

 

Of the feedback submitted electronically to the district website, a total of 4,465 comments were collected.  Of these, 65.9 percent favored a post-Labor Day start, and 34.1 percent preferred a pre-Labor Day start.

 

At some of the stakeholder meetings, some participants asked about the outcome of the voting by the Calendar Committee.  Of the 15 members who were able to vote, 12 voted yes to recommend a pre-Labor Day start and three voted no.  The Administrators Association of San Diego (AASD) and the San Diego Education Association (SDEA) both formally submitted communication to the Committee indicating the organizations officially voted to support a pre-Labor Day start date.

 

Some participants also asked about start dates for San Diego County and other districts for comparison. For the 2015-16 School year, San Diego County Schools:  

  • 5 Districts start in July: Chula Vista Elementary, National San Diego, San Ysidro, South Bay, and Sweetwater
  • 4 Districts start the second week in August (10-14): Borrego Springs, Fallbrook UESD, La Mesa Spring Valley, and Warner Springs
  • 17 Districts start the third week in August (17-21): Bonsall, Cajon Valley, Encinitas, Escondido UHSD, Escondido USD, Fallbrook UHSD, Jamul-Delzura, Julian UHSD, Julian USD, Lemon Grove, Oceanside, Poway, Ramona, San Pasqual, Vallecitos SD, Valley Center, and Vista
  • 9 Districts start the last  week in August (24-28): Cardiff, Coronado, Dehesa, Del Mar USD, Rancho Santa Fe  SD, Santee, San Diegeuto USD, Ramona, and Solana Beach
  • 5 Districts start the first week in September (8/31 – 9/4): Alpine, Carlsbad, Grossmont, Lakeside, Mountain Empire, and San Marcos
  • San Diego Unified is the single, local district that starts POST Labor-Day

In Orange County of the 28 districts, 26 start in August/first week in September; two are POST-Labor Day

 

In Los Angeles County of the 10 districts, nine start in August/first week in September; one is POST-Labor Day

 

In addition to the calendar presentation and outreach, staff were also directed to provide historical attendance data for the 1998-99 school year when the traditional calendar began the week before Labor Day.  Data were collected for the two years prior, 1996-97 and 1997-98, and the two years after, 1999-2000 and 2000-01. 

 

First week enrollment (for five days) beginning in August was very high (97.34 percent), just as initial weeks’ attendance are also quite high, and is 0.3 percent points lower than the first week (four-day) attendance immediately after Labor Day.

 

Figure 1. Weekly attendance rates at district-managed schools before, during, and after Labor Day week, 1997 to 2001.

 

Annual district data for the same schools for these five years were also analyzed.  For the single school year (1998-99) in which school began prior to Labor Day, district attendance was higher than for the four school years immediately before or after.  Based on the weekly and overall attendance data, it is unlikely that a pre-Labor Day start would cause any overall decrease in attendance; it may even result in an overall increase in annual district attendance and ADA-related funding.

FISCAL IMPACT: No loss to the district.  

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. (All staff except for site administrator and custodial staff were excused.)

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The CDE permits districts to claim Average Daily Attendance (ADA) and instructional time credits for days on which schools are forced to close.  Approval of the request allows the school agency to receive credit for the lost ADA for the day of closure and for the missed instructional time.  Once the credit is approved, the district is no longer required to make up the day, and receives the ADA and instructional minutes as though the emergency did not occur.

 

The J-13, along with the board's affidavit, will be submitted to the County Office of Education and subsequently forwarded to the CDE.

 

This request, if approved, will maintain the district's ADA funding at the level that otherwise would have been received if the fire had not occurred.

BACKGROUND: Proposition 30, The Schools and Local Public Safety Protection Act of 2012, approved by the voters on November 6, 2012, temporarily increases the states sales tax rate for all taxpayers and the personal income tax rates for upper-income taxpayers.

 

The new revenues generated from Proposition 30 are deposited into a newly created state account called the Education Protection Account (EPA). School districts, county offices of education, and charter schools (Local Education Agencies or LEAs) will receive funds from the EPA based on their proportionate share of the statewide revenue limit amount, which includes charter school general purpose funding.

 

A corresponding reduction was made to an LEA's revenue limit or charter school general purpose state aid equal to the amount of their EPA entitlement. For 2012-13, LEAs received EPA payments at the end of June, 2013.  Beginning with the 2013-14 Fiscal Year, LEAs received these EPA payments on a quarterly basis which commenced in September 2013. 

 

Proposition 30 requires all districts, counties, and charter schools to report on their web sites an accounting of how much money was received from the EPA and how that money was spent and that the board acknowledge that the EPA funds will not be spent on administrators or other administrative costs.  Proposition 30 also requires that the use of EPA funds be determined by the governing board at an open public meeting prior to any expenditures being recorded for 2014-15.
