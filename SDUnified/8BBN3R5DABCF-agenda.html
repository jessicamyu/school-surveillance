FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Holly Drive Leadership Academy (HDLA) currently operates a K-8 charter school serving approximately 140 students.  The school is currently located on a private facility at 2716 Madison, Ave., San Diego, California 92116; but has accepted a facility under Proposition 39 to relocate at Webster Elementary School.

 

The district granted a charter to HDLA and renewed it in June of 2009.  Since that time, HDLA has taken steps to be operated by the HDLA, a nonprofit public benefit corporation.  At the time of the renewal, transition to a 501(c)(3) nonprofit public benefit corporation was a condition of renewal; this material revision to the charter is being undertaken to satisfy that condition.

Staff recommends the board approve the material revision to the charter of HDLA to transition from “arm-of-the-district” status to being operated by a nonprofit public benefit corporation the HDLA 501(c)(3), effective July 1, 2011 to June 30, 2014, the remainder of the term of its current charter.  Academic performance will be summarized and reviewed when HDLA seeks renewal of its charter in 2014.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the two-year transportation plan consistent with the board’s direction of May 10, 2011. The plan will:
• Eliminate all non-mandated transportation resulting in the district providing mandated transportation only in the 2013-14 school year.
• Refine and expand district procedure for parent reimbursement for providing transportation
• Increase the base fee for transportation from $340 to $420 (2011-12) to $500 (2012-13)
• Purchase and install ridership tracking system (2012-13)

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  Complete elimination of non-mandated transportation would result in an annual savings of approximately $3.1 million when fully implemented.  Under the proposed plan the elimination would be phased, resulting in approximately $1.7 million savings in year one of the plan and an additional savings of $1.4 million in year two of the plan.

 

These transportation changes will likely create overcapacity issues at a number of schools.  The initial projection of facilities construction costs to accommodate the overcapacity at eleven school sites is estimated at $56 million.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  The district will spend approximately $8.6 million on non-mandated transportation during the 2010-11 school year.  Other proposed budget actions for the 2011-12 school year will reduce non-mandated transportation by $3.75 million independent of this item.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The abolishment of non-mandated transportation will result in the elimination of approximately 40-50 Transportation Department personnel.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: At the May 10, 2011, special board meeting, district staff provided information regarding the impact of eliminating all non-mandated transportation.  The Trustees directed the staff to develop a two year plan to eliminate all non-mandated busing, increase the use of parent reimbursement; increase the fee for transportation; reserve some of the savings for technology improvements; and seek the Transportation Tiger Team’s assistance in developing the plan.

 

This plan will eliminate transportation to approximately 6,000 students by the start of the 2013 -14 school year, resulting in transportation services being provided only to students in mandated programs.  The remaining mandated programs include students transported under Special Education and Program Improvement, and students meeting requirements for transportation under the Mckinney Vento Act (homeless students) and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA 504).

 

It is difficult to determine how the students and families will respond to the change in transportation.  In projecting the impacts of this change on resident school capacities, staff assumes all students who are no longer provided transportation will return to their resident school.  This scenario would create significant overcapacity issues at 11 schools (For listing of schools, see attached May 10 board agenda exhibit 'BOE Memo Re Mandated Transportation Programs'), generating the potential for temporary and/or permanent construction.  Initial estimates provided by the district’s Facilities Planning and Construction Department indicate the need for a significant construction effort costing up to $56 million.  This would likely require realigning priorities within the district’s capital programs. 

 

Administrative Procedure 5415 provides for partial reimbursement to a parent /guardian for use of a private auto to transport a Special Education student under some conditions.  Following the board’s direction, Transportation and Special Education staffs will work to expand the use of this form of transportation.  However, transportation, as a Special Education related service must be made available to all eligible students.

 

Following the board’s direction, the Transportation Department will increase the fee for transportation, making equal increases of $80 per year in the coming two years to achieve the board’s direction to increase the base fee from $340 to $500.  Staff recommends a fee program of charging the base fee for the first rider in the family and reduced rates for additional students within the same family, charging 50 percent of the base fee for the second rider in the family and no additional cost to the family for the third or more riders.

 

Other proposed budget actions have resulted in a $7.85 million reduction to the Transportation Department for the 2011-12 school year.  These actions will result in a projected impact to approximately 4,200 students, many of whom in the worst case will not get a bus ride, while others would be impacted by a significant change such as a longer ride, greater distance to bus stop, etc.

BACKGROUND:  At the May 10 special meeting, the board received a report from consultants Jones Lang Lasalle, who were hired by the board to analyze the district’s 27 properties that have been declared excess by the Advisory Committee on the Utilization of Excess School Property (ACUESP).  Following the presentation, the board directed staff to prepare an outline for a potential study of all of the district’s property.

 

Due to the scope and complexity of the project, a consultant will need to be hired through a RFP process.  This phase will take approximately four months. 

 

A two phase process for the study is envisaged, and outlined in the attachments.  Following the hiring of a consultant, the first phase would include data collection (of facilities, demographics, the district’s academic master plans, and ongoing studies reviewing potential closure or consolidation of school sites) to make a preliminary determination of sites that merit further study, and sites that are core to the district’s educational mission and require no further study.  A steering committee whose composition can be determined at a later date would develop the goals and objectives of the project.  A number of broad strategies would be developed that would be aligned with the district’s Strategic Process for Quality Schools and the steering committee would present these strategies for board approval.  The first phase would take approximately four months to complete.

 

The second phase’s duration is dependent on how many properties merit deeper study as a result of the first phase.  These properties would be assessed in terms of such factors as their highest and best use, zoning issues, value range, timing and risk assessment.  A final strategic plan on the disposition of those properties, including impacts such as moving costs, renovations, etc, would be the final product to be presented for the board’s consideration.

RECOMMENDATION:  Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Support for Assembly Bill 751 (Furutani) School District Budget Planning Process. 

 

AB 751 (Furutani) updates the budget process for school districts.  Specifically, the bill changes the budget certification process by requiring county offices of education to distinguish between those school districts at risk of not meeting ascertainable financial obligations in the next two years and those flagged due to an assessment of risk in the third budget year.

 

Current county office certification results do not distinguish between a school district that is at immediate risk of being unable to meet its financial obligations the next year, and a school district that has two full years to improve the alignment of revenue and expenditures and may take additional time to refine its budgetary plan as more reliable information becomes available regarding revenue projections and fiscal policy parameters (e.g. flexibility options).  AB 751 would require county offices of education to make this type of distinction.

 

AB 751 maintains the existing requirement for a three-year budget planning process, unless a school district receives a waiver to present two-year budgets from both its local county office of education and the Superintendent of Public Instruction.
