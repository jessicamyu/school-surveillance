According to the American Heart Association (AHA), without immediate, effective cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), a person’s chance of surviving sudden cardiac arrest decreases seven to 10 percent per minute.

 

There is hope for cardiac arrest victims, communities with AED programs including comprehensive, hands-on CPR, have achieved survival rates of 40 percent or higher.

 

Hands-on psychomotor CPR training in 5th, 7th, and 9th grade physical education classes can deliver lifesaving techniques and help increase the likelihood that individuals suffering a cardiac arrest will receive high quality CPR.

 

The San Diego County Board of Supervisors has joined with the San Diego Unified School District along with the AHA on this important cause, funding $100,000 for CPR in the Classroom kits, which will provide mannequins in order to facilitate effective hands-on CPR.

 

The district supports and will encourage 5th, 7th, and 9th grade students, as part of their physical education program, to learn how to perform cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) and how to use an automated external defibrillator (AED).

 

AEDs are currently available at every school campus in the San Diego Unified School District and all staff, students, volunteers, and visitors are encouraged to be familiar with their use and are urged to use them in a cardiac emergency.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17388 requires a governing board of a school district, prior to the sale, lease, or rental of any excess real property (except rentals not exceeding 30 days) shall, appoint a district advisory committee to advise the board in the development of districtwide policies and procedures governing the use or disposition of school buildings or space in school buildings which is not needed for school purposes.  The specific duties of the committee are defined by Education Code section 17390 to be as follows:

 

(a) review the district's projected school enrollment and determine the amount of surplus space and real property the district has;

(b) establish a priority list of surplus space and real property that will be acceptable to the community;

(c) cause to have circulated throughout the attendance area a priority list of surplus property and provide and provide for hearings of community input to the committee on acceptable uses of space and real property, including sale or lease of surplus property for child care development purposes pursuant to Education Code section 17458;

(d) make a final determination of limits of tolerance of use of space and real property; and

(e) forward to the district governing board a report recommending uses of surplus space and real property.

 

These statutes were enacted by the Legislature in 1996, and amended in 1988, to provide for community input regarding the use of surplus property within the attendance area of the district.  However, the formation and aforementioned actions of the committee are mandated only "prior to the sale, lease, or rental of excess real property."   There is no requirement for such a committee to be maintained in perpetuity.  

 

Historically, this district has maintained the committee regardless of whether the board intends to take any action which would require it do so; and submits its annual property management plan (PMP) to the committee for its consideration prior to it being presented to the board.  This was last done in November of 2015.  However, none of this is required by statute.  Further, the district has no current intent to declare any new property to be surplus property.  (The last property to be declared surplus was Amici Park in 2014.)  Therefore, there is no longer any legal requirement for the board to maintain the committee.  

 

Therefore, staff recommends the committee be dissolved at this time.  If and when the board should determine a need to seek community input on the sale, lease, or rent of surplus property in the future,  or to substantially change the use or character of property since it was last considered by the committee, it can then be reconstituted; a process which should take no more than two to three months.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, reassignment of special education responsibilities, expansion of grade levels, or other significant revision.

 

Material revisions of a charter are governed by the standards and criteria set forth in Education Code section 47605, and shall include a reasonably comprehensive description of any new requirement of charter schools enacted into law after the charter was originally granted or last renewed.

For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 14, 2016, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607.
