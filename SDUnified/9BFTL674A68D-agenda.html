RECOMMENDATION: Denial of the petition to establish the Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation Charter School.

 

Pursuant to Education Code section 47605(b), the governing board of the school district shall not deny a petition for the establishment of a charter school unless it makes written factual findings, specific to the particular petition, setting forth specific facts to support one or more of the following findings:

  • The charter school presents an unsound educational program for the pupils to be enrolled in the charter school.
  • The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the petition.
  • The petition does not contain the required number of signatures.
  • The petition does not contain an affirmation of the conditions set forth in Education Code section 47605(d).    
  • The petition does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of all the elements set forth in Education Code section 47605(b).

FINDINGS: Staff recommends denial of the petition to establish a new charter school, Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation, based on the following findings.

  1. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the petition because the petitioners’ past history is one that San Diego Unified regards as unsuccessful.

    This is the third attempt made by the petitioners to obtain approval from San Diego Unified to open the Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation charter school.  The table below summarizes the chronology of petition submissions.

Third Petition submitted to San Diego Unified

Third Public Hearing

 

The First Petition was withdrawn due to an unrealistic financial plan that failed to include any reference to the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF), special education revenue or expenditures.

The Second Petition was withdrawn due to deficient signatures as well as an unrealistic financial plan.  With regard to the financial plan:

  • The Second Petition projected a negative ending fund balance and negative cash flow.
  • The cash flow included $90,000 of “Intracompany Transfers In” and $150,000 of “Intracompany Transfers Out” but does not provide details about the transactions.
  • The budget incorrectly used the same LCFF rate for different grade spans and did not include any supplemental grants.

In addition to submitting multiple petitions to the San Diego Unified, as summarized in the table below, the petitioners have previously closed a charter school, “Magnolia Science Academy San Diego 2,” due to low enrollment.

Magnolia’s Statewide Charter Schools Have Not Been Successful:

 

Pacific Technology School Santa Ana (PTSSA) statewide benefit charter school operated by the Magnolia Educational and Research Foundation (MERF), the same charter management organization that would operate the Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation if approved. 

 

In June 2013, MERF voluntarily closed one of two PTSSA charter schools.  The second PTSSA charter school is scheduled to close in June 2014.

 

This MERF-operated charter school recently received a letter of concern from the California Department of Education.  Specifically, Tom Torlakson, State Superintendent of Public Instruction, stated in a memorandum to the State Board of Education dated October 17, 2013:

 

“Currently, the CDE is working with Pacific Technology School Santa Ana (PTSSA) to address fiscal concerns related to enrollment, negative fund balance, and inadequate reserves.  PTSSA has responded to the CDE letter of concern dated April 4, 2013, by informing CDE that the negative fund balance and inadequate reserves are corrected by waiving the management fee of Magnolia Educational and Research Foundation (MERF) and intercompany loans between PTSSA and MERF, MERF’s donation, increasing the student enrollment after the completion of the facilities construction.”  (Emphasis added.)

 

Many of the MERF-operated charter schools, including the school proposed in the Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation petition, include an automatic transfer of 11 percent of revenue to the Magnolia management organization (MERF).  If enrollment projections are met, the 11 percent transfer represents approximately $257,312 beginning in the 2016-17 fiscal year for the Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation charter school. 

  1. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the petition because the petitioners have presented an unrealistic operational plan for the proposed charter with respect to facilities and enrollment. 

    The petition does not adequately describe the types and potential location of facilities needed to operate the size and scope of the proposed educational program.  The petitioners have identified the following zip codes in focusing their search for facilities: 92101, 92102, 92103, 92104, 92105, 92108, 92110, 92115, and 92116, which represents approximately 45 square miles.  As summarized in the table below, the petitioners request for facilities under the Proposition 39 (Education Code section 47612) process has been withdrawn. 

Date

11/1/2013

11/27/2013

San Diego Unified sent Letter of Concern regarding ADA projections.

12/13/2013

The petitioners are unlikely to reach their projected first year enrollment of 150 students in 2014-15.  The documentation submitted as part of the petitioners Proposition 39 application for facilities demonstrated that a significant portion of recent recruitment efforts for students to enroll at the proposed “Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation” appear to be misguided with respect with which charter school these families are interested in attending.  The petitioners submitted 107 forms from families to demonstrate that student interest in enrolling at the new school, Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation.  Only 7 out of the 31 families called said they were interested in enrolling at the new school, Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation.  Most families expressed that the form they completed was to communicate their interest in remaining at the existing school, Magnolia Science Academy San Diego, and not the new school Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation.  Historically, despite having an extra year to plan and recruit students to “Magnolia Science Academy San Diego 2,” the petitioners decided to close a charter school mid-year due to a low enrollment of just 8 students in 2010.

  1. The Magnolia Science Academy Next Generation petition does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of the governance structure of the school, which is required by Education Code section 47605(b)(5)(D).  The petition does not include adequate evidence that the organizational and technical designs of the governance structure of the proposed charter school reflect that there will be active and effective local representation of interested parties.  The governance structure proposed in the petition does not require that any charter board members be local San Diego residents.  Currently, 5 out of 6 of the Magnolia board members reside in Santa Clara County and Los Angeles County. 
  1. The petitioners are seeking to open the school in September 2014.  The current version of the charter petition was submitted on December 11, 2014, well after the November 1 deadline set by district policy.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Charter School Facility Committee has reviewed and recommended subject charter school facilities projects

 

BACKGROUND:  On September 4, 2012, the board passed a resolution establishing the San Diego Unified School District Charter School Facility Committee ("Committee") for the purpose of providing recommendations concerning acquisition, construction, reconstruction, rehabilitation, or replacement of charter school facilities, including the furnishing and equipping of charter school facilities. The Committee is comprised of seven members, including five charter school board members or employees, and two individuals with expertise, knowledge or background related to charter school operations/facilities. Committee members were nominated by the Superintendent after consultation with the charter school community and appointed by the board to two-year terms.

 

The committee held its initial meeting on April 2, 2013.  Since that time, the committee has held a total of 14 publicly noticed meetings. On May 6, 2013, the Committee formed a work plan ad-hoc sub-committee.  Among other tasks, this ad-hoc subcommittee was charged with developing a process for accepting and evaluating requests for Prop Z funds.  Since that date, the Committee and subsequently appointed ad-hoc subcommittees have worked to develop, discuss and revise a process for evaluating capital bond fund requests from the charter school community.  

 

At the Propositions S and Z updates public meeting on December 3, 2013, the board asked district staff to bring the Committee's process for evaluating capital bond fund requests from the charter school community to a January 2014 board meeting. The committee developed guidelines, planning grant application and rubric, and project grant application, and were reviewed at the January 7 board meeting, attached as Exhibit C.  

 

On January 7, 2014, the board discussed draft policy recommendations for the expenditure of Prop. Z charter school facilities funds. The draft policy recommendations are addressed in item E.6. on today's agenda.  The board passed a resolution directing district staff to review and comment on recommendation from the Charter School Facility Committee prior to board action.

BACKGROUND: On January 14, 2014, the Board of Education and Superintendent Marten adopted a joint resolution regarding Vision 2020: Working Together for Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.  The updated resolution before the board and superintendent today, includes updates to the dates, times, and locations of the five Vision 2020 forums to be held in each of the Board Trustee subdistrict areas.  The dates, times, and locations for the forums are as follows:

 

Friday, February 7, 2014   5:00-7:00 p.m., Memorial Preparatory Academy, Media Center, 2850 Logan Avenue, 92113

Tuesday, February 18, 2014  5:30-7:30 p.m., Patrick Henry High School, Media Center, 6702 Wandermere Drive, 92120

Monday, February 24, 2014  7:00-9:00 p.m., Mission Bay High School, Cafetorium, 2475 Grand Avenue, 92109

Monday, March 10, 2014  7:00-9:00 p.m., Crawford High School, Media Center, 4191 Colts Way, 92115

 

The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education has adopted Vision 2020 as a destination to arrive at, in support of students served by San Diego Unified.  The district is entering into a period of critical decisions that will need to be made, such as the Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP), the budgeting process, labor relations, and federal budget implications.  The board and superintendent seek to ensure that the vision and values of the San Diego community and its' stakeholders focus our lens through which these decisions will be made around these important topics.  Student, parent, employee, community, and other stakeholders voices need to inform the academic program and instruction necessary for community-based school reform.

 

The board and superintendent will jointly conduct, "Vision 2020: Working Together for Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood" forums to engage stakeholder participation and gather the community voice to impact the critical decisions that will be made moving forward this year.
