RECOMMENDATION:  Annual Review of the Regional Occupational Program (ROP) Enrollment and Adoption of the Recruitment Plan.

 

Education Code Section 52304.1 requires districts offering courses in ROP to annually review and assess participation of eleventh and twelfth grade pupils in ROP and to prepare an annual plan to increase enrollment of these pupils.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  There are no district budget implications associated with approval of this recruitment plan.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  There is no expense history associated with this recruitment plan which is informational only.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None. All staffing required to implement this plan is provided through ROP funding from the County Office of Education and State of California.

 

BACKGROUND:  Established in 1969, ROP is a state-funded program designed to prepare individuals 16 years of age or older to qualify for: entry-level employment opportunities, career advancement, and/or entry into advanced education programs in community colleges or four-year colleges.  State funding granted to San Diego Unified School District by ROP is overseen by the district Office of College, Career and Technical Education (CCTE). This structure and office has been implemented to ensure a confluence of efforts in the development of innovative programs and courses of study. Each sequence of courses will have high quality, standards and competencies-based curriculum that lead to a mastery of skills and a continued passion for life-long learning.  The integration of core academics, career technical content and career exploration are presented in a personalized, supportive and caring environment of high expectations for all students. These programs prepare students for the challenges and opportunities of the 21st century. To ensure a successful transition from the critical years of childhood to young adulthood, CCTE equip students with the skills they need to become responsible citizens, contributing members of society and gainfully employed.

 

Over the years, district staff have implemented ROP promotion and recruitment practices that have resulted in annual enrollment increases of eleventh and twelfth grade students.  The total percentage of high school to adult student enrollments has steadily increased over the past fifteen years.  High school enrollment has increased from a low of 37 percent in the 1992-93 school year to 99 percent in the 2008-10 school year.  The district is in compliance with the passage of AB 2448 which required that adult students comprise no more than 10 percent of ROP enrollment by July 1, 2010.
 

All CTE courses, beginning with capstone courses that qualify for ROP funding, have been, or are in process, for UC "a-g" approval.  As of July 1, 2010, 27 CTE Capstone courses meet UC "a-g" requirements.  As a CCTE Key Performance Indicator, to date 68 percent of the capstone courses offered now qualify for UC "a-g" classification.  Additional submissions have been made for UC "a-g" consideration for the 2010-11 school year, with 5-8 capstone courses currently in preparation for review at the next UC "a-g" application period.

 

Information on the broad scope of CCTE was presented at the July 13, 2010 board workshop. Attached to this submission is an update on the status of UC "a-g" course submissions for CTE Capstone courses that qualify for ROP funding. Courses have been accepted by UC, submitted to UC, have been slated to be submitted to UC, or identified for course closure. Policy review and recommendation consistent with this plan are forthcoming during this school year.

  1. the governing board met before the end of the eighth week of school and did not take place during or immediately following school hours; and
     
  2. ten days notice of the public hearing was posted in at least three public places (district website, Eugene Brucker Education Center, Instructional Media Center) stating time, place, and purpose; and
     
  3. every pupil, Kindergarten through Grade 12, has been provided with a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials in each of the following areas: mathematics, science, history/social science, and English language arts; and
     
  4. every pupil enrolled in a world language or health course has been provided a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials; and

  6. instructional materials for students in Kindergarten through Grade 8 were purchased from an approved standards-aligned state adoption list; and
     
  7. new standards-aligned instructional materials for Grades 7-12 in world languages were adopted by the Board of Education on April 13, 2010; and
     
  8. each pupil in Grades 9-12 in world language classes has been provided a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials from this adopted list by September 7, 2010.

Pursuant to Education Code Section 60422, ten days notice of the public hearing was posted containing the time, place, and purpose of the hearing.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  The district uses funding from various sources, including state funds from the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) and state lottery funds to purchase textbooks and instructional materials according to the state and local adoption cycles.  Once the provision of standards-aligned instructional materials in the four core academic subject areas is determined through this resolution, IMFRP funding typically may be spent on other instructional materials pursuant to Education Code 60242.  Education Code 42605 provides further flexibility with the use of these funds for any educational purpose for the 2008-09 fiscal year to the 2012-12 fiscal year, inclusive.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

BACKGROUND:  AB 1781 established the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) that, starting with the 2002-03 fiscal year, combined three previous funding sources for instructional materials.  The program requires that districts must use funds to ensure each pupil, K-12, is provided with a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials in the four core subject areas: English language arts, mathematics; science; and history/social science.  Additionally, in accordance with State regulation, the governing board must also certify the standards-aligned basic instructional materials are provided by the beginning of the school term that starts no later than 24 months after those materials were adopted.  Education Code 60422.1 waives this 24 month requirement for the 2008-09 to the 2012-13 fiscal years, inclusively.  The governing board of a school district is not required to provide pupils with instructional materials by a specified period of time following adoption of those materials by the state board.  This does not relieve school districts of their obligations to provide every pupil with standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, per Education 1240.3.  Once the governing board certifies it has provided each pupil with standards-aligned instructional materials, the district may use 100 per cent of any remaining IMFRP funds to purchase other instructional materials consistent with the content and cycles of the curriculum frameworks.  Education Code 412605 provides further flexibility with the use of these funds for any education purpose for the 2008-09 fiscal year to the 2012-13 fiscal year, inclusive.

 

For students in Kindergarten through Grade 8, the instructional materials were purchased from an approved standards-aligned state adoption list as required by California Code of Regulations (CCR), Title 5, Section 95311.  In addition, the board adopted new standards-aligned instructional materials for Grades 7-12 in world languages on April 13, 2010, and these instructional materials were provided to each pupil in these classes by September 7, 2010, which is the start of the first school term.

RECOMMENDATION:  Conduct a public hearing and approve Resolution of the Sufficiency of Textbooks and/or Instructional Materials and certify that:

  1. the governing board met before the end of the eighth week of  school and did not take place during or immediately following school hours; and
     
  2. ten days notice of the public hearing was posted in at least three public places (district website, Eugene Brucker Education Center, Instructional Media Center) stating time, place, and purpose; and
     
  3. every pupil, including English learners, has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both, that are aligned to the content standards and cycles of the curriculum framework adopted by the State Board of Education: mathematics, science, history/social science, and English language arts; and
     
  4. every pupil enrolled in a world language or health course has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both, and are consistent with the content and cycles of the curriculum frameworks adopted by the state board; and
     
  5. laboratory science equipment is available to science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12 inclusive; and
     
  6. “sufficient textbooks or instructional materials” means each pupil, including English language learners, has a textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home and this does not require two sets of textbooks or instructional materials for each pupil; and
     
  7. beginning 2008-09 through 2012-13 fiscal years, “sufficient textbooks or instructional materials” also means that all student, who are enrolled in the same course within the San Diego Unified School District, have standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials from the same adoption cycle; and
     
  8. all district schools have conducted classroom instructional materials inventories, insufficiencies have been remedied, and principals have submitted “Principal’s Certification of Textbook Sufficiency” statements.

 Pursuant to Education Code Section 60422, ten days notice of the public hearing was posted containing the time, place, and purpose of the hearing.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  The district uses funding from various sources, including state Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) funds and state lottery funds to purchase textbooks and instructional materials according to the state adoption cycle. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of the adoption by requesting transfers of textbooks and instructional materials through the district’s Instructional Materials Office using the Destiny Textbook Management System or by purchasing instructional materials with funding provided to support growth in student enrollment or replacement of lost and/or damaged materials. 
 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

BACKGROUND:  In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a public hearing no later than the eighth week of the school year to determine, through a resolution, whether each pupil in the district has sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English/language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. Access to two sets of instructional materials is not required.

 

Current Williams Legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12.  The Science Department developed basic core instructional materials lists for Biology, Chemistry, Earth Science, and Physics classrooms.  All schools conducted inventories and principals signed and submitted Science Equipment Sufficiency Certifications.

 

Williams Settlement Legislation forced districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of our annual resolution of sufficiency.  Administrative Circulars 10, 11, and 12, dated September 7, 2010, were posted notifying school sites of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Certification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”  Teacher Certification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency forms were included for completion by classroom teachers and returned to principals by September 17, 2010.  After completing the inventory and taking action to remedy insufficiencies, each principal signed the Principal Certification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency form and returned it to the Instructional Materials Office by September 24, 2009.  In the event of insufficiencies, an addendum was faxed to principals for signature, verifying the instructional materials ordered to achieve sufficiency had been received and the school was indeed sufficient. 

 

Additionally, 27 elementary schools, seven K-8 schools, seven middle schools, and 15 senior high schools were visited by a team from San Diego County Office of Education.  All 56 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials.  All 15 senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve waivers for Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers to qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to be submitted to the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing. Attached is a list of the teachers.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  Twenty-five of 250 CCTE teachers require the waiver while they complete their training this year allowing them to continue to provide instruction in the 16 CTE programs they represent at more than eight high schools.

 

BACKGROUND: California Education Code Section 44253.1 requires all teachers to have an active and valid English Learner authorization if assigned to a classroom that has one or more students designated as an English Learner. Because of the Williams Settlement legislation, a review of this Education Code has determined that the need for an English Learner authorization also applies to all Career Technical Education (CTE) and JROTC certificated staff employed by the district. Teachers who hold a bachelor’s degree and completed an approved teacher preparation program leading to the issuance of a regular education credential or special education credential, but did not hold an English Learner authorization, qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to cover them in their assignments while completing the requirements to obtain a Clear CLAD Certificate.

Due to the fact that Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers are not required to hold a bachelor’s degree or complete an approved teacher preparation program to be issued a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing, this sizable group of district teachers do not qualify for an Emergency CLAD Permit. On March 19, 2008, the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing announced its approval of SB 1292 giving all holders of Designated Subjects Teaching Credentials an option to obtain an English Learner authorization. Prior to this approval, there was no legal option available to the holder of a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential to be issued any form of English Learner authorization. On September 26, 2008, the San Diego County Office of Education began the first session of training for qualifying teachers employed in the county.

After working with the San Diego County Office of Education, the district has been advised that all Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers will need to apply for CLAD Waivers with the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing in order not to be considered mis-assigned for the 2010-11 academic school year. These waivers will be valid for the 2010-11 school year but can be renewed for an additional year in the event the San Diego County Office of Education cannot accommodate the county’s entire population of teachers who need to complete the training.

RECOMMENDATION: Authorize the use of Agreement No. 08-44-685 with Virco Manufacturing Corporation as awarded by Wichita Public School District No. 259 for the purchase of classroom furniture.  The term of this Agreement is from January 1, 2011 through December 31, 2011.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  The amount of Virco classroom furniture purchased during FY 2009-10 totaled $548,311.24.  Since July 1, 2010, the amount of Virco classroom furniture purchased totaled $725,421.17.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  When furniture is purchased and shipped to the district's warehouse, it is received and inspected by district stock clerks, and then it is delivered to its final destination by warehouse truck drivers.

 

BACKGROUND:  Since 1956, the district has purchased its classroom furniture from the Virco Manufacturing Company.  Virco manufactures quality classroom furniture that withstands decades of daily use by multiple students in district classrooms, providing dependable service well beyond the furniture’s warranty period.  Virco furniture has evolved during this time to meet the changing needs of the district’s classrooms.  Virco classroom furniture meets current standards established by the Business and Institutional Furniture Manufacturer’s Association (BIFMA) and accepted by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI).  The Virco classroom furniture installed at Lincoln High and the newly installed furniture at both Washington Elementary and Hoover High are ADA-compliant.  Virco products comply with California Air Resources Board (CARB) regulations on formaldehyde emissions from composite wood products and the federal Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act of 2008 (CSPIA).

 

By using this cooperative agreement, the district has continued to further reduce its cost by leveraging its purchases as part of a $100 Million nationwide Virco contract used by other school districts and governmental agencies. This agreement provides the lowest cost, net to buyer, that Virco will sell its classroom furniture, and it provides the district with multiple delivery options and greater discounts for larger orders which further reduces costs.

 

Because Virco is the manufacturer, it provides turnkey support to the district, from pre-sales collaboration with school principals and staff to the “last mile” delivery of furniture to the district’s warehouse or school site.  Virco's headquarters and manufacturing facility is in Torrance, California, with an additional manufacturing facility in Conway, Arkansas.  In anticipation of the start of the 2010-11 school year, classroom furniture was ordered from Virco as late as July and August for Lafayette and Logan elementary schools; Marston and Millennial Tech middle schools; Hoover, iHigh, Madison and San Diego high schools; and Early/Middle College and San Diego Metro Career/Mesa Community College .  Despite the district's lateness in ordering this classroom furniture just weeks before the beginning of the school year, all these orders were completely delivered by Virco prior to the first day of school.

 

Under Government Code section 6500 et seq, a school district may purchase products from a contract that has been competitively bid by a Joint Powers Authority, consortium, or cooperative so long as that school district has membership in that entity. A school district may only purchase the products specified in the cooperative or consortium contract at the prices specified in that cooperative or consortium contract.
