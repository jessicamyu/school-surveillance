BACKGROUND: The son of migrant farm workers, César E. Chavez learned about the oppression of field laborers firsthand and directed his life goals and aspirations toward educating his fellow farm workers to understand their rights to a safe workplace and a just wage. In 1962, Chavez founded the United Farm Workers of America. From his studies in civic action and his study of the work of Gandhi and Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., Chavez developed and adhered to principles of non-violence in his crusade for peace and justice. César E. Chavez said, “Real education should consist of drawing the goodness and the best out of our own students. What better book can there be than the book of humanity.” César E. Chavez and legions of loyal supporters dedicated themselves to the education of migrant workers and their children through the establishment of better schools, fighting to empower farm workers through voter registration, and teaching community members how to organize themselves to effectively represent their interests with government, school, and financial institutions. California schools are authorized to commemorate César E. Chavez with student community service projects and are directed to provide instruction on the farm labor movement in the United States and the role of César E. Chavez. The successful leadership development of César E. Chavez Service Clubs, organized by San Diego Unified and Urban Corps of San Diego, are now being emulated in California’s most populated cities throughout the state. San Diego Unified dedicated César E. Chavez Elementary School, in September 1997, to provide an education program for more than 500 students.

The Coronavirus pandemic is increasing racism against people of Asian descent both emotionally and physically harming the AAPI community. Racism and xenophobia directed at Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders have been exacerbated by the pandemic. There has been a rise of discrimination and hate crimes against AAPI communities. San Diego Unified has a long-standing commitment to address systemic and structural racism as part of our commitment to become an anti-racist district.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the attendance boundary changes for the 2021-22 year included in the attached Board Report, as listed below:

  1. Adjustment to attendance area boundaries of Carson Elementary, Chesterton Elementary, Fletcher Elementary and Linda Vista Elementary in the Kearny High School cluster, with legacy attendance areas to apply to current students and their concurrent siblings; and
  2. Removal of optional area between Carson and Fletcher elementary schools.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable. District staff presented the plan (Attachment 2 to the Board Report) at the Kearny Cluster school/community group and solicited public comment. Outreach was extended to parents, school site staffs and community stakeholders, as is outlined in the board report (Attachment 1 to the Board Report).

 

BACKGROUND: At the April 21, 2015, Board Workshop "Logistics & Implementation of Quality Neighborhood Schools in Every Neighborhood," one of the "Concepts to Action" involved analyzing the district's optional attendance areas through the lens of providing clear articulation patterns within clusters to strengthen neighborhood schools. There are currently nine optional areas, reduced from twenty-five (Exhibit 1 to the attached Board Report).

 

The Carson-Fletcher Optional Area (Exhibit 2 to the Board Report) was created in 1975. Reasons for its creation are unclear now, 45 years later. The enrollment choices of the majority of students living in this area can be accommodated through a boundary adjustment rather than by the continuation of this optional area.

 

In alignment with the Workshop's Concept to Action, staff analyzed the Carson-Fletcher optional area and recommends dividing the area among three Kearny Cluster elementary schools: Carson Elementary, Fletcher Elementary and Linda Vista Elementary (Area 1 of Exhibit 3, and Exhibit 4 of the Board Report). Separately, to improve student safety and strengthen neighborhood cohesion, a minor adjustment is proposed between Chesterton Elementary and Linda Vista Elementary near the intersection of Genesee Avenue and Whitney Street (Area 2 of Exhibit 3, of the Board Report).

 

Staff analyzed attendance area boundaries in consideration of factors outlined in Board Policy 5116a, “School Attendance Boundaries.” This included enrollment patterns, facility capacity, community input, student safety, street networks, and educational programs.

 

School and cluster leaders reviewed the draft proposal, then district staff reached out to community representatives, and presented the proposed adjustments to the Kearny Cluster. Families of Carson, Fletcher, and Chesterton Elementary Schools were contacted about this proposed attendance area adjustment through school messenger distributions in January 2021. An email address was provided, inviting families to contact the district directly with questions, comments, or concerns.

 

Existing students living in the changed attendance areas will continue attending their current schools, along with any concurrently enrolled younger siblings. New and future students will be subject to the new attendance area boundaries.

On February 2, 2021, the board approved an open and transparent, inclusive search process for the recruitment and selection of a permanent Superintendent upon the resignation of Superintendent Cindy Marten. The plan includes: 

  • An Advisory Committee that reflects the diversity of our students and the San Diego Community.  
  • The selection process shall be supported by an organization tasked with leading town halls, listening sessions and community engagement meetings. The Advisory Committee will draft a Request for Proposals (“RFP”) which will be used to solicit proposals from interested organizations by the end of March 2021. These proposals will be reviewed by the Advisory Committee that will make the recommendation to the Board for the organization that will lead town halls, listening sessions and community engagement meetings. Town Halls will be held in late Spring and early Summer. This will lead to the creation of a vision for the future of the district, and the identification of the qualities of a Superintendent to lead that vision.
  • The Advisory Committee will utilize the information gathered during the town halls, listening sessions and community engagement meetings to develop the job posting.
  • The position will then be posted by the end of July 2021.  The recruitment will open to qualified candidates from around the globe.
  • The Advisory Committee will screen the candidate application packages and will recommend up to ten (10) candidates to be interviewed by the Board in September 2021.
  • The Board will select up to three (3) finalists by October 2021.
  • Community forums will be held in early November 2021 for the public to meet the finalists and provide feedback.
  • A permanent Superintendent will be selected by the beginning of December 2021 to allow for a month-long transition into the position.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: Approval of the proposed License Agreement (Proposed Project) is subject to the requirements of the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et. seq., as amended). However, the Proposed Project is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 – Existing Facilities and 15323 - Normal Operations of Existing Facilities for Public Gatherings (Class 23).

 

A Class 1 exemption consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. Demolition and removal of individual small structures is also included (CEQA Guidelines §15301). The proposed License Agreement would result in the temporary use of existing school sites by the County for a limited term and would not result in the expansion of existing use. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 exemption is appropriate.

 

A Class 23 exemption consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose. For the purposes of this section, “past history” shall mean that the same or similar kind of activity has been occurring for at least three years and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323). Some of the proposed schools that will be utilized by the County have been used as polling places in the past. In addition, the proposed License Agreement does not represent a permanent change in the operation of the existing school sites. Therefore, the application of a Class 23 exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

 

Environmental Analysis: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location: The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundary of existing school sites. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact: The Proposed Project would not result in new construction activities, result in a permanent change in existing school operations, or result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect: The Proposed Project includes the execution of a license agreement for the temporary use of school sites to be used as polling places. No new construction or permanent operational activity is proposed on- or off-site that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways: The Proposed Project does not require the construction of new facilities and therefore, would not directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not substantially damage scenic resources within a state scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites: According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources: The Proposed Project does not require the construction of new facilities and therefore, would not result in a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource as described in CEQA Guidelines Section 15064.5(b)). Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.
