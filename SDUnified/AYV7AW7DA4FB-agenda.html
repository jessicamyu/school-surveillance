RECOMMENDATION: Staff recommends the board approve the renewal of the charter of Holly Drive Leadership Academy (“HDLA”) for a five-year term beginning July 1, 2019, and ending June 30, 2024, with the following clarification:

 

1. Clarification noted below for page 51 regarding SELPA Affiliation.

2. Clarification noted below for page 60 regarding Educational Design as Guide.

Revise the Educational Design as Guide section as detailed: The educational design outlined in this petition represents a meaningful guide for developing specific educational programs, student outcomes, and methods of assessment at Holly Drive Leadership Academy. 3. Clarification noted below for page 123 regarding Dispute Resolution

Revise the Dispute Resolution section as detailed: The Principal and Superintendent, or their respective designees, shall informally meet and confer in a timely fashion to attempt to resolve the dispute, not later than five (5) business days from receipt of the dispute statement. The educational program described in the HDLA charter petition includes the following characteristics:

  • Ensuring that students are exposed to a variety of resources and experiences (“place-based learning”), both in and outside of the local community.
  • Character development, a nurturing environment, and leadership skills are emphasized alongside standards-based curriculum.
  • Hands-on, project based learning.

Holly Drive Leadership Academy (HDLA), founded by John Walton in 1999, is in its fifth charter term. The school’s mission has been and continues to be to create an institution that provides a small, safe, family-like, educational safe haven for low performing, under-represented, at risk youth in the Southeast San Diego area. HDLA’s approach has resulted in the transformation of numerous at risk youth into high school and college bound scholars. The school uses love, acceptance, experiences, and rigor to produce students who are high school ready and college bound. Multiple measures are used to assess student achievement, grow their creativity, and inspire them to greatness. HDLA is proud to be a leader in teacher efficacy for at risk youth and proud to do its part to undergird and pass the baton to SDUSD high schools by sending its students back to district high schools ready to take on the challenges and rigor of a high school education.

 

Holly Drive Leadership Academy believes the greatest gift you can give someone is access and exposure. The “classroom without walls” approach is the cornerstone of the educational program, and provides numerous unique off campus workshops and opportunities. By broadening a student’s exposure it changes their mindset and provokes them into reaching higher and gives them the realization that the world is bigger than Southeast San Diego; and opportunities are out there for those who work for them. This approach has increased attendance, lowered suspension rates, increased school spirit and morale, and advanced learning. It is also increasing graduation rates.

 

Holly Drive Leadership Academy is humbled to have been afforded the opportunity to not only establish Holly Drive Leadership Academy, but be part of the original pioneers in the charter movement by providing school choice to families regardless of income levels. HDLA is committed to being a mission driven academy where students are given a high-quality education and an opportunity to close achievement gaps in a safe, nurturing, loving environment. The school will continue to provide an educational program with committed faculty, staff and teachers; many who have been with HDLA since its inception.

 

Over the next five years, the school’s plans include continuing to train and study the frameworks for best practices (specifically in math), develop classrooms with advanced technology, increase thematic units for deeper learning, implement increased lab work and fully commit to providing each and every one of the students with a quality education.

RENEWAL ANALYSIS

 

When considering a petition for renewal, pursuant to Title 5 of the California Code of Regulations section 11966.4(b)(1), the district governing board shall consider the past performance of the school’s academics, finances, and operations in evaluating the likelihood of future success, along with future plans for improvement, if any.

 

ACADEMICS

 

As the charter granting authority, pursuant to Education Code section 47607(a)(3)(A), the district shall consider increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils served by the charter school as the most important factor in determining whether to grant a charter renewal. For purposes of this section, “all groups of pupils served by the charter school” means a numerically significant pupil subgroup served by the charter school, as defined in Education Code section 52052(a)(3).[1]

 

Per Education Code section 47607(b), a charter school shall meet at least one of the following criteria before receiving a renewal:[2]

  1. Attained its Academic Performance Index (API) growth target in the prior year or in two of the last three years both schoolwide and for all groups of pupils served by the charter school.
  2. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API in the prior year or in two of the last three years.
  3. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API for a demographically comparable school in the prior year or in two of the last three years.
  4. The entity that granted the charter determines that the academic performance of the charter school is at least equal to the academic performance of the public schools that the charter school pupils would otherwise have been required to attend, as well as the academic performance of the schools in the school district in which the charter school is located, taking into account the composition of the pupil population that is served at the charter school.
  5. Qualified for an alternative accountability system pursuant to Education Code section 52052(h).

Assembly Bill 1808 recently amended Education Code section 52052(f) which now states:

 

“For any program identified in law that utilized a calculation pursuant to the former Academic Performance Index established pursuant to this section, as this section read on January 1, 2018, the 2013 growth calculation shall be applied for those purposes. For purposes of paragraphs (1) to (3), inclusive, of subdivision (b) of Section 47607, alternative measures that show increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils schoolwide and among numerically significant pupil subgroups shall be used.”

As the charter granting authority, pursuant to Education Code section 47607(a)(3)(A), the district shall consider increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils served by the charter school as the most important factor in determining whether to grant a charter renewal. For purposes of this section, “all groups of pupils served by the charter school” means a numerically significant pupil subgroup served by the charter school, as defined in Education Code section 52052(a)(3).[1]

 

Per Education Code section 47607(b), a charter school shall meet at least one of the following criteria before receiving a renewal:[2]

  1. Attained its Academic Performance Index (API) growth target in the prior year or in two of the last three years both schoolwide and for all groups of pupils served by the charter school.
  2. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API in the prior year or in two of the last three years.
  3. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API for a demographically comparable school in the prior year or in two of the last three years.
  4. The entity that granted the charter determines that the academic performance of the charter school is at least equal to the academic performance of the public schools that the charter school pupils would otherwise have been required to attend, as well as the academic performance of the schools in the school district in which the charter school is located, taking into account the composition of the pupil population that is served at the charter school.
  5. Qualified for an alternative accountability system pursuant to Education Code section 52052(h).

Assembly Bill 1808 recently amended Education Code section 52052(f) which now states:

 

“For any program identified in law that utilized a calculation pursuant to the former Academic Performance Index established pursuant to this section, as this section read on January 1, 2018, the 2013 growth calculation shall be applied for those purposes. For purposes of paragraphs (1) to (3), inclusive, of subdivision (b) of Section 47607, alternative measures that show increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils schoolwide and among numerically significant pupil subgroups shall be used.”

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the revision to the charter petition of San Diego Cooperative Charter School to revise its admissions preferences effective March 6, 2019. The revisions would create the following two new admission preferences:

  1. A preference for students attending or residing within the boundaries of the two district elementary schools that are located closest to the charter school’s two sites.
  2. A preference for students living with active military service members.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Staff recommends the board grant the revision to the charter petition of San Diego Cooperative Charter School (“SDCCS”) to revise its admissions preferences, effective March 6, 2019. The revisions would create two new admission preferences:

  1. A preference for students attending or residing within the boundaries of the two district elementary schools that are located closest to the charter school’s two sites.
  2. A preference for students living with active military service members.

San Diego Cooperative Charter School is operated by the Theresa Hessling Charter School Project nonprofit public benefit corporation. The charter petition for SDCCS was renewed in March 2017, for a term beginning July 1, 2017, and ending June 30, 2022. Prior to June 30, 2018, the nonprofit corporation also operated San Diego Cooperative Charter School 2 (“SDCCS2”). As of July 1, 2018, SDCCS2 has “merged” into SDCCS. SDCCS is now one school that serves Grades K-8 at two district-owned sites: 1) 7260 Linda Vista Road (92111), and 2) 3550 Logan Avenue (92113), a co-located site with Emerson/Bandini.

 

The first preference listed above will make SDCCS eligible to receive SB740 funding. The two district schools that are closest to the SDCCS locations are Linda Vista Elementary and Emerson/Bandini. SB740 funding provides annual assistance to eligible charter schools for facilities rent and lease expenditures. Eligible charter schools may be awarded $1,117 per unit of classroom-based ADA, up to 75 percent of the annual facilities rent and lease costs. To be eligible for SB740 funding, charter schools must meet one of the following criteria:

  1. 55 percent or more of the student enrollment at the charter school site is eligible for prior year Free or Reduced-Price Meal Eligibility (“FRPM”).
  2. The charter school is physically located in the attendance area of a public elementary school in which 55 percent or more of the pupil enrollment is eligible for prior year FRPM, and the school site gives an admissions preference to pupils who are currently enrolled in that public elementary school and to pupils who reside in that school’s attendance area.

In 2017-18, approximately 27.5 percent of students enrolled at SDCCS were socioeconomically disadvantaged.

 

San Diego Cooperative Charter School currently leases four portables that are located on the Linda Vista site. The portables house Grades 1-2, and account for approximately 92.70 ADA for the site. The annual lease cost for 2018-19 is approximately $30,515, of which $21,534 will be reimbursed under SB740.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, reassignment of special education responsibilities, expansion of grade levels, or other significant change.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve the resolutions to eliminate certificated and classified services.

 

Resolutions and exhibits include:

  • Certificated Particular Kinds of Services (PKS)
  • Administrators Association of San Diego (AASD) Classified Supervisors
  • Operational Support Services (OSS)
  • Office Technical and Business Services (OTBS)
  • Paraeducators (PARA)

 

FISCAL IMPACT: All actions taken by the board will be to prepare a balanced budget for 2019-20.  The impacts and effects of these position eliminations may be subject to negotiations with the district’s bargaining units.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The specific positions to be eliminated are in the Certificated Particular Kinds of Service Resolution and the Resolutions in the Matter of the Reduction or Elimination of Classified Services: Administrators Association of San Diego (AASD); Operational Support Services (OSS); Office Technical and Business Services (OTBS); and Paraeducators (PARA).

 

Approval and full implementation of the personnel resolutions will result in a reduction in currently performed certificated and classified services, and a corresponding reduction in permanent and probationary employees (as appropriate), as well as vacant positions.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The purpose of this item is to ensure the district maintains its strong fiscal position in the coming school year. All items referenced pertain to school year 19-20. For the current school year, San Diego Unified is operating within a balanced budget, as reflected by the positive certification issued by our financial oversight agency, the County Office of Education.

 

Positive certification reflects the County’s assessment we have sufficient funds in place to meet our financial obligation for the coming two years. The structural deficit that haunted the district for years has been eliminated. Most of the savings have come through deep cuts to the central office departments. Together, the Board and staff have worked with our partners across all bargaining units to put every available dollar to work serving students in the classroom. This item continues that focus.

 

At its January 822, 2019 meeting, the Board asked staff to consider a targeted approach to future budget reductions, as opposed to across the board cuts through tools like a reduced work year. For that reason, the reductions outlined in this item are focused and limited.

 

Reflecting the commitment to put students’ needs first, the staffing changes outlined in this document are based on student-based budgeting recently completed by all schools within the district. This means student demand is the primary element schools consider in adding and eliminating positions. Other key factors include the district’s commitment to student equity.

 

Parent input has also led to many of the staffing changes listed in this item. Historically, families have found the services and placement of our Childhood Development Centers too restrictive to meet their needs. In 2017, the district began a transition to school campus-based preschool programs, which included a push to enroll more students. That effort has been successful, and preschool enrollment jumped to 96% of state-funded capacity during the first year of that initiative. This item continues the new shift in emphasis by phasing out our remaining CDCs, while continuing to provide service to all students of those facilities. All impacted staff and families at these centers have already been contacted by staff.

 

The district has worked hard in recent years to collaborate with our bargaining units to provide a compassionate response to all employees who have their positions impacted by the arbitrary nature of education funding in our state. Employees impacted by this item have already been asked to check their seniority to assist with reassignment. Each will also be offered personalized supports to assist with future career options. This level of detailed support includes possible solutions such as training excessed preschool teachers to meet our many open positions in Special Education.

 

Certificated staff

For permanent certificated employees, the Education Code requires that the board identify the particular kinds of services or programs that may be reduced or eliminated in the upcoming fiscal year. For probationary certificated employees, Article 19 of the collective bargaining agreement between the district and SDEA allows the board to determine that reductions are necessary due to lack of funds. The list of services to be reduced are stated as a full-time equivalent (FTE) rather than a number of employees. It is determined based on factors related to potentially disputed issues regarding seniority (and deviation therefrom), certificates, and qualifications for certain positions to displace less senior employees. Temporary certificated employees will receive a separate reminder notice that their services will end at the close of the 2018-19 school year.

 

Classified staff

Resolutions in the matter of the elimination of the classified services are the result of the budget reductions. Of the positions listed in the attached resolutions and exhibits, nearly half of the positions are vacant.
