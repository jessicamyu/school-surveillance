BACKGROUND: Women of every race, class, and ethnic background have made historic contributions to the growth and strength of our nation in countless recorded and unrecorded ways and women have played and continue to play critical economic, cultural, and social roles in every sphere of American life by constituting a significant portion of the labor force, providing the majority of the volunteer labor force and establishing charitable, philanthropic, and cultural institutions. Women make up 76 percent of the nation's educators and play a leading role in educational institutions across the globe, including San Diego Unified. As recently as the 1970s, women's history was virtually an unknown topic in the K-12 curriculum or in general public consciousness. In 1980 President Carter issued the first Presidential Proclamation declaring the week of March 2-8, 1980, as National Women's History Week, during which he asked Americans to focus their observances on women leaders who struggled for equality such as Susan B. Anthony, Sojourner Truth, Lucy Stone, Lucretia Mott, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, Harriet Tubman, and Alice Paul. One hundred years after the ratification of the 19th Amendment giving women the right to vote, the effects of this momentous step forward are clear to see, with women serving as early leaders in the forefront of every major progressive social change movement, including the industrial labor movement, the civil rights movement, and the peace movement, to create a more fair and just society for all. San Diego is proud to include among its women leaders philanthropist Ellen Browning Scripps and horticulturist Kate Sessions, for whom elementary schools in the San Diego Unified School District are named; philanthropists Joan Kroc and Audrey Geisel, whose contributions to children's causes benefit San Diego Unified students every day; educational leaders Bertha Pendleton, San Diego's first female Superintendent, Constance Carroll, Chancellor of the San Diego Community College District, and Adela de la Torre, President of San Diego State University; political leaders Lucy Killea, Maureen O'Connor, Christine Kehoe, Toni Atkins, Lorena Gonzalez and Shirley Weber; community activists Linda LeGerrette, co-founder of the Cesar Chavez Service Clubs, Andrea Guerrero, Executive Director of Alliance SD, andNorma Chavez-Peterson, Executive Director of the San Diego ACLU; and many, many others.

5. Resolution in Support of Actions that will Educate Families and Encourage a Complete 2020 Census Count (McQuary/Barrera)
Meeting
Mar 10, 2020 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of Actions that Will Educate Families and Encourage a Complete 2020 Census Count.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The decennial Census occurs every ten years to enumerate every person residing in the United States, and is fundamental to ensuring that all residents of our great city have access to fair representation, essential services, and economic development. An accurate count of persons living in San Diego, CA in the 2020 Census is vital to determine the representation of individuals, families, and communities in our local, state, and federal government. The 2020 Census count of children living in San Diego, CA will be the basis of federal education funding allocated to our schools over the next ten years, specifically the federal programs serving our students living in poverty and students with disabilities. The population data derived in the 2020 Census will be used by both government and businesses to determine which neighborhoods and communities in San Diego, CA should receive services and investments like libraries, health clinics, and grocery and retail stores. The decennial Census has historically undercounted populations in large, urban areas such as ours. The populations that are prevalent in urban areas like San Diego, CA are at high risk of being undercounted, including young children, people of color, low-income households, English learner families, foreign-born residents, and households with limited Internet access, according to the US Census Bureau. Children are more likely to be missed in the Census count if they reside in complex households that are also common in urban areas, such as multi-generational households, extended families, and multi-family households. During the 2020 Census, there is a heightened risk of an undercount due to the digital divide, language access issues, and local community mistrust in the federal government. Local and state governments, as well as community-based organizations, can play a critical role in counteracting the undercount risks, reaching hard-to-count populations, and ensuring their communities get counted in 2020. It is paramount for the school board, superintendent, district leadership, and parent organizations to communicate the importance of Census 2020 to families and community members to ensure everyone is counted. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports local efforts and plans of action to ensure a complete count of all students and their families residing within the school district on the 2020 Census. The San Diego Unified School District, through its Family Engagement Department, is committed to working in partnership with other local civic, business, and community leaders, the State of California, and others to engage, educate, and count every resident in San Diego, CA in the 2020 Census.

FISCAL IMPACT: All actions taken by the board will be to prepare a balanced budget for 2020-21. The impacts and effects of these position eliminations may be subject to negotiations with the district’s bargaining units. The district's Fiscal Year 2019-20 Unrestricted General Fund, Second Interim Report depicts a projected positive ending fund balance of $71.8M, with a net decrease in fund balance of $17.8M. This financial report was developed using various assumptions and projections as highlighted in the exhibits. The 2019-20 total General Fund Restricted and Unrestricted ending balance is $101.3M.

With the adoption of $84.2M of solutions for 2020-21, Fiscal Year 2020-21 Unrestricted General Fund indicates a positive ending fund balance of $33.5M. This report also accounts for a projected shortfall in 2021-22 of $106.8M.

 

BACKGROUND: The purpose of this item is to ensure the district maintains a balanced budget in the coming school year. All items referenced pertain to school year 2020-21 school year. For the current school year, San Diego Unified is operating within a balanced budget.

 

Since 2017-2018, the District has utilized a targeted approach to future budget reductions, as opposed to across the board cuts through tools like a reduced work year. For that reason, the reductions outlined in this item are focused and limited.

 

Reflecting the commitment to put students’ needs first, the staffing changes outlined in this document are based on student-based budgeting recently completed by all schools within the district. This means student demand is the primary element schools consider in adding and eliminating positions. Other key factors include the district’s commitment to student equity.

 

For permanent certificated employees, the Education Code requires that the board identify the particular kinds of services or programs that may be reduced or eliminated in the upcoming fiscal year. For probationary certificated employees, Article 19 of the collective bargaining agreement between the district and SDEA allows the board to determine that reductions are necessary due to lack of funds. The list of services to be reduced are stated as a full-time equivalent (FTE) rather than a number of employees. It is determined based on factors related to potentially disputed issues regarding seniority (and deviation therefrom), certificates, and qualifications for certain positions to displace less senior employees. Temporary certificated employees will receive a separate reminder notice that their services will end at the close of the 2019-20 school year.

 

The district has worked hard in recent years to collaborate with our bargaining units to provide a compassionate response to all employees who have their positions impacted by the arbitrary nature of education funding in our state. Employees impacted by this item have already been asked to check their seniority to assist with reassignment and have an opportunity to participate in the transfer process in the spring. Each will also be offered personalized supports to assist with future career options.

On June 25, 2019, the Board of Education adopted the Fiscal Year 2019-20 budget, under the single budget adoption cycle. On December 10, 2019, the board approved the 2019-20 First Interim that became the new district budget.

 

California Education Code Section 42130 requires that the Board of Education approve the Second Interim Financial Report of the district's budgetary status as of January 31, on or before March 15. The board is also required to certify to the state that the district's projected financial condition at this point in time is one of the following:

 

a) Positive Certification

b) Qualified Certification

c) Negative Certification

BACKGROUND: Advancement Via Individual Determination (AVID) provides an annual college readiness system service membership and license fees for district schools. Sites receive access to AVID curriculum guidebooks and publications.

FISCAL IMPACT: Revenue will be determined at a later date. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Pursuant to Assembly Bill x1 26, enacted June 28, 2011, the Redevelopment Agency of the City of San Diego ("RDA") dissolved and the City of San Diego, in its capacity of Successor Agency, became responsible for fulfilling the enforceable obligations of the former RDA and disposing of its assets, including its real property.  As part of the wind down of the former RDA, the Successor Agency submitted its proposed Long-Range Property Management Plan, which was approved as amended by Department of Finance (DOF) on October 15, 2015.  The approved plan designated the North Park Gateway ("Woolworth Building") and the property commonly referred to as "Tailgate Park" as properties to be transferred to the City for future development.

 

Under Code section 34180(f), in exchange for the City retaining the Property for future development, the local taxing entities must enter into a compensation agreement under which the City compensates the other local taxing entities in accordance with their proportional shares of base property tax revenues, as determined pursuant to Code section 34188, for the value of the Property.  Under Code section 34180(f), if no agreement is reached on valuation of the Property, the value will be the fair market value as of the 2011 property tax lien date as determined by an independent appraiser approved by the Oversight Board.  These agreements fulfill that requirement. 

 

The agreement for the North Park property provides for distribution based on the 2011 appraised valuation of $1.4 million.  The district's share is 43.44 percent after deducting costs incurred in connection with the transaction.

 

The Tailgate Park agreement is more complex.  This property is worth substantially more that the 2011 appraised valuation of $8.4 million if acquired by a developer and developed in accordance with historical redevelopment objectives.  Therefore, in order to maximize the net proceeds of the disposition of this property, this agreement provides the City of San Diego will identify a developer to purchase the property through an RFP process, who will then be required to develop the property subject to specific agreed-upon development requirements.  The taxing entities will be paid their proportionate share when the transaction is complete.  As with the Woolworth Building, the district's share will be 43.44 percent.

BACKGROUND: The proposed project generally consists of the installation of a new relocatable building and new shade shelter for Elevate Charter School at Vista Grade Elementary School campus at 5606 Antigua Blvd., San Diego, CA 92124.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code Section 20111(b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000.00 or more. The notice calling for bids was published once a week for two successive weeks in accordance with Public Contract Code section 20112. Bids were due and opened on February 6, 2020. The district received a total of four bids from Conan Construction, Inc.; De La Fuente Construction, Inc.; GEM Industrial Electric, Inc.; and Handy Industrial. After evaluating the bids, staff determined GEM Industrial Electric, Inc., to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.

 

Additionally, consistent with the May 10, 2011 board approved resolution in support of service-disabled veteran-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE), which established a three percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities, the recommended contractor, GEM Industrial Electric, Inc. submitted a three percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Proposed Project is subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15303 (Class 3) – New Construction/Conversion of Existing Structures and 15314 (Class 14) – Minor Additions to Schools.

 

A Class 3 exemption consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures; installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures; and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure. The numbers of structures described in this section are the maximum allowable on any legal parcel. The Proposed Project would include the installation of a new shade structure for the existing school, therefore the application of a Class 3 Categorical Exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

A Class 14 exemption consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314). The Proposed Project consists of the installation of one relocatable (portable) classroom building that would not result in the increase in existing school capacity by more than 25 percent. The existing and projected number of students and classrooms is more fully discussed in Attachment A. Therefore, the application of a Class 14 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school campus. The Proposed Project is consistent with the existing use on the project site. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction timeframe of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes a minor additions to an existing school. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. There are no public vantage points from which the proposed improvements would be visible. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not substantially damage scenic resources within a state scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Furthermore, according to the National Register of Historic Places (NRHP) guidelines, properties completed at least 50 years ago must be considered for eligibility for designation in the NRHP. Vista Grande Elementary was constructed in 1977 and does not meet the 50-year eligibility threshold. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

Approval of this item approves the Proposed Project as described, and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and approves the agreement with GEM Industrial Electric, Inc., to furnish and install a relocatable building and new shade shelter for Elevate Charter School at the Vista Grande Elementary School campus.
