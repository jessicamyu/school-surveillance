RECOMMENDATIONS: Approve the following firms to provide Construction Management Multi-Prime (CMMP) professional services: 1) Barnhart, Inc.; (2) Gafcon, Inc.; (3) O’Connor Construction Management, Inc.; (4) Swinerton Management & Consulting, Inc.; and (5) Vanir Construction Management, Inc.

Aprove authorization to negotiate agreements with the above firms for submission to the board for approval at a later date.

FISCAL IMPACT: There is no financial impact at this time.

BACKGROUND: Passage of Proposition S (Prop S) requires construction of $150 million per year in work over a multi-year period. In order to comport with the timely and economical completion of the bond projects, the district proposes to initiate several construction projects based on a construction management multi-prime delivery (CMMP) method.

Government Code section 4525 et seq. allows a school district to hire professional services such as construction management based on “demonstrated competence and the professional qualifications necessary for the satisfactory performance of the services required.” Toward that end, on June 11, 2009, the district advertised Request for Qualifications (RFQ) No. PS-90-132-02 for competitive proposals with respect to construction management services for five CMMP projects. The RFQ was advertised in the San Diego Daily Transcript, with DemandStar, a national website, and through the district’s Facilities Outreach database, Twenty-five proposals were received by the June 30, 2009 district deadline. A five-member evaluation panel consisting of two Senior Facilities Project Managers, the district’s Controller, a former member of the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee (ICOC), and a senior construction consultant reviewed the proposals based on best value. From this initial paper screening, ten firms were “short-listed” for interviews.

Interviews with the ten firms were held on August 13, 2009. Each firm was allotted one-half hour to answer six questions by a five-member panel. Three questions examined the firm’s delivery process using a CMMP construction model, and three questions specifically addressed outreach and local participation. The interview panel included two representative from the San Diego County Office of Education, a former district principal, the Executive Director of Auxiliary Services, and a former member of the ICOC.

The five firms receiving the highest ranking by the interview panel were: (1) Barnhart, Inc.; (2) Gafcon, Inc.; (3) O’Connor Construction Management, Inc.; (4) Swinerton Management & Consulting, Inc.; and (5) Vanir Construction Management, Inc. All firms have local San Diego offices.
CM Multi-Prime and the District

The five proposed CMMP projects are: (1) Clairemont High School – New Auto Program Building; (2) Madison High School – New Multi-Media Building; (3) Hoover High School - Green Construction Program; (4) Morse High School – New Child Development Center Building; and (5) Madison High School – Stadium Upgrades (Multiple Projects). The professional services provided by these five firms include development of construction bid packages using the district’s design documents, assisting district staff to advertise and award the trade contracts, and performing post-award construction management and contract administration.

Under the CMMP form of construction contracting the owner (the district), in essence, performs the General Contractor role, coordinating multiple contracts with trade-specific firms for overall project execution. The project documents are divided into smaller trade-specific packages and advertised under firm, fixed price sealed bid procedures. Pursuant to California Public Contract Code section 20111, public works construction contracts that are advertised under firm fixed price sealed bidding procedures are awarded to the lowest responsive and qualified bidder.

Under the CMMP method, the construction manager administers, as the district’s agent, the multiple trade contracts that the district has with the individual trade contractors, also known as “prime” contractors.

One of the key factors reviewed to determine if a prime contractor is qualified to perform public works construction is the payment procedure and performance bonding. Emerging Business Enterprises (EBEs) are often limited in their bonding capacity due to market requirements from construction sureties. Utilizing the CMMP model, larger public works projects are divided into smaller value contracts thereby reducing bonding requirements. This model allows a larger number of trade-specific companies to achieve bonding requirements and, therefore, to be enabled to compete in a sealed bid, firm fixed price bidding procedures. Although it is not a guarantee to that this model will increase EBE participation, it is the expectation that this model will allow for greater opportunities for EBEs to compete for and participate in the district’s construction program.

Proposed Outreach Strategy

As noted above, CMMP allows an excellent opportunity for outreach opportunities to EBEs throughout the bidding process for local and small business participation. CMMP is an important tool that could greatly improve the district’s ability to meet its outreach objectives. There are two important features of the approach that are of particular significance: 1) the ability to conduct meaningful outreach to qualified subcontractors not typically considered by general contractors, and 2) the opportunity for qualified trade contractors to bid for projects directly as a prime contractor for which they would not otherwise have adequate financial and bonding capacity. The district’s recently constituted Business Outreach Program staff has been conducting informal meetings with contractors, contractor advocacy organizations and labor unions, seeking feedback on the concept of CMMP. The response to this concept has been overwhelmingly favorable.

By reducing the size and complexity of bids to more manageable sizes for EBEs, it is anticipated the smaller EBE staffs will be able to submit competitive bids. For each proposed project in lieu of advertising one General Construction contract, it is anticipated that each project will include the advertisement of anywhere from 5 to 12 or more smaller trade-specific jobs (e.g. site work, mechanical/electrical, foundation, steel framing and masonry, landscaping contracts). This process provides multiple opportunities for EBE firms to prepare bids and to compete for projects.

The FPC Business Outreach Program enthusiastically endorses the CMMP approach. District staff believes that CMMP approach will enhance EBE participation within the overall Prop S program.

    RECOMMENDATION: Adopt the revisions to Administrative Procedure (AP) 3113, School Utilization/Reconfiguration/Closure Planning: Staff and Community Process.

    FISCAL IMPACT: None.

    BACKGROUND: During the 2008-09 school year, the district was confronted by an unprecedented budget deficit. Various efforts to overcome the financial shortfall included consideration for closure and/or reconfiguration of small elementary schools. This endeavor prompted board discussion about the associated process. Staff has researched the closure/reconfiguration process and herein submits a proposed revision, the result of extensive research of other agencies' practices and consultation with a wide range of district and community stakeholders.

    Summary of Modifications to the Existing Procedure

    • The procedure was expanded to consider not simply closure of a school site, but also reconfiguration to another use.
    • The procedure was reviewed for compliance with existing district policies and board governance policies; some district policies referred to in the current procedure were removed because they did not directly relate to the subject matter of this procedure.
    • The creation of a “trigger event” (a report on enrollment and capacity utilization at the end of the school year) to enable the board to decide whether to initiate the procedure, including the creation of the Community Advisory Committee (CAC) as a board- and superintendent-appointed committee comprised of individuals from professional and educational communities that could bring expertise to the group’s deliberations.
    • A timeline lasting approximately nine months, from the decision of the board to begin a study through to the board’s decision on the recommendations of the CAC.
    • Improved community engagement through CAC meetings, a first and second reading of the board report on changes to school utilization, with community meetings held between the two readings to gather more input.
    • The inclusion of the “best practices” framework from the California Department of Education.
