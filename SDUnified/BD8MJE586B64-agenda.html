BACKGROUND: Our work is about widening the sphere of success by engineering equitable learning conditions for all students. We believe classrooms can be places of hope where teachers and students imagine the kind of society we could live in and where educators design meaningful and inclusive educational experiences in order for students to be agents of change and make this hope a reality. The San Diego Unified School District is a leader in preschool and early learning. Annually, the district invests its resources in programs that provide high-quality instruction to our youngest learners.

 

The State of California has also recognized the importance of early learning and authorized transitional kindergarten beginning in 2010. In July 2019, under the California Department of Education, Program Requirements for California State Preschool Programs (CSPP), Education Code 48000(h) authorized school districts to co-mingle and expand their TK programs to include students 4 years in age. 

 

Beginning in the 2020-21 school year, the district will build continuity across early learning by developing a rich cross-system of relationships to leverage resources, maximize expertise, and ensure continuous improvement.  To meet this objective, the district will expand the enrollment of four-year-old students in our TK classrooms, will offer three-year-old students preschool programs and will offer parents of three- and four-year-old students with disabilities options for their classroom setting based on the individual student’s need.

 

We have engaged teachers and parents in the process to develop this plan. The information presented in this item has been significantly revised since it was first shared in February 2020 based on the feedback from stakeholders. The Early Learning Plan includes a process to continue engagement for planning the 2021-22 school year which will be brought to the Board of Education before it is implemented.

2. REVISED 7/8/2020: Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing June 19, 2020, as Juneteenth in San Diego Unified (Evans/Whitehurst-Payne)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing June 19, 2020, as Juneteenth in San Diego Unified, a holiday to be observed every year, as a day of celebration of the past, present, and future of Black resilience, culture, and liberation. We call on educators to teach the importance of Juneteenth and current movements such as “Black Lives Matter” that advance liberation efforts for the Black community with classroom lessons throughout the year; just as we fight for equity, justice, and basic human rights through each interaction with youth, the principles of the Black Lives Matter movement and the promise of liberation that Juneteenth celebrates should be explicitly talked and discussed year round. We encourage staff, students, and community members to use this day to reflect on the historical oppression of the Black community and engage in individual and collective action to break the cycle of systemic racism that exists today through: commitment to education, self-improvement, advocacy, and activism. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Juneteenth is the oldest celebration of the emancipation of African-American slaves and celebrates our collective commitment to liberty and equality in accordance with the Constitution of the United States, along with the Emancipation Proclamation, issued January 1, 1863, and the 13th Amendment to the Constitution which abolished slavery, ratified by Congress on December 6, 1865. President Abraham Lincoln first issued the Emancipation Proclamation effective January 1, 1863, freeing the slaves in the South. However, southern slave owners ignored that order. On June 19th, 1865, Union soldiers arrived in Galveston, Texas and enforced the president’s order, freeing the final African-American slaves two and a half years after it was first decreed. Through other systems of oppression, such as sharecropping, Jim Crow, redlining, Black Codes, mass incarceration, exclusionary policies, police violence, and segregation, the exploitation of the Black community continued past slavery and persists to this day, affecting the physical and mental health, safety, and education of the Black community. Liberation from these systems of oppression must include ongoing assessment and commitment to personal and professional growth within the educational, economic, social, and political arenas. This liberation is manifested in ways such as educational and financial literacy; equitable access to quality homes, jobs, schools, and service providers; and indistinguishable outcome predictability based on racial identity. The accurate representation of the contributions and experiences of the African-American community were, for generations, and continue to be, systematically excluded from and misrepresented in history textbooks, mainstream media, and classroom discussions, teaching San Diego Unified students about Black history and contemporary events is a critical affirmation of the fact Black history is American history. The San Diego Unified School District opposes and rejects any form of oppression and pledges to support the Black community and their intersecting identities by working to achieve genuine equity, access, and success for our students, employees, and communities which we serve. Resources to support education and advocacy around Juneteenth can be found at http://culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com/june.html. Contact the Department of Youth Advocacy at 619-732-3366 or on their website: youthadvocacy.us for more information.

Jun 23, 2020 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Supporting a Call for Freedom Summer 2020 and focus on eliminating those barriers that prevent our students of color from receiving an equitable education. Under the direction of the Superintendent, led by the Division specifically charged with advocating for our students of color, the Department of Youth Advocacy will collaborate with students to plan for a Board workshop during the month of July on the issue of racial justice and inequity. The focus of the workshop will be on reviewing and strengthening the district's key strategies in achieving racial justice and equity, with an emphasis on student voice. The Freedom Summer workshop should include a review of relevant data reflecting our progress at eliminating education barriers based on race with an emphasis on student voices in the workshop. The topics to be reviewed should include:

 

  • Interrupting discriminatory practices related to student discipline 
  • Interrupting racism on our campuses, building safe, respectful and nurturing campuses
  • Interrupting discriminatory practices related to grading, while moving to mastery based grading
  • Staff Diversity
  • Progress towards becoming a Restorative District
  • Progress toward becoming a Trauma and Resilience Informed District
  • Developing Community Schools
  • Implementation of Ethnic Studies for all

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.


BACKGROUND: The murder of George Floyd while in police custody on Memorial Day, 2020, has laid bare the persistence of racism within American society and 400 years of racial inequality have also been exposed by the different ways the Covid-19 pandemic has impacted communities of color, resulting in higher rates of infection and higher morbidity rates. As San Diego Unified prepares to physically reopen schools on August 31, 2020, our goal should not simply be to recreate the system in place before March 13, 2020, rather we should seek to reopen as a better system, one focused on rooting out systemic racism in our society. This commitment is consistent with our past action by the board and the school leadership that has worked to address racial inequality. The most prominent results of these efforts have been improved preparation and graduation rates for African American students, higher academic achievement rates for students of color and a commitment to teach the history of all peoples through ethnic studies. Following the death of George Floyd, San Diego Unified has continued to work on equity issues, celebrating the Juneteenth holiday for the first time and banning carotid artery holds by School Police. The many hundreds of our students who have taken part in Black Lives Matter protests -- students of all races and ethnicities -- call on us to do more to eliminate the underlying foundations of racism in our society.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Supporting Assembly Constitutional Amendment 5. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.


BACKGROUND: The California Constitution was amended by Proposition 209 in 1996, adding Section 31 of Article 1, to prohibit the use of race, sex, color, ethnicity, or national origin as a factor considered in public employment, education and contracting. Prior to the passage of Prop. 209, the State of California was succeeding in closing race and gender disparities through affirmative action and equal opportunity programs. The passage of Prop. 209 has eliminated targeted programs and tools that could help close the achievement gap. Prop. 209 has led to a 12-60 percent reduction in admission and enrollment at the University of California by students of color. The effects of Proposition 209 have limited the ability of women, families of color, and low-wage workers to fully participate in California’s growing economy. Contract awards to women-owned business enterprises dropped to 3.8 percent in the years following Prop. 209’s passage. California’s minority and women business enterprises have lost the equivalent of $1 billion in potential public contracting opportunities. The Opportunity for All Coalition is working to repeal Prop. 209. Assemblymembers Weber, Gipson, Santiago, and Gonzalez have introduced Assembly Constitutional Amendment 5 (ACA 5), a resolution to propose to the people of the State of California an amendment to the Constitution of the State. Assembly Constitutional Amendment 5 would change by a vote of the people the Constitution of the State of California by repealing Section 31 of Article 1. Passage of ACA 5 and ratification by a vote of the people in the State of California would re-arm policymakers with the tools they need to address inequities in public employment, education and contracting. Repealing Prop. 209 would break the barriers that prohibit San Diego Unified’s procurement department from utilizing mechanisms such as set asides and sheltered markets to contract with women and minority owned firms. The State of California is only one of eight states with an anti-equal opportunity ban and ACA 5 would enable voters of California to democratically voice their views about solutions to gender and racial inequities.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: It is the mission of the San Diego Unified School District that all San Diego students will graduate with the skills, motivation, curiosity and resilience to succeed in their choice of college and career, in order to lead and participate in the society of tomorrow. Schools are a critical place for students to develop the civic knowledge, skills and values needed to effectively contribute to and participate in our democracy. Civic engagement is a critical component of a functional democracy, and San Diego Unified strongly supports the constitutionally ordained rights of all to freedom of speech and peaceful assembly. The San Diego Unified Board of Education approved a Resolution in Support of Civic Learning for All Students in April 2015, and a Resolution in the Matter of Designating October 2016 as Civil Discourse and Civic Engagement Month on October 4, 2016, supporting meaningful and polite civil dialogue in the classroom and on school campuses which analyzes all points of view with a critical lens. San Diego Unified students, and students of color in particular, are impacted by issues of race, law enforcement, and gun violence. It is recognized San Diego Unified students are currently engaging and organizing peacefully around the issues of police violence, racism and the Black Lives Matter movement, and reports of local and national use of tear gas and projectile munitions, such as rubber bullets, against peaceful demonstrators, during protests of the death of George Floyd, at the hands of police are deeply alarming. Tear gas and projectile munitions can cause death and serious injury and have seriously injured protesters during protests of the death of George Floyd at the hands of police. Tear gas is a chemical irritant which causes coughing, sneezing and respiratory irritation and can disperse into and indiscriminately impact widespread areas. Over 1,000 health professionals in the United States have signed an open letter calling for a reduction in harm to those protesting racism and injustice, stating respiratory irritants make the respiratory tract vulnerable to infection by increasing inflammation and causing coughing. The world is experiencing an international pandemic caused by the novel coronavirus (COVID-19) which has disproportionately impacted communities of color, and the novel coronavirus is spread through droplets expelled when those infected cough. Use of tear gas induces coughing and sneezing in densely packed demonstrations and can exacerbate the spread of novel coronavirus pandemic, and the aforementioned health professionals have opposed the use of any chemical irritants that could increase risk of COVID19 spread.

6. Resolution in Support of the Student Bill of Rights (Patterson/Barrera)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of the Student Bill of Rights for all San Diego Unified School District students.

  1. All students in San Diego Unified School District have the right to receive an education, including an elementary, middle, and high school education with a diploma.
  2. All students have the right to receive academic, collegiate, and career guidance for an appropriate choice of post-secondary opportunities.
  3. All students have the right to receive teaching from qualified and skilled teachers preparing them for post-secondary education and/or careers.
  4. All students have the right to know the academic expectations of each of their classes, including grading systems.
  5. All students have the right to access resources related to their education.
  6. All students who cannot provide for any of these resources and qualify for financial assistance should be able to receive them.
  7. All students have the right to effectively learn from their mistakes by going through a safe educational process focused on self-improvement.
  8. All students have the right to attend a school free of verbal, physical, mental, emotional, and sexual abuse.
  9. All students have the right to safety, security, personal privacy, and protection as provided by the school.
  10. All students have the right to feel safe and comfortable in their school.
  11. All students in school have the right to be treated equally, with respect and dignity.
  12. All students in San Diego Unified schools have the right to equitable access to every program, resource and club districtwide, so that they may pursue any goals they want.
  13. All students have the right to be respected by every other student and school staff.
  14. All students have the right to learn in an environment that does not discriminate against them and reflects the variety of backgrounds in the student body.
  15. All students have the right to express themselves and their beliefs through oral, written, artistic, and fashion.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In San Diego, serious inequities within our communities continue to be an issue and are further exacerbated during the 2020 COVID-19 Global Pandemic. In the San Diego Unified School District, the mission of creating an equitable education system is prevalent in all district decisions, and strong education is a basic integral right for all students. In an effort to cultivate student agency and recognition of fundamental students' rights, it is integral that school boards across California adopt a localized student bill of rights that ensures students are equal partners in the education they receive. It is integral for schools to protect the rights of all students and ensure that students feel equipped and prepared to continue their education even in challenging and adaptive circumstances. It is crucial for school districts to ensure direct and uncompromised access to educators and resources during such difficult and challenging times. Students are vital and important stakeholders to any education system, and strong student engagement and participation is crucial to the success of San Diego Unified School District. Strong student voice in local education policy is not common, and allowing students a platform to speak out against the injustices in their community ensures accurate representation of all students. The adoption of a student bill of rights for all San Diego Unified School District Students is necessary for the success of San Diego Unified. The Student Advisory Board of San Diego Unified will yearly review this student bill of rights in order to ensure accurate student representation. If a student identifies their school as not upholding their rights specified in this Resolution, they have the right to speak out in an effort to change the system around them.

BACKGROUND: SELF is a Joint Powers Authority created under Title 1, Division 7, Chapter 5, Article 1, of the CA Government Code. SELF is a member-owned, statewide partnership of public educational agencies providing quality pooled programs for excess coverage. The district has a self-insured retention of $350,000 and purchases excess insurance for costs and liabilities of $350,000 to $5 million from CSAC.  The District currently purchases excess liability insurance coverage for costs and liabilities in excess of $5 million from SELF.  

 

The significant increase in premium and an additional assessment of $4.61 per student based on ADA for the years the district was a member of SELF reflects the insurance market's response to high dollar verdicts and settlements in across the state CA schools, including traumatic brain injuries, molestation and abuse cases.  Additionally, AB 218 eliminated the previous statute of limitations for recovery of damages in abuse cases, including repressed memory sexual abuse cases. This unpredictability has led to insurers demanding higher premiums, and/or higher retentions and in some cases, abandonment of the CA market. The SELF board accordingly raised its coverage rates to ensure sufficient funding to anticipate future losses.
