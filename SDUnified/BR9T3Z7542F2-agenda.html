RECOMMENDATION: As a service-providing organization, the San Diego Unified School District's employees are its most valuable resource. Today's recognition includes recognition of employees with 40, 30, 20, 15, 10 and 5 years of service. When the district returns to in-person work, each employee will receive a certificate and pin. The Superintendent and the Board of Education extend their most sincere gratitude to these individuals. 

 

Today, we celebrate milestones of 125 employees.

 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: As San Diego Unified celebrates progress toward meeting the inspirational goals contained in Vision 2020, we are taking time to celebrate those employees who have helped us achieve this progress. The number one predictor of student success is the presence of an experienced, high quality teacher in the classroom, according to the Learning Policy Institute. (Source: “Report: Teacher qualifications best predictor of student success,” By Amelia Harper, May 16, 2019, Education Dive). That study, which was written last year and co-authored by the Chair of the State Board of Education, found high quality teachers have helped San Diego schools do better than the rest of the state. (Source: “California’s Positive Outliers: Districts Beating the Odds,” by Anne Podolsky, Linda Darling-Hammond, Christopher Doss, and Sean Reardon, May 16, 2019, Learning Policy Institute). Educators also provide crucial services in addition to teaching. They are critical to keeping students safe at school. School staff also provide healthy, nutritious meals to children who do not have access to food at home. Nearly 100,000 schools/institutions serve school lunches to 29.8 million students each day, including 20.2 million free lunches. (Source: School Nutrition Association.) Given the clear association between the health and success of our students and the well-being and effectiveness of our workforce, we publicly recognize employees for their continuous years of service to the students, families and communities of San Diego Unified.

Both longevity and seniority dates are dictated by the Education Code as well as the applicable collective bargaining agreement. The date used to determine this service award is based on the individual's permanent status as an employee, temporary or hourly work is not used in the determination of the service award recognition. Service recognition awards will be awarded on a monthly basis. If you believe an error has been made, you may contact the Human Resource Service Division by emailing jobs@sandi.net.

4. Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing February 2021, as National Black/African American History Month
Meeting
Jan 26, 2021 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of recognizing February 2021, as national Black/African American History Month and recognize the tremendous contributions made by African Americans to the development of our culture, communities, and nation; and our ongoing commitment to fostering an anti-racist, anti-biased and restorative district for all students. Districtwide activities during the month of February, include library card design in collaboration with the San Diego Public Library, community leader read alouds, artistic and cultural events, virtual field trips, and the Black Lives Matter Call to action week. Each year, in honor of Black History Month, San Diego Unified School District’s Central Office will fly the Black Lives Matter flag. The flag signifies our commitment to creating a just, equitable, and empathetic world. It is a symbol of inclusion, especially for our students of color. The flag will be a reminder that history, including Black history, is not just something of the past. History is constantly being made, and this flag and this movement are a part of it. Together, we demonstrate our commitment to a quality educational experience that is equally accessible to all students, free from racist and bias practices. Not only during the month of February, but throughout the year, it is important that we, as a district, seek to understand how to thoughtfully and holistically address African-American disparities in experience, quality of educational and social interactions, and outcomes as well as each of our roles in dismantling racist, biased, and oppressive systems and practices in order to move towards greater equity in our educational system and the greater community. Black lives matter.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Americans of African descent helped develop our nation in countless ways, some recognized, some unrecognized and unrecorded. The origin of Black History Month began in 1915, half a century after the Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery in the United States, and grew out of “Negro History Week,” the creation of noted historian Carter G. Woodson and other prominent African-Americans who established as a month-long celebration to reflect on the history, teachings, contributions, and achievements of African-Americans. In 1976, Black History month was officially established as a month-long celebration to reflect on the history, teachings, contributions and achievements of African-Americans and is a national tradition established in response to the inadequate and often times biased depiction of Black history and African-American communities in history books and schools’ curricula. Since the beginning of American history, people of African descent have fought and died for the advancement of the essence and substance of American democracy; an ideal from which they have frequently been excluded. Black history reflects an unbreakable spirit of perseverance and pride in its struggles and triumphs in the face of racism, bias, prejudice, and oppression to equally share in the opportunities of a nation founded upon the principles of freedom and liberty for all people. The history and contributions of African-American citizens have consistently been overlooked, misinterpreted, and undervalued in the curriculum of public education institutions prior to the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and still today. We recognize that “matter” is the minimum for Black students—that state education data reflects the egregious disparities in experience, treatment, opportunity, access, visibility, and academic success. We are committed to addressing these disparities through our Black Youth Call to Action, focusing on staff diversity, ethnic studies, grading practices, and student discipline and will be held accountable to progress by the Independent Citizens Oversight Committee on Racial Justice and Equity. Black History Month will offer excellent opportunities to focus the energy, attention, commitment, and action of the entire community on closing the health, experience, opportunity, and achievement gaps for Black students and families with a critical examination by our staff of our progress toward and execution of San Diego Unified School District’s Black Youth Call to Action and is a vehicle to promote anti-biased, anti-racist, restorative, and culturally sustaining practices on every school campus and district office.  Black history curriculum and materials are available to all San Diego Unified educators through our Ethnic Studies curriculum, Identity-Based Curriculum Modules, and our Digital Cultural Awareness Center. We are committed to Ethnic Studies for ALL students and the ongoing professional learning opportunities for all District staff to engage in critical self-awareness and anti-biased/anti-racist leadership; and we are encouraging educators, students and families to learn about the heritage and achievements of African-Americans.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing Student Health and Well-Being 

that urges the legislature to stand in support of student mental health and vote in favor of Senate Bill 14; supports that an excused absence of “personal illness” includes mental health reasons and indicates as such in Administrative Regulation (AR) 5113 regarding student absences; and continues to work in partnership with existing districtwide student groups via school counselors, mental health providers, school psychologists, nurses and other student services personnel to ensure an effective implementation of mental health outreach programs, including ongoing education on youth risk factors, offer mental health resources onsite and in the community, as well as wellness resources to enhance student and family well-being. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In the San Diego Unified School District, the mental health and well-being of students remains and will always be a top priority of this district. According to the CDC, suicide remains the second leading cause of death for students aged 15-19 in the United States. San Diego Unified currently hosts suicide prevention and emergency procedural trainings for all counselors in order to ensure a compliant, competent, prepared, and supportive administration at every school site and is committed to continuing district efforts to prevent, intervene early, and address youth suicide as well as create strategic planning to enhance student wellness. In the San Diego Unified School District, wellness and mental health district initiatives are student-centered and created to reduce mental health stigma and improve mental health awareness of students and school communities, and awareness around mental health. Senate Bill 14 sponsored by California State Senator Anthony Portantino would require that at least 50 percent of school employees be trained in Youth Mental Health First Aid as well as formally change the state absence code to allow for mental health as an excused absence. The San Diego Unified School District encourages student leadership and student agency in promoting and leading wellness and mental health efforts for all students, and therefore, the Student Wellness Education and Resources (SWEAR) Committee supports student wellness efforts by having student representatives from every high school in our system. San Diego Unified has created a Wellness Policy which includes efforts of the Wellness Council composed of an integrated team that includes students, staff, and community stakeholders committed to working together to support and enhance the health and well-being of all district staff, students and families. In the era of COVID-19 the health and well-being of students is already burdened by new and challenging learning and home environments. According to Mental Health America, the number of youth experiencing Severe Major Depressive Episodes has increased by 126,000 compared to the previous year. Students are vital and important stakeholders to any education system, and strong mental health support requires centering the voices of students, recognizes the value of mental health education and is committed to recognizing that students’ mental health is equally important to their physical health.

6. Resolution in the Matter of Support for Ending Fracking and Oil Drilling in California (Patterson/Barrera)
Meeting
Jan 26, 2021 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Action (Consent)
Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Support for Ending Fracking and Oil Drilling in California that the San Diego Unified School District urges Governor Newsom to cease issuing new oil permits and to develop a more comprehensive plan for phasing out fracking in California; calls on Governor Newsom to keep his previous promises about transitioning California away from fossil fuels and existing oil production; continue to recognize climate change and climate justice as issues of importance to the San Diego community; and continue to transition to renewable energy sources with the urgency called for by the climate crisis.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The rates of fracking are increasing in the United States, and according to the United States Energy Information Administration, are projected to increase to 44 percent by 2040.California is the nation’s seventh largest oil-producing state, extracting over 200 million barrels of oil per year, mainly through fracking, and estimated to contain half of the nation’s heavy oil. Iin the first six months of 2020, Governor Gavin Newsom’s administration issued 2,691 new permits for fracking or reworking old permits. Governor Newsom has a plan to end the issuance of new hydraulic fracking permits by 2024; however, in order to avert the effects of climate change, action needs to be taken immediately. Communities across California are usually excluded from knowing what chemicals are being pumped by fracking companies in their neighborhoods, and there is no federal requirement for drillers to report what chemicals they use. Normally state provisions are inadequately enforced for fracking which endangers individuals living near those areas. In studies where scientists research the effects of fracking on health, over 80 percent report serious risks. Fracking, both gas and oil, leaks methane which is up to 105 times as powerful as carbon dioxide as a greenhouse pollutant. Scientists from around the world have concluded that the probability that anthropogenic greenhouse gases have caused the global temperature to increase in the last 50 years is over 95 percent. Scientists have concluded that methane released from fracking wells is about 86 times more powerful at trapping heat in our atmosphere than carbon dioxide. If the number of fracking sites increases, the number of air pollutants and amount of methane in our environment will increase to unsustainable levels that threaten the health and future of San Diego Unified students and by the mid-21st century, researchers project that the number of days with a heat index over 100℉ will double and days with an index of 105 degrees Farenheit will triple nationwide due to climate change. The American Academy of Pediatrics states that children are more vulnerable to heatwaves and the CDC has found that the leading cause of disability and mortality among high school and college athletes is heat illness, and the individuals at the greatest risk are from ages 15-19. Climate change predominantly fueled by burning fossil fuels has resulted in unprecedented natural disasters, devastating storms, historic droughts, wildfires, and extreme heat which may affect San Diego Unified students’ physical and mental health. In August and September 2020, California recorded five of the six biggest wildfires in the state in 90 years. Fracking releases high, dangerous amounts of air pollution that have been connected to the cause of severe headaches, childhood leukemia, asthma symptoms, birth defects, and cardiac problems. Children are more vulnerable to the diseases caused by bad air quality because they spend more time outside, have immature/developing lungs, and breathe in more per body weight; and 92 percent of people living near fracking sites in California are Black, Indigenous, and people of color. San Diego Unified students who reside in low-income communities and communities of color, including Barrio Logan, City Heights, and Southeast San Diego, are disproportionately affected by climate change and environmental impacts, including significantly worse air pollution causing residents to develop asthma at more than twice the national average, heat island effect, and elevated infectious diseases. Fracking and climate change widen the disparities between children's physical and mental health as a result of racism and poverty and the California PTA passed a resolution that recognized the issues that climate change presents to children’s health and urges action around averting the effects of the climate crisis. In 2014, the San Diego Unified Board of Education acknowledged the threat climate change poses to youth’s health and adopted a resolution in support of the Parent Teachers Association’s initiative to tackle climate change and in 2015, the San Diego School Unified District created a plan to transition to 100 percent renewable energy by 2035 in order to mitigate the effects of climate change. The San Diego School Unified District currently has a climate action plan that reviews the ways the district can make a positive impact on climate change and how it can become an environmental leader in San Diego County and has shown support of student leadership in fighting for a fossil fuel free future.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the 2021-22 Civic Center Rate Schedule for Theaters (Attachment 1), reflecting the results of the Real Estate Department’s analysis based on the NBS study (Attachment 2), which will adjust rates for theaters to achieve full cost recovery.  Upon approval, the new theater rental rates will go into effect July 1, 2021 through June 30, 2022, with no rate changes applied to Free User groups.  

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Current theater rental rates recover approximately 28-35 percent of district costs, depending on user rate category.  Approval of this item will increase theater rental rates to recover 100 percent of district costs. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not Applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In 2018, the board adopted the results of a comprehensive Civic Center Rental Rate analysis conducted by NBS, completed on May 4, 2018.  This analysis documented the district’s operational and capital costs associated with the rental and use of school facilities by the public and translated those costs into hourly rental rates.  The calculation of the hourly rental rates conformed strictly to the requirements set forth in the Civic Center Act. 

 

Due to available data at the time of the 2018 analysis, rental rate calculations for all district theater facilities were grouped together within the Large Auditorium rate category.  Following the board’s adoption of the 2018 analysis, the district’s Real Estate department, with the Visual and Performing Arts department, requested an additional focused study by NBS of the rental rates for its higher-level theater facilities:

 

  • Creative Performing Media Arts Middle School (CPMA)
  • Patrick Henry High School (Henry)
  • Lincoln High School (Lincoln)
  • School of Creative and Performing Arts (SCPA)

 

These theater facilities have been identified as facilities with a higher degree of technology capital, and operational costs than the district’s other large auditorium facilities. These theater facilities are currently utilized by the district for its highest level of creative performing arts programs. Rental of these facilities by the public would be for similar theater production uses.

 

The outcomes of this study effectively establish a Theater Rental Rate as separate from the currently charged Large Auditorium rental rate for theater facilities.  The development of these updated rates for theaters comply with the regulation defined by the Civic Center Act. 

 

When approved, staff recommends new pricing for theaters go into effect July 1, 2021.  Any Civic Center rental permits issued from July 1, 2021 through June 30, 2022 will be at the new approved theater rates.  Approval of this item will allow the district to annually increase the 2021-22 Civic Center Rate Schedule for Theaters by CPI, until the next rental rate study.  A new rate study is recommended every five years to ensure fair and reasonable civic center rates that achieve full cost recovery.
