4. Informational Update on the State Board of Education’s Adoption of the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) Emergency Regulations and the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP) Template
Meeting
Jan 21, 2014 - Special Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION:  Government Relations Director, Martha Alvarez, will provide a status report on the State Board of Education’s (SBE) January 16, 2014 regular meeting where they adopted the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) emergency regulations and the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP) template.  This item is for information only. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  On July 1, 2013, Governor Jerry Brown signed AB 97 (Chapter 47, Statutes of 2013) to enact the historic education funding legislation called the LCFF.   There are several key issues that led to the creation of the LCFF.  Local school leaders, parents, teachers, advocates, students and other stakeholders considered the previous revenue limit and categorical funding model to be overly complex and inefficient.  One of the main goals of LCFF was to reduce state bureaucracy and increase flexibility and accountability at the local level so those closest to the students can make decisions and ensure that student needs are met.  The funding formula, which primarily consists of base, supplemental and concentration grant funding, allocates resources based on a Local Educational Agencies’ student demographics and replaces most state-funded categorical programs.  The Department of Finance estimates that the formula will be fully funded in eight years, but implementation of the LCFF begins in 2013-14. 

 

The LCFF statute, subsequently amended by SB 97 (Chapter 57, Statutes of 2013), authorized the SBE to adopt regulations that govern how the expenditure of funds should be managed to demonstrate compliance with the new law.  In addition, the LCFF statute authorized the SBE to adopt on or before March 31, 2014, the LCAP templates for use by school districts, charter schools and county offices of education to support local adoption and annual review of the three-year LCAP.

 

At the January 16, 2014 State Board of Education meeting, the SBE unanimously approved the proposed LCFF emergency spending regulations and the LCAP template.  Once approved by the Office of Administrative Law and filed with the Secretary of State, the emergency regulations will remain in effect for 180 days.  During the time the emergency regulations are in effect, the SBE is required to conduct the regular rulemaking process in order to adopt permanent regulations.  During this time, the SBE could make further modifications to address outstanding issues, as well as revise the LCAP template to make the layout for clear.

 

The LCFF spending regulations provide guidance on how LEAs calculate how they “increased or improved services for low-income students, English learners and foster youth (“unduplicated students generating the supplemental and concentration grants”) in proportion to the increase in funds apportioned for supplemental and concentration grants,” as required by the LCFF statute.  The spending regulations contain various definitions, including “increase” and “improve,” and describes a proportionality calculation for LEAs to use while developing their LCAP.  LEA’s must prepare a three-year LCAP prior to the submission of LEA budgets to oversight agencies on July 1, 2014, and the LCAP must be annually updated by July 1 of each year.  The LCAP must describe annual goals for each of eight identified state priorities, describe specific action necessary to achieve those goals, and list and describe annual expenditures implementing the specific actions for all students and identified subgroups.

 

After the January 16 SBE meeting, SBE staff will work on guidance materials for LEA’s to use.  It is anticipated that many of the specific examples received from LEAs will be incorporated into a document that features best practices and may also be used to inform the development of the evaluation rubric, which the SBE must adopt by October 2015, and will be used by County Offices of Education as guidance for what they must evaluate while reviewing and approving the annual LCAP.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2014–15 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The DAC had preliminary discussion of the Title I ranking student counts on November 20, 2013, and reviewed the proposed site allocations at its meetings on December 18, 2013, and January 15, 2014.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources. To that end, with the 2014-15 Title I district allocation anticipated to be the same as in 2013-14, the proposed plan allocates the same overall amount to schools in 2014-15, that they collectively received in 2013-14.

 

The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). The DAC, at its November 20, 2013 meeting, was provided a preliminary report showing student counts on which funding allocations would be based (actual funding amounts were not available). At the December 18, 2013 meeting, the DAC discussed a report that reflected the status quo—both in method and in total Title I funding allocated to sites. An action at the meeting established a subcommittee to review the district's Title I spending plan. The subcommittee met on January 8, 2014, to develop recommendations for the board and for the DAC general body. The DAC again reviewed the proposed 2014-15 Title I Ranking at its January 15, 2014 meeting.
