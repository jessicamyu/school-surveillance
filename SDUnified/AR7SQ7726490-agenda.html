The initial charter petition for HS Middle was approved on January 29, 2013, for a five-year term beginning July 1, 2013, and ending June 30, 2018. The public hearing to consider the level of support for HS Middle was held on March 6, 2018.

 

HS Middle is seeking a renewal of its charter for a five-year term beginning July 1, 2018, and ending June 30, 2023. When HS High renewed its charter in 2016, it included an expansion of grade levels to serve Grades 6-12, and anticipated that the HS Middle charter would expire on June 30, 2018, without being renewed. This decision was made after it was determined that HS High would receive Proposition Z funds in an amount sufficient to purchase a facility large enough to house all of Grades 6-12. More recently, a property was identified for purchase that was smaller than initially planned, and it does not have the capacity to hold Grades 6-12. HS Middle is requesting a renewal of its charter for a five-year term, as required by statute, but it plans to cease all middle school operations in June 2020, after the current sixth grade class completes middle school. Although the HS High charter is approved for Grades 6-12, based on the recent facility projections, the school plans to only serve Grades 9-12 in the future, and does not want to have middle school test scores mixed in with the high school scores for only a two-year period.

 

Both HS High and HS Middle are located at 3910 University Avenue, which is within the attendance boundaries of Central Elementary, Wilson Middle, and Hoover High School. Currently, HS Middle serves Grades 6-8, and HS High serves Grades 9-12.

As the charter granting authority, pursuant to Education Code section 47607(a)(3)(A), the district shall consider increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils served by the charter school as the most important factor in determining whether to grant a charter renewal.

 

Per Education Code section 47607(b), a charter school shall meet at least one of the following criteria before receiving a renewal:

  1. Attained its Academic Performance Index (API) growth target in the prior year or in two of the last three years both schoolwide and for all groups of pupils served by the charter school.

  2. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API in the prior year or in two of the last three years.

  3. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API for a demographically comparable school in the prior year or in two of the last three years.

  4. The entity that granted the charter determines that the academic performance of the charter school is at least equal to the academic performance of the public schools that the charter school pupils would otherwise have been required to attend, as well as the academic performance of the schools in the school district in which the charter school is located, taking into account the composition of the pupil population that is served at the charter school.

  5. Qualified for an alternative accountability system pursuant to Education Code section 52052(g).

     

    Assembly Bill (“AB”) 484 amended Education Code sections 52052(e)(2)(F) and 52052(e)(4) to allow schools that do not have an API calculated in 2013–14 through 2016-17 to use one of the following criteria to meet legislative and/or programmatic requirements:

  1. The most recent API calculation;

  2. An average of the three most recent annual API calculations; or

  3. Alternative measures that show increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils schoolwide and among significant groups. The decision to use one of these criteria may be made on a program by program basis and is a local decision.

BACKGROUND: The public hearing to consider the level of support for the petition to establish National University Academy 1001 STEAM (“NUA”) was held on February 6, 2018. NUA previously submitted a charter petition to the district which was denied by the board on May 16, 2017.

 

NUA is currently authorized by Lakeside Union School District. The charter petition for NUA describes a non-classroom-based educational program with a STEAM focus. Page 24 of the petition states that, “NUA 1001 STEAM offers a unique independent study program that provides students in grades 6-12 with both site-based and online support.” Students also participate in project-based learning activities, and are supported by professors from National University’s School of Engineering and Computing (page 21).

 

The current charter petition was submitted to the district as part of a transition plan to make the one site within the district’s boundaries compliant with current geographic requirements. The petition is for a non-classroom-based program that will serve Grades 6-12 at a non-district facility located at 3678 Aero Court, San Diego, 92123, which is located within the attendance boundaries of Angier Elementary, Taft Middle School, and Kearny High School. On page 38 of the petition, the school has indicated that its students come from 22 zip codes within San Diego Unified School District’s boundaries. “The majority of the students come from the following areas: Oak Park (10.78%), Linda Vista (20.58%), Clairemont (7.84%), Kearny Mesa (14.70%) and Mira Mesa (15.68%).”

 

National University Academy operates approximately eight other sites outside of the district’s boundaries. One of the sites is authorized by La Mesa-Spring Valley School District as of July 1, 2017, and the remaining sites are under the charter petition authorized by Lakeside.

 

NUA proposes to be authorized by San Diego Unified School District as of July 1, 2018, and is requesting a five-year term beginning July 1, 2018, and ending June 30, 2023. In preparation for the March 6, 2018 action date, staff recommended denial of the charter petition based on six findings. These findings, listed below, have been corrected, and updated information provided by the petitioner that satisfies requirements for the approval of a new charter is included.

 

Finding 1. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition based on a budget that presents a financial plan that is not fiscally viable because it overstates revenues and understates expenses, resulting in negative ending fund balances.

 

NUA submitted a revised budget and cash flow which has corrected the previously identified deficiencies. Revenues have been adjusted and incorporate the State’s determination at its March 14-15, 2018 meeting, that National University Academy qualifies to receive 100% of its LCFF funding for fiscal years 2017-18 and 2018-19. Expenses that were previously missing from the budget have now been included. Staff analysis has determined that the annual projected ending fund balances meet the district’s minimum reserve requirement of the greater of 3% of expenditures or $50,000.00.

 

Finding 2. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because, on November 14, 2017, the National University Academy governing board authorized a $253,125.00 transfer of funds to the NUA Robla charter school near Sacramento. This transfer resulted in local funds generated by and for students residing in San Diego, Riverside, and Orange Counties being used for a charter school located hundreds of miles away.

 

As stated earlier, National University Academy currently operates multiple school sites in Southern California. In order to prevent future transfers of money and insulate NUA, staff has requested and NUA has agreed, that it will have its own bank account(s), and will create its own board of directors and bylaws. The bank account(s) and board of directors will be separate and independent from the other National University Academy accounts and board(s). The NUA board will be responsible for the operations and oversight of only NUA, and shall include no more than two current members of the National University Academy board(s).

NUA has revised this section of the charter petition to include more detailed supports and interventions for socioeconomically disadvantaged students, including, access to the computer lab for students and parents, tutoring, twice weekly modified block schedules for math and reading, small group re-teaching, assistance in the Learning Lab, and weekly monitoring and check-in by teachers.

Analysis of these inventories is compared to student enrollment by the Instructional Materials Office to ensure sufficient quantities of district-adopted student instructional materials are available to meet the needs of pupils and teachers across the district. Surpluses of existing instructional materials are identified as school enrollments change and redistributed to fill shortages. Purchases are made to accommodate inventory shortages districtwide due to growth and replacement and distributed to schools in need. Since March 29, 2011, the board has approved the annual adoption and purchase of instructional materials and textbooks from 2011-12 through 2017-18 for district students and teachers.
