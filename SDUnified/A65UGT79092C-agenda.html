RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, reassignment of special education responsibilities, expansion of grade levels, or other significant change.

BACKGROUND:  On May 24, 2016, the board conducted a public hearing and adopted a resolution certifying the Environmental Impact Report (EIR) for the Project.  In addition to the specific findings made in support of certifying the EIR, the board approved the Project and authorized staff to carry out the Project consistent with the EIR. 

 

Subsequently, a group of 17 residents from Point Loma filed a lawsuit against the district claiming the district violated the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) in certifying the EIR.  The attorney representing the residents also submitted a letter claiming that the district violated the Brown Act in approving the Project because the project approval was not separately identified in the agenda description. 

 

Agenda Description and Public Notice of Board Actions – Certification of EIR

The May 24, 2016 agenda was published and available on the district’s website on May 20, 2016.  The agenda description stated that the item was a public hearing and “Resolution Certifying Final Environmental Impact Report Pursuant to California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) for Point Loma High School Whole Site Modernization and Athletic Facilities Upgrades Project.”  The detailed agenda , which was also published and available for public review on May 20, 2016, itself also included the specific findings and recommendations for board consideration, including approval of the Project.  Also, the Resolution certifying the EIR was included as an attachment to the agenda item and specifically referenced approval of the Project.  Many residents, students, interested persons and legal counsel for a group of residents spoke at the public hearing primarily commenting on the merits of the Project.   

 

Although it is evident that the public was aware that the board was considering approval of the Project and the detailed agenda expressly referenced the approval of the project, district staff, in an abundance of caution, has brought this item forward as a separate agenda item in order to cure the alleged Brown Act violation. 

 

Proposed Project

The Project includes the multi-phase construction and operation of upgraded athletic facilities and whole site modernization at Point Loma High School (Project) in the City of San Diego. The improvements are identified in Propositions Z and S and meet the goals and objectives of Vision 2020  and the 2008 Long Range Facilities Master Plan. The proposed improvements are summarized below:

  • Install four 20 ft tall grandstand light poles on the home side bleachers (max height of 45 ft above grade)
  • Install four 70 ft on the inside track, marked and lighted in accordance with FAA regulations
  • Remove two speaker poles on the home side of the field and replace with four 29 ft poles
  • Construct one-story, 616 sf electrical substation with signal panel enclosure
  • Demolish Building 800 containing a library and 11 classrooms
  • Construct a new three-story building, approximately 37,000 square feet in size, containing 20 classrooms and a library/media center
  • Renovate Buildings 200 and 300
  • Construct external security enclosures and new architectural facades
  • Construct/improve bus turnout
  • Demolish auto shop and reconfigure/expand adjoining parking lot
  • Construct one-story, 125 sf ticket booth on northwest end of field
  • Construct 500-seat visitor side aluminum bleachers
  • Construct 140 sf press box at home side grandstand, marked and lighted in accordance with FAA regulations (max height of 34 ft above grade)
  • Construct elevator with maximum height of 40 ft above grade
  • Install three 20 ft speaker poles on the visitor side 
  • Construct one-story, 392 sf storage building
  • Construct one-story visitor restroom and concession building
  • Install two 30 ft tall bleacher light poles  
  • Replace PA system
  • Demolish Building 400 containing 13 classrooms
  • Construct new cafeteria/food services building
  • Construct custodial supply building
  • Modify service drive between new cafeteria and boys’ locker building
  • Provide basketball courts
  • Demolish classroom wing of Building 600 containing 7 classrooms
  • Construct elevator and stair access to the upper floor of Building 600
  • Construct new academic landscape/hardscape (including passive and active use areas)
  • Renovate gymnasium/boys’ locker room/ROTC Building
  • Renovate girls’ locker/gymnasium
  • Renovate auditorium
  • Renovate administration facilities

The proposed improvements will be constructed in phases and the first phase of construction is currently expected to begin in the Fall of 2016.   Because the district is still in the process of bidding the construction, the precise fiscal impact has yet to be determined.  However, the cost per phase will be identified when the contracts for construction are awarded.

 

The Project would not substantially change the operations of the campus. The school calendar would remain consistent with the district calendar for a traditional high school program.  The whole site modernization component of the Project would result in a net loss of 13 classrooms, and the capacity of the high school would not increase. During construction of Phase 2, no temporary classroom facilities would be required; displaced seats caused by the demolition of Building 800 would be accommodated in underutilized classrooms in Buildings 400 and 600. Up to five portable facilities, however, may be installed in the auto shop area for use as a temporary library.

 

Environmental Process

The district certified the EIR for the Project on May 24, 2016.  No further environmental documents are required to support approval of the Project.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt staff findings that Evangeline Roberts Institute of Learning Charter School (ERIL) has not met the eight agreed-upon outcomes contained in its approved charter; affirm that the requirement for ERIL to meet those outcomes in the time required or voluntarily surrender its charter without the need for the board to initiate revocation proceedings were material to the board's granting of ERIL's renewal petition on February 3, 2015; and affirm that the Board of Education would not have approved ERIL's renewal petition at its February 3, 2015, without these material terms.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  District staff was previously assigned to provide special education services to students attending ERIL.   Due to closure of ERIL, those staff members will be reassigned.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  On February 3, 2015, the Board of Education held a public hearing to consider ERIL's petition for renewal of its charter.  Prior to the public hearing, over the course of several months, district staff in the Charter School Office worked with ERIL to address strong concerns about ERIL's educational program and management practices.  The result of this collaboration was that eight outcomes were developed which ERIL agreed to meet by dates specified, or voluntarily surrender its charter without the need for the district to initiate revocation proceedings.  These outcomes and ERIL's agreement to surrender its charter should it fail to meet them were incorporated into ERIL's charter as presented to the Board of Education at the public hearing on February 3, 2015. 

 

When the public hearing on renewal of ERIL's petition came forward on February 3, 2015, district staff presented the Board of Education with two alternative recommendations for action; either approve the charter as presented, which included the eight required outcomes and ERIL's agreement to surrender its charter without the need for revocation should it fail to meet them; or deny the renewal petition based on findings that 1) ERIL presented an unsound educational program based on its performance to date; 2) that ERIL presented an unsound educational program based on its failure to meet the charter renewal eligibility requirements set forth in Education Code section 47605, and that ERIL was demonstrably unlikely to implement the program set forth in the petition based on its failure to maintain active corporate status for a significant portion of its prior charter term. 

 

The board voted to approve the charter as presented, which included the aforementioned commitment by ERIL to meet the eight specified outcomes by dates certain, or voluntarily surrender its charter.  ERIL's agreement to meet the the eight outcomes and its agreement to voluntarily surrender its charter were material to staff's recommendation and the board's decision to approve that recommendation rather than deny the petition for renewal.  The board, further, delegated to staff responsibility to make objective findings as to whether ERIL had met the specified outcomes. 

 

The board is now asked to affirm those findings, and also to affirm that incorporation of ERIL's agreement to meet eight specified outcomes by dates certain or surrender its charter without the need for revocation proceedings were material to the Board's approval of ERIL's renewal petition, which would otherwise have been denied. 

 

Regarding the staff concerns which led to conversations with ERIL leading up to the February 3, 2015 public hearing, at the time of its renewal, ERIL had a limited amount of academic data available for analysis.  Staff had additional concerns, including but not limited to that ERIL failed to maintain active corporate status and was suspended at the time of the public hearing.  In in an effort to establish outcomes that could be included to support renewal of the school’s charter, district staff met and communicated with the ERIL Principal and ERIL Board President multiple times in the months leading up to the school’s renewal.  Below is a summary of the events that occurred as part of this process:

BACKGROUND: The ST Math program was developed and is owned by MIND Research Institute.  It is an instructional program that is designed to boost math comprehension through visual learning.  The program uses interactive, graphically-rich animations that visually represent mathematical concepts to improve conceptual understanding and problem-solving skills.

 

The K-6 grade level, instructional program is self-paced, language independent, with mastery-based objectives.  It is closely aligned to common core state standards and integrates with core instruction.

 

All elementary and K-8 schools will be provided with a life-time license which includes project management, start up implementation, teacher/administrator professional development, ongoing consulting with personalized support as well as monthly school and district reports.  Twenty-one elementary schools currently purchase and use ST Math.  Purchasing for all elementary schools will be a cost savings for each of these sites. 

 

At the end of year one, the Leadership and Learning Division, along with San Diego Education Association (SDEA) will determine which schools are not utilizing ST Math to its fullest potential.  The program will then be discontinued at those schools and a credit based on individual licensing costs will be applied for each school that no longer utilizes the program.

 

Beginning with year two, there will be an ongoing license renewal based upon the number of school sites that continue to utilize the original license.

 

The district received $1,403,597.00 in discounts which reduced the cost to the current amount.

RECOMMENDATION: Ratification of Agreement with The Regents of the University of California on behalf of the University of California, San Diego (UCSD) to provide full scholarships to the UCSD Sally Ride Science Junior Academy for Girls - Summer STEAM Workshop for 150 middle and high school girls at Mission Bay High School and Pacific Beach Middle School (SV-17-0219-19).  The term of this agreement is July 11, 2016 through July 29, 2016.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND:  This agreement provides $22,500 in scholarships to 150 female students to attend the Sally Ride Summer STEAM workshop at UCSD. The Sally Ride Science Junior Academy for Girls offers fascinating and fun learning experiences in science, technology and engineering, with applied mathematics and art design (STEAM) for girls. During these workshops, students assume the roles of space explorer, ocean engineer or computer scientist as they immerse themselves in hands-on projects. Top-notch STEAM instructors lead these workshops, serving as both teachers and role models. The workshops also incorporate real-life stories of vibrant women conducting research in each field. Their examples inspire students and help make STEAM careers more accessible.  On the last day of each session, students participate in a showcase to share what they created and learned with students in other workshops. 

 

Mission Bay High School and Pacific Beach Middle School will incur the costs to transport the students to UCSD, and also the cost for lunch meals provided to students while attending the Junior Academy.

BACKGROUND: Pursuant to Administrative Procedure 3300, Naming of Schools and School Facilities, this is a request to approve the naming of the tennis courts at Lincoln High School after Dr. Ruben A. Carriedo. The School Names Committee has reviewed this request and unanimously concurs with this recommendation.

 

The Lincoln Class of 1970 is requesting this naming of the tennis courts. Dr. Carriedo served as teacher, counselor, ASB advisor and tennis coach from 1969 to 1976. During that time Dr. Carriedo earned the reputation of having a profound influence on students. He was encouraging and helped students develop a growth mindset regarding their potential for a promising future. He taught students to be leaders, risk-takers, and problem-solvers. He was instrumental in the success of many students who would be the first in their families to attend college.

 

According to the Class of 1970, "As Lincoln's tennis coach, Carriedo had to approach students who were reluctant to be on a fledging team, and had to groom these young students to appreciate the lessons that tennis offers, like the notion of being on time, being prepared, working hard, and how to overcome adversity. He introduced these young men to important lessons of perseverance, teamwork, sportsmanship, goal setting, and leadership skills. He nurtured those important life skills, all the while building self-confidence of these young tennis players who were not often the focus of high school accolades."

 

Carriedo left a lasting legacy that is still remembered by faculty, students, and community members today.

The project will include the following components:

  • Improve the existing kindergarten ramp, main entry and the alley/fire lane entry.
  • Construct a new site accessibility ramp and stairs within the kindergarten playground area.
  • Improve the main entry and alley/fire lane entry areas consisting of accessible parking (restripe existing parking stalls), ramp, stairs, and an accessible path of travel from Venice Street.
  • Remove and replace existing planting and concrete paving within portions of the campus as needed during construction.

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111 (b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more.  The district advertised once per week for two consecutive weeks as required by the Public Contract Code section 20112.  The District received seven (7) bids and were opened on June 9, 2016.  The district received bids from Blue Pacific Engineering & Construction, DuWright Construction, Inc., Just Construction, Inc., MTM Builders, Inc., Team C Construction, Western Rim Constructors, Inc., and Willkom, Inc.  Team C Construction has been determined to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.  On May 10, 2011, the board approved a resolution in support of service-disabled, veterans-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled, veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a 3 percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities.  The recommended contractor, Team C Construction submitted a 3 percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA Analysis:

 

The Project qualifies for the categorical exemptions identified below.

 

Minor Alterations to Land (Class 4)

Class 4 consists of minor public or private alterations in the condition of land, water, and/or vegetation which do not involve removal of healthy, mature, scenic trees except for forestry or agricultural purposes. Examples include, but are not limited to minor trenching and backfilling where the surface is restored (CEQA Guidelines §15304[f]).

 

The Project would require minor grading during the construction and installation of the proposed improvements. However, all graded areas would be restored. All work would occur entirely within the developed campus and would not require the removal of any healthy, mature or scenic trees.  

 

Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14)

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25% or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314).

 

The Project would not result in the addition of new classrooms or other facilities that would increase the number of classrooms or seating capacity.

 

Categorical exemptions may be not be relied upon if the application of the exemption triggers any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2.  Those exceptions to exemptions are analyzed in Attachment A to the NOE.  As set forth in Attachment A, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemptions.

 

Approval of this item approves the Project as described, authorizes and directs staff to file a Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk and awards the construction contract for the Project to Team C Construction.

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, reassignment of special education responsibilities, expansion of grade levels, or other significant change.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Staff recommends that the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education grant the revision of the charter of Holly Drive Leadership Academy for the charter school to operate independently for special education purposes by participating as an LEA member of the El Dorado County Charter SELPA, effective July 1, 2016.
