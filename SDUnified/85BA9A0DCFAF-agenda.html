Subject
F.04. Board Discussion of Budget Analysis Process (Evans)
Meeting
Apr 14, 2009 - 04/14/2009
Type
Action, Information
As a school board we had to approve severe budget cuts to meet our March 15 interim budget report deadline. We had to propose cuts to which we would normally be opposed.

Our final budget decisions will be made in June. Between now and then we will be studying possible additional revenues, as well as other possible cuts to balance our budget.

First, before making decisions about budget cuts we will be appealing to the state for our fair share of the federal stimulus dollars, we will explore maximum flexibility in the funds we are given, and we will seek reimbursement through other types of programs. While this may mitigate some of the cuts, they will still be severe.

Second, we are not going to make budget cuts or reverse budget cuts in isolation. All of the budget items will be considered together, because for every budget item that is saved, another must be cut.

Third, we are going to continually push for accurate information on finances by whatever means necessary, whether internally or externally. We will also require complete information on the impact of cuts. Both factors will be carefully considered in our decision.

Fourth, we will maintain close contact with our constituent groups as we make these decisions.

Fifth, it is our goal to make budget decisions on the following principles:

a) Cuts must be made as far away from the classroom and school site as possible. For this reason we have given priority to funding school sites over central administration.

b) Our employees who work with and near our students comprise our most valuable resource and we place a high priority on maintaining a stable workforce.

c) When sacrifices must be made we believe that it should be shared by all, from the top to the bottom of the organization.

Finally, we realize that the uncertainty of the budget situation is anxiety provoking for everyone. However, it is very important that these decisions be carefully deliberated. We appreciate the hard work that teachers, administrators and support personnel continue to do on a daily basis for our children. We will only succeed by working together as parents, teachers, administrators, the Board of Education, and the San Diego community. We will strive to make this as open and transparent as possible.

I.21. Crosscultural, Language Academic Development (CLAD) Waiver for Career Technical Education and JROTC Teachers (Board Governance Policy: OE-10)

Approval of waivers for Career Technical Education and JROTC Teachers to Qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural, Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit. If approved, the waivers will be submitted to the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing. Attached is the list of teachers.

California Education Code Section 44253.1 requires all teachers to have an active and valid English Learner authorization if assigned to a classroom that has one or more students designated as an English Learner. Because of the Williams Settlement legislation, a review of this Education Code has determined that the need for an English Learner authorization also applies to all Career Technical Education (CTE) and JROTC certificated staff employed by the district. Teachers who hold a bachelor’s degree and completed an approved teacher preparation program leading to the issuance of a regular education credential or special education credential, but did not hold an English Learner authorization, qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural, Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to cover them in their assignments while completing the requirements to obtain a Clear CLAD Certificate.

Due to the fact that Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers are not required to hold a bachelor’s degree or complete an approved teacher preparation program to be issued a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing, this sizable group of district teachers do not qualify for an Emergency CLAD Permit. On March 19, 2008, the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing announced its approval of SB 1292 giving all holders of Designated Subjects Teaching Credentials an option to obtain an English Learner authorization. Prior to this approval, there was no legal option available to the holder of a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential to be issued any form of English Learner authorization. On September 26, 2008, the San Diego County Office of Education began the first session of training for qualifying teachers employed in the county.

After working with the San Diego County Office of Education, the district has been advised that all Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers will need to apply for CLAD Waivers with the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing in order not to be considered mis-assigned for the 2008-09 academic school year. These waivers will be valid for the 2008-09 school year but can be renewed for an additional year in the event the San Diego County Office of Education cannot accommodate the county’s entire population of teachers who need to complete the training.

Agreement with Ramona Paving and Construction Corp, Inc., effective April 15, 2009 through April 14, 2010.

Introduction

This is a request to award an asphalt contract to the lowest responsive and responsible bidder with State School Facilities Funds. It represents a strategy that balances and optimizes the capacities of district staff with those of an external firm. The following narrative outlines the roles and responsibilities and strategy to maximize benefit to the district.

Background

One of the largest physical assets within San Diego Unified Schools District’s physical plant is asphalt pavement. The system includes playgrounds, parking lots, driveways, walkways, drainage swales and ramps totaling more than 21 million square feet. The current replacement value of the system exceeds $100 million.

This physical asset must be maintained to minimize the cost of ownership. Asphalt deteriorates from exposure to weather (heat, rain, etc.) mechanical wear (such as dirt and grit, vehicle and foot traffic) and environmental impacts (rodents, fluids from vehicles, underlying soil failures). This maintenance plan addresses these conditions and extends the asset service life, maximizing return on investment in a cost effective manner.

Maintenance Strategy

Physical Plant Operations (PPO) maintains and repairs the district’s asphalt systems utilizing in-house and contract resources. Primary aspects in the maintenance of asphalt include: preventive maintenance (PM) and reactive maintenance (site-generated repair orders).

Preventive maintenance (PM) is the most predictable and most cost-effective facet of maintaining a usable and appropriate surface for students and staff. PPO supports a five-year recurring maintenance cycle that includes asphalt repair, slurry seal and line stripping. Reactive maintenance tends to small, isolated asphalt repairs and safety concerns such as potholes and cracks.

Labor requirements for asphalt maintenance in the coming year exceeds 35,000 hours. Fully utilized, the PPO, seven-man asphalt crew can deliver approximately 14,000 to 15,000 hours per year. PPO asphalt crews are staffed and equipped to support small PM projects and for response to reactive maintenance activities. Expanding capabilities would require significant capital expenditures and staffing increases. Ramona Paving is an experienced general engineering contractor that specializes in asphalt pavement construction and maintenance and is equipped to accomplish larger scale projects.

Workload

California Public Contracting Code 20114 requires the district to contract projects that exceed 750 labor hours or $21,000 in material costs (Force Account). As a result, in FY 07/08, 19,000 hours of asphalt maintenance were delivered through contracted services. IDIQ contracts provide predictable delivery and costs for projects. The IDIQ contract will only be utilized after the in-house forces are at capacity or the scope of the project exceeds force account requirements. The Asphalt Service Schedule (below) represents 4.1 million square feet of asphalt to be serviced within the next year, at a cost of $2.4 million. This schedule, fully utilizes PPO crews, tasking them with 2.1 million square feet of asphalt maintenance at an estimated cost of $1.2 million.
