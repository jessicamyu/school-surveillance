Type

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct Public Hearing and approve General Waiver Request to the California Department of Education for Roosevelt Middle School to raise class size targets required in the Quality Education Investment Act (QEIA) for Grade 6 from 15.9 to 20.9, and Grade 7 from 15.7 to 20.7.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Roosevelt Middle School has been in Program Improvement (PI) for six years. Roosevelt's Program Improvement Plan included reduced class sizes in key content areas. The district provided additional teachers to the school, in addition to those allocated by the district's formula, to help the school implement the class size component of their Program Improvement Plan. Roosevelt's 2006-07 baseline of 20.9 for Grade 6 and 20.7 for Grade 7 was already significantly lower than a typical middle school when they began receiving QEIA funding. The facilities at Roosevelt cannot accommodate a further reduction in class size.

Due to the state budget situation, the funds that were once provided by the district to reduce class size at Roosevelt are no longer available. Additionally, to balance the 2009-10 budget, the district raised class sizes at all grade levels, thus reducing the number of teachers that are allocated to the school by the district's formula. This waiver does not affect the Grade 8 target.

In April 2009, Roosevelt teachers voted favorably to raise class size targets to 20.9 for Grade 6 and 20.7 for Grade 7. The Roosevelt teachers' vote was followed by a presentation to the school's site council, which also agreed with the proposal to raise the QEIA ratios as stated in the waiver request.

Title II Funding & TIIG
RECOMMENDATION: Approve an Advanced Placement (AP) Action Plan to improve equitable access to high quality AP courses and to provide support systems to enhance student participation and performance in these courses for all district students, especially low-income and traditionally underrepresented students.

FISCAL IMPACT: Implementation of the AP Action Plan would be supported primarily through Title II funding ($223,253) currently allocated for professional development. In addition, $4,050 would need to be allocated in unrestricted funding to support the implementation of a district AP advisory council (i.e., teacher release time, materials, etc.). (See Appendix B of the report for strategies and associated projected costs by funding source.)

BACKGROUND: The purpose of the plan is to expand student access to and improve student participation and performance in AP courses districtwide. The plan, modeled on implementation strategies included in the AP Incentive Program Grant 2, reflects a basic level of support for eleven schools: Clairemont, Crawford Complex, La Jolla, Lincoln, Mira Mesa, Morse, Patrick Henry, San Diego Complex, San Diego School of Creative and Performing Arts, Scripps Ranch, and University City. The administrator of the AP Incentive Program Grant 2 will work collaboratively with the GATE office to ensure consistency of services to all schools.

Key Issues:
  • San Diego Unified School District has worked to broaden access to the AP curriculum and to enable a wider and more ethnically diverse proportion of students to achieve success in AP.
  • Significant inequities remain, however, as evidenced by the underrepresentation of Hispanic, African American, and socioeconomically disadvantaged students in AP course enrollments as well as in the numbers of tests taken and passed by these groups. (See Appendix A).
  • The AP Incentive Program Grant 1, which has proven successful in providing AP opportunities to traditionally underserved students, and the AP Incentive Program Grant 2, provide models for this proposal. (See Appendix B).
  • The AP Incentive Program Grant 1 ends September, 2009. This Action Plan would allow schools participating in Grant 1—Clairemont, Crawford, Lincoln, Morse, and San Diego—to continue successful AP initiatives. In addition, those high schools not involved in AP Incentive Program grants would be served.
  • In order to address inequities and to build on the groundwork established by the AP Incentive Program Grant 1, this proposal recommends strategies to increase student access to AP courses and to enhance student preparedness for and ability to succeed in this rigorous coursework.
  • The implementation of the AP Action Plan will provide professional development to prepare teachers from participating schools to work with all students and all learning styles.
  • The plan will also increase parent and community knowledge of the importance of underrepresented student enrollment in AP and/or advanced classes.

RECOMMENDATION: It is the recommendation of the Gifted and Talented Education (GATE) Program to end GATE testing after Grade 5.

FISCAL IMPACT: Potential cost savings to the district.

BACKGROUND: Approximately 15,000 students are reviewed each year by the Gifted and Talented Education (GATE) Program. Students are universally tested in Grade 2, and then reviewed for initial testing or retesting in Grades 5 or 7. With this modification, all students will continue to be tested in Grade 2. Grade 5 students will have an additional opportunity to be reviewed and assessed.

Secondary schools have a host of evaluative means to determine the most advantageous placement for each student and have requested greater jurisdiction over pupil assignments. A comprehensive student evaluation sheet that calls for administrator and teacher input has been developed to serve as a template to assist school counselors in making placements (see attached exhibit). Schools may use these as guidelines in determining placement in both GATE Cluster and GATE Seminar classes for students who may be new to the district, may not have had an opportunity to be tested or may not have met previous criteria for actual GATE identification.

As a consequence of this action, approximately 1,200 fewer students will be assessed, potentially resulting in a cost savings to the district and providing greater autonomy to school sites.

This includes helping students move from risk to resiliency and to achieve academic and social success in primary and secondary schools and beyond; helping teachers promote more positive behavior in the classroom and create more engaging learning environments for at-risk students; and helping families create the kind of stable, structured, emotionally and physically healthy environments that contribute to the academic and social success of their children. The program calls for the creation of a school leadership team that will put in place a set of comprehensive interventions for students, families and teachers for five targeted students at each school. Intervention therapists/caseworkers, with the support of school leadership teams, will ensure that disengaged students become connected to services in each of the following areas: creative outlets, academic support, recreational opportunities, emotional counseling, and social supports.
