RECOMMENDATION: Adopt proclamation in recognition of the 35th Anniversary of the San Diego Unified School District Filipino Language Program and celebrate October 2022 as Filipino American History Month. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In 1989, the Filipino community rallied to advocate the offering of Filipino language classes in the San Diego Unified School District. As we enter the 35th year of the District’s Filipino language program, the Board acknowledges the important role community organizations such as the Filipino American Educators Association of San Diego County (FILAMEDA) had in the creation and continued success of this program. The celebration of Filipino American History Month in October of each year commemorates the first recorded presence of Filipinos in the continental United States, which occurred on October 18, 1587, when “Luzones Indios” came ashore from the Spanish galleon Nuestra Senora de Esperanza and landed at what is now Morro Bay, California. In 1992, the late Dr. Fred Cordova, along with his wife, Filipino American National Historical Society (FANHS) Founder Dr. Dorothy Laigo Cordova, first introduced October as Filipino American History Month with a resolution from the FANHS National Board of Trustees. FILAMEDA’s goal is to foster fellowship, understanding, and cooperation among educators and to work on various educational literacy, art, and sociocultural projects to preserve the culture, noble traditions, and priceless heritage of the Filipino people. In 2009, U.S. Congress recognized October as Filipino American History Month in the United States. Filipino Americans are the second largest Asian American group in the nation and the third largest ethnic group in California, after Latinx and African Americans. 4,716 students in the San Diego Unified School District are Filipino-American. Filipino History Month is a time of reflection and remembrance and provides an opportunity for all people to learn and appreciate Filipino Americans’ historic contributions with various activities and events to bring awareness of the significant role Filipinos have played in American history.

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and adopt a Resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System, and/or providing student access to digital versions of Board approved instructional materials using a computer.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code section 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. Per Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must be provided access to a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials. The materials may be in a digital format as long as each pupil, at a minimum, has and can access the same materials in the class and to take home, as all other pupils in the same class or course in the school district and has the ability to use and access them at home. The board is required to adopt a Resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable.

 

Williams Settlement Legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12. The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core, lab equipment for science laboratory courses. All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation also requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual Resolution of Sufficiency. Administrative Circulars 1, 2, and 3, dated August 29, 2022, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency." Principals were directed to complete an instructional materials inventory, remedy any insufficiencies, and confirm instructional materials sufficiency by completing and returning the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 16, 2022.

 

Thirty elementary schools, five TK-8 schools, eleven middle schools, four senior high schools, and one atypical school were visited by a team from the San Diego County Office of Education. All 51 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials. Additionally, all four senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

BACKGROUND: The National Bullying Prevention Month campaign was founded in 2006. The San Diego Unified School District calls for all schools to be safe, inclusive neighborhood learning centers, and each quality neighborhood school to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.” School district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing updated and revised protocols and procedures such as the Bullying & Intimidation Prohibition Policy , participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved. San Diego Unified has partnered with the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and their No Place for Hate program which provides participating schools with anti-bias and anti-bullying resources to help create sustainable change that leads to an improved school environment. San Diego Unified has implemented programming to support schools in building positive school climates and a culture of upstanders through "Ally Action,” bullying prevention efforts, and student-led capacity building for peers as well as school site staff. The San Diego Unified Council of ASB Presidents fully supports October as National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within the month and throughout the year. Students’ participation in anti-bullying activities provides opportunities for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and learning skills to be aware of and speak of injustices, learning to be allies and upstanders, as they advocate for positive school environments. 
  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying which people may experience including, but not limited to, cyber, verbal, physical, and exclusion. 
  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus, the consequences of bullying and the many ways in which they can activate their voice to promote social change.
  • Support students to determine solutions to stop bullying and promote positive interactions between students. 
  • Engage educators and families in raising awareness about the many factors that affect bullying and work to mitigate them, giving highlighted importance to our LGBTQIA+ students.
  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying, advocate for inclusive and positive school communities while raising awareness about the complexities of bullying.
  • Work toward addressing and eradicating incidences of bullying on all school campuses; and

San Diego Unified School District offers lesson plans, video resources, and print resources through the Digital Cultural Awareness Center at culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com and information about Ally Action Programming, bullying prevention, and bullying intervention at youthadvocacy.us.

BACKGROUND: The month of October is nationally recognized as Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual/Pansexual, Transgender and Gender Nonconforming, Queer/Questioning, Intersex, and Asexual Plus (LGBTQIA+) History Month. The San Diego Unified School District is committed to maintaining safe, inclusive environments wherein each and every one of its students can thrive; including:

  • Implementing practices that support LGBTQIA+ students as prescribed by the School Success and Opportunity Act, AB 1266, which went into effect on January 1, 2014. 
  • Options for name and gender marker changes on all unofficial school records to reflect the student’s name and gender marker that is consistent with the student’s gender identity irrespective of the gender listed on the pupil’s records.
  • Addressing students by the name and pronouns consistent with their gender identity, without the necessity of legal documentation or a change to the student’s official district record.
  • Options for the use of facilities consistent with their gender identity, irrespective of the gender listed on the pupil’s records (CSBA Policy Brief); and

The San Diego Unified School District acknowledges the importance of teaching our youth about the historical and present oppression and discrimination that impacts the LGBTQIA+ community, the positive and accurate contributions of the LGBTQIA+ community, and the ways in which we can  proactively build advocacy and allyship with the LGBTQIA+ community. The Department of Youth Advocacy provides learning opportunities, professional development and workshop opportunities to all SDUSD schools, educators, and administrators; and develops resources, leadership opportunities and pathways for the youth themselves to engage their schoolmates in year-round dialogue that preventatively mitigates for harm and humanizes ALL students for being nuanced individuals who bring value to the school community. The San Diego Unified School District offers lesson plans, media, and print resources to support schools in observing LGBTQIA+ History Month through the Digital Cultural Awareness Center at culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com and information/resources for students, families, educators at youthadvocacy.us. October is also designated as National Bullying Prevention Month, and staff members and students are developing and coordinating activities that proactively reiterate their commitment to diversity, inclusion, affirmation, and safety, including: 

  • Promoting student awareness of the various forms of bullying including cyber, verbal, physical, and social exclusion on the basis of actual or perceived race, religion, national origin, disability, neurodiversity, socio-economic status, language, immigration status, sexual orientation, gender identity and/or gender expression; 
  • Implementing history and social science (HSS) curriculum that highlights the contributions of members of the LGBTQIA+ community and allows students to see accurate and respectful reflections of themselves [and their classmates] in the curriculum as prescribed by the FAIR Education Act, SB 48, which was signed into law on July 14, 2011. 
  • Attending professional development to ensure that all staff and administrators are updated on the most recent policies, protocol, and best practices to support and guide LGBTQIA+ students to greater access, affirmation, and opportunities for success.

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District believes children’s lives are the most important priority. Opioid use has been on a constant incline for high school aged students. New, increased amounts of fentanyl-laced opioid drugs dramatically increase the rates of overdose. Recently, brightly colored fentanyl, referred to as “rainbow” fentanyl has begun circulating in illegal markets. The San Diego Unified School District has stocked Nasal Naloxone in its schools with students in sixth grade and above since 2018. School Police officers, Nurses, health office staff and other staff on campus are trained to administer Naloxone at every district school site with students in sixth grade and above.

Services are targeted within six elementary, three middle and two high schools. Project objectives: (1) Expand access to World Language courses leading to the Seal of Biliteracy, (2) Effectively use computer-assisted instruction or distance learning, to establish course offerings of one world language course not currently offered. Project activities are planning, needs assessment and professional development, implementation of an after-school enrichment Foreign Language Elementary Experience (FLEX) program, addition of an introductory world language course at middle schools, provision of AP World Language preparation courses or tutoring to students at high schools and to establish one new online world language course. Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): Section 15300.2 of the State CEQA Guidelines specifies several exceptions to the use of a categorical exemption.

 

  • Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the Project is to be located – a Project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant, therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the Project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies. The Proposed Project consists of repairs to existing facilities where no changes in use would occur. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

  • Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource. Scripps Ranch High School was constructed in 1993 and is not considered a historic resource.
