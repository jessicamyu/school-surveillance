RECOMMENDATION: As a service-providing organization, the San Diego Unified School District's employees are its most valuable resource. Today's recognition includes recognition of employees with 40, 30, 20, 15, 10 and 5 years of service. Certificates and pin will be delivered to emloyees via school mail. The Superintendent and the Board of Education extend their most sincere gratitude to these individuals. 

 

Today, we celebrate milestones of 79 employees.

 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: This is a celebration of our employees. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: As San Diego Unified celebrates progress towards meeting the inspirational goals contained in Vision 2021, we are taking time to celebrate those employees who have helped us achieve this progress. The number one predictor of student success is the presence of an experienced, high quality teacher in the classroom, according to the Learning Policy Institute. (Source: “Report: Teacher qualifications best predictor of student success,” By Amelia Harper, May 16, 2019, Education Dive). That study, which was written last year and co-authored by the Chair of the State Board of Education, found high quality teachers have helped San Diego schools do better than the rest of the state. (Source: “California’s Positive Outliers: Districts Beating the Odds,” by Anne Podolsky, Linda Darling-Hammond, Christopher Doss, and Sean Reardon, May 16, 2019, Learning Policy Institute). Educators also provide crucial services in addition to teaching. They are critical to keeping students safe at school. School staff also provide healthy, nutritious meals to children who do not have access to food at home. Nearly 100,000 schools/institutions serve school lunches to 29.8 million students each day, including 20.2 million free lunches. (Source: School Nutrition Association.) Given the clear association between the health and success of our students and the well-being and effectiveness of our workforce, we publicly recognize employees for their continuous years of service to the students, families and communities of San Diego Unified.  

 

*This service recognition award is not used to calculate or verify the seniority date of an employee nor is the recognition part of the formal personnel processes that calculate the date for longevity pay. Both longevity and seniority dates are dictated by the Education Code as well as the applicable collective bargaining agreement. The date used to determine this service award is based on the individual's permanent status as an employee, temporary or hourly work is not used in the determination of the service award recognition. Service recognition awards will be awarded on a monthly basis. If you believe an error has been made, you may contact the Human Resource Service Division by emailing jobs@sandi.net.

5. Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing May 2021, as Asian Pacific Islander Heritage Month
Meeting
Apr 27, 2021 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing May 2021, as Asian Pacific Islander Heritage Month in honor of the Asian American and Pacific Islander communities in San Diego and their commitment to making this region a better place. The board also supports Senate Bill 937, the COVID-19 Hate Crimes Act, and urges the Congress to approve and the President of the United States to sign this bill into law.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: May is recognized as Asian Pacific American Heritage Month throughout the United States due to the enormous contributions of Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders (AAPIs) throughout the history of this great Nation. Chinese immigrants played a significant role in advancing American commerce as they laid the final ties of the transcontinental railroad at Promontory Summit, Utah in 1869, as a result of years of physical and manual labor. Over 110,000 Japanese American citizens sacrificed their opportunity to chase the American Dream and contribute to society when they were restricted as second-class citizens to living in internment camps in the 1940s. Filipino American migrant workers taught us the value of multicultural coalition building in the 1960s and 1970s, through their struggle for workers’ rights in lockstep with Chicano workers in Delano, California to form the United Farm Workers’ Movement. In the 45th year since the fall of Saigon, we must honor the resilience of the Southeast Asian community, particularly the Vietnamese, Cambodian, Thai, Laotian, Hmong, Mien, and Burmese communities, as they continue to flee conflict and relocate their lives in the United States. The Indigenous People of Melanesia, Micronesia, and Polynesia are teaching us the cultural significance of preserving and promoting one’s values and language in the context of assimilation. Korean immigrants have shown us how to overcome significant challenges as they have endured anti-immigrant policies throughout the course of American history. South Asian Americans, particularly those that are Muslim, Hindu, and Sikh, are fighting against discrimination today as they are targets of suspicion and violence. On the 55th anniversary of the Immigration Act of 1965, San Diego has significantly benefited from waves of migration that have contributed to the fabric of the region. The over 350,000 Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in San Diego County contribute to the richness and diversity of this beautiful region. The Asian American and Pacific Islander population is the fastest growing population in the region. The ethno-linguistic diversity of Asian Americans and Pacific Islander communities in San Diego embodies the 21st century American experience. Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in San Diego have made significant contributions as business owners, public servants, teachers, activists, health practitioners, engineers, scientists, artists and other occupations. The vibrant Asian American and Pacific Islander community will celebrate with their diverse friends, families, neighbors, classmates, colleagues, and parishioners during the month of May. The surge of anti-Asian hate crimes during the coronavirus pandemic is testing the fabric of our nation, rather than reflecting and celebrating the countless ways the AAPI community has shaped our nation’s history. Congress has introduced Senate Bill 937, the COVID-19 Hate Crimes Act, to ensure an expedited review of COVID-19 hate crimes, and to provide law enforcement with guidance on online reporting and cultural competency, and issue guidance to mitigate racially discriminatory language. We stand in solidarity with our Asian and Asian American students, families and communities, advocating for safety, restorative justice and healing. This elected body is committed to educating the public about the history of Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in the United States, California, and San Diego, and supports the teaching of Mandarin Chinese at Barnard Elementary, Pacific Beach Middle, and Mission Bay High Schools, Japanese at Scripps Ranch High School, and Filipino at Bell and Challenger Middle Schools, and Mira Mesa and Morse High Schools.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The development of technology and increased availability of digital data layers make it possible for staff to identify and consider specific conditions when carrying out their responsibility to analyze and modify attendance area boundaries. Specific conditions to be considered in this work include:

  1. Areas where canyons or other impediments prevent direct vehicular or pedestrian access to the assigned school
  2. Attendance area boundary lines that “jog” and create arbitrary division within a block
  3. Alignment with the City’s established neighborhoods
  4. Infill development of housing that was not factored in to the boundary establishment
  5. Safe routing to avoid locations of crime reports
  6. Safe routing to avoid locations of pedestrian and bicycle incidents

The methodology and criteria listed in this report are aligned with existing policy, as detailed in the attached report.

 

This item does not increase the frequency or pace at which attendance area boundary reviews are performed.

 

The approach described in the report intends to employ the best available data and digital tools with the goal in boundary adjustment work to recommend the best connection to the neighborhood school, to refine boundaries previously limited by past technologies, to support community cohesion, and to route students safely.

RECOMMENDATIONS: Approve the use of California Education Code §44258.3 as the local assignment option for the use of an alternative means for completion of the prescribed course of study for high school graduation in Physical Education per Subsection (b) of Education Code §51225.3.

 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Approval of this local assignment option provides an alternative means for completion of the prescribed course of study for high school graduation in Physical Education.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: N/A.  

 

BACKGROUND: 

As identified in the District’s Course of Study, certain BMD and JROTC courses provide Physical Education credit. Teachers are deemed qualified by the credential they hold. All Basic Military Drill (“BMD”)/ Reserve Officer Training Corps (“ROTC”) teachers hold a Designated Subjects Special Subjects Teaching Credential (DSSS), they must add the Teaching Assignment Option or a Special Teaching Assignment (“STA”) allow these individuals to grant credit in PE.  

 

Approval of amendments to Title 5 of the California Code of Regulations (CCR) section 80037 pertaining to Designated Subjects Special Subjects Teaching Credentials established a Special Teaching Authorization (STA) in Physical Education effective January 1, 2015. Holders of a DSSS credential in BMD and ROTC may elect to add the STA in Physical Education upon completion of specific requirements.  The STA in Physical Education added to a DSSS credential in BMD or ROTC will provide the holder with a distinct physical education authorization limited to the areas of basic military drill and physical fitness training. The STA will not authorize instruction in any physical education courses that are offered outside of BMD and ROTC programs.

 

Holders of DSSS credentials in BMD and ROTC who do not have the STA listed on their documents will still be authorized, by school board action, to teach Physical Education in the context of BMD or ROTC courses that have been approved to carry high school graduation credit for Physical Education per Education Code section 44258.3. Education Code section 44258.3 allows local school districts to assign any credential holder to teach departmentalized classes, with their consent, in Grades K-12 irrespective of the designations on their teaching credentials. The teacher's competence must be verified according to policies and procedures established by the governing board consistent with the language of the statute; Board Policy 4113 and the accompanying AR: Assignment, along with Administrative Regulation 6142.7: Physical Education and Activity fulfill this requirement.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the license agreement is a project subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Section 15301 – (Class 1) Existing Facilities. A Class 1 CEQA exemption consists of the “operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures…” (CEQA Guidelines §15301). The proposed activity involves the approval of a new license agreement with YMCA of San Diego County for the use of office space and a parking area.  YMCA of San Diego County has been authorized to use district facilities at PBMS under a previous license agreement since April 1, 2017. Use of facilities at PBMS would not be expanded when compared with their use under the prior license agreement and therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified Environmental Impact Report (EIR).
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project is consistent with the past and present use of the project site. The YMCA has been operating at PBMS under a similar agreement since 2017. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. The Proposed Project includes the approval of an agreement that allows use of the site consistent with past and present use. No expansion of use is proposed. Therefore, approval of the Proposed Project would not result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes an agreement to allow the YMCA to operate consistent with existing use. No construction is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction or demolition of any buildings that would directly, or indirectly, affect a historic resource.  Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.
