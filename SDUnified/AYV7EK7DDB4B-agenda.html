FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: During his term in office, President Barack Obama acknowledged and emphasized the importance of community colleges to the nation's economic health. The attainment of post-secondary degrees and certificates is necessary in order for students to be prepared for and competitive in the workforce. American higher education, including community colleges, has increased in cost, requiring students to take on years of debt before embarking on a career, thereby limiting their access to educational opportunity. President Obama proposed that the first two years of a community college be tuition free and the national College Promise Campaign and local, state-based campaigns have been established to further this concept. San Diego Promise was established in 2016 to ensure that no deserving student is denied the opportunity to attend the San Diego Community College District due to lack of resources. Promise pilot program students have higher course completion and retention rates and grade point averages than their non-Promise counterparts. Assembly Bill 19 (Santiago), passed in 2017, has established the California College Promise, a program providing free tuition for one year at California community colleges for qualifying first-time, full-time students. Governor Gavin Newsom has recommended expansion of the California College Promise to include a second year and included funding in  his proposed 2019-20 budget. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports Assembly Bill 2 (Santiago) Community Colleges: California College Promise, which would extend to a second year the efforts implemented by AB 19 to provide first-time, full-time students free tuition. The Board of Education authorizes the Superintendent to provide the Board's resolution of support to members of the California legislature, especially in the legislative delegation from San Diego, urging support for AB 2.

No

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in Support of AB 30 (Holden) Community Colleges: College and Career Access Pathways Partnerships.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The connection between a college degree and economic stability has been effectively documented, making college access and preparation a social and economic justice issue. Across the United States there is a growing emphasis on school and community college collaboration to prepare students to be college and career ready. California should do more to help schools and community colleges improve their college readiness and college enrollment rates. The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports dual enrollment in college courses as a viable and effective method to prepare any student, including those who have struggled academically and who may have had no initial interest in pursuing a postsecondary degree or credential, to complete high school and enter college. Through dual enrollment partnerships school districts and community college districts create clear pathways of aligned, sequenced coursework that allows students to easily and successfully transition to for-credit, college-level coursework leading to an associate degree, transfer to the University of California or the California State University, or a career technical education (CTE) credential or certificate. Since the passage of AB 288 (Holden), which established College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) agreements to facilitate the establishment of concurrent enrollment partnerships, the San Diego Unified School District has seen a significant increase in high school students enrolled in San Diego Community College District classes, and those students upon matriculation to community college are more likely to place at transfer level reading, writing and math and have higher retention and success rates than first-time to college students.  The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports Assembly Bill 30 (Holden) Community Colleges: College and Career Access Pathways partnership, which would extend and streamline the important partnerships implemented by AB 288 dual enrollment programs.

Under Government Code sections 6500 et. seq, the governing board has determined that it is in the best interests of the district to utilize this consortium contract to purchase supplies. BACKGROUND: The California Department of Education (CDE), as a condition of California’s federally-approved, Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) state plan, is required to identify the state’s lowest-achieving five percent of Title I schools for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI). In addition, any high school with a graduation rate below 67 percent must also be identified for CSI. California’s list of schools was released to the public on January 31, 2019.

 

The California School Dashboard is used to identify CSI schools based on color combinations for schools’ overall performance on the state indicators. The specific criteria are:

  • Schools with all red indicators
  • Schools with all red but one indicator of any other color
  • Schools with five or more indicators where a majority are red
  • Schools with all red and orange indicators

District CSI Schools. The list of district-managed schools identified for CSI is provided above, and in the attached graphic (CSI Schools.png). The colors in the graphic reflect how “all” students at each of the schools performed for each of the state indicators. It is important to note that colors on the Dashboard are assigned based on two factors: status and change. As such, two schools with similar status on an indicator (e.g., each had the same chronic absenteeism rate) but with different change values (rate declined, a positive change, and rate increased, a negative change) could result in colors up to two levels apart.  

 

The district must partner with stakeholders, including school principals, and other school leaders, teachers, and parents to locally develop and implement the School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA), including CSI requirements, for each identified school. The SPSAs will follow the district’s normal timeline for development and approval. Further, the district must include in its LCAP a summary of the CSI strategies/activities implemented at all CSI schools as well as the steps taken to monitor and evaluate the effectiveness of the plans.
