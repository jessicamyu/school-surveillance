RECOMMENDATION: Approve the Variable Term Waiver Request (WV1Form) for Designated Subjects teachers so they remain in compliance with Education Code Section 44253.3.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Eighteen Designated Subjects teachers require the waiver while they complete their Specially Designed Academic Instruction in English (SDAIE) training or successfully pass the California Teacher of English Learners (CTEL) exams this academic year allowing them to continue classroom instruction.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: California Education Code Section 44253.1 requires all teachers to have a valid English Learner authorization if assigned to a classroom that has one or more students designated as an English Learner. Because of the Williams Settlement legislation, a review of this Education Code has determined that the need for an English Learner Authorization (ELA) also applies to all Career and Technical Education (CTE) and Junior Reserve Officers Training Corps (JROTC) certificated staff employed by the district. Teachers who hold a bachelor’s degree and have completed an approved teacher preparation program leading to the issuance of a regular education credential or special education credential, but do not hold the English Language Arts (ELA), qualify for an Emergency Cross-Cultural Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to cover them in their assignments while completing the requirements to obtain a Clear CLAD Certificate.

 

Because CTE and JROTC teachers are not required to hold a bachelor’s degree or complete an approved teacher preparation program to be issued a Designated Subjects Career Technical Education Teaching Credential, this sizable group of district teachers does not qualify for the CLAD Permit. However, on March 19, 2008, the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CTC) announced its approval of SB 1292 giving all holders of Designated Subjects Teaching Credentials an option to obtain the ELA upon successful completion of a SDAIE course. Prior to this approval, there was no option available to the holder of a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential to be issued any form of ELA. On September 26, 2008, the San Diego County Office of Education (SDCOE) began the first session of training for qualifying teachers employed in the county.

 

The district has been advised by SDCOE that all Designated Subjects teachers who do not have the ELA must apply for a CLAD Waiver with the CTC in order not to be considered mis-assigned for the 2012-13 academic school year. These waivers will be valid for the 2012-13. All teachers listed are within their preliminary credential and must complete SDAIE as a term of renewal and continued employment with the district.

 

Pursuant to CTC leaflet CL-622 (9/10), Emergency CLAD permits may be reissued to an applicant four times authorizing a maximum of five years of service to complete the requirements for the English Learner/CLAD Certificate. CTC has not determined a similar strategy for reissuing the CLAD Waiver while an applicant waits to be accepted into a CTC-approved SDAIE program to complete the requirements for the Certificate of Completion of Staff Development. Special professional development opportunities have been, and will continue to be, secured for teachers to meet this requirement.

 

The new CTE program standards, which went into effect September 1 , 2010, include training for the instruction of English Learners that is limited to providing SDAIE services. The implementation process has begun and will conclude September 1, 2013. Current Designated Subjects Credential holders will have until July 31, 2013, to complete the ELA.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:   The DAC discussed the Title I ranking options on November 14, 2012 and will review options again at its meeting scheduled on December 19, 2012.


BACKGROUND:  The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources. Key factors that affect the school allocations are:

  • Potential addition of 33 schools to Program Improvement status
  • K-12 student enrollment at district-managed schools decreased by 1,724 students from 114,064 in 2011-12 to 112,340 in 2012-13
  • The number of free and reduced-price meal eligible students has increased over the past several years
  • Impact of the district continuing in Program Improvement

The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). The DAC, at its November 14, 2012 meeting, considered two sample funding scenarios based on example funding amounts (actual funding amounts were not available). The first method replicated the current one used in 2012-13, one with a sliding scale that provides about $430 per student to schools with 99 percent poverty and reduces the per student allocation by about $6 for each point drop in percent poverty; the lowest poverty schools (those at 40 percent) receive $26 per student. The second sample method, based on DAC member request, established a minimum per student allocation (about $150) for the schools at 40 percent poverty and increased the allocation by about $3.50 per student for every point increase in the poverty level. In this second scenario, the highest poverty schools would be funded at about $360 per student. The DAC will again review the Title I Ranking method at its December 19, 2012 meeting.

 

A potential new use of Title I funds has been proposed by staff from the Early Childhood Education Program. The attached proposal includes funding several positions (e.g., nurse, counselor, early childhood teachers) to support the program, at a cost of $1,358,814.

 

For context to inform discussion, two sample funding scenarios for 2013-14 are attached, as well as the scenario adopted for 2012-13. Scenario A reflects a similar approach to that used for 2012-13 and Scenario B reflects the approach suggested by the DAC.

BACKGROUND: The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School believes the establishment of a partnership between the district and the San Diego Education Association (SDEA) to support the recruitment and retention of new teachers would be mutually beneficial to the district and SDEA.
