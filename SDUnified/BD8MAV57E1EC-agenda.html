RECOMMENDATION: Issue proclamation in honor of Reverend George Walker Smith and recognize his extraordinary efforts to lead the way towards equal education for all students in San Diego County and beyond, and hereby proclaim February 28 as Reverend George Walker Smith Day in the San Diego Unified School District.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Reverend George Walker Smith was born in a small town in Alabama on April 28, 2019. In 1953, Reverend Smith attempted to register to vote in Alabama and was denied. Shortly after, Reverend Smith moved to Pennsylvania where he successfully registered to vote. When Reverend Smith arrived in San Diego in 1956, he founded the Golden Hill United Presbyterian Church, which eventually became the Christ United Presbyterian Church of San Diego, where he served as pastor until his retirement in 2000. Soon after moving to San Diego, Reverend Smith became one of the City’s foremost community leaders, and in 1963, he became the first African American to hold political office in San Diego with his election to the San Diego Unified School District’s Board of Education. During Reverend Smith’s four consecutive terms on the San Diego Unified board he served as President and advocated for a change in the district’s employment practices which led to more inclusive staffing and the employment of almost 800 African American and Latino teachers by the end of his fourth term in 1979. With the intervention of the United States Office of Education and the Superior Court, Reverend Smith was able to overcome board opposition and establish the Voluntary Ethnic Enrollment Program (VEEP) and Magnet program to help eliminate racial imbalance in San Diego schools. On a national level, Reverend Smith served as president of the Council of Great City Schools (CGCS) in 1972, and president of the National School Boards Association (NSBA) in 1976. In 1989, Reverend Smith co-founded the Innovative Preschool Project, subsequently establishing the School of Success, now known as the McGill School of Success Charter. Sadly Reverend Smith passed on February 15, 2020, at 91 years old.

1. Update to the District's Lead in Drinking Water Policy; BUDGETED: GENERAL FUND, ROUTINE RESTRICTED MAINTENANCE ACCOUNT AND MEASURE YY FUNDS
Meeting

RECOMMENDATION: Approve a change in the district's lead in drinking water policy.  Approve implementation of the policy.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $400,000.00. Budget Strings: 5686-81500-00-5100-8100-0000-01000-0000 (annual filter maintenance); 9XXX-90504-00-5751-8500-0000-21500-8613 (Hydration Stations install with in-house forces).

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Since April 2017, the district has been determining the lead content in its drinking water and taking steps to reduce the lead content when levels were found to be high.  With more than 2,500 water samples taken and a focus on the highest risk schools identified by City sampling, there are no drinking outlets that currently test above 5 parts per billion (ppb).

 

As the district endeavored to understand the lead in drinking water issue and to ensure students were not being harmed, the district took an aggressive approach to sampling drinking water and established a threshold for lead content of 5 ppb, three times more stringent than the national requirement established by the Environmental Protection Agency.  Additionally, in 2017, the board directed staff to continue to develop a lead in drinking water program, and by 2020, review options to reduce the district's threshold even lower, to 1 ppb.  In response to the board's direction, district staff recommends the following actions:

  1. Provide drinking water at all district sites solely through filtered water outlets.  These filtered outlets will generally provide lead content below 1 ppb -- the district's long term goal.  Installing water filters at the point of use, as close to the water outlet as possible, is the best way to ensure consistent control of lead in drinking water.
     
  2. Restate the district's lead in drinking water policy with filtered outlets achieving 1 ppb, but establishing an action level at 5 ppb to allow for variations in test results and other factors:

      "All drinking water outlets will provide filtered water that is expected to reduce lead in water content to below 1 ppb. Through continued quality assurance sampling of water, any water outlet that tests for lead content above 5 ppb will be immediately removed from service."
     
  3. Replace some drinking fountains with ADA compliant filtered Hydration Stations (combination drinking fountain/bottle filling stations).  Remove remaining drinking fountains from service.  This will ultimately result in fewer drinking water outlets on campus, but will provide water of a higher quality.  In addition, through the use of reusable bottles and the bottle filling stations, water will be more portable and available for students throughout the campus.
     
  4. In concert with this approach, revise the district's lead in drinking water plan to provide confirmation testing after filter installation and to provide random quality assurance testing of filtered outlets to ensure filter performance.  Whole-site testing in advance of new unit and filter installation would no longer by necessary.

Through PPO's efforts of testing and remediation, we have resolved the schools with higher test results and aged infrastructure - those schools of high suspicion for lead in drinking water.  All drinking water outlets at these schools are now at a lead content level of 5 ppb or lower.  Additionally, we have gathered enough information through our testing to determine trends and project future results.  In short, drinking water outlets and nearby plumbing fixtures that have had little use are more likely to show a higher lead concentration.  Removing unused/little used drinking water fountains is a component of the plan moving forward.

 

With installing new filtered Hydration Stations, all non-filtered drinking water outlets will be removed from service, including drinking fountains in elementary classrooms.  Sinks will remain in the classroom for hand washing but the drinking fountain bubbler will be removed.  This action was successfully piloted at Clay Elementary School in January 2020.

 

It should be noted that our water has always been of high quality and sink faucets and non-filtered drinking water outlets can be used in an emergency such as an extended lockdown with no concern for health impacts (although our expectation is that this need will be limited with the students utilizing reusable water bottles).  These sinks have not, and under this plan will not, be tested for lead content.  Site administrators will annually, or more often if needed, educate staff and students to avoid use of sink faucets for drinking water under regular circumstances.  It is at each site administrator's discretion whether to add signage in areas near these sinks (e.g., restrooms) as reminders.

 

This program will result in the removal of existing drinking fountains and the installation of approximately 2,000 drinking Hydration Stations across the district.  Other areas of the school site such as kitchens, nurse's offices, and teachers' lounges will receive water filter installations connected to existing fixtures.  There is an added benefit to installing filters in that the filters will not only remove lead but also many other potential contaminants.

 

The work will be performed by PPO in-house forces.  The work was specifically identified in Measure YY and will be bond-funded.  In addition, Hydration Stations will be incorporated into new building construction and whole-site modernization projects carried out by Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC).  The program is planned to be completed by August 30, 2024.  

 

Until each school is reached, it is possible that other water outlets will continue to have lead levels higher than the district's stringent standards.  The following precautions are being taken to mitigate the risk to students:

  • Schools with the highest chances of lead in water dealt with first.  There is a strong correlation between the number of water outlets with high lead levels found during the five samples-per-school program (completed in 2017) and the number of water outlets found to have high lead levels once all of a school's outlets were tested (2018-2019). As of June 2019, any school with one or more positive results of the five that were sampled, has already been corrected school-wide.  The chance that any water source will have lead in the remaining 155 schools (i.e., schools where all five samples tested negative in 2017) certainly does exist, but it is significantly lower.
  • Flushing.  Flushing water lines is an effective method of eliminating lead that accumulates in water lines after hours and days of non-use.  Flushing is particularly important after long periods of non-use, such as winter and summer breaks. Flushing will be mandatory at all schools until the entire school has been remediated using the program described above.
  • Elementary before secondary.  Lead ingestion damages the health of young children more than it does older children and adults.  This is because younger children have more rapidly growing nervous systems and body organs and because they absorb ingested lead more effectively than older children from their gut.  As such, as this remediation program spreads from cluster to cluster, elementary schools will be remediated before middle schools.  High schools will be last.
  • District has been conservative. In schools where the highest lead levels were found in water outlets, blood testing of students, including young elementary students, has never yet demonstrated a circumstance where the school source of lead had been responsible for high blood lead level.  Yet the district is not only still committed to a comprehensive program of eliminating all lead, but it is adhering to a far stricter standard than the water level published in federal guidelines (<5ppb versus the federal <15ppb).

The annual filter maintenance cost is estimated to be $400,000 and will be an added cost to General Fund/Routine Restricted Maintenance Account.  PPO will also carry out a quality assurance testing program of drinking water outlets into the future to ensure the filters perform as intended and the district's overall lead in drinking water program remains in line with a district objective of 1 ppb.

(Attachment 1, SV-20-0883-99), a federally-qualified health center to provide school-based health care services at Central and Rosa Parks elementary schools. The term of this agreement is September 1, 2019 through August 31, 2022.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Revenue: $2,400.00 annually; Expense: $50.00. Budget Strings: XXXX-04003-00-8699-0000-40003-0000; 5498-04003-00-5302-8200-0000-40003-0000 (San Diego County Recorder’s Office for CEQA NOE filing fee).

BACKGROUND: This Health Center Facility License Agreement will enable San Diego Family Care dba Mid-City Community Clinic, Inc., to continue the services it provides to district students. Currently, there are five other school-based health centers (SBHCs) and one mobile medical unit in the district. SBHCs increase access to health care services to students and their families. Research has shown that SBHCs improve student health outcomes and decrease school absences, drop-outs and behavior problems. SBHCs can provide primary medical care, integrated behavioral health, oral health care, health education and insurance enrollment services.

 

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The proposed license agreement to provide health care services at two district sites (Proposed Project) is a subject to CEQA, however, the project is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines section 15301 – Existing Facilities (Class 1). Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. The Proposed Project involves the execution of a license agreement between San Diego Family Care and the district to continue to operate existing community health clinics on a portion of each of the district owned properties of Central and Rosa Parks elementary schools. No construction or other improvements would be required as part of the license agreement, and no expansion of use is proposed beyond existing operations at each health center. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location: Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact: All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect: A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways: A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites: A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources: A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Analysis: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location: The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school sites. The Proposed Project is consistent with the existing operations on the project site as the health centers have operated at each site since 2010. Furthermore, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact: The Proposed Project would not result in new construction activities or result in a change in existing operations and would not result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect: The Proposed Project includes the execution of a license agreement for an existing use. No new construction or operational activity is proposed on- or off-site that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about existing school site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways: The Proposed Project does not require the construction of new facilities and therefore, would not directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not substantially damage scenic resources within a state scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites: According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources: The Proposed Project does not require the construction of new facilities and therefore, would not result in a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource as described in CEQA Guidelines section 15064.5(b). Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

Approval of this item approves the Proposed Project as described, and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk (Attachment 2), and ratifies the license agreement between the district and San Diego Family Care to provide school-based health care services at Central and Rosa Parks elementary schools.

15303 (e). New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures. 15311. Accessory Structures. Class 11 consists of construction, or placement of minor structures accessory to (appurtenant to) existing commercial, industrial, or institutional facilities.   1. Location. Classes 1, 2, 3, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Generally the surrounding land uses at each site consist of residential housing and the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, school operations at each site would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.

No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about each of the school sites or surrounding area. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because grading and trenching into non-native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.
