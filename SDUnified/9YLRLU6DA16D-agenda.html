1. District Accountability Report on LCAP Goal 3: Quality Leadership, Teaching, and Learning
Meeting
Mar 8, 2016 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board on LCAP Goal 3: Quality Leadership, Teaching, and Learning.

 

LCAP GOAL 3: Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student leaning opportunities and outcomes.

 

Actions and Services:

 

3.3  - District systems to develop instructional capacity

  1. Provide systems of support at the school and district level to enhance instructional practices, professional learning, attendance and success.
  2. Support site-based professional learning through Professional Learning Communities (PLC).
  3. Provide cluster-level support and learning opportunities through Common Core Cluster Support Teachers, English Learner Support Teachers, and other district staff.

3.4 - Develop leadership capacity

  1. Area Superintendents and school leaders participate in a comprehensive professional development plan to support instructional leadership directed at improved teaching and learning in the classroom (e.g., the 5 Dimensions of Teaching and Learning, Learning Cycles, Leadership Practices)
  2. School leaders participate in regular Professional Learning Communities to plan, design lesson, examine student data, and engage in collaborative learning structures and processes.
  3. Supports are provided at multiple levels of leadership to foster positive and collaborative student and staff relationships, positive school climate, and students' academic success.
  4. Develop or identify an appropriate metric to measure quality leadership (such as a Quality Leadership Continuum)

3.5 - Professional development - standards, instruction, and support

  1. Schools develop, monitor and refine a Site Professional Development Plan with a clear emphasis on the successful implementation of the Common Core and ELD state standards and high levels of student achievement
  2. Common Core Support Teachers, English Learner Support Teachers, and EL resource teachers collaborate and work alongside principals, teachers, and school staffs to build capacity around student-centered practices and state Common Core, ELD, Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS), and other content areas.
  3. The content of professional development supports the effective implementation of the ELD state standards and reflects school, district and state priorities including the integration of digital tools and relevant technology platforms.
  4. Provide professional learning for teachers of early learners focused on quality reading instruction and research-based interventions, including differentiated instruction for students in biliteracy and dual language programs.
  5. The Special Education Department, the Office of Leadership and Learning, the Office of Secondary Schools, the Office of Language Acquisition, and Area Superintendents collaborate to support schools in providing quality professional learning and instructional support for students with disabilities and English Learners.
  6. Provide supports and training for special and general education teachers and support staff who teach and support students with special needs, including students with disabilities, English Learners, and gifted and talented students.
  7. Provide relevant professional learning for all support staff to build capacity to efficiently and effectively perform their duties to support student learning.
  8. Provide professional development on laws and topics pertaining to foster youth (e.g., AB 490, graduation requirements, trauma informed care resources).
  9. Provide district staff with needed information, resources, and services to support the education of foster youth.
  10. Develop or identify an appropriate metric to measure quality professional learning (such as a Quality Professional Learning for All Continuum).

Vision 2020, Quality Indicator Destination:  Quality Leadership

Schools have a clear rationale, vision, and high expectations for achievement and immediacy for change in a positive and productive learning environment.  School leaders collaborate with and motivate the school community providing support, sharing leadership, paying attention to the work and magnifying best practices using the appreciative inquiry lens.  Schools know students' learning needs and can recognize if instruction is improving student learning through classroom visits and analyzing data focused on student achievement.  School leaders monitor belief systems and teach the importance of embracing diversity and respecting all cultures.  The school community understands why change is important and collectively acts to ensure that all students have a quality education.  Leaders positively influence the attitudes of students, staff, parents, and the community in setting high expectations for all students.  Leaders bring systemic change towards a culture that embraces diversity, respects all cultures, and ensures the development and implementation of educational programs that maximize academic achievement of all students.  Leaders are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.

 

Vision 2020, Quality Indicator Destination:  Quality Teaching

Effective teaching requires careful crafting of learning communities built on trust and respect, as well as routines, expectations, resources, and strategies that support students' intellectual engagement in learning.  Effective teaching happens through thoughtful planning focused on defined outcomes for student learning and leveraging of teachable moments.  Effective teaching is revealed in the ways in which teachers respond to the strengths and needs of individual students while engaging and supporting all students in their learning.  Effective teaching is a form of inquiry wherein teachers use evidence and analysis of students' strengths and struggles, and their own performance, to guide their practice in support of student learning.  Effective teaching relies on engagement within a professional learning community.  Teachers in California have a professional responsibility to provide students with safe and caring learning environments, where students' differences are celebrated and supported, and they acquire the knowledge, skills, strategies, and concepts they will need for successful participation in an increasingly technological and global society.  Teachers are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity. 

 

Vision 2020, Quality Indicator Destination:  Professional Learning

Schools hold professional learning among their top priorities for the school community.  They recognize that universal high expectations for all students require ambitious and continual improvements in curriculum, instruction, assessment, leadership practices and support systems.  These improvements require effective professional learning to expand educators' knowledge, skills, practices and dispositions.  Schools demand effective professional learning focused on substantive results for themselves, their colleagues, and their students.  Schools artfully combine deep understanding of and cultural responsiveness to the community they serve with high expectations and support for adult learning in order to achieve school and school system goals.  They embed professional learning into the organization's vision by communicating that it is a core function for improvement and by establishing and maintaining a public and persistent focus on educator professional learning.

 

BACKGROUND:  Each month, Superintendent Marten provides a District Accountability Report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services, and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's District Accountability Report each month, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report, and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation and suggested next steps.  The complete LCAP document is available at www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

Action, Second Reading

Board Trustees John Lee Evans and Richard Barrera will discuss Board Governance Policies, specifically the Governance Culture (GC) and Board/Superintendent Relationship (B/SR) policies.  Trustees Evans and Barrera will propose additions to the Governance Culture policy statements.  The board considered these proposed changes at the February 23, 2016 meeting as a first reading.  Board consideration, discussion, and action is proposed for the March 8, 2106 meeting.

 

Board Governance Policy B/SR-1, Single Point of Direction, remains unchanged and is in effect as follows: “The superintendent is the board's sole point of direction to the operational organization.  The board will set the direction for the operational organization only through the superintendent, functioning as the Chief Executive Officer.  However, nothing herein shall prohibit a constructive, two-way dialogue with students, staff, parents, and the community as a means to engage all stakeholders in the work of the board and the district.”

 

The suggested revision to Board Governance Policy GC-7, Board Members' Code of Conduct, includes language to address a future circumstance where a new member may have a student currently attending a district school.  The revision to GC-8, Board Member Conflict of Interest, creates a new requirement for all board members to participate in a training session on conflict of interest laws and ethics whenever a new board member is seated.

 

More expansive revisions are suggested for GC-9, Process for Addressing Board Member Violations.  The revisions would create additional responsibilities for the superintendent in the event that a board member attempts to direct district staff on an operational matter or displays unprofessional behavior to a staff member.

BOARD/STAFF INTERACTION

 

 Violation:

 

A board member attempts to direct staff on an operational matter or displays unprofessional behavior to a staff member

 

 Action:

  1. Superintendent will discuss the problem with the board member and the staff member(s) and will issue a memorandum to all board members reporting the issue.
  2. If the matter is not satisfactorily resolved or if the offending behavior recurs, the superintendent will place an item on an open board meeting agenda for discussion by the full board with the superintendent.
  3. The superintendent shall establish and maintain a procedure for employees to report the above violations in a safe, non-retaliatory manner, so that she/he may report alleged violations to the full board.

CONFLICT OF INTEREST LAWS AND ETHICS

 

Within 60 days of seating a new member on the Board of Education, the full board will participate in a training session, to be presented by experts, on conflict of interest laws and ethics.

 

DUAL ROLE AS TRUSTEE AND PARENT OF STUDENT(S)

 

Within 60 days of seating a member on the Board of Education, who also has a child who attends a San Diego Unified School District school, the superintendent and staff of the school will meet with the member to address the manner in which all parties will discuss the means to ensure that the role as a parent and the role as a board member are clearly distinguished

BACKGROUND: On December 18, 2015, U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) Secretary Julián Castro and U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) Secretary Tom Vilsack announced that communities could apply to be designated as a Promise Zone under the third and final round competition. Promise Zones are high poverty communities where the federal government partners with local leaders to increase economic activity, improve educational opportunities, leverage private investment, reduce violent crime, enhance public health, and address other priorities identified by the community. Through the Promise Zone designation, communities work directly with federal, state, and local agencies to give local leaders proven tools to improve the quality of life in some of the country’s most vulnerable areas.

 

District staff has collaborated with other partners in supporting development of the application by the lead agency, the City of San Diego. This collaboration was similar to that for a round two submission that was unsuccessful. A new requirement of HUD, this round is for partners to submit a preliminary MOU.

 

It is important to understand that the Promise Zone designation is not a grant. However, those partners involved in communities with Promise Zone designations are awarded extra points on federal grant applications and those points could result in more successful applications. In addition, all Promise Zones receive priority access to federal investments that further their strategic plans, federal staff on the ground to help them implement their goals, and five, full-time AmeriCorps VISTA members to recruit and manage volunteers and strengthen the capacity of the Promise Zone initiatives.

 

Promise Zones must be contiguous, geographic areas with populations between 10,000 and 200,000 residents with a poverty rate above 32.5 percent. The proposed Promise Zone for this application (map attached) has been condensed compared to the one in district's unsuccessful round two application; feedback from the prior round indicated that the poverty rate was too low compared to other applicants and thus, the boundaries were drawn to maximize competitive advantage.

 

The activities and metrics included in the Promise Zone goal to improve educational opportunities are aligned with those delineated in the district Local Control and Accountability Plan. The Promise Zone application recognizes that we must collaborate with many community partners to achieve educational goals; community health, safety, and employment all contribute to improved educational outcomes. If San Diego Unified’s Promise Zone application is successful, district will then enter into a final, binding MOU that defines the role and responsibilities to achieve the goals set forth.
