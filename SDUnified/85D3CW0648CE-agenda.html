2. Plans for Small High Schools--San Diego and Crawford Educational Complexes
Meeting
Jun 8, 2010 - Regular Meeting
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve new operational plans for the San Diego and Crawford Educational Complexes as developed by the school leadership, faculty, staff, and parent community with the goal of refocusing educational programs to increase student achievement and creating operational efficiencies to achieve budget savings of $295,728. 


FISCAL IMPACT: On February 23, 2010, the board approved targeted savings of $295,728 from the consolidation of ten small high schools at the San Diego and Crawford Educational Complexes. On March 9, 2010, the board clarified its position and approved targeted savings of $295,728 from new operational plans to be developed by each educational complex.  This recommendation retains that projected budget savings be included in budget projections for the 2010-11 budget.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The two educational complexes have developed new operational plans.  Additional plans have been developed by district staff.  Some plans include staffing adjustments.

BACKGROUND:  On June 16, 2009, the board discussed the district’s small high schools. Topics discussed by individual board members included:

  • Refrain from making decisions about structure of small high schools for the 2009-2010 academic year.
  • Permit minor administrative adjustments that do not impact the integrity of the school.
  • Avoid pitting small high schools against each other.
  • Improve the quality of unsuccessful small high schools.
  • Leave successful small high schools alone.
  • Consider converting/combining small high schools; for example moving from four schools to three or two on a complex.

The board reached consensus and offered direction to the superintendent to critically examine small high schools without making drastic changes for the 2009-2010 academic year.

 

Following the Budget Workshop on February 9, 2010, district staff were directed to brainstorm ideas for small high school consolidation. On February 18 and 22, 2010, the Interim Superintendent and district staff met with the principals of the San Diego and Crawford Educational Complexes, respectively, to discuss the idea of each community developing a plan to address each Educational Complex’s academic performance needs and effective/efficient operations. 


On March 3 and 15, 2010, respectively, Board Members Barrera and Jackson, together with the Interim Superintendent and district staff, held meetings with the faculty, staff, and administration of the Crawford and San Diego Educational Complexes.  The goal of these meetings was to articulate the board’s intent that each complex would be charged with developing a new operational plan to achieve budget savings of $295,728 (i.e., $29,572.80 per small high school) and strengthen overall focus on increasing student achievement levels.

 

The San Diego and Crawford Educational Complexes established project teams and sub-teams to examine each small high school at the educational complex utilizing various data sources to determine options that are more efficient and/or more effective; consider best instructional practices to inform possible scenarios; retool/revamp bell schedules, support services, administrative structures, course offerings, etc. and explore other school elements/ideas to improve student engagement, performance, and achievement.  Each project team was asked to research, design, and develop at least one plan/scenario in response to the board’s direction to develop strategies for improving campus-wide academic performance while realizing $295,728 in savings.  Additionally, the Office of Small School Innovation launched www.sandi.net/smallhighschoolsas a webspace to warehouse key information about the work of each educational complex and as a means of promoting transparency and openness with the process.  The webspace includes a review of board discussions on small high schools, surveys and results, artifacts from meetings, and research.

 

On Tuesday, May 25, 2010, the board reviewed a draft version of restructuring phase-in plan for the schools at the San Diego and Crawford High Educational Complexes.  These plans are attached.  Based on discussions during the board meeting, school representatives were asked to:

 

1. Revisit their plan and development goals and benchmarks to measure achievement gains as a result of the new implementation strategies.

2. Determine a reduction in funding the schools could absorb or formulate a plan to generate new funding sources to replace funds eliminated or reduced by the district.

 

Responses to the board's requests are attached.  The expectation is that these plan and growth trends will be reviewed annually by the board to monitor progress being made.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve the continued use of selected district assessments in 2010-11, including benchmark exams in English, language arts, and mathematics in Grades 1-8; end-of-course exams in Grades 6-12; and Writing and Reading Assessment Profile (WRAP) or Developmental Reading Assessment (DRA) in Grades K-2.  This recommendation will provide the district with the continued ability to support the measurement of student achievement as identified in the Goals for Student Achievement (GSA), Operational Expectations (OE), the Local Educational Agency (LEA) Plan, Single Plans for Student Achievement (SPSA), School Assistance and Intervention Team (SAIT) Progress Reporting, and the district's Promotion/Retention Plan.  


FISCAL IMPACT:  The estimated cost for the continued use of selected district assessments in the 2010-11 school year is $480,000.00, General Fund unrestricted.


PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  In 2009-10, the district budgeted $1,178,604 for district assessments.


IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  It is estimated that 3.0 FTE will be required to continue the use of selected district assessments in the 2010-11 school year.


BACKGROUND:  A number of district assessments are used to inform student course placements, monitor student growth, determine need for intervention programs, and help teachers adjust their instruction according to student need.  Among these are benchmark exams that are district-developed, standards-based assessments in the content areas of English, language arts, mathematics, and science.  Schools assess students in English, language arts, and mathematics at Grades 1-8, and in algebra and geometry three times during the school year to determine students' understanding of content taught during each interval of instruction.  Science benchmark exams are also administered at Grades 3, 4, and 5.  Benchmark assessments provide teachers with information about student performance in relation to standards and are used to inform instructional and programmatic decisions.  End-of-course exams are also district-developed, standards-based assessments that are intended to serve some of the same purposes as benchmark exams.  Other district assessments, such as the Writing and Reading Assessment Profile (WRAP), Gates-MacGinitie Reading Test, and Practice California High School Exit Exam (CAHSEE) are used as screening and diagnostic tools to inform student program placement and interventions appropriate for individual students or groups of students.


On February 23, 2010, as part of the budget reduction strategy, the board approved the elimination of  all central office funding for non-mandated assessments in the 2010-11 school year.  This action eliminated funding for test booklets, materials, processing of non-mandated assessments, and 5.0 FTE from the Assessment Services Department.  The total amount of the reduction was $1,178,604.

RECOMMENDATION:  Authorize continued participation in the Distinguished Teacher in Residence (DTiR) Program with California State University San Marcos (CSUSM) through June 30, 2012.  The district entered into a Distinguished Teacher in Residence Agreement with CSUSM on July 1, 2007.  A district teacher has been selected to participate as Distinguished Teacher in Residence from July 1, 2010 through June 30, 2012.  The district will discontinue participation in the program at the end of the teacher's service. 


FISCAL IMPACT:  Per the Agreement, the district contributes $5,500 per year to participate in the Distinguished Teacher in Residence consortium.  During the years that a district teacher serves as Distinguished Teacher in Residence, the district also pays for the teacher's salary and benefits while on-loan to the university.  The university reimburses the district $45,000 per year for each year of the teacher's service.  The approximate cost for a district teacher to participate in the program during the 2010-12 two-year period is $115,330. 


PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  $5,500 (No district teachers participated in the program during the 2009-10 school year - the most recent district teacher to participate in the program served from July 1, 2007-June 30, 2009).


IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  During the time that a district teacher serves as Distinguished Teacher in Residence, the impact to district staffing is that a teacher is on-loan to the university and is not teaching in a district classroom.  (A district teacher does not serve every year, but is eligible on a rotating basis with other districts in the DTiR consortium).


BACKGROUND:    The district has participated in the CSUSM Distinguished Teacher in Residence (DTiR) Program since 2000 as part of a consortium comprised of 12 public school districts in San Diego County (currently Carlsbad Unified, Encinitas Union, Escondido Union, Escondido Union HS, Oceanside Unified, Poway Unified, Ramona Unified, San Diego Unified, San Marcos Unified, Solana Beach, Temecula Valley Unified, and Vista Unified).  The DTiR position carries with it a high level of prestige and affords the district the opportunity to be directly involved with university teacher preparation programs at the local level.

 

The DTiR Program evolved out of a mutual desire for school districts to be directly involved in teacher preparation at the university and provides a critical link between College of Education faculty and the public school districts that hire the university's students upon completion of their teacher preparation programs.  DTiRs share their classroom teaching experience with a wide spectrum of pre-service teachers in a variety of teacher credential program courses.  DTiRs teach university courses appropriate to their area of specialization, supervise student teachers, advise teacher preparation students, and participate in college governance and on College of Education committees.  Opportunities are provided to team teach, provide guest lectures in specialized content areas, conduct research and facilitate student teaching seminars.  DTiRs may conduct applied research with university faculty members, and are encouraged to incorporate such research activities into their own professional growth plans.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Agreement CS-90-1010-29 with KFP Construction, Inc. dba Fulton Enterprises to furnish and install chiller at Ibarra Elementary School. Agreement effective June 9, 2010 through September 7, 2010.


FISCAL IMPACT: Twelve bids for this project were received on April 29, 2010, ranging in costs from $603,129 to $911,280.  Bids were received from KPF Construction, Inc. dba Folton Enterprises, All Star Services Corporation, Fordyce Construction, Inc., M.A. Stevens Construction, Inc., Riverside Construction Company, Inc., Alpha Mechanical Heating & Air Conditioning, Inc., Ahrens Corporation, AMISI Corporation dba American Industrial Services, University Mechanical & Engineering Contractors, Inc., Jackson & Blanc, Accurate Engineering Integrated Construction Services, Inc. and A.O. Reed & Company.  KFP Construction, Inc. dba Fulton Enterprises has been determined to be the low responsive and responsible bidder with a bid of $603,129, and the district’s acceptance of additive alternate 1 for test and balancing of the existing air system at a cost of $35,200, for a total cost of $638,329, which is within the project’s budget.  Budget string: 9150-02130-00-6200-8500-0000-21300-8610


PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.


IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The project does not require district personnel to perform services.  Services will be performed by KFP Construction, Inc. dba Fulton Enterprises.


BACKGROUND: This project is to provide a complete central plant chilled water and piping distribution system from the central mechanical plant to all existing air handling units. Included in the scope of work is chilled water piping inside each of the existing air handling unit rooms, performing air balancing for all existing HVAC systems, performing chilled water system hydronic balancing, and providing required plumbing such as make-up water to the chiller plant and condensate drain piping.


Per Public Contract Code Section 20111 (b), the district must publicly bid any public project work that exceeds $15,000.  This project also exceeds the limits for the in-house force account set forth in Public Contract Code Section 20114.
