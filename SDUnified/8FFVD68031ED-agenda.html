10. Report on Disposition of Certain Excess Real Properties; BUDGETED: N/A (OE-10)
Meeting

RECOMMENDATION: Board direction requested on the disposition of certain excess real properties.

 

Staff is requesting board direction on whether to continue with the proposal to sell properties as laid out in the plan submitted to the County Office of Education, or to instead investigate the potential for long-term revenue generation.

  • Barnard Remnant Parcel
  • Mission Beach
  • Bayview Terraces 11

FISCAL IMPACT: Potential addition of approximately $20 million if properties sold and valuation realized; unknown if board directs an alternative course of action.  Unknown cost of relocation of staff at Mission Beach site if property sold.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: Estimated maintenance expenses and unused site fees of approximately $152,000 per year.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: If properties sold, staff at the Mission Beach site would need to be relocated.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The district Advisory Committee on the Use of Excess School Property (ACUESP) has been informed of the options contained in this report.

 

BACKGROUND: As part of its budget planning, the board was required to submit a balanced budget for 2011-12, to the County Office of Education by June 30, 2011, and in addition, proposals for balancing the budget in 2012-13.  One item on the list of proposals included the possibility of selling district, real estate assets to the value of $21 million.  This brief report is an update on district activity since the end of 2010-11, and asks for board direction as to whether to proceed with further sales at this point.

 

Activity to Date:  Following board direction, district staff has successfully sold two properties.  Board approval of the final purchase agreements for these two properties is part of the October 11, 2011 agenda:

  • Item I.3. Fairbrook Property (bid of $4,400,000 received from Ryland Homes, $1,050,000 above the valuation of the property)
  • Item I.4. Armstrong Street Property (to the San Diego Community College):  $1,600,000.

The $6,000,000 raised from the sale of these properties could be available as part of the district’s plan to meet its projected budget shortfall for 2012-13, as transmitted to the County Office of Education in June 2011; there has also been some discussion about whether it should be used to meet a potential 2011-12 mid-year budget cut, as may be required by the State of California. 

 

Other Candidates for Sale:  In May 2011, the board received a report on the district’s excess school properties from consultant Jones, Lang LaSalle, which provided estimated valuations and ranked the properties in terms of ease of disposition.  The three most ‘disposable’ properties are:

  • Barnard Remnant Parcel ($1,600,000 - $2,200,000)
  • Mission Beach ($11,000,000 - $12,000,000)
  • Bayview Terraces 11 ($1,500,000 - $2,250,000)

Inclusive of the $6,000,000 about to be realized with the sale of the Armstrong and Fairbrook properties, sale of these properties could raise approximately $20,100,000 to $22,450,000, if the estimated valuations were realized.

 

While the sale of these properties would assist in the district’s efforts to balance its 2012-13 budget (or meet the potential need for mid-year 2011-12 cuts), the sale of these properties would mean that these assets would no longer be available to the district for potential annual revenue generation through, for example, offering the properties for redevelopment through a long-term lease.  If the properties are not sold, further cuts to district operations and programs would instead be necessary in order to balance the budget.

 

District staff is requesting board direction on whether to continue with the proposal to sell these properties, as laid out in the plan submitted to the County Office of Education, or to instead investigate the potential for long-term revenue generation.

RECOMMENDATION: Direct staff to solicit Requests for Information (RFI) on the potential redevelopment of the Normal Street property (Education Center) as a precursor to developing a Request for Proposals (RFP) that would include board selected options collected as a result of the RFI process.
 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.  The RFI process is intended to solicit ideas, concepts and strategies for the creation of a multi-use strategy for the existing Education Center property (excluding Birney Elementary).  It is believed that developers, real estate consultants and management groups would respond to the RFI in the hope that their concepts would be selected and become the basis for a formal RFP as a second phase. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The district Advisory Committee on the Utilization of Excess School Property (ACUESP) has been informed of the intention to begin the process of soliciting input re the potential redevelopment of the Normal Street site.

 

BACKGROUND: At the June 14, 2011 meeting, the board directed staff to develop an RFP for the joint development of the Normal Street site.  Staff began this process, but was shortly afterward directed to suspend work while discussions occurred with City officials and other parties.  Subsequently, it was determined that the first step of what is likely to be a complex real estate transaction would be to request RFIs on concepts and strategies for how to promote multiple uses of the Normal Street site, including the ability to rebuild an Education Center from the revenues generated by other uses on the site.  It is proposed that RFI submittals would be reviewed by a committee to be composed as the board directs, and that the most feasible options would then be presented to the board for their direction and the subsequent creation of an RFP based on that direction. 

 

Meetings held to this point with city officials and planners indicate that there is initial support for such an effort.  Community engagement would need to be conducted as part of the overall development effort.  It is believed that the actual development, community engagement, and all planning could be conducted at no cost to the district.  Multi-use such as retail, housing, and an educational center have been considered.  The current Education Center has great need for upgrades and American Disabilities Act (ADA) compliance work.  The district does not have the funds to pursue this work and Proposition S funds are earmarked for schools.  Therefore, some type of development funded revenue must be used to overcome these obstacles.  It is also believed that a revenue stream for the district could be created from on-going utilization of the space.  The existing historical building, Annex 1 would be utilized as is currently planned, for a library space.  The neighboring space at the Department of Motor Vehicles center is currently planned for similar joint use development. 

 

If the board authorizes staff to proceed with an RFI, real estate and strategic sourcing staff will work together to create the same and advertise it in a timely manner.

14. Direction to Solicit Requests for Interest (RFI) - Overall Real Estate Management; BUDGETED: YES, PROPERTY MANAGEMENT FUNDS (OE-9)

RECOMMENDATION: Direct staff to solicit Requests for Information (RFI) on the overall management of the district’s real estate portfolio.
 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.  The RFI process is intended to solicit concepts from developers, real estate management firms and other similar organizations that may ultimately lead to a Board decision to authorize an RFP for overall management of the school district real estate portfolio.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The district owns hundreds of properties valued at millions of dollars throughout the City of San Diego, all unencumbered.  The overwhelming majority of these is currently, and will continue to be, essential to the district’s primary mission of delivering a quality education to district students.  However, as the potential for school closure rises, the disposition of existing excess properties is re-examined, and district financial resources continue to decline, there is interest in developing a long-term strategy to maximize revenue from sale of or investment in district properties.  With the decline of state resources, the district must develop its own strategies to overcome the deficit. 

 

Even after the reorganization of the district real estate group, there will be insufficient staff capacity and expertise to develop the strategies for managing the best and highest use of the district’s real estate portfolio.  Staff is therefore requesting approval to solicit RFIs from providers of real estate services that would offer strategies, ideas and plans on best practices of portfolio management, which could include concepts such as consultancy or a trust to manage assets and make recommendations.  Staff believes there are firms who would respond with such strategies at no cost to the district.  Such strategies could include contracting with a single or multiple developers, real estate managers or consultants to provide overall direction and counsel on the best and highest use of all district properties, with a goal towards maximizing revenue over time instead of simple sale of assets. 

 

The RFI process would therefore, be at no cost to the district.  A committee whose composition would be determined by the board, but which would include the participation of the district’s Advisory Committee on the Utilization of Excess Property (ACUESP), would review responses and make recommendations to the board.  Interested parties who provide input could then be eligible for any subsequent RFP the district may issue pertaining to this overall real estate management of the district’s hundreds of properties.

 

Staff recommends the approval of an RFI as stated and developed con-jointly by the real estate group and strategic sourcing.
