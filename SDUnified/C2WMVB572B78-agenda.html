PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Assembly Bill No. 361 (“AB 361”) was enacted by the California Legislature and is intended to “improve and enhance public access to . . . local agency meetings during the COVID-19 pandemic and future applicable emergencies, by allowing broader access through teleconferencing options.” AB 361 permits local legislative bodies subject to the Brown Act and/or the Badgley Keene Act to hold virtual meetings, without needing to publish the physical locations of Board members or make those locations open to the public, if there is a proclaimed state of emergency (pursuant to Section 8625 of the California Emergency Services Act) and either: (i) state or local officials have imposed or recommended measures to promote social distancing, or (ii) there is a finding by the local legislative body that, as a result of the emergency, meeting in person “would present imminent risks to the health or safety of attendees.” Governor has proclaimed a state of emergency pursuant to Section 8625 of the California Emergency Services Act. Since the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, in compliance with public health orders and for the protection all involved persons, the Board of Education (“Board”) has held its meetings telephonically via Zoom and provided for public comments to be provided via teleconferencing rather than allowing in-person attendance by the public at the Education Center. The COVID-19 pandemic continues to present an imminent health risk to the public which require certain specific health and safety measures be maintained. The District’s central office facilities present specific challenges in maintaining COVID-19-related safety protocols required for the health and safety of attendees, which include, but are not limited to, adequate ventilation and security, enforcement of masking requirements, and maintenance of recommended social distancing. The health and safety risks are presented by providing public access at the remote locations from which Board members currently attend Board meetings remotely. The Board hereby finds that conducting Board and/or committee meetings in person in light of the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic presents an imminent risk to the health and safety of prospective attendees.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of recognition and honor of Jennifer Madai Moreno. The Board of Education urges the US Senate to pass and for the President to sign H.R. 3665 creating the Jennifer Moreno Department of Veterans Affairs Medical Center in San Diego and recognizing the legacy of an extraordinary San Diegan and graduate of San Diego Unified.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Jennifer Madai Moreno was born in San Diego and raised in Logan Heights by her single mother after the early loss of her father. Jennifer Moreno attended San Diego High School from 2002 through 2006 and was an active member of the San Diego High School Junior Reserve Officer Training Corps (JROTC), which is a Department of the Army Honor Unit with Distinction, the highest rating by the Army. Moreno was also chosen to be a member of the San Diego Unified School District Brigade Staff and became the top junior marksman in California through the Civilian Marksmanship Program. Lars Staack, Army lieutenant colonel, retired, heads the junior ROTC program at San Diego High School and says his students still recognize the contributions made by Moreno, who he recalls as “an exceptional student, an exceptional cadet, an exceptional Army officer and an exceptional person. She is really an inspiration for all of us.” Moreno attended the University of San Francisco on an ROTC scholarship, where she studied nursing, becoming the first person in her family to go to college, being chosen there for Leadership Development Training and eventually achieving the highest level of physical fitness in her ROTC unit before graduating with a Bachelor of Science degree in nursing in 2010. Moreno received her commission in the U.S. Army as a second lieutenant Nurse Corps officer, served as a clinical staff nurse in the medical-surgical unit and earned her certification as a medical surgical registered nurse before she volunteered for and was selected into the Special Operations Command Cultural Support Team program. Moreno was deployed to Afghanistan in June 2013 with the Army’s 75th Ranger Regiment, where she was attached to a joint special operations task force as a Cultural Support Team member, tasked with outreach to the Afghan women they encountered. Three months into her first tour, on October 6, 2013, Captain Moreno was attached to a U.S. Army Ranger unit on a night mission in the Zhari district in Kandahar province to capture a high-value Taliban target when four explosive devices were triggered, and Captain Moreno was killed trying to render aid to a fellow soldier. Moreno was awarded a Combat Action Badge, the Purple Heart, and the Bronze Star with a “V” device for valor and promoted to captain posthumously for her heroic acts and unwavering support of her comrades. At 25 years old, Moreno became the first nurse Combat Support Team member to die in action and the first combat casualty to be buried at Fort Rosecrans National Cemetery in San Diego since the post-9/11 conflicts began. The enduring legacy of Captain Moreno is visible in the number of young women coming from medical fields seeking out voluntary assignments to join the Cultural Support Team program. Today marks the 80th anniversary of the attack on Pearl Harbor and has been set aside by Congress to remember the 2,403 service members and civilians who were killed during that attack, as well as the valor and legacy of all who served. Hundreds of thousands of women served in World War I and World War II and women service members now make up 20 percent of military personnel, yet only two of the Veteran Administration’s 1,293 healthcare facilities have been named for female veterans. On November 15, 2021, the US House of Representatives unanimously passed legislation from Congressman Mike Levin and co-sponsored by the entire California delegation to designate the medical center of the Department of Veterans Affairs in San Diego, California, as the Jennifer Moreno Department of Veterans Affairs Medical Center.
