RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution recognizing September 2015, as Attendance Awareness Month. The Superintendent and other appropriate staff will continue to provide assistance to raise public awareness on the importance of improved attendance.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes the connection between school attendance and academic achievement.

 

The district also recognizes the importance of working together with schools, families and communities to promote a positive culture of daily attendance for all students and significantly reduce chronic absenteeism by addressing barriers that keep children from getting to school.

 

Improving school climate, reducing instruction missed due to discipline issues, and implementing school wide positive behavior intervention and support systems and restorative justice practices can have a significant impact on improving attendance.

 

Schools and school districts must do more to track, calculate, and share data on how many and which pupils are chronically absent so that schools and communities can work to deliver the right interventions for the right pupils.

 

The district’s primary source of revenue is based on average daily attendance (ADA). The district loses $44.77 per day for every absent student.

 

Given the ongoing budget crisis, it is crucial to note that a 1 percent increase in attendance would translate to an additional $6 million in revenue for the district.

 

San Diego Unified is committed to monitoring and addressing attendance at the first signs of chronic absence.

RECOMMENDATION: Receive Year 1 Report from Strategic Community Consulting, UCSD School of International Relations and Pacific Studies, on the Youth Opportunity Pass (YOP) Pilot Program, review program implementation to date, and approve the following recommendations for the 2015-16 school year:

  1. Continue to provide bus passes to the students that received them during the previous school year who are still enrolled at the four participating schools (Crawford, Hoover, Lincoln and San Diego high schools).  Existing funds in the district's account at MTS allow for provision of passes through the months of July, August, and September 2015 to the 443 students who are still enrolled.  In fall 2015, an Amendment to the district's existing agreement with MTS (GD-16-0185-20) will be brought forward for board approval to purchase passes for the remaining qualifying students for the months of October 2015 through June 2016. (Funding in the amount of $200,000 for this program, including the cost of bus passes, is included in the district's 2015-16 adopted budget.)
  2. From the budgeted $200,000, provide funding to the four sites as needed for clerical support of the program.  (Funding in the amount of $200,000 for this program, including the cost of clerical support, is included in the district's 2015-16 adopted budget.) 
  3. Authorize the district to participate in a proposed study on transit equity being conducted by UC Davis and endorsed by The California Endowment in which the district would provide attendance and achievement data.
  4. Support Mid-City CAN's continued advocacy in state and local level policy changes that call for making public transit available at no cost for youth.

FISCAL IMPACT: Not to Exceed $200,000 for continuation of this program in the 2015-16 school year, including the cost of bus passes and clerical support. Funding is included in the adopted 2015-16 district budget.  Budget string: 5638-00027-00-5875-1000-1110-01000-0000. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $200,000 in 2014-15, and $150,000 in 2013-14.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: The program is implemented by existing district staff (Dropout Prevention Office, school site staff at Crawford, Hoover, Lincoln, and San Diego high schools) 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  On April 9, 2013, the board entered into a partnership with the City of San Diego and Mid-City Community Advocacy Network (CAN) to develop a Youth Opportunity Pass (YOP) pilot program that would provide no cost bus passes to up to 1,000 students at Crawford, Hoover, Lincoln and San Diego high schools during the 2013-14 school year.  The passes were to be available to support school attendance for students with greatest need or students in crisis who were not eligible for district transportation services. The program was designed to provide positive opportunities for transit-dependent youth in San Diego by encouraging and incentivizing regular school attendance, providing a safe, reliable method of transit to and from school, and increasing student access to extracurricular and job opportunities, while investing in future bus ridership.  The program was to be evaluated regarding the impact on school attendance and student achievement.

 

In the 2013-14 school year, the district provided $150,000 and the City of San Diego provided $200,000 toward the implementation of the pilot program, for a total of $350,000.  The funding was used to purchase 1,000 Compass Cards to for the period of November 2013 through June 2014, for program outreach and enrollment through Mid-City CAN, and for program evaluation through the UCSD School of International Relations and Pacific Studies.  Approximately 850 passes were issued to students during the eight-month period of November 2013 through June 2014.  Since 1,000 passes had already been purchased but only 850 were activated, MTS allowed the district to use its remaining balance to re-load passes during the months of July and August 2014, thus providing participating students with continued access to public transportation during the summer break.

 

In the 2014-15 school year, the district provided $200,000 to continue the program, so that a full year of data could be collected.  The funding was used to provide passes to the students that had received them during the previous school year who were still enrolled at the four schools, and for continued outreach through Mid-City CAN. Approximately 550 students of the original 850 were eligible for continued participation.  

 

The County of San Diego, via the Office of Supervisor Ron Roberts, contributed $9,000 to MTS for the program for the 2014-2015 school year.  The City of San Diego, via the Offices of Councilmembers Gloria, Emerald and Alvarez, contributed $9,000 to the program for the 2015-2016 school year.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Since early 2000, a UC San Diego team based in the Department of Economics has worked collaboratively with San Diego Unified to identify timely and relevant policy questions of interest to teachers, administrators, and parents in San Diego Unified, and also to the statewide and national education research communities. Through a series of books, academic papers, research summaries, and briefings, the team has explored determinants of achievement in the district, the impact of literacy reforms, the multifaceted forms of school choice in San Diego, how the California High School Exit Examination has affected students, the impact of San Diego’s Career and Technical Education (CTE) program, the effects of the use of the Mathematics Diagnostic Testing Project in the district, San Diego Unified’s new “college prep for all” graduation policy, and the progress of district English Learners. Several ongoing research projects focus on the high school and postsecondary impact of school choice, students’ academic trajectories and policies to narrow achievement gaps in San Diego, and charter school effects on long-term outcomes. In June 2013, San Diego Unified and UC San Diego were awarded a partnership grant by the U.S. Department of Education’s Institute of Education Sciences (IES). And, in June 2015, the partnership was awarded a second IES grant, this time in the Continuous Improvement Research in Education grants program.    

 

The early work at UC San Diego was led by Julian Betts, Professor in the Department of Economics, and Andrew Zau, Senior Statistician. They, working with doctoral candidates and alumni of the doctoral program, built strong and collaborative relationships with numerous San Diego Unified central office departments and staff. In December 2009, Karen Volz Bachofer, former Executive Director of San Diego Unified’s Research and Evaluation Division joined the team at UC San Diego. In May 2010, the first SanDERA MOA was finalized and a SanDERA Executive Committee was formed. Standing Executive Committee members from UC San Diego are Julian Betts, SanDERA Executive Director, Andrew Zau, SanDERA Senior Statistician, and Karen Bachofer, SanDERA Director; SDUSD members are Ron Rode, Director of the Office of Research and Development, Peter Bell, Director of Data Analysis and Reporting, and Dina Policar, Director of Instructional Data Support. SanDERA has established a twelve-member Board of Advisors that meets twice yearly.

 

The SanDERA partnership is built upon a foundation of trust, detailed attention to data quality and security, a common desire to engage in academic research that will impact the quality of public education in San Diego and beyond, and significant time commitment of skilled researchers and administrators at both San Diego Unified and UC San Diego. Contributing to the success of the partnership has been a “no surprises” policy – SanDERA does not initiate new research before consulting with San Diego Unified officials and obtaining formal consent, and district officials are briefed on research findings before publication or presentation at conferences or any other public forums for the purposes of fact-checking and providing “critical friend” comments. At the same time, it should be noted that San Diego Unified maintains an arm’s length relationship with SanDERA when it comes to decisions to publish and to publicize results. Thus, the district provides frequent input, including valuable insights that regularly inform the research findings, but it does not influence conclusions drawn by the researchers.  A final factor key to the success of the relationship has been a willingness on the part of the UC San Diego researchers to take the time to learn the intricacies of district data, district programs, and district academic policies and procedures in order to ensure accurate and appropriate reporting of findings.     

 

The UC San Diego/San Diego Unified research partnership – and, since 2010, SanDERA – has benefited from the financial support of numerous entities, including the U.S. Department of Education, the Public Policy Institute of California (PPIC), The William and Flora Hewlett Foundation, the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, the Atlantic Philanthropies, the Girard Foundation, the California Academic Partnership Program, the Smith Richardson Foundation, the Yankelovich Center for Social Science Research at UC San Diego, and others. PPIC has been particularly supportive – publishing several books and related materials summarizing research findings emanating from the collaborative work.

 

Renewal of the MOA will continue to provide San Diego Unified with a vital, respected, and productive research partnership that will continue to respond to research questions that inform important policy and practice issues relevant to the district and education on a broader scale.

BACKGROUND: On June 9, 2015, the board approved the following district administrators to be the designated California Interscholastic Federation (CIF) Representatives to League: Ana Alvarez, Carmen Garcia, Harry Shelton, Ann Menna, Richard Nash, and Chuck Podhorsky. This amendment to the designated representatives is to replace Ana Alvarez with Scott Giusti.

 

The CIF requires each school district of every league in the state to annually designate their league representatives. The designated league representatives must be approved by the school district and/or their governing board.

 

California Education Code section 33353 (a) (1), requires that the boards, after joining CIF, designate their representatives to CIF Leagues. The representatives of boards are the only persons who make recommendations to the CIF Board of Managers, at the league and section levels, that affect athletics. Upon approval of this agenda item, CIF representatives to the League will be Carmen Garcia, Harry Shelton, Ann Menna, Richard Nash, Dr. Chuck Podhorsky, and Scott Giusti.  Selection process of representatives is voluntary and encompasses the district's geographic boundaries.

 

Superintendent Marten is the district representative on the CIF San Diego Section Board of Managers.
