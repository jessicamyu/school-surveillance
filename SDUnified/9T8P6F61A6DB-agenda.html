IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Collective negotiations contracts require that employees be notified of any substantial changes to instructional calendars, with the provision that proposed changes may be bargained if requested. Bargaining unit engagement on the multi-year transition was initially held in fall of 2014. CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

CONSULTATION WITH OTHER STAKEHOLDERS:  Staff has completed a thorough, inclusive communication process with each of the 16 schools included in the second phase of the multi-year plan. The approach included providing notification regarding the calendar change and encouraging a dialogue about how to work together to plan for a successful transition. Activities included:

  • Early collaboration and planning with area superintendents and principals to plan stakeholder outreach tailored to each school’s unique needs
  • Presentations made to parent/family meetings at each school (i.e. coffee with the principal, family Friday, special parent assemblies, PTA)
  • Presentations made to regularly scheduled staff meetings at each school, with attendance by Payroll and HR staff to answer any staff questions
  • Presentations made to each cluster at a regularly scheduled cluster meeting
  • Translation of informational materials into Spanish, Tagalog, and Vietnamese, and other languages if requested by school
  • Accessible calendar transition website kept current with electronic copies of all informational material
  • Regular, ongoing communication with central office departments (i.e., Transportation, Special Education, Food Services, Extended Learning, Facilities, and Leadership and Learning) to monitor, plan, and implement activities related to the transition
  • Notification via central office departments to community partners providing before and after school services in order to plan for potential schedule changes

See Attachment 2 for a full list of each school’s communication efforts and activities.

 

BACKGROUND:  On June 18, 2013, the board directed staff to conduct an in-depth review of transitioning all district schools to a common calendar. On January 14, 2014, the Superintendent provided information to the board on instructional calendar planning for 2014-15 and beyond. As part of comprehensive cluster master planning completed in spring 2014, on May 27, 2014, and June 3, 2014, the board approved the conversion of four year-round calendar schools (Ericson, Mason, and Walker elementary schools in the Mira Mesa Cluster, and Sequoia Elementary in the Madison Cluster, respectively) to the traditional calendar effective for the 2015-16 school year. At that time, the board directed staff to return with a proposal to transition all remaining year-round schools in the district, with special consideration to schools serving military families and cluster or pathway alignment.

 

The multi-year transition and outreach plan was initially presented to the board on October 28, 2014 as an informational first reading.  On December 9, 2014, the board approved the multi-year plan and the first phase of 15 schools to convert from the year-round to traditional calendar. The board directed that staff return each year to provide a review of the remaining phases of the plan as well as the notification efforts made for families and staff.

 

A number of factors were considered in development of the multi-year transition plan, including:

  • Number of students from military families enrolled
  • Alignment with high school learning pathways
  • Overall cluster alignment (i.e. only year-round school in the cluster)
  • Transitioning whole clusters together to the extent possible to assist with a smooth transition for families
  • Providing for multiple years of transition to plan for the investment in multi-year budget planning
  • Desire to provide equity for clusters throughout the district making the transition
  • Multi-year transition to allow ample time for ongoing communication and planning with families, school staff, other stakeholders, and district departments

The first phase of implementation included 15 schools that began the 2015-16 school year on the traditional calendar. Key areas that will be monitored throughout this first year of implementation include measuring attendance in the summer months, measuring potential changes in enrollment, reminders to transitioning school staff with regard to payroll options, and monitoring any potential cost savings.

 

Following this second phase, the multi-year plan has two remaining phases. The 2017-18, or third phase, will include the 14 year-round schools in the Crawford and Hoover clusters. The 2018-19, or fourth and final phase, will include the nine year-round schools in the San Diego cluster. All district schools will be on a common traditional calendar for the 2018-19 school year.

  • Phase 1-A:  The expedited installation of classroom air conditioning systems in the hottest 2,000 classrooms, primarily in Climate Zone 3 schools, is substantially complete (see Attachment A for a list of schools included).

On September 15, 2015, the board passed a motion that directed the Superintendent to have staff develop a plan to equip every classroom and critical space with air conditioning.

  • Phase 2:  Expedite the installation of air conditioning systems in classrooms and other primary use spaces (see Attachment D for a list of spaces included) at schools in Climate Zones 1 and 2 (see Attachment B for a list of schools included).  Expedite the installation of air conditioning in all the remaining, qualifying education spaces at Climate Zone 3 schools.  Design and construction activities are scheduled to begin in early 2016, and be completed by 2019.

Schools formerly scheduled to receive their Whole Site Modernization (WSM) project after 2019, will now be expedited to receive air conditioning.  These expedited air conditioning schools will have the scope and budget for air conditioning removed from their WSM project.  Some adjustments to WSM scope in Climate Zone 1 schools will be necessary as air conditioning was not originally budgeted for these schools.  Attachment C includes the list of schools scheduled to receive air conditioning included with the scope of their WSM projects before 2019.  Separate custom design-bid-construction projects may be used to air condition unique education spaces not suited for the template design/construction approach.  Staff may adjust individual school air conditioning system designs, procurement approaches and project schedules in order to most expeditiously and efficiently execute the overall plan.  This plan will not impact the program schedule of projects approved by the board on June 2, 2015 (see Attachment E).  Future WSM projects may provide insulation, efficient windows, and other building improvements needed increase the performance and energy efficiency of installed air conditioning systems.

 

Concurrent and future Solar Photovoltaic (PV) projects are planned to offset the increased energy use resulting from the added air conditioning systems. Please see the accompanying slide presentation for cost and time estimates of projected PV offsets.  Additional air conditioning maintenance and repair technicians will be required to properly service the additional air conditioning systems.  The additional annual cost of air conditioning maintenance and repair is approximately $1.4 million per year. 

 

Until recently, only 40 percent of San Diego Unified classrooms were air conditioned.  During the 1940s, 50s, and 60s, when the population of San Diego grew significantly, schools were typically constructed without air conditioning.  In the 1970s, air conditioning systems were included in many new San Diego Unified schools.  In the mid-1980s, the district began a practice of constructing schools without air conditioning systems.  During this period inland schools such as De Portola Middle School and Scripps Ranch High School were built without air conditioning.

 

On November 3, 1998, voters approved Proposition MM.  The district committed funds in Proposition MM to study, begin planning, and implementation of limited thermal relief measures.  As part of this planning effort, the board approved the Phase I Climate Assessment Study on February 27, 2001, and Phase II on October 9, 2001.  The study developed heating, ventilation, and air conditioning requirements by getting results from sensors in the classrooms.  Based on these results, the board approved a classroom temperature of 78 degrees to be used as the basis for determining whether a classroom is selected for thermal mitigation in the future, when funding allowed.  As a result, the district was divided into three climate zones: Zone 1 (coastal areas), Zone 2 (central areas of the district), Zone 3 (inland areas with the hottest schools).  On October 9, 2001, the board approved District Policy E-4100 along with a site-ranking list.   Based on the limited funds in Proposition MM, the board directed district staff to utilize passive measures such as (ceiling fans and window tinting, for example) to provide relief to classroom spaces in the hottest areas.  Consistent with E-4100, new schools in Climate Zones 2 and 3 were also designed and constructed to be air conditioning ready, but not to include air conditioning.  Climate Zones 2 and 3 were designated for future air conditioning, when funding allowed.

 

Funding was allocated to install air conditioning in Climate Zone 3 schools when Proposition S was approved by local voters in 2008.  In 2012, voters passed Proposition Z which included provisions for the installation of permanent air conditioning in schools located in all climate zones.  Air conditioning charter schools may be funded by the Proposition Z, charter school pro-rata allocation.  Charter school projects are reviewed by the Proposition Z Charter School Facilities Committee prior to board consideration. 

 

On February 12, 2013, the board approved the expedited installation of permanent single package, air conditioning systems in the hottest 2,000 classrooms in the district, and subsequently approved the installation of air conditioning in nurse’s offices, indoor assembly areas, and other critical spaces at the hottest schools, primarily located in Climate Zone 3. Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC) developed an expedited project delivery method consisting of template design solutions, fast-track Division of State Architect (DSA) reviews, and indefinite delivery indefinite quantity construction contracts that, along with whole site modernization projects, resulted in the rapid installation of air conditioning in the hottest 2,000 classrooms.  FPC is currently using the same expedited project delivery method to complete the installation of air conditioning in nurse’s offices, indoor assembly areas, and other critical spaces at the hottest schools.  On February 4, 2014, the board approved accelerated air conditioning installation for charter schools located on district-owned property in accordance with a recommendation of the Charter Schools Facilities Committee.  Recently, FPC and Physical Plant Operations staff have installed temporary portable air conditioners to create cool zones in schools where less than 10 percent of space is air conditioned.

 

Staff will update the board on the status of this air conditioning project during the scheduled semi-annual bond program updates.
