CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  The District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

 

BACKGROUND: As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA).  The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

 

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle.  The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA.  Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.  Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.

 

Additionally, the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review.  Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

 

The local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2014-16 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 176 schools. 

 

A copy of each 2014-16 SPSA, which includes the budget summary and required signed assurances page, is available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

BACKGROUND: Plan for Identifying Gifted and Talented Education (GATE) Students  Annually, about 12,000 district students are tested for GATE eligibility.  Students in Grade 2 are universally tested and the identification process consists of five steps:  search, assessment, documentation, review and identification/placement.  In addition, students who were tested and found ineligible for GATE services, but scored at the 85 percentile or above (with two or more advanced CST scores) can be re-tested once.  With regard to "new-to-district" students, current practice is to test transfer students in Grades 3, 4, and 5 only. 


Currently, the Raven Progressive Matrices test is utilized for assessments.  The Raven is an untimed, non-spatial reasoning test that becomes progressively more difficult as the students work through the test.  Scoring of the Raven is normative and based on the student’s age.  All students in the district, regardless of socioeconomic, linguistic, or cultural backgrounds and/or disability, have an opportunity to be screened for GATE identification beginning in the second grade.

 

At present, eligibility considerations include performance on the Raven, state achievement tests and review of other pertinent educational factors (i.e., economic, language, special education).

 

This proposal will revise the current GATE identification process, including the criteria for eligibility, retesting, assessment of transfer students, and appeals.

 

Further collaboration will continue with the GATE workgroup to address, revise, and recommend refinements to district administrative procedures and board policy to align with the proposed GATE identification process.

1. Resolution in the Matter of Support for Dropout Prevention and Recovery Efforts and Designating August 2015, as Dropout Recovery Month (Foster/Evans)
Meeting
Jun 23, 2015 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of support for dropout prevention and recovery efforts and designate August 2015, as Dropout Recovery Month.

 

BACKGROUND: Civic Enterprises, America’s Promise Alliance, and Alliance for Excellent Education, among others, indicate that reengaged students aid the nation’s social well-being and become valuable resources through increased civic engagement and in ensuring the realization of the future workforce which strengthens our communities and the economy.

 

It is widely recognized that out of school youth often require wide-ranging, innovative, and intensive supports to return to school to earn a high school diploma or GED, as pupils drop out of high school due to the overwhelming effects of challenging living conditions such as homelessness, violent surroundings, abuse or neglect, and catastrophic family health events.

 

Schools and programs that engage with the aforementioned obstacles exhibit the commitment to transforming at-risk youth to being at promise of success, and thus achieve dropout recovery success through the use of state-of-the-art technology; career technical education to reach a variety of learning modalities; accelerated learning pedagogies; competency-based rather than seat time-based instruction; and open entry and open exit enrollment.

 

Building a Grad Nation indicates that the United States cannot achieve its graduation goals without increasing California’s Latino and African American graduation rates, and recognizes that total dropouts across all races cost the nation its economic competitiveness at $93 billion annually and $1.6 trillion in lost revenues and increased social services over dropouts’ lifetimes.

 

The Alliance for Excellent Education estimates that if only one-half of the dropouts from a single year earned a diploma, the economic benefits to California would be an additional $1.4 billion in earnings annually for the reengaged pupils and an annual increase in state and local tax revenues of $167 million.

 

Dropout prevention and the reengagement of students who dropout merits on-going prioritization, as research suggests dropouts earn less, die younger, are more likely to be imprisoned and are reliant on public assistance, and are more often victims of violence.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supports dropout prevention, reengagement of dropouts, and recovery efforts, specifically by designating August 2015, as Dropout Recovery Month, in honor of the schools, programs, and staff who work to reengage pupils, and in honor of the pupils who overcome significantly personal challenges to reengage in the education system in preparation for college, a future career and active civic engagement.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supports local, state, and federal initiatives to increase graduation rates and dropout recovery efforts.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District remains committed to implementing the Pathways Program, an innovative, city-wide partnership across both public and private sectors that mitigates dropout risk through outreach to at-risk students; assessment of each student’s personal strengths and needs; referrals to community services; reenrollment support; education and other social and emotional interventions; and the development of reengagement activities, such as life plans to keep students enrolled through graduation and/or job training.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supports the service of students through the continued use of creative teaching strategies, alternative assessments, and adequate resources in dropout prevention programs and atypical/alternative high schools.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District commends both the staff that have assisted pupils in the pursuit of education and the pupils that have reengaged or overcome personal challenges to remain engaged to prepare for future college attendance and careers.

BACKGROUND:  The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1960, was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) ACT of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards.  The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty.  Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of "children in poverty" residing within the district boundary.  The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement. 

 

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time.  To that end, at its January 27, 2015 meeting, the board approved the school allocation method (Title I Ranking Report) and anticipated Title I revenue for 2015-16.  The 2015-16 Proposed Title I Budget (Attachment 1) allocates the same overall amount to schools in 2015-16, that they collectively received in 2014-15.

BACKGROUND:  The Board of Education has annually adopted a resolution for issuance of TRANs for the upcoming fiscal year, most recently at the June 25, 2014 meeting for the 2014-15 fiscal year.

 

The district receives a substantial portion of its annual income from property taxes, which are traditionally paid by taxpayers on or before December 10 or April 10.  As a result, the district must borrow funds each year in advance of these dates to pay operational expenses in the July to December timeframe.  Thus, funds are borrowed at the beginning of the fiscal year using TRANs notes and repaid within an eight to twelve–month period.  Short-term borrowing using TRANs is necessary to ensure the district meets its financial obligations on a cash basis.

 

On February 10, 2015, the board approved a pool of firms to provide financial underwriting services to support the sale of Proposition S and Proposition Z bonds and TRANs issuances.  The district has annually utilized this approved pool of firms from which to select the Underwriters that will purchase and sell the district's annual TRANs.  The district solicited proposals from the firms in this approved pool of providers. BACKGROUND: In the 2013-14 school year, PowerSchool replaced Zangle and became the Student Information System (SIS) of record.  With any new enterprise-wide system, there will be customizations required to make it fit into the organization it supports.  Now that the PowerSchool SIS has been used for almost two full school years, staff have documented several areas in PowerSchool that are highly recommended/required to enhance or customize for a better, more efficient system for school site staff, and parents/students along with automating more data checks and edits as it is entered for better data quality.
