RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of recognizing June 2020 as LGBTQIA+ Pride Month for all schools and students in the San Diego Unified School District, increase awareness and visibility around the LGBTQIA+ community, and renew the district's ongoing commitment and education plan to promote safe and affirming school spaces for students, families, and educators that focuses on intersectional diversity, acceptance, and unconditional support for LGBTQIA+ students, families, and educators.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender, Queer, Intersex, Asexual, plus (LGBTQIA+) Pride Month is currently celebrated in June each year to honor the 1969 Stonewall Uprising in Manhattan. The Stonewall Uprising was a tipping point for the Gay Liberation Movement in the United States and was led by two transgender women of color, Marsha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera.

 

Two presidents of the United States have officially declared June as LGBT pride month. President Bill Clinton declared June "Gay and Lesbian Pride Month" in 1999 and 2000. From 2009 to 2016, each year he was in office, President Barack Obama declared June LGBT Pride Month; “I call upon all Americans to observe this month by fighting prejudice and discrimination in their own lives and everywhere it exists.” – Proclamation 8529 by U.S President Barack Obama, May 28, 2010.

 

The San Diego Unified School District is committed to safety, inclusion, and affirmation for all students through developing and coordinating activities, training, and opportunities for the SDUSD community including:

  • Creating visibility and awareness exhibits at the Eugene Brucker Education Center and on district campuses to honor the month and all students, staff, and families who identify as LGBTQIA+, such as flying the LGBTQIA+ Pride Rainbow flag and Transgender Pride flag.
  • Implementing history and social studies curriculum that highlights the contributions of members of the LGBTQIA+ community and allows students to see themselves accurately reflected, as prescribed by the FAIR Education Act, SB 48, which was signed into law on July 14, 2011.
  • Offering ongoing and student-centered opportunities for education, advocacy, and advancement for LGBTQIA+ students, families, and staff.
  • The adoption and implementation of comprehensive bullying/harassment policies that specifically enumerate sexual orientation, gender identity, and gender expression with clear and effective systems for reporting and addressing incidents that students experience.

Nationally, 59.5% of LGBTQIA+ students feel unsafe at school because of their sexual orientation and 44.6% because of their gender expression. A hostile school climate affects students’ academic success and mental health. LGBTQIA+ students who experience victimization and discrimination at school have poorer educational outcomes and psychological well-being.

 

The availability and the utility of LGBTQIA+-related school resources and supports help offset the negative effects of a hostile school climate and promote a positive learning experience.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution in Support of Assembly Bill 493, Teachers: Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender, Queer and Questioning Pupil Resources and Training. The Board of Education renews its commitment and education plan to promote safe and affirming schools spaces for students, families, and educators that focuses on intersectional diversity, acceptance, and unconditional support for San Diego Unified School District staff.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: All students deserve and need safe and supportive school environments in which to learn.

 

Despite much progress, California lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer, questioning, intersex, asexual, plus (LGTBQIA+) students often face verbal, physical, and online harassment, which has significant effects on their academic achievement.

 

Sixty-four percent of such middle and high school students surveyed by the California Department of Education last year reported being bullied. Nearly half had seriously considered suicide.

 

These problems have direct effects on how students perform in school. GLSEN's 2017 survey data shows that the average grade point average for LGBTQ students who experience higher levels of harassment is significantly lower than for LGBTQ students who experience lower levels of harassment, and that nearly 35 percent of LGBTQ students report missing school because they felt unsafe or uncomfortable, with over 10 percent missing four or more full days in the month immediately preceding the survey.

 

Creating supportive learning environments for LGBTQ students improves their performance in school. Students in schools with peer support clubs report lower levels of victimization, are more likely to report that school personnel intervened when they witnessed harassment, and are less likely to miss school because of safety concerns.

 

Assembly Bill 493, commencing with the 2021-22 school year, would require each school operated by a school district or county office of education and each charter school to provide online training at least once every two years to teachers of students in grades 7 to 12, inclusive, and to all other certificated employees at that school, on school site and community resources for the support of lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer, and questioning (LGBTQ) students, and strategies to increase support for LGBTQ students and thereby improve overall school climate, as specified.

 

The San Diego Unified School District is committed to safety, inclusion, and affirmation for all students through developing and coordinating activities, training, and opportunities, including:

  • Implementing history and social studies curriculum that highlights the contributions of members of the LGBTQIA+ community and allows students to see themselves accurately reflected, as prescribed by the FAIR Education Act, SB 48, which was signed into law on July 14, 2011. The San Diego Unified School District piloted identity-based curriculum modules at 15 schools with over 50 teachers in the 2018-19 school year.
  • Offering ongoing and student-centered opportunities for education, advocacy, and advancement for LGBTQIA+ students, families, and staff with 163 schools receiving training on "LGBTQIA+ 101: LGBTQIA+ Advocacy and Awareness;" 23 schools receiving training on "LGBTQIA+ 102: FAIR Education Act and Social Justice;" 10,000 "OUT for Safe Schools" badges distributed; and GSA clubs established at 51 middle and high schools.
  • The adoption and implementation of comprehensive bullying/harassment policies that specifically enumerate sexual orientation, gender identity, and gender expression with clear and effective systems for reporting and addressing incidents that students experience with "Ally Action" programming was piloted at 14 schools in the 2018-19 school year that focused on bullying prevention and intervention and culminated in student-led professional development for the school staff.

  • San Diego Unified School District Cluster Governance Committee representative who is a parent or guardian of a child enrolled in the district

  • San Diego Unified School District Charter School Facility Committee representative
  • American Society of Civil Engineers San Diego Section representative
  • San Diego Building and Construction Trades Council representative
  • Construction industry organization representative

BACKGROUND:  The UCSD college level coursework program offers high achieving students the opportunity to fulfill high school graduation requirements while gaining college preparation skills and lower division transfer credits in science, technology, engineering, arts, and math disciplines. The 2019-20 program will be offered at La Jolla, Morse, and Point Loma high schools.


The courses will be taught by UCSD Extension instructors and will be made available to students on the high school campus outside of the regular instructional day. This program is grounded on the  concept that academic rigor, combined with the opportunity to save time and money, is a powerful motivator for high school students to work hard in meeting the serious intellectual challenges of the future.


Participation in the program will provide students with the opportunity to increase the number of college credits they can earn while still in high school, make higher education more accessible, affordable, and attractive by bridging the divide between high school and college readiness, facilitate the transition of motivated high school students to higher education, create valuable opportunities to reach out to high school students, and challenge students to utilize their high school career in a fully productive manner.


These courses are normally taken after earning admission to the University of California and to ensure the success of the pilot program, however, the courses will be held at district high schools that have existing necessary academic support and high concentrations of students who are academically ready for University of California level coursework. Students eligible for the program must meet all high school level course prerequisites, take the Scholastic Aptitude Test, American College Test or Advanced Placement courses, pass Integrated Math 3 and complete the University of California 'a-g' course sequence.


UCSD will offer a total of 10 courses for the 2019-20 school year, one or two courses to the three high schools. Additional schools will be added at the discretion of the district to fulfill a total of 10 courses. The cost per course is $10,000.00.

BACKGROUND: The State Water Resources Control Board (SWRCB) adopted the Drought Response Outreach Program for Schools (DROPS) Guidelines on August 19, 2014. DROPS is focused on projects that reduce stormwater pollution and provide multiple benefits including water conservation, water supply augmentation, energy savings, increased awareness of water resource sustainability, and reduced dry weather runoff.  San Diego Unified School District, in partnership with Groundwork San Diego, applied for and was awarded, a grant from the SWRCB as part of its DROPS program. On January 24, 2017, the Board of Education ratified an agreement with the SWRCB to accept grant funds on behalf of Gompers Preparatory Academy, and Horton Elementary and Millennial Tech Middle Schools. It is anticipated that the district will receive approximately $1,243,445 from the SWRCB as part of the DROPS grant program to implement the proposed projects.

 

PROJECT LOCATION AND DESCRIPTION: The proposed projects include the construction of new bioswales and pervious pavement at the following district school sites:

  • Gompers Preparatory Academy, 1005 - 47th St., San Diego, CA 92102
  • Horton Elementary School, 5050 Guymon St., San Diego, CA 92102
  • Millennial Tech Middle School, 1110 Carolina Ln., San Diego, CA 92102

 

Generally, the bioswales proposed for each school site are needed to capture and filter storm water run-off from the adjacent building roofs, asphalt play surfaces and decomposed granite play surfaces before the water enters the local storm drain system. The bioswales contain a polyethylene barrier, a perforated underdrain connecting to the existing storm drain catch basin, irrigation, and native vegetation plantings. Minor grading and excavation would be required to form the drainage swale. Trenching for the perforated underdrain would require a cut to a depth of one and a half feet deep and three feet wide. The trenches would be backfilled with crushed rock and a special bio retention soil. The remainder of the excavation would be backfilled with excavated materials. Any excess soil removed off-site would be disposed of at the discretion of the future contractor and could be stockpiled off-site or sent to a landfill. Construction equipment required to facilitate construction would include a bobcat type mini-excavator and trucks for importing and hauling of soil and other construction materials.

 

Construction of the bioswales at Horton Elementary would occur concurrently with the existing joint use field project currently under construction and would begin on July 11, 2019, and end on August 23, 2019. Construction of the bioswales and pervious pavement at Gompers Preparatory Academy and Millennial Tech Middle School would begin on August 2, 2019, and end on October 18, 2019.

 

The first bioswale at Horton Elementary School would be located on the east end of the campus adjacent to a classroom building and encompasses 150 square feet. The second bioswale would be located on the southeast end of the newly constructed joint use field and encompasses 1,800 square feet for a total disturbed area of 1,950 square feet or 0.05 acres.

 

The proposed bioswales on the Gompers campus would be located on the north side of the Library (building 14), on the north side of buildings 8, 10 and 11, west of building 6, west of the north parking lot, along 47th Street and encompass a total of 7,230 square feet. Pervious pavement on the Gompers campus is proposed for the west end of the north parking lot and encompasses 900 square feet of parking spaces. The total disturbed area for the proposed Gompers bioswales and pervious pavement is approximately 8,130 square feet or 0.18 acres.

 

The proposed bioswales at Millennial Tech Middle would be located on the south side of the Administration Building, on the south side of the parking lot, and encompass 1,920 square feet. There is a proposed swale on the existing EarthLab site on district property adjacent to Millennial Tech Middle that encompasses 2,200 square feet. Pervious pavement on the Millennial Tech Middle School campus is located at the east parking lot and encompasses 2,400 square feet of parking spaces. The total disturbed area for the proposed Millennial Tech Middle School bioswales and pervious pavement is approximately 6,520 square feet or 0.14 acres.

 

California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The installation of bioswales and pervious pavement at three district sites is subject to CEQA, however, the project is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Section 15303 – New Construction or Conversion of Existing Structures (Class 3). Class 3 consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures; installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures; and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure. The number of structures described in this section are the maximum allowable on any legal parcel. Examples of this exemption include accessory (appurtenant) structures. The proposed bioswales represent a small new drainage facility connected to the existing stormwater drainage system and, therefore, the application of a Class 3 categorical exemption is appropriate for the proposed project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The proposed project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the proposed project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The proposed project would occur entirely within the boundaries of each school property. Installation of bioswales would be consistent with the operation of a school. Generally the surrounding land uses at each site consist of residential housing, open space areas, and a freeway. Implementation of the proposed project would not extend beyond the joint use area or into open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at each school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the proposed project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The proposed project includes the construction of bioswales at each school site. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the proposed project. As such, there is nothing unusual about each of the school sites or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the proposed project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. The intent of the proposed project is to improve storm water runoff which would provide environmental quality enhancements to existing storm water runoff. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the proposed project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The proposed project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, none of the school sites are included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the proposed project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking because major grading and trenching into non-native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District proposes to award a contract for battery replacement and uninterrupted power supply (UPS) repairs at the existing Serra Data Center. The Data Center is on the north side of Serra High School campus. Built in 2013, it is a single story 10,000 square feet building with two classrooms. Inside the building is a 2,000 square foot server room, two UPS units in a 950 square foot electrical room and two battery cabinet assemblies in a 280 square foot battery room. Since the UPS units were installed in 2013, the batteries are reaching end of life cycle; the DC capacitor bank is three years overdue for replacement and need to be replaced. Blowers and fans also need to be replaced. Construction would occur beginning in July of 2019 and take approximately two weeks.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111(b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more. The notice calling for bids was published once a week for two successive weeks in accordance with Public Contract Code section 20112. Bids were due and opened on June 14, 2019. The district received a total of three bids from My Electrician, Inc., Rod Fuller Electric Corp., and Total Online Protection LLC. After evaluating the bids, staff determined Total Online Protection LLC to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.

 

Additionally, consistent with the May 10, 2011, board-approved resolution in support of service-disabled veteran-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a three percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities. The recommended contractor, Total Online Protection LLC, submitted a 100 percent DVBE participation in its bid.  

 

CEQA ANALYSIS: Battery replacement and UPS repairs at the Serra High School Data Center (Project) is a project subject to CEQA; however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Section 15301 – Existing Facilities (Class 1). A Class 1 exemption consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of use.

 

As described above, the Project consists of repair and maintenance of the existing Serra High School data center and would not result in the expansion of use. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

 

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Project to ensure that the above exceptions will not prohibit application of the exemption for the Project.

  1. Location. Construction activities associated with the Project would be contained to an existing district campus and the existing site use will not change as a result of the implementation of the  Project. The  Project will not impact environmentally sensitive areas or an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern because implementation of the Project will be contained to the interior of an existing building. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. The Project is generally considered repair and maintenance of existing facilities, which is consistent with ongoing operations of the campus. Ongoing operations of an existing school is not a cumulative impact. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Project is not considered an unusual circumstance as these repair and maintenance activities is common throughout the campus. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The  Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the Department of Toxic Substance Control, no district properties are included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines § 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at the site is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking.  Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

 

Approval of this item approves the Project as described, and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk, and awards the agreement with Total Online Protection LLC, for Battery replacement and UPS repairs at the Serra High School Data Center.
