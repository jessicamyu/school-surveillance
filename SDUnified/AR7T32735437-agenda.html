Project Components and Benefits

Consistent with the RFP, the joint occupancy agreement envisions the future of the Scripps Mesa site as a high-quality, shared development with the following primary features:

 

District STEAM Lab Facility, Community Meeting Space and Garden

  • Innovative District facility featuring STEAM lab and makerspace (modeled from successful District partnership with Qualcomm Thinkabit lab)
  • Meeting and conference room spaces for District and community use through Civic Center Act rentals

Multi-family Residential Community

  • 264 units of “Class A” multifamily for-rent housing and resident amenities
  • Of those units, 22 units designated affordable at 50 Percent Area Median Income 
  • Affirmative marketing of units to District employees
  • Community garden, an amenity requested during initial outreach with cluster and neighborhood members

This mixed use represents both the highest and best use of the land as well as added District and community benefit. The proposal is also consistent with the Board-approved guiding principles for the real estate utilization strategy as follows:

 

Board Guiding Principle

Scripps Mesa Proposal

 

Maintain ownership of District property where appropriate

District will continue to own the property.

Generate on-going revenue

Project will generate guaranteed near-term and ongoing revenue for the full term of the 66 year agreement.

 

District and community facility will be built at no cost to the District (capital value of the building estimated at $2.5 million).

Develop properties as neighborhood centers providing neighborhood services (Vision 2020 goal)

STEAM Lab will provide new learning opportunities for students throughout the District and is consistent with cluster academic program alignment.

 

Community meeting space and community garden offer new neighborhood services to residents.

Residential component provides high-quality rental housing, including affordable units, designed to complement neighborhood fabric.

 

Residential component of the project will offer new housing for families who can attend cluster neighborhood schools Dingeman Elementary, Marshall Middle, and Scripps Ranch High.

Provide for community engagement and input in the development process

STEAM Lab concept and overall project design was shared through community engagement, facilitated through strong collaboration between District instructional and operations staff.

 

Staff is working collaboratively with Innovations Academy and the Scripps Ranch Farmers Market on a transition plan.

CEQA Environmental Review Process

The District has prepared a Draft Environmental Impact Report (DEIR) for the Proposed Project (Project) in accordance with the requirements of CEQA (Public Resources Code Section 21000 et seq.) and CEQA Guidelines (California Code of Regulations Sections 15000 et seq.) The District issued a Notice of Preparation (NOP) to prepare a DEIR on January 6, 2017 and the NOP was available for public review and comment from January 6, 2017 to February 6, 2017. A public scoping meeting was held at the Scripps Ranch High School Library, 10410 Falcon Way, San Diego, CA 92131 on January 18, 2017, to collect comments concerning the scope and content of the DEIR.

 

The DEIR was circulated for a 46-day public review period, between January 19, 2018, and March 6, 2018. A Notice of Availability (NOA) of the DEIR was published in the San Diego Union-Tribune and the La Prensa Spanish language newspaper. Copies of the NOA were filed at the County of San Diego and with the California State Clearinghouse. Copies of the DEIR were sent to the State Clearinghouse, state and local agencies, and community entities and organizations. Additionally, copies of the DEIR were available for review at the Scripps Miramar Ranch Library, 10301 Scripps Lake Drive, San Diego, CA 92131; San Diego Unified (Physical Plant Operations Center Annex, Room 5), 4860 Ruffner Street, San Diego, CA 92111; and, Online at: https://www.sandiegounified.org/environmental-reviews. 

 

Comments received on the DEIR during the comment period and associated responses are included with the FEIR (Attachment 2). The District received 66 comment letters from public agencies, community organizations, and individuals during the 46-day public review period. Minor revisions to the DEIR were made in response to some of the comments and are shown in underline where text was added or in strikeout where text was deleted. The revisions to the EIR do not result in new significant impacts and do not warrant recirculation of the EIR.

 

The FEIR concludes that implementation of the Proposed Project would result in less than significant impacts for all CEQA resource topics as described in Appendix G of the CEQA Guidelines. Feasible mitigation measures have been identified to mitigate potentially significant impacts to less than significant related to air quality and noise and vibration. Those mitigations and the analysis of the impacts after mitigation are discussed in the MMRP (Attachment 3).Written Findings (Attachment 4) have been prepared for each of the significant effects. Approval of the project authorizes staff to file the NOD (Attachment 5) with the Recorder/County Clerk of San Diego County, California, and Governor’s Office of Planning and Research, State Clearinghouse and Planning Unit.

2. Public Hearing and First Reading of the Proposed 2018-19 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP)
Meeting
Jun 19, 2018 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Information, First Reading, Public Hearing

The Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) is an essential element of California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF).  It is a three-year, district-level plan that is updated annually.  The 2018-19 LCAP is the second year of the three-year plan. The plan describes the school district's key goals for students as well as the actions (with expenditures) the district will take to achieve the goals and the measures used to indicate progress.  The LCAP also includes an Annual Update, which reflects the implementation of the current year's LCAP (2017-18).  The San Diego Unified School District has aligned its six LCAP goals and supporting actions and expenditures to the board-adopted Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.

 

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and first reading of the district's proposed 2018-19 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP). A workshop was conducted on June 7, 2018, at which the Superintendent and staff provided information to the board and the public on implementation of Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood and its alignment with the proposed 2018-19 LCAP.  Each goal was reviewed, and plans for implementation of each goal in the 2018-19 school year were presented. The proposed 2018-19 LCAP will be presented to the Board of Education for final reading and adoption on June 26, 2018. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The proposed 2018-19 LCAP is submitted for first reading in conjunction with the first reading of the proposed 2018-19 budget.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: The Annual Update section of the proposed 2018-19 LCAP includes 2017-18 actions, services, and estimated actual expenditures.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) and the District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC) serve as the district's LCAP advisory committees. During 2017-18, regular updates on LCAP implementation were presented to the board and public at regular board meetings, and the presentations were available on the district's LCAP webpage for dissemination to all stakeholders. The early draft 2018-19 LCAP was presented to the DAC on May 16, 2018, and to the DELAC on May 17, 2018, for review and comment.  The full document is posted on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.  

 

BACKGROUND: The LCAP is intended to be a comprehensive planning tool.  For school districts, pursuant to Education Code Section 52060, the LCAP must describe, for the school district and each school within the district, goals and specific actions to achieve those goals for all students and each subgroup of students in Education Code Section 52052, including students with disabilities, for each of the state priorities and any locally identified priorities.

 

The district's local priorities (LCAP Goals) are:

 

1. Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.

2. Access to Broad and Challenging Curriculum: Students have equitable access to rigorous, well-rounded, standards-aligned curricula and access to and skill in applying technologies to leverage learning, assuring readiness for a full range of post-graduation options. 

3. Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning: Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.

4. Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture: Schools provide safe and well-maintained facilities and positive learning climates and instructional practices that are culturally responsive, challenge bias, and support the academic, social, emotional, and physical needs of students. 

5. Parent and Community Engagement with Highly Regarded Neighborhood Schools that Serve Students, Families, and Communities: Parents and community members are engaged and work within and across schools to support student learning. Neighborhood schools are highly regarded for their quality as well as their service as centers for extended learning and enrichment opportunities, and academic and social services.

6. Well-Orchestrated Districtwide Support Services and Communications: All parts of the organization - personnel and infrastructure - work together to support student success through quality schools in every neighborhood.

 

The State priorities are:

 

1. Basic: The degree to which teachers are appropriately assigned and fully credentialed in the subject areas and for the students they are teaching; students in the school district have sufficient access to the standards-aligned instructional materials; and school facilities are maintained in good repair.

2. Implementation of State Standards: The implementation of state board adopted academic content and performance standards for all students, which are:

  • English Language Arts - Common Core State Standards (CCSS) for English Language Arts
  • Mathematics - CCSS for Mathematics
  • English Language Development (ELD)
  • Career Technical Education
  • Health Education Content Standards
  • History-Social Science
  • Model School Library Standards
  • Physical Education Model Content Standards
  • Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS)
  • Visual and Performing Arts (VAPA)
  • World Language; and
  • How the programs and services will enable English Learners to access the CCSS and ELD standards for purposes of gaining academic content knowledge and English language proficiency.

3. Parental Involvement: Description of efforts the school district makes to seek parent input in making decisions for the school district and each individual school site, promotion of parent participation in programs for unduplicated students and students with exceptional needs.

4. Student Achievement: Performance on statewide assessments, percentage of students who have successfully completed courses that satisfy the University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) entrance requirements, or programs of study that align with the state board approved career technical education standards and framework; the percentage of English Learners who make progress toward English proficiency; the English Learner reclassification rate; the percentage of students that pass an Advanced Placement examination with a score of 3 or higher; and the percentage of students who participate in, and demonstrate college preparedness pursuant to the Early Assessment Program, or any subsequent assessment of college preparedness.

5. Student Engagement: School attendance rates, chronic absenteeism rates, middle school dropout rates, high school dropout rates, and high school graduation rates.

6. School Climate: Student suspension rates; student expulsion rates; other local measures including surveys of students, parents, and teachers on the sense of safety and school connectedness.

7. Course Access: Student enrollment in a broad course of study including courses described in Education Code Section 51210 and Section 51220(a)-(i), as applicable; programs and services developed and provided to unduplicated students an students with exceptional needs.

8. Other Student Outcomes: Student outcomes, if available, for courses described in Education Code Section 51210 and Section 51220(a)-(i), as applicable.

BACKGROUND:

Proposition 30, The Schools and Local Public Safety Protection Act of 2012, approved by the voters on November 6, 2012, temporarily increases the state's sales tax rate for all taxpayers and the personal income tax rates for upper-income taxpayers.

 

The new revenues generated from Proposition 30 are deposited into a newly-created state account called the Education Protection Account (EPA). School districts, county offices of education, and charter schools (Local Education Agencies or LEAs) will receive funds from the EPA based on their proportionate share of the statewide revenue limit amount, which includes charter school general purpose funding.

 

A corresponding reduction was made to an LEA's revenue limit or charter school general purpose state aid equal to the amount of their EPA entitlement. For 2012-13, LEAs received EPA payments at the end of June 2013. Beginning with the 2013-14 fiscal year, LEAs received these EPA payments on a quarterly basis which commenced in September 2013.

 

Proposition 30 requires all districts, counties, and charter schools to report on their websites an accounting of how much money was received from the EPA, how that money was spent, and that the board acknowledge that the EPA funds will not be spent on administrators or other administrative costs. Proposition 30 also requires that the use of EPA funds be determined by the governing board at an open public meeting prior to any expenditures being recorded for 2017-18.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): Section 15300.2 of the State CEQA Guidelines specifies several exceptions to the use of a categorical exemption.

  • Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the Project is to be located – a Project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant, therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the Project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  • Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  • Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  • Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway.   • Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  • Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Costs related to the design, construction and maintenance of EV charging stations will be the responsibility of SDG&E, including California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) and permits. Additionally, SDG&E will provide corresponding Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) upgrades to the EV charging stations, at no cost to the District.

 

The operation of these charging stations will not affect the District’s utility budget as the charging stations will be electrically fed and metered separately from the school district, and each EV customer will register and pay for power through their own residential electricity bill.  Based on the requirements of the Power Your Drive program, only District employees will be eligible to use the EV charging stations.

 

The Proposed Project includes the installation of EV charging stations within existing parking lots at three District sites. At the PPO site, 20 existing parking spaces would be converted to 18 new EV stalls. Nine double bollard chargers would be installed to provide EV charging to the 18 stalls. Approximately 225-feet of trenching would be required to connect the EV charging stations to a new proposed meter and existing transformer. Upgrades to the existing transformer would be required. At the transportation administration site, 14 existing parking spaces would be converted to 12 new EV stalls. Six double bollard chargers would be installed to provide EV charging to the 12 stalls. At Lincoln High School, 11 parking spaces would be converted into 10 New EV Stalls. Two single and four double bollard chargers would be installed to provide EV charging to the 10 stalls. Approximately 340-feet of trenching would be required to connect the EV charging stations to a new proposed meter and existing transformer. Upgrades to the existing transformer may be required. Energy use would be metered and charged to the registered user and not the District.

 

Underground trenching from the existing switch gear would occur entirely within existing district property. No mature trees, sensitive vegetation, or habitat would be distributed. The proposed charging stations would be installed between December 2018 and January 2019. Installation at each site would take approximately two-weeks and would not require large construction equipment such as cranes, graders, or excavators. Construction is scheduled during winter break to avoid disruptions to staff and students.

  • Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time, is significant.
  • Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.

  • Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

BACKGROUND: The district purchases various sizes and colors of xerographic copy paper throughout the year for school sites and departments. The district is an Associate Member of the North County Educational Purchasing Consortium (NCEPC), therefore is eligible to use NCEPC competitively awarded contracts. Under Public Contract Code (PCC) Section 20118, the governing board has determined that it is in the best interest of the district to utilize this "piggyback" contract to purchase xerographic copy paper. PCC 20118 provides that the district may purchase personal property from contracts already competitively bid by another public agency. The district may only order products specified in the contract at the prices specified in the contract. The district will utilize line 1C of the bid for the purchase of carload paper at $23.60 a case. 

 

On July 8, 2014, the board received the final report from the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee. Included in this report as a "Dream Big" idea was a recommended action to join or create larger purchasing cooperatives. A purchasing cooperative establishes various contracts which aggregate the total purchases from many government entities resulting in lower costs to governmental entities who then utilize these contracts.

RECOMMENDATION: Ratify Agreement with DD Office Products, Inc. dba Liberty Paper to provide xerographic copy paper 8.5" X 11"(GD18-1091-19). Authorize the execution of any documents necessary to effectuate these Agreements. The term of this Agreement is June 1, 2018 through June 20, 2018.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount: $46,000.00. Budget string: 5814-00000-00-9520-0000-0000-66001-0000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: For the period February 14, 2018 through June 11, 2018: $247,743.97.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The District intends to utilize the Los Angeles County Office of Education Bid (17/18-1607) for the urgent purchase of two (2) truckloads (1,680 cases of paper) of xerographic copy paper 8.5" x 11" from DD Office Products, Inc. dba Liberty Paper​ at $24.95 p/case​​​​​​, in order to reduce the backorder log from schools district-wide.  

 

On December 9, 2009, the board approved a plan to restore xerographic copy paper 8.5" X 11" in the district warehouse. The Distribution Services Department has been delivering xerographic copy paper to schools and administrative departments on an as-required basis since its approval.

 

On July 8, 2014, the board received the final report from the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee. Included in this report as a "Dream Big" idea was a recommended action to join or create larger purchasing cooperatives. A purchasing cooperative establishes various contracts which aggregate the total purchases from many government entities resulting in lower costs to governmental entities who then utilize these contracts.
