BACKGROUND: Fahari Jeffers was born in San Diego on May 8, 1954 to Jessie Pearl Russell-Jeffers and Joseph Louis Jeffers, both natives of Russell Union in Norlina, North Carolina. Fahari means “magnificent and rare” in Swahili. At the age of 14, Fahari dropped out of Morse High School and became homeless. Despite her many personal challenges, Fahari became a Youth Leader with the Black Power organization NIA – joined a dance troupe and taught classes in Afro-American culture to her peers. Inspired to continue her education, Fahari returned to Snyder Continuation School, finishing a year and a half of studies in six months, earning a high school diploma in 1971 at the age of seventeen. Fahari moved on to earn a BA in Psychology from SDSU, Master of Public Administration from National University and Juris Doctorate from Thomas Jefferson School of Law. In 1978, under the guidance and training of Cesar Chavez, Fahari and her husband Ken Seaton Msemaji co-founded the United Domestic Workers of America, a labor union empowering home healthcare workers. Today, UDW is the largest union in San Diego County, and one of the fastest growing unions in California and the United States. UDW became the third union in American history founded by people of color, following A. Phillip Randolph’s Sleeping Car Porter’s Union, and Cesar Chavez’s United Farmworkers. Fahari went on to serve as President of the Black Federation of San Diego County, the San Diego Convention Center Board of Directors, received the Ford Foundation Leadership Award and was honored in 2018 as an inductee in the San Diego Women’s Hall of Fame. Following her retirement from UDW in 2005, Fahari became a leader of Educate for the Future, a community-based grassroots organizing effort advocating for greater support and resources for public schools in San Diego Unified and throughout California. Fahari overcame multiple health issues during her life, including asthma, thyroid, diabetes and myasthenia gravis, and sadly passed away on October 30th, 2019.

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA). Title I, one of several programs under ESSA, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the district and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of "children in poverty" residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The proposed 2020-21 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2019-20 meal eligibility percentage of 40 percent or higher. There were four schools whose percentage rose above 40 percentage from last year and who will be added to the Title I Program for 2020-21. These schools are Tierrasanta and Hage elementary schools; DePortola Middle School; and University City High School. In addition, seven additional schools fell below 40 percent from last year and are recommended to be grandfathered for one year. These schools are Gage and Ericson elementary schools; Pacific Beach and Standley middle schools; Henry, San Diego Metropolitan, Regional, Career and Technical, and iHigh Virtual Academy high schools. The total 2020-21 Title I Entitlement is approximately $38,000,000.00.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.  Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education received various training regarding specific plan requirements. Specifically, the DAC received information and provided feedback on the timeline and components of developing the SPSA beginning with their January 2019 meeting: January 23 (Take to Your SSCs), February 20 (SPSA Update), March 20 (Take to Your SSCs), and September 18 (SPSA Timeline Update). 

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. SPSAs are created under this part may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeed Act (Public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.

 

Additionally, Financial Planning, Monitoring and Accountability (FPMA) staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs.  Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the FPMA Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.  The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2019-20 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 162 schools.
 
Area superintendents periodically review site plans during visits to schools and discuss progress with principals as determined by the measurable outcomes.  In addition, Financial Planning, Monitoring and Accountability resource teachers monitor progress made, and work with area superintendents and principals to evaluate the plans.

 

Electronic copies of each 2019-20 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required, signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item; hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103.

BACKGROUND: The origin of Black History Month began in 1915, half a century after the Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery in the United States, and grew out of “Negro History Week,” the creation of noted historian Carter G. Woodson and other prominent African Americans. With the Civil Rights Movement and a growing awareness of black identity, Black History Month or National African American History Month evolved to an annual celebration of achievements by black Americans and a time for recognizing the central role of African Americans in US history. The truth must be disseminated and American history must include an accurate account of the contributions of African Americans and all ethnic groups, not just in the month of February, but all year. African American history reflects a determined spirit of perseverance and cultural pride through struggles to equally share in the opportunities of a nation founded upon the principles of freedom and liberty. The contributions of African Americans from all walks of life and their endeavors to learn and thrive throughout history and make unforgettable marks in our Nation as artists, scientists, educators, business people, influential thinkers, members of the faith community, athletes and political and governmental leaders, reflects the greatness of the United States. African Americans constitute 8.2 percent of the students in the San Diego Unified School District. The San Diego Unified School District wishes to highlight the beauty and richness of Black History month, while creating classrooms where students can reflect on how their identities can shape the way they experience the world, acknowledge the courageous fight for the rights, liberties, and freedoms for all Americans, by observing and honoring Black History Month and encouraging educators, students, and families to learn about the heritage and achievements of African-Americans. Districtwide activities highlighting the contributions of African Americans will be offered each school day during the month of February, including musical performances, leadership workshops, movies, a Harlem Renaissance Faire, a Black Minds Matter Resource Fair, pep rallies, college information and scholarship opportunities, historical presentations and displays, artistic and cultural events, and field trips, a calendar of which is available on the San Diego Unified School District website. African American history curriculum and materials are available to all San Diego Unified educators.

RECOMMENDATION: Authorize staff to proceed with development and implementation a Captive Insurance Program or establishment of a Joint Powers Authority (JPA), to provide for Non-Insured/Retained General Liability and Workers' Compensation Exposures of the district.  Coverage will be for insured retention claims, lawsuits, and settlements involving general liability and workers' compensation claims, including allocating $2,000,000 in initial capital to be paid from anticipated insurance recoveries in the upcoming fiscal year.  The captive program will be fully developed and obtain all necessary state and federal authorizations during the period beginning board approval date through the end of June 2020, and shall be operational on July 1, 2020.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The captive program or JPA will be funded with a combination of funds that would otherwise be deposited in Fund 67 to cover the district's self-insured retention and proceeds from anticipated insurance recoveries by Legal Services in conjunction with the Insurance and Risk Services Department over the upcoming fiscal year, which is anticipated to exceed the minimum $2 million in seed money needed to establish the fund; General Liability Fund (6701) and Workers Compensation Fund (6703).

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: In Fiscal Year 2020, there will be no impact on staffing. In Fiscal Year 2021, there will be an increase in staffing by one position.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Yes

 

BACKGROUND: Over the past several years, the cost of casualty insurance has continually increased at double digit percentage rates. The cost of general liability insurance has gone from approximately $786,000 in 2017 to $1.25M in 2019, and the projections indicate it will continue to rise for the foreseeable future.  There are several factors involved in this matter, including but not limited to, an increase in sexual molestation claims against school districts, traumatic brain injury claims involving current and former student-athletes around the country as well as the increased cost of litigation. Specific to California is the new law, which went into effect as of January 1, 2020, that extends the statute of limitations (SOL) for sexual abuse cases from the age of 26 to the age of 40. The potential for these now revived claims to reopen has further caused projections to rise and the possibility of insurers limiting or excluding coverage for these sorts of claims is now a distinct possibility.  With this backdrop, the district's Manager, Risk and Insurance conducted a top-down review of the district's exposures and potential alternatives to traditional insurance mechanisms. Creation of a captive insurance program was determined to be the best program available. It not only controls costs, it also will earn revenue over time so that the initial allocation of $2M will be paid back by year 7 of the program and commencing in year 8, the program will earn positive revenue to help defray the cost of excess casualty and workers compensation insurance as well as to pay for the overhead of the Insurance and Risk Services Department for the district.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: Approval of the agreement for construction and subsequent operation of the joint use field and site improvements at Innovation Middle School is a project under CEQA, Public Resources Code section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The approval of the agreement for demolition of portable buildings at Carver Elementary School is subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities. Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. Removal of the 13 existing portable buildings represents a minor alteration to an existing public facility and would not result in the expansion of use of the school. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 Exemption is appropriate for this project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.
