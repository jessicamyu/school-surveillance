FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: During the Civil Rights Movement, the campaign to recognize the history, struggle, contributions, and achievements of Hispanic Americans gained traction. In 1968, California Congressman George E. Brown introduced House Joint Resolution 1299 to commemorate the contributions of Hispanic and Latinx Americans, authorizing President Lyndon B. Johnson to proclaim the week of September 15, 1968 as the first National Hispanic Heritage Week. In 1988, President Ronald Reagan declared September 15 through October 15 as National Hispanic Heritage Month. Hispanic Heritage Month begins on September 15th and ends on October 15th to coincide with the independence dates of five Latin American countries who declared their independence from Spain in 1821: Costa Rica, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, and Nicaragua. Hispanic Heritage week commemorates Chile’s independence from Spain on September 18, 1810. Hispanic Heritage week commemorates Mexico’s independence from Spain on September 16, 1821. Hispanic Heritage week commemorates Belize’s independence from Great Britain on September 21, 1981. Each year, Americans are encouraged to deepen their understanding of the Latinx community by celebrating the histories, cultures and contributions of Americans whose ancestors represent pre-Columbian civilizations included the Olmect, Maya, Zapotec, Teotihuacan, Mixtec, and Aztec, demonstrating kingdoms, empires, sophisticated cities, intellectual accomplishments and vibrant indigenous cultures developed in parts of Mexico, Central and South America, prior to Spanish conquest. The United States history includes indigenous cultures such as Latinx communities that express and demonstrate incredibly diverse cultures, represented by dialects, languages and richly historical cultures. The United States Latinx and Hispanic population comprises 60.4 million people or 18.5 percent of the population, 30.3 percent of the population of San Diego County, and 46.5 percent of the San Diego Unified student body identify as Hispanic. The Latinx or Hispanic communities continue to contribute significantly to the political, economic, and social development of the nation. The San Diego Unified School District will continue its commitment of eliminating barriers that prevent our Latinx students from receiving equitable access and commit to becoming an Anti-Bias Anti-Racist Restorative district that centers Ethnic Studies for all. The San Diego Unified School District recognizes the diversity reflected within the City of San Diego as well as its students and staff and respects the Latinx community’s contributions, culture, and heritage. The San Diego Unified School District recognizes the diversity represented within Latinx communities, such as Black and Indigenous Latinx populations, and commits to creating spaces where the identities of these students are represented and celebrated year-round.

According to the U.S. Surgeon General, e-cigarettes and other electronic smoking devices contain nicotine, which is a highly addictive drug that has immediate biochemical effects on the brain and body, alters adolescent brain development, and is toxic in high doses. Compared with older adults, the brain of youth and young adults are more vulnerable to the negative consequences of nicotine exposure. The effects include addiction, priming for use of other addictive substances, reduced impulse control, deficits in attention and cognition, and mood disorders. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and other analyses have found that tobacco products such as e-cigarettes and other electronic smoking devices contain toxins, carcinogens and components suspected of being harmful to humans and has acknowledged that consumers of e-cigarette products currently have no way of knowing whether e-cigarettes and similar products are safe or how much nicotine or other potentially harmful chemicals are being inhaled. Further, there is no regulatory oversight of the manufacturing process, and injuries and deaths have occurred from e-cigarettes and similar products and the liquid nicotine they use. There is a popular misconception that the use of e-cigarettes is safe, it should be noted and commented to students and families that nicotine delivery is vastly more efficient/potent with e-cigarettes than with traditional cigarettes. Big tobacco continues to profit from the research-proven fact that the earlier a person starts to use tobacco, the harder it will become for them to quit. Big tobacco targets young people and vulnerable communities with tobacco products that mask the harsh taste of tobacco with flavors highly appealing to youth (including bubble gum, cotton candy, grape, gummy bear, and menthol flavors). Big tobacco has historically targeted communities of color with aggressive marketing of mentholated tobacco products, resulting in disproportionate rates of tobacco-related disease and death (e.g., 95 percent of Black youth smokers, 61 percent of Asian youth smokers, and 58 percent of Latino youth smokers report use of menthol-flavored cigarettes). Big tobacco has over 900 locations selling its products in the City of San Diego and more than one third of those are within 1,000 feet of a school. Local jurisdictions in California have the authority to decrease the availability and use of tobacco products by youth by eliminating the sale of flavored tobacco products and implementing effective tobacco retail licensing programs.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change recently released their sixth and most dire assessment report yet, with UN Secretary-General António Guterres characterizing the report as “nothing less than a code red for humanity.” The World Meteorological Organization has recorded a fivefold increase in the number of weather, climate, and water-related disasters over the past 50 years, which will become even more frequent as a consequence of ongoing climate change. Record heat waves and fires continue to ravage California, while the South recovers from Hurricane Ida, the second-most damaging hurricane to strike the state of Louisiana. Climate change is likely to increase the incidence of zoonotic disease, of which the consequences are clearly evident due to the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic. COVID-19 has clearly demonstrated that disasters exacerbate existing social, economic, and racial inequalities. US leadership has made strides in recognizing the urgent need to combat the climate crisis by rejoining the Paris Agreement and setting ambitious emissions goals but has not yet passed all of the policies needed to achieve said emissions reductions. Congress is drafting a $3.5 trillion budget reconciliation package to be voted on no later than September 27th, which could contain funds devoted to emissions reductions and a Just Transition away from fossil fuel dependence. The 2021 United Nations Climate Change Conference is to be held beginning October 31st, 2021, with potential to spur more rapid and drastic climate action and collaboration in the US and internationally. The San Diego Unified Board of Education has previously recognized the threat from global warming by calling on the California State Teachers’ Retirement System (STRS) and the California Public Employees’ Retirement System (PERS) to divest their investment portfolios of stocks in fossil fuel companies. The Board of Education has adopted Resolutions in Support of Community Choice Aggregation, PTA-Climate Change is a Children's Issue, and Supporting Climate Science Literacy. The San Diego Unified School District’s “Dream Big Solutions for a Sustainable Future” plan ensures district climate action and climate consciousness and its Climate Action Plan commits to 100% renewable energy by 2035. San Diego Unified is taking steps towards meeting its goals by retrofitting schools with more efficient air conditioning and lighting systems; and more than 29,000 lighting fixtures have been converted to LED, offsetting 1,300 metric tons of CO2 gas emissions; and projected district solar energy production by the end of 2021 will offset 8,700 tons of CO2. San Diego Unified has funded the construction of hundreds of acres of parks and green spaces through the joint-use partnership with the City of San Diego; and reduced litter and plastic waste by nearly 4.7 million pieces through the replacement of utensil packets with individual forks, spoons, and napkins. Students from San Diego Unified have taken a leadership role in confronting global warming through participation in “Green Teams,” Eco Clubs, Environmental Justice Clubs, and other student groups. The “Love Food Not Waste” program has prevented 202,775 pounds of prepared food from ending up in landfills, provided 169,120 meals to the hungry, reduced food costs, and eliminated 110,107 pounds of CO2 gas emissions. The San Diego Unified School District has previously supported youth activists by adopting a resolution pressuring Governor Newsom to immediately phase out oil extraction. The San Diego Unified School District has supported prior student protests for the Global Climate Walkout in 2019. There will be a Global Climate Strike and Walkout on September 24th, 2021 led by youth climate leaders to demonstrate to world leaders at all levels of government that the people, especially the youth, are not satisfied with the insufficient response to climate change.
