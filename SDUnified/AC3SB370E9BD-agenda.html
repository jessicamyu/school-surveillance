RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution recognizing May 22, 2017, as Harvey Milk Day in San Diego Unified.

 

BACKGROUND: Harvey Milk was born May 22, 1930, in Woodmere, New York.

 

Harvey served in the United States Navy during the Korean War as a diving officer aboard the submarine rescue ship USS Kittiwake and was later transferred to Naval Station, San Diego to serve as a diving instructor with the rank of Lieutenant.

 

Harvey was a tireless advocate of fairness for all Americans, both as a candidate for office and as a San Francisco Supervisor.

 

In 1977, Harvey was elected to the San Francisco Board of Supervisors, becoming the first openly gay man to be elected to public office in California. This milestone achievement gave hope to millions of gays and lesbians across the country that a day would come when they could live their lives openly and honestly without fear of discrimination.

 

As a Supervisor, Harvey worked with others to secure passage of San Francisco’s landmark Gay Rights Ordinance. This ordinance extended employment protections to gays and lesbians in San Francisco, and it became a model for anti-discrimination legislation throughout California and the nation.

 

In 1978, Harvey traversed the state to campaign against Proposition 6, which was known as the Briggs Initiative. Had it passed, Proposition 6 would have required California school districts to fire openly gay and lesbian teachers solely because of their sexual orientation. The proposition was defeated in the November 1978 election in part because Harvey successfully appealed to Californians’ basic sense of fairness.

 

Although Harvey’s life was cut short, he was a hero and role model, and his legacy of hope, tolerance, and equality lives on.

The intent of the Legislature, as stated in California Education Code Section 47601, in enacting the Charter Schools Act, included providing opportunities for the establishment of schools that operate independently from school districts, in order to expand pupil learning experiences, encourage the use of different and innovative teaching methods, and provide pupils with expanded choices in the types of educational opportunities available in public schools.  In comparing NUA to the nearest district (Kearny High) and charter (School for Entrepreneurship and Technology, formerly known as Coleman Tech Charter School) schools, there are some similarities, including serving many of the same grade levels, and offering a STEAM focus.  Coleman Tech was also partnered with and received support from a university (Coleman University).  The most significant difference is that the primary educational program offered at NUA is a non-classroom based model.  It is noted that the uniqueness of the educational opportunities offered by a charter school are not a basis for the approval or denial of the charter petition.

 

Education Code Section 47605(b) states that the governing board of a school district shall not deny a petition for the establishment of a charter school unless it makes written factual findings, specific to the particular petition, setting forth specific facts to support one or more of the following findings:

  1. The charter school presents an unsound educational program for the pupils to be enrolled in the charter school.
  2. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the petition.
  3. The petition does not contain the number of signatures required by subdivision (a).
  4. The petition does not contain an affirmation of each of the conditions described in subdivision (d).
  5. The petition does not contain reasonably comprehensive descriptions.

10.  The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because the school has presented an unrealistic financial and operational plan as the budget does not appear to include all expenditures.  Page 124 of the petition indicates that “all staff members will participate in the NUA Board approved Salary Schedule, which includes health benefit packages.”  However, the budget and multi-year projections did not include health benefit costs for full time instructional aides.  District staff has estimated that the cost for the health benefits totals approximately $16,800 per year for two instructional aides.

11. The petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the charter petition because the budget is based on overstated ADA and incorrect assumptions, resulting in a financial plan that is not sustainable and not fiscally viable.  The financial information presented in the petition has incorrect enrollment and ADA.  Because of this, district staff had to adjust salaries, benefits, supplies, and operational items in order to accurately review the budget information. 

 

The ADA used to calculate revenues includes Grades K through 5, even though the school has not submitted a petition to serve these grades.  NUA used an outdated version of the LCFF calculator to project revenues.  A more recent calculator was made available on January 11, 2017, over a month prior to NUA submitting the petition and financial documents.  The more recent calculator includes updated COLA and LCFF gap funding percentage assumptions and provides for the extension of EPA from Prop. 55.  Based on district staff calculations, LCFF revenues are overstated by $284,992, $350,189, $527,217, and $544,097 in fiscal years 2017-18 through 2020-21, respectively.

 

Calculations for both Proposition 20 and non-Proposition 20 lottery funds include ADA for grades K-5, even though the school has not submitted a petition to serve these grades.  Because of the inclusion of these ADA, Lottery revenues are overstated by $5,415, $6,498, $9,727, and $10,700 in fiscal years 2017-18 through 2020-21, respectively.

 

The budget and multi-year projections include College Readiness Block Grant (CRBG) funding.  This categorical program was made available for fiscal year 2016-17.  There is no guarantee the program will be made available in future years.  CRBG is included at $75,000 each fiscal year from 2017-18 through 2020-21, resulting in an overstatement of revenue.

 

After adjusting the budget and multi-year projections for overstated enrollment and ADA, and correcting revenue and expenditure assumptions, the ending fund balance for fiscal year 2017-18 is projected to be $(307,572).  Negative ending fund balances for fiscal years 2018-19 through 2020-21 are also projected, and would vary in size based on whether the school remained a school of the district for special education purposes.  If they do, ending fund balances for 2018-19 through 2020-21 are projected at $(615,192), $(959,390), and  $(1,329,937), respectively.

 

The budget and multi-year projections include One-Time Funds for Outstanding Mandate Claims.  The Governor’s Proposed Budget includes one-time funds of approximately $48 per ADA for fiscal year 2017-18.  There is no guarantee these funds will be available in future years.  One-Time Funds for Outstanding Mandate claims are overstated $33,463, $38,887, and $44,581 in fiscal year 2018-19 through 2020-21, respectively.

BACKGROUND:  The proposed project involves the redevelopment of Wilson Middle School and the relocation of Central Elementary School to the Wilson Middle School Campus; however, the two schools would remain as two separate independent schools (as opposed to a consolidated program). In general, under the proposed project, Wilson Middle School and its associated facilities would be located within the central and western portions of the proposed project site, and Central Elementary would be constructed within the eastern portion. The proposed redevelopment involves the following:

 

Wilson Middle School

  1. There would be a total of approximately 30 classrooms, band room, orchestra room, physical education room, and locker rooms; several rooms to support theater arts, gallery, and tools/robotics; an outdoor work area; a theater with a stage, a media room, a reading room, a second-story multi-purpose room, a kitchen, a food court, a lunch shelter, a custodian room, and a room for an afterschool program.
  2. Classrooms would consist of 20 general, 3 special education, 5 science, and 2 open classrooms. All special education classrooms would be on the ground level, and 14 general classrooms and one science classroom would be on the second floor.
  3. Building 100 would be at the corner of Orange Avenue and 37th Street.
  4. Full capacity is anticipated to be 1,000 students, which would involve a decrease in the 1,505-student existing capacity by approximately 500 students. However, this would represent an increase of approximately 380 middle students over the existing enrollment of 625 students. 
  5. It is anticipated that the proposed project would also require an increase in staff from an existing 46 personnel to approximately 75 personnel.

Central Elementary School

  1. There would be a total of 46 classrooms, 3 daycare rooms, a two-story administration building, library, a multi-purpose room, a warming kitchen, a custodian room, and a lunch shelter.
  2. The breakdown of the classrooms consists of 30 general, 1 science/PE, 5 special education, 6 kindergarten, and 4 pre-kindergarten classrooms.
  3. A total of three play areas would be provided within the Central Elementary side of the campus.
  4. Full capacity is anticipated to be 1,000 students. This would involve an increase in capacity of 50 elementary school students beyond the existing capacity of 958 students.
  5. It is anticipated that the proposed project would also require an increase in staff from an existing 78 personnel to approximately 100 personnel. 

Shared Facilities

  1. Implementation of ornamental vegetation that would be scattered throughout the proposed project area.
  2. A fire lane accessed along El Cajon Boulevard would run through the middle of the site, and a surrounding fence would be installed around the proposed Central Elementary School campus.
  3. A new storm drain network would be installed to cover the entire site.
  4. A joint-use field would be added along Orange Avenue toward the middle of the block between 37th and 39th Streets.
  5. Parking for the proposed project would consist of a total of 286 spaces: 239 parking spaces in a below-grade parking structure, 29 existing spaces in Lots 1 and 2 along 39th Street, 18 spaces along Orange Avenue, and the existing 28 angled parking spaces that are provided along 37th Street.
  6. Phase I Street and Public Improvements: traffic calming features to enhance pedestrian access and ensure pedestrian safety along Orange Avenue.
  7. Sustainable Design Features: photovoltaic (PV) panels and other sustainable features.
  8. Environmental Process

The district has complied with the requirements of CEQA (Public Resources Code Section 21000 et seq.) and CEQA Guidelines (California Administrative Code Section 15000 et seq.) for the project. A Notice of Availability of the Draft EIR was published in the San Diego Union-Tribune and La Prensa and filed at the County of San Diego and California State Clearinghouse. Copies of the Draft EIR were sent to the State Clearinghouse, state and local agencies, and community entities and organizations. The Draft EIR was made available at the following locations:

Environmental Impacts

The district received 3 comment letters on the Draft EIR, from the Governor’s Office of Planning and Research, State of California Native American Heritage Commission, and the California Department of Toxic Substances Control. No letters from individuals were received.

 

Responses to all comments received on the Draft EIR are included in the Final EIR (Attachment B). However, no revisions to the Draft EIR were necessary in response to the comments received. None of the revisions made have changed the conclusions of the Draft EIR or are considered significant new information. The Final EIR concludes that the project would result in significant and unavoidable impacts related to increased traffic delay at intersections and exceeding allowable vehicle/capacity ratios. While the implementation of mitigation would reduce the impact related to operational traffic impacts, it would not fully reduce them to a less-than-significant level and are significant and unavoidable at certain intersections in the year 2021. All other environmental topic areas studied, per Appendix G of the CEQA Guidelines, were found to have no impact or a less-than-significant impact on the environment. Feasible mitigation measures have been identified to mitigate potentially significant impacts related to air quality, cultural resources, hazards and hazardous materials, and noise and vibration. They have been included in the Mitigation Monitoring and Reporting Program (Attachment C).   

 

Written Findings (Attachment D) have been prepared for each of the significant effects. Additionally, because the project would result in significant and unavoidable environmental effects that cannot be avoided or lessened, the proposed approval of the project would require the board to adopt a Statement of Overriding Considerations (Attachment D), which requires the board to balance the benefits of the project against its unavoidable environmental effects. Should the board determine that the benefits of the project outweigh the unavoidable adverse environmental effects, these effects may be considered “acceptable,” and the project may be approved, at which time the board may direct staff to file the Notice of Determination (Attachment E).

BACKGROUND: Commencing April 13, 2016, San Diego Unified started experiencing a persistent series of computer attacks. These attacks caused daily interruptions to all district instruction and operations services reliant on Internet access. These attacks are classified as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) intrusions. DDoS attacks are essentially a coordinated storm or large volume of server requests that overwhelm a targeted system and eventually render it unusable. For example, the hackers sent more than 100 million concurrent connection requests to PowerSchool. This volume of requests causes the specific system to become overwhelmed, which then floods the district’s central system and causes failure. While these attacks are underway, the district’s infrastructure is unable to discern the legitimate traffic from the malicious web traffic. From a student or staff perspective, it causes Internet delays or failed Internet connections. The Integrated Technology and Support Services (ITSS) team is acutely aware of the severity of the situation and negative impact on teaching and learning, and we believe this solution will minimize interruptions to learning. 

 

A Request for Proposal was issued to determine an optimal technical and cost solution to minimize interruptions to district operations and services reliant on internet access.  On October 25, 2016, the district received six proposals for a network appliance to stop Distributed Denial of Service attacks and allow normal traffic to and from the internet.

 

The proposals were received from:

  • AT&T
  • Corero
  • ​DataLink
  • ​Presidio
  • Radware
  • ​Vector

An evaluation committee, consisting of district staff from the Integrated Technology Support Services and Finance Divisions assessed all proposals received. This agreement with Corero Network Security Inc., will provide a network security server that will recognize and help stop DDoS attacks, while allowing legitimate traffic and access to the Internet to continue.  This agreement will upgrade and expand information systems and technology throughout the district to support 21st century classrooms, efficient operations, student and parent services.  After a thorough RFP evaluation process that included the industry leaders in network security, the evaluation committee and ITSS recommends approval.

  1. Determine that the Project is exempt from further environmental review under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) and direct staff to file the Notice of Exemption (NOE) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk and California Office of Planning and Research State Clearinghouse.
  2. Approve the site plans for the De Portola Middle School furnishing and installation of a steel canopy shelter above lunch line area Project (Proposed Project).  This Project will be attached to roof the structure of an existing building and will cantilever over the area to be covered. 
  3. Approve Agreement with SDSTATURE for furnishing and installing steel canopy shelter above lunch line area consistent with the approved site plan at De Portola Middle School (CZ-17-0841-08).

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount of $132,293.00 consisting of $132,243.00 (contract amount) and $50.00 (Notice of Exemption filing fee).  Budget strings: 9327-90112-00-6200-8500-0000-21312-8531 and 9327-90402-00-6123-8500-0000-21400-8531. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111 (b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more.  The district advertised the Proposed Project in accordance with the Public Contract Code section 20112.  Bids were opened on March 9, 2017.  The district received a total of seven bids from Blue Pacific Engineering & Construction, Fordyce Construction, Inc., Grace Builders Inc., Handy Industrial, Khavari Construction, Inc., SD Remodeling, Inc., SDSTATURE. 

 

After evaluating the bids, staff determined SDSTATURE to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder. Additionally, consistent with the May 10, 2011, board approved resolution in support of service-disabled, veteran-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled, veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a 3 percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities.  The recommended contractor, SDSTATURE submitted a 6.03 percent DVBE participation in its bid.  The construction contract is effective as of May 19, 2017 and continues until the Proposed Project is complete.

 

The project consists of the installation of a steel canopy lunch waiting line shelter and minor site accessibility improvements in a parking lot at De Portola Elementary School located at 11010 Clairemont Mesa Boulevard, San Diego CA  92124.

 

The proposed steel canopy would be installed on the existing cafeteria facade at building 800 over the lunch serving line to protect the students from weather while they are receiving food. This new canopy matches others canopies already on campus. 

 

In addition, minor site accessibility improvements will be constructed, which are: partial demolition and replacement of existing sidewalks and curbs to change the slope to comply with current code.  Installation of raised detectable warning strips on sidewalks leading to crosswalk.  Painting of pavement with ADA striping to denote new crosswalk per current ADA code.

 

Environmental Review

The Project is subject to CEQA, Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The Project, however, is exempt from expanded environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Class 1, Existing Facilities and Class 11, Accessory Structures, as further described in the attached NOE and summarized below:

 

15301. Existing Facilities (Class 1) / 15311. Accessory Structures (Class 11). The Proposed Project would result in minor alterations to the existing cafeteria building 800 and existing sidewalks but would not change the existing uses and operations within the school, will not result in expansion or use beyond that existing at the time of the determination, and will not require any disturbance to native soils that would impact an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 and Class 11 categorical exemption is appropriate. Categorical exemptions may be not be relied upon, however, if the application of the exemption triggers exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2.   As set forth in Attachments 1 and 2, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemption, and approval of the project would not compromise the environmental quality of the site and surrounding area. Approval of this item authorizes and direct staff to file, with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk a Notice of Exemption in substantial conformance with the attached Notice of Exemption and awards the construction contract to SDSTATURE.
