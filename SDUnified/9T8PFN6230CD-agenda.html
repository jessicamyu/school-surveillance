RECOMMENDATION: Approve early admission, at the beginning of the 2015-16 school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a Transitional Kindergarten (TK) program as per recent changes to the California Education Code (EC) as a pilot program within the Henry Cluster, specifically Marvin, Benchley-Weinberger, Hearst, Hardy, Green, Dailard, Foster, and Gage elementary schools.

 

This 2015-16 pilot in the Henry Cluster will allow the district to study the community interest, impact on teaching and learning, and the impact on enrollment of extending the TK eligibility date deadline from December 2, 2015 to March 2, 2016. It will help us determine both the challenges and benefits in moving this expanded program into additional schools the following year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The pilot program is subject to a potential staffing adjustment of up to four teachers based on enrollment projections, which would result in an approximate cost to the district of $427,428.84. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Potential staffing adjustment of up to 4 teachers based on enrollment projections.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The laws applicable to transitional kindergarten were changed as a result of Assembly Bill (AB) 104, the Education Omnibus Trailer Bill. The new law, effective July 1, 2015, permits local educational agencies and charter schools for the first time the option to grant early admission, at the beginning of the school year, for pupils who are otherwise too young to attend a TK program. AB 104 amended the California Education Code (EC) to add subsection (B) to EC 48000(c)(3) and states the following.

 

(B) (i) For the 2015-16 school year and each school year thereafter, a school district or charter school may, at any time during a school year, admit a child to a transitional kindergarten program who will have his or her fifth birthday after December 2, but during that same school year, with the approval of the parent or guardian, subject to the following conditions:

 

(I) The governing board of the school district or the governing body of the charter school determines that the admittance is in the best interest of the child.

 

(II) The parent or guardian is given information regarding the advantages and disadvantages and any other explanatory information about the effect of this early admittance.

 

(ii) Notwithstanding any other law, a pupil admitted to a transitional kindergarten program pursuant to clause (i) shall not generate average daily attendance for purposes of Section 46300, or be included in the enrollment or unduplicated pupil count pursuant to Section 42238.02, until the pupil has attained his or her fifth birthday, regardless of when the pupil was admitted during the school year.

 

We would like to move forward with a pilot program within the Henry Cluster, specifically Marvin, Benchley-Weinberger, Hearst, Hardy, Green, Dailard, Foster, and Gage elementary schools. These schools were selected because there are no district preschool options within this cluster and the principals expressed a strong interest.

 

This small-scale pilot will allow the district to study the community interest, impact on teaching and learning, and the impact on enrollment of extending the TK eligibility date to March 2, 2016. It will help us determine both the challenges and benefits in moving this expanded program into additional schools the following year.

 

By moving forward with this pilot program at the start of the 2015-16 school year Leadership and Learning will be able to provide the pilot schools with the supports and resources they will need to assure that our youngest learners have access to TK programs by the first day of school.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution supporting Circulate San Diego's Vision Zero Plan to reduce traffic fatalities to zero in the next ten years, and recommend district staff participate, as appropriate, in Circulate San Diego’s Vision Zero Advisory Committee meetings.

 

BACKGROUND: On average one person each day is seriously injured or killed on the road while walking, biking, or driving the streets of San Diego.

 

A high percentage of traffic injuries and fatalities involve pedestrians, bicyclists, and other vulnerable users.

 

Traffic fatalities are the leading cause of accidental death among children ages 0 to 13 in the City of San Diego.

 

Having safe walking and biking routes to school encourages enrollment in neighborhood schools and supports the San Diego Unified Vision 2020 goal of Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.

 

San Diego Unified is pursuing a Climate Action Plan and safer conditions for walking and biking can help reduce the reliance on motor vehicles to transport students and staff to and from school.

 

The City will increase in population by approximately 30 percent by 2050, thereby increasing demand for safe walking and bicycling.

 

Vision Zero provides a framework for reducing traffic deaths to zero through a combination of safe engineering measures, education, and enforcement practices.

 

Vision Zero has been adopted in many cities throughout the country, most notably in New York City which has seen the lowest number of pedestrian fatalities in its first year of implementation since documentation began in 1910.

 

Circulate San Diego is convening an Advisory Committee to advance Vision Zero goals.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supports the vision of reducing traffic deaths to zero by 2025, by prioritizing safety within infrastructure projects already underway, and combining with education and awareness programs.

BACKGROUND: An election was held for the San Diego Unified School District, County of San Diego, California on November 4, 2008, at which electors of the district approved, by more than 55 percent of the votes cast, Proposition S authorizing the district to issue up to $2.1 billion in general obligation bonds pursuant to California Constitution Article XIII (A), to be used for the specific school facilities projects set forth in Proposition S.

 

Pursuant to Proposition S, the district shall only issue bonds if it projects that the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on the proposed bonds, when combined with the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on presently outstanding bonds of the district in each year, will not exceed the rate levied in 2007-08 (being $66.70 per $100,000 of assessed valuation) to pay debt service on the presently outstanding bonds alone, referring to bonds issued under Propositions MM and S. Assembly Bill 182 (AB 182), effective January 1, 2014, requires that any time a CAB is issued that the board consider the item at two consecutive meetings.  This is the first of the two meetings; the second is scheduled for September 29, 2015.  Other key requirements in AB 182 are a 25-year maximum term of the bonds, prepayment option for any bonds longer than 10 years, and maximum repayment ratio of 4:1 for debt service to principal amount. The bond issue is being structured to be in compliance with AB 182 with a term of 25 years, call feature after year 10, and a projected repayment ratio of less than 4:1 for all series of bonds.

 

As part of the current plan of finance for the Proposition S Bond Program, the district anticipates issuing one or more series of bonds, as needed, every year, until the full authorization has been issued.  The plan proposes the sale of bonds that allow for the compounding of interest.  [The district's financial advisor and underwriting team will discuss the bond financing model and available options for upcoming Proposition S new monies transactions and recent Proposition S issuances.]

BACKGROUND: A multi-divisional team reviewed and updated the district's hot weather protocols in the 2014-15 school year.  Current protocols are outlined in Administrative Procedure 4032: Operation of Schools During Extremely Hot Weather, and Administrative Procedure 5003: School or Site Closure/Early Dismissal of Students. 

 

As part of the successor Collective Bargaining Agreement between the district and San Diego Education Association (SDEA), which was approved by the board on May 23, 2015, the parties agreed to “meet and review existing district procedures pertaining to hot weather in order to ensure meaningful and concise plans,” occurring prior to June 15, 2014, with any recommendation going to a July 2015 board meeting. 

 

As agreed, the parties convened a meeting on June 19, 2015, involving SDEA, parents, and relevant internal departments charged with planning for and assisting with implementation of hot weather plans.

 

During the meeting, collective experiences from the September 2014 modified days resulting from extreme heat were solicited, and the current Administrative Procedure 4032 was reviewed to better address future protocols and procedures during extreme hot weather.

 

The outcomes of the debriefing were shared with the board in July 2015, and included the following:

  1. Staff will review Administrative Procedure 4032 to include or recommend inclusion of the following:
    a. Language that states each site needs a Hot Weather Plan
    b. Clarity around staff's ability to be released or flexible leave after all students are released on modified days
    c. Clarity that the heat plan needs to be developed collaboratively among school administrators and school staff
     
  2. Suggestions that future facility design, coordinated by Facilities Planning and Design, take into account:
    a. Placement of air conditioning units in classrooms to maximize effect and minimize disruption to instruction
    b. Consideration of construction and design materials that do not conduct and radiate heat in their adjacency to classrooms, including but not limited to hardscape elements such as asphalt, concrete or gravel, and construction and design features like metal walkways or handrails.
    c. Site and classroom-based control of temperature controls in classrooms during hot weather
  1. Suggestions that school principals coordinate with and/or designate site staff to:
    1. Include the site’s Hot Weather Plan in the same location as the School Safety Plan on file at each school.
    2. Conduct a physical review prior to the start of each school year to ensure all air conditioned instructional and health spaces are cooling properly.  Inform Physical Plant Operations of any concerns.
    3. Ensure a ‘cool’ zone or zones has been identified for instructional use during hot weather and that the locations are known to all staff.
  1. Central office staff will assist in ensuring uniform implementation by:
    1. Providing a template for Hot Weather Plans.
    2. Providing a checklist for principals or site administrators to use with custodial or other staff to determine if their instructional spaces and health offices/centers are air conditioned.
    3. Providing guidelines for making up instructional minutes if modified days are required.
    4. Providing guidance to principals or site administrators regarding staff release during modified days to ensure staffing needed to provide supervision of students who must remain on campus while also accommodating reasonable needs of some staff to be released to attend to personal matters related to the minimum day operations.
  1. Staff would present to the board all outcomes of the meeting and any recommendations.  

SDEA recommended that the board expand or speed up the process of approving and installing air conditioning to other schools not on the current approved list and which used data on the 2,000 hottest classrooms in the district, primarily in Zones 2 and 3.  Schools in the ‘coastal’ zone (Zone 1) were not included in the original bond measures. Specifically, the request is to look at more than zip code in determining which schools or classrooms are expedited for air condition. This might include reviewing characteristics such as how classrooms are located and situated on campuses.

 

Staff noted that any changes to the scope of the A/C installation plan would require use of bond funds not currently allocated for that purpose, and subsequent community outreach and board engagement and approval.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $240,000 Proposition Z Funds.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Senate Bill 1324 (Lowenthal), Chapter 308, Statutes of 2006, was enacted into law effective on September 18, 2006.  This bill affects relocatable buildings that do not meet Field Act standards but that are approved by the Federal Department of Housing (DOH) and the State Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD).

 

This law requires that HCD approved relocatable buildings can no longer be used as school buildings.  Education Code Section 17292(c) requires "...the governing board of the school district shall adopt a resolution by October 30, 2015, certifying to the State Allocation Board that commencing September 30, 2015, the relocatable building is no longer being used as a school building."

 

Originally the district had 28 HCD trailers at 28 elementary and charter school sites.  These trailers were used in a variety of ways to include, staff lounges, counseling centers, principal’s office, custodial office, PE storage, pre-/post- school programs, and parent rooms.  These HCD trailer were never certified by the Division of the State Architect’s office and were placed on site under a temporary waiver that will expire as of September 30, 2015. 

 

Of the 28 trailers, all but 14 have already been removed from school sites.  HCD trailers remain at the following sites: Carver, Dewey, Lindbergh Schweitzer, Loma Portal, Marshall, Nye, Oak Park, Pacific Beach, Penn, Ross, Lindbergh Schweitzer, Spreckels, and Sunset View elementary schools; and Darnall Charter School.

 

The site administrators at the remaining 14 HCD trailer sites have been notified of the requirement to vacate the trailers ahead of the new school year.  Site administrators and Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC) staff have worked together to identify alternate space on campus to house activities formerly housed in the HCD trailers.  FPC staff will visit the affected sites to ensure compliance with SB 1324.

 

FPC staff is coordinating removal of the remaining 14 HCD trailers.  All of the HCD trailers are expected to be removed from district schools by June 30, 2016.

BACKGROUND: The California Public Utilities Commission (CPUC) regulates privately owned, electric, natural gas, telecommunications, water, railroad, rail transit, and passenger transportation companies.  The CPUC ensures that consumers have safe, reliable utility service at reasonable rates, protecting against fraud, and promoting the health of California’s economy.  Generally, every three years, Investor Owned Utilities (IOU) will initiate a major regulatory proceeding with the CPUC called a General Rate Case (GRC).  The GRC occurs in two phases: Phase I. Determines how much revenue the utility is approved to collect and Phase II. Is the rate design phase of determining which class of service rates (class of service rates are ratepayer groups such as, residential, small commercial, large commercial, etc.), or in rare cases, new class of service rates will change to pay for the Phase I determined revenue.

 

On November 14, 2014, San Diego Gas and Electric (SDG&E) filed a General Rate Case application to request an increase in revenue for calendars years, 2016, $133 million (7.5 percent), 2017, $96 million (5.0 percent), and 2018, $96 million (4.8 percent).  This Phase I application continues litigation and is well defended for all rate payers by various stakeholder groups.  SDG&E concurrently filed a Phase II application on April 13, 2015.  This begins the process of “cutting up” the Phase I revenue pie; this phase II determines which ratepayers will have a change in rate and in what amount of an increase or decrease to their bills.  This means the Phase II affects subgroups of ratepayers rather than all ratepayers as in the Phase I process.  In the Phase II proceedings it is more likely for groups that have similar operations or rate tariffs to come together as stakeholders and collectivity act in the proceedings.

 

In this Phase II GRC, the County Office of Education spearheaded the San Diego county schools coming together to defend recent year and future years of rate shock to hit county schools, of which San Diego Unified is no exception.  Current estimates indicate San Diego county school districts will experience, on average, a 39 percent increase in electricity costs this year with approximately 33 percent attributable to rate increases (38 districts and county offices included in data set). If this trend continues, it indicates a year over year increase of $25 million to $30 million for all San Diego County districts and an estimated $3.8 million increase for San Diego Unified.  School districts have few options for increasing revenue in response to increased operational costs and consequently, this electricity cost increase is consuming approximately 19 percent of the 2014-15 increase to the LCFF Base Grant county-wide.  This trend is unsustainable and is impeding the ability of school districts to achieve the main objective of the LCFF and LCAP: to increase or improve services for all students.

 

There are two avenues of relief for county schools to secure future fiscal management of these electric rates.  The county Chief Business Officers, as a group, are pursuing both of these actions:

 

1. Action by the State Legislature to: recoup funds lost to cost increase currently being experienced, obtain a permanent protective cap to prevent future rate shock, and to remove school districts from the commercial rate tariff and apply either a new or existing rate tariff that recognizes fixed revenue restraints and the non-commercial electricity use profile of school districts;

 

2. Act to participate as a party to the SDG&E Phase II GRC before the CPUC (Note: SDS already met the May 12, 2015, timeline to become a party to the proceedings).  The results of being a party to the Phase II rate case can have similar outcomes as in the above item one.  As in either case above an unfavorable result of no rate relief and increases may occur. 

 

The MOU with the County has a not to exceed cost of $61,000 to be prepared for the worst case scenario.  However, with the vast majority of districts within the county participating, the current cost estimate is $25,000 for the proceedings. Ratification of the MOU and project addendum number one, moves San Diego Unified forward in collective action with the San Diego Coalition for Electricity Cost Reduction (SDS) to secure a more favorable electrical rate.

 

The accompanying resolution affirms the district's commitment to reduce energy costs and explore solutions, including but not limited to potential legislation to provide school districts statewide with certainty regarding electricity rates.

This approval authorizes the continued purchase of office supplies from Independent Stationers, Inc. through Office Solutions, Inc.  This change will authorize the district to purchase office supplies directly from Office Solutions, Inc., using Independent Stationers Inc., as the wholesaler in the supply chain.  This change allows district employees to purchase from a technologically superior website and streamlines the purchasing and invoicing process.  Los Angeles County Office Supplies Contract No. MA-IS-1140050, Amendment 13 dated July 31, 2012, allows local dealers to directly supply their customers through the Conventional Sourcing Model Enhanced (CSME).  In addition, this approval extends the term of the agreement from September 10, 2014 through December 31, 2015, as authorized by the Los Angeles County Office Supplies Contract No. MA-IS-1140050, Amendment 35 dated March 2, 2015 (also attached herein).

 

With the approval of this authorization to continue purchasing office supplies from Independent Stationers, Inc., through Office Solutions, Inc., and with the existing authorization to purchase office supplies from Office Depot, Inc., the district will continue to provide its schools and departments a choice in both the selection and costs of office supplies.  While the prices in both agreements are similar, they are not identical.  By comparing prices, schools and departments can then purchase the appropriate office supplies for its location at the lower cost.  The Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department will continue to work with both suppliers to further reduce the cost of the district's office supplies.

 

On July 8, 2014, the board received the final report from the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee.  Included in this report as a "Dream Big" idea was a recommended action to join or create larger purchasing cooperatives.  A purchasing cooperative establishes various contracts which aggregate the total purchases from many government entities resulting in lower costs to governmental entities who then utilize these contracts.  California Public Contract Code 20118 provides that a district may purchase personal property from contracts already competitively bid and awarded by another public agency.  The district may only purchase the items specified in the award at the prices specified, and it may not purchase services from these contracts.
