This cost is based on a maximum of 28,064 students at $13.50 per student. The per-student price includes on-site administrator training for up to 150 district staff members, and on-site "trainer of trainers" professional development for up to 50 district staff members. The Measures of Academic Progress (MAP) program was identified for implementation in the district's 2009-10 Strategic Plan and funding has been allocated for this purpose.

BACKGROUND: The Northwest Evaluation Association's (NWEA) assessments computerized adaptive Measures of Academic Progress (MAP) tests provide educators the information they need to improve teaching and learning. Educators use the growth and achievement data from MAP tests to develop targeted instructional strategies and to plan school improvement. MAP test results help educators to make student-focused, data-driven decisions (See Exhibit I for additional information).

    • Highlighted features of MAP tests:
    • They provide a challenging test for every student. Students are not expected to get every question right or every question wrong.
    • They are dynamically built based on the achievement level of the student and will give accurate and reliable information for every student.
    • They are untimed. Students should be given as much time as needed to complete a test, as long as they are making progress.
    • Tests are given for internal accountability. They provide educators information about students within the district, school, or classroom and are used for their own purposes.
    • They measure growth and show how students are progressing. Because the data can be looked at historically and is consistent from season to season, growth can be measured.
    • Results are received immediately. They are displayed at the end of the test event.
    • Students can be tested up to four times a year in Reading, Language Usage and Mathematics in order to monitor their growth.
Up to 42 elementary/K-8, 13 middle and six high schools will participate in this program. There are also benefits specific to district schools in that the use of MAPs eliminates the need to administer the Gates/McGinitie test, and more significantly, MAPs will effectively serve as a specialized learning contract for struggling students.

Each petitioning charter school was given two weeks to amend their charter petition and submit for board ratification. The schools are now operating their own special education services under their new SELPA. Adoption of this amendment aligns the schools' charter petitions with their current practice and formally removes all legal liability from San Diego Unified School District. FISCAL IMPACT: None

BACKGROUND: California Education Code Section 44253.1 requires all teachers to have an active and valid English Learner authorization if assigned to a classroom that has one or more students designated as an English Learner. Because of the Williams Settlement legislation, a review of this Education Code has determined that the need for an English Learner authorization also applies to all Career Technical Education (CTE) and JROTC certificated staff employed by the district. Teachers who hold a bachelor’s degree and completed an approved teacher preparation program leading to the issuance of a regular education credential or special education credential, but did not hold an English Learner authorization, qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to cover them in their assignments while completing the requirements to obtain a Clear CLAD Certificate.

Due to the fact that Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers are not required to hold a bachelor’s degree or complete an approved teacher preparation program to be issued a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing, this sizable group of district teachers do not qualify for an Emergency CLAD Permit. On March 19, 2008, the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing announced its approval of SB 1292 giving all holders of Designated Subjects Teaching Credentials an option to obtain an English Learner authorization. Prior to this approval, there was no legal option available to the holder of a Designated Subjects Teaching Credential to be issued any form of English Learner authorization. On September 26, 2008, the San Diego County Office of Education began the first session of training for qualifying teachers employed in the county.

After working with the San Diego County Office of Education, the district has been advised that all Career Technical Education and JROTC teachers will need to apply for CLAD Waivers with the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing in order not to be considered mis-assigned for the 2009-10 academic school year. These waivers will be valid for the 2009-10 school year but can be renewed for an additional year in the event the San Diego County Office of Education cannot accommodate the county’s entire population of teachers who need to complete the training.
