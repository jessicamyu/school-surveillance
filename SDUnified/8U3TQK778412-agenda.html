RECOMMENDATION:  Ratification of Memorandum of Understanding with the San Diego County Superintendent of Schools to provide assistance to improve teaching and learning for the Madison cluster (Field, Hawthorne, Lafayette, and Sequoia elementary schools) through the use of the Instructional Rounds process (PS-13-0500-76).  The term of this Agreement is August 1, 2012 through June 30, 2013.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None. All services and materials will be provided by SDCOE at no cost to the district.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.  This is a new agreement.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  The San Diego County Superintendent of Schools (SDCOE) has been engaged in the work of Instructional Rounds since 2009.  Professor Lee Teitel from the Harvard Graduate School of Education has assisted in building capacity and expertise of SDCOE staff.  As a result, SDCOE is able to provide support and assist districts in building capacity in the instructional improvement process.  SDCOE will share expertise and models in supporting the application of Instructional Rounds to the instructional improvement process, promote collaboration across sites and districts to improve teaching and learning, and facilitate/disseminate communication between district and school networks.

 

Implementation of Instructional Rounds includes an initial onsite collaborative meeting with the school principal and the leadership team/staff, a two-day overview of the Instructional Rounds process, and facilitation of eight Instructional Rounds network site visits.  All materials, professional development, and reading supplies provided by the SDCOE.

 

The Instructional Rounds model cultivates instructional leaders from within educational organizations and helps these leaders to understand and improve at scale the level and quality of teaching/learning.  The emphasis is on developing a “learning community” of instructional leadership for district administrators, principals, assistant principals, and teachers from which high quality teaching and learning is identified, deconstructed, and strategically developed in classrooms.  The result is a focused and insightful approach to effective instructional leadership and teaching/learning excellence.

Emily Rubin, MS, CCC-SLP is the director of Communication Crossroads, a private practice in Carmel, California. She is a speech-language pathologist specializing in autism, Aspergers Syndrome, and related social learning disabilities. As a former adjunct faculty member and lecturer at Yale University, she served as a member of their Autism and Developmental Disabilities Clinic.  She has also served as an instructor for the Communication Sciences and Disorders Department of Emerson College in Boston, Massachusetts where she has developed courses to prepare graduate level students for addressing the needs of children with autism and their families. Video examples will be integrated to illustrate how the SCERTS practice principles can be applied to ensure that curriculum modifications and learning supports are implemented to ensure that students are: 1) maintaining active engagement in everyday activities, 2) making smooth and independent transitions across natural routines, and 3) expressing their emotion and using coping strategies in a conventional manner.

8. U.S. Department of Education, Race to the Top - District Competitive Grant Program (Barnett and Evans)
Meeting
Sep 25, 2012 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action, Information

Superintendent Kowba will report on the Race to the Top - District (RTTT-D) competitive grant program recently announced by the U.S. Department of Education and his decision to not submit an application.

 

BACKGROUND:  Staff have carefully evaluated the grant opportunity and thoroughly analyzed the cost benefit potential to the district and the potential of a successful application balanced against the costs and effort necessary to prepare a submittal.  After weighing all the factors carefully, the Superintendent decided not to divert limited resources to a time consuming and costly application for this grant so that the district can continue to use its time and resources on the continuation of school improvement efforts that are yielding positive results for students, as recently demonstrated in another year of academic gains shown on the 2012 CST results.

 

The deadline to submit applications for the Race to the Top – District (RTTT–D) grant program is October 30, 2012, and the district did not submit a notice of intent to apply for funding, but that action does not preclude us from submitting an application should we see a change of circumstances.  Thorough analysis led to the following conclusions:

  1. The RTTT–D grant criteria are similar to prior RTTT programs under which California and seven districts applied collectively and were not funded in that competition.
     
  2. Only 15 to 25 grants will be awarded among a potential 15,000 districts across the country — poor odds given Item 1 above.
     
  3. Although the application deadline is October 30, 2012, conditions in the grant (review by the mayor and state department of education) effectively move the deadline to early October
     
  4. The time and effort required to generate a strong application at the beginning of the school year would distract key staff involved in the important and time-sensitive work of staffing and preparing schools.
     
  5. Receipt of grant funds would not represent a net increase in funds to plug budget holes linked to decreased state revenues. Although the largest grant amount is $40 million, it is likely that only a few large grants would be awarded and that more smaller grants are more likely.  It is also important to note that grant period of four years, which would mean the district would have to commit financial resources upfront in year one and use other district resources to implement the requirements of the grant that would be funded over four years.  Therefore, the timeline of costs and resource commitments would be dictated by the grant rather than district priorities.
     
  6. The district has closed contracts with its teachers' and principals' unions that would have challenged its ability to implement, by 2014–15, teacher, principal, and superintendent evaluation systems that include criteria not currently used in San Diego Unified. The following information related to teacher evaluations outlines the grant conditions which would be difficult and costly to implement within the timeframe necessary to demonstrate a grant proposal that could be funded:
  • Teacher evaluation system means a system that: (1) is used for continual improvement of instruction; (2) meaningfully differentiates performance using at least three performance levels; (3) uses multiple valid measures in determining performance levels, including, as a significant factor, data on student growth [emphasis added] (as defined in the notice) for all students (including English learners and students with disabilities), as well as other measures of professional practice (which may be gathered through multiple formats and sources, such as observations based on rigorous teacher performance standards, teacher portfolios, and student and parent surveys); (4) evaluates teachers on a regular basis; (5) provides clear, timely, and useful feedback, including feedback that identifies and guides professional development needs; and (6) is used to inform personnel decisions.

Given the long odds of being awarded a grant and the limiting factors enumerated above, the Superintendent concluded that there is too little potential benefit to warrant the time and resources required to submit an application.
