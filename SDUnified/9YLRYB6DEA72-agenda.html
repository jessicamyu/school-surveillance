RECOMMENDATION: Approve proclamation recognizing the Japanese Friendship Garden.  The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education extends its appreciation and acknowledges the strong ties between the Japanese Friendship Garden of San Diego and its namesake garden Sankeien in Yokohama.

 

BACKGROUND: San Diego and Yokohama have America’s first and longest sister city relationship on the west coast, initiated in 1955, by Mayor Dail and Mayor Hiranuma.

 

The Japanese Friendship Garden (JFG) originated in concept and design as part of the San Diego Yokohama Sister City Society affiliation, and today spans more than eleven acres in Balboa Park as a cherished public institution and a visible legacy of the sister city relationship.

 

San Diego-Yokohama sister city affiliations have given gifts to the JFG available to the public today including: the Charles Dail Memorial Gate in 1968, the marble memorial with an original song “San Diego, the Beautiful” in 1982, the entrance boulder inscribed with the namesake “Sankeien” in the calligraphy of late Yokohama Mayor Michikazu Saigo in 1990, and the Lantern of Friendship in 1997.

 

The JFG has served as a central Japanese and horticultural institution for official delegations, ceremonies, festivals, and educational resource for the community, and continues to expand its importance and impact in that role. The two gardens in San Diego and Yokohama, both named Sankeien, look forward to collaborating to provide benefits and awareness to their respective publics.

 

JFG in Balboa Park has been sponsoring field trips and hosting assemblies in partnership with Barnard Asian Pacific Language Academy. The community outreach program has also committed to supporting Mission Bay Cluster Schools to educate and spread the beauty of Japanese culture in alignment with the mission to help students be global citizens.

 

For the first time, JFG, in collaboration with the San Diego Yokohama Sister City, has adopted Crown Point Junior Music Academy to receive a Japanese garden on their campus.

 

Kanazawa High School students in Yokohama, Japan will be forming a club to assist in developing the Crown Point garden through cultural exchanges via on line collaborations.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: Pursuant to Administrative Procedure 3300, Naming of Schools and School Facilities, this is a request to approve the renaming of the San Diego Early/Middle College to East Village High School, an Early College in Partnership with San Diego City College. The School Names Committee has reviewed the supporting documentation required for the request and concurs with the renaming.

 

On June 10, 2008, the board approved the Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with San Diego City College to establish an early/middle college, on the campus of San Diego City College.

 

On August 5, 2008, the board approved establishment of San Diego Early/Middle College.

 

On September 23, 2008, the board approved a revision to the MOU with San Diego City college regarding San Diego Early/Middle College.

 

The school opened in fall 2008, and the name has confused people ever since, according to Liz Larkin, the school principal. The principal and the staff believe the new name better exemplifies what the school is and is not. The name explains that the school is a high school with an early college focus. "East Village" provides a geographic location.

 

This year, San Diego Early/Middle College and San Diego City College received a $100,000 Middle College High School grant from the California Community State Chancellor's Office to enhance their partnership, including building and sustaining the early college program. Part of the grant money is being used to reverse the school's decline in enrollment by re-branding it with a new name in order to raise the school's profile in the community and recruit more students. Re-branding efforts include working with a marketing firm along the City College Graphic Arts Department.

 

A petition was circulated to the San Diego Early/Middle faculty and staff, which resulted in a 100 percent support of the name change. A survey was conducted with parents and students, with 70 percent of parents supporting the change and 68 percent of the students supporting the change. The San Diego Community College District Board voted in support of the name change on March 10, 2016.
