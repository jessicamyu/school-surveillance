Meeting

Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All (Vision 2020 Quality Indicator and corresponding LCAP Goal 1.  This report will focus on students with disabilities.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destination:  Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate the significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring that every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gap goals are implemented.  All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly or indirectly to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.  

 

LCAP Goal 1:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups. 

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:  

  • 1.1.a.2. Central office will support area superintendents, principals, and school staffs to design site-based assessment systems that promote a culture of revision and continuous improvement of student learning based on individual site needs.  
  • 1.7.7. and 1.7.8. To provide for improved outcomes and close the achievement gap for students with disabilities, the Special Education Division will coordinate, design, and provide for specialized and age-appropriate programs, services, and supports for students, birth to age 5, TK - Grade 12, and ages 18-22.  Programs and services will include developing, implementing, and monitoring quality student-centered Individualized Education Plans (IEP), including documentation and adherence to requirements and timelines; and using the specific recommendations of the Special Education Division's WestEd study for the purposes of improvement (i.e., appropriate staffing supports, improved service delivery models, training, etc.). 

BACKGROUND:  At regular, public board meetings Superintendent Marten provides a report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions and services.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation.  The complete LCAP document is available on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

6. Resolution to Oppose the 2018 Federal Budget Proposal to Cut Education and Health Care Funding (Evans/McQuary)

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution to oppose the 2018, federal budget proposal to cut education and health care funding that will negatively impact San Diego Unified students who are performing better academically than state and county averages, and have the highest graduation rates and lowest dropout rates of large urban school districts in California, and urge our elected representatives to stop the erosion of public K-12 funding in FY 2018 and beyond.

 

BACKGROUND: A world class public education with sufficient funding to support students with various needs is essential for the future success of our nation and students.

 

Under the Fiscal Year (FY) 2018 proposed federal budget, California would lose $400 million in federal K-12 education funding.

 

The full budget proposal requests $9 billion in cuts to federal education programs and will have both direct and indirect consequences for low-income students and their families, as well as the public schools serving them, and represents a massive shift in federal priorities.

 

Significant K-12 education cuts proposed include the elimination of the 21st Century after school program, severe reduction of traditional Title I formula grants to schools, elimination of the Title II-A program for effective instruction, elimination of the new Title IV Academic Enrichment Block Grants, the Striving Readers Program, elimination of Impact Aid Federal Properties funding, and a reduction to Career and Technical Education (CTE) state grants.

 

Any reductions in Medicaid funding will have a severe impact on the health and education of low-income students in San Diego Unified affecting their everyday readiness to learn and their absences due to sickness.

 

The health and related services provided to students with disabilities are federally required under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) and the Rehabilitation Act, and the loss of Medicaid funding for these services will shift an even larger share of these costs onto school district budgets.

 

The San Diego Unified School District is committed to providing essential services to all students and supports increased investment – and not funding cuts – for critical K-12 programs, particularly Title I for disadvantaged students, Title II for professional development and class size reduction, Title III for English learners, and IDEA for students with disabilities.

1. Public Hearing and First Reading of the 2017-20 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP): BUDGETED: N/A

Jun 13, 2017 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information, First Reading, Public Hearing

The Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) is an essential element of California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF).  It is a three-year, district-level plan that is updated annually. The plan describes the school district's key goals for students as well as the actions (with expenditures) the district will take to achieve the goals and the measures used to indicate progress.  The LCAP also includes an Annual Update, which reflects the implementation of the current year's LCAP (2016-17).  The San Diego Unified School District has aligned its six LCAP goals and supporting actions and expenditures to the board-adopted Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.


RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and first reading of the district's proposed 2017-20 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP).  A workshop was conducted on June 6, 2017, at which the Superintendent and staff provided information to the board on the implementation of Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood and its alignment with the proposed 2017-20 LCAP.  Each goal was reviewed, and plans for implementation of each goal in the 2017-18 school year were presented.  The 2017-20 LCAP will be presented to the Board of Education for final reading and adoption on June 27, 2017. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The 2017-20 LCAP is submitted for first reading in conjunction with the first reading of the proposed 2017-20 budget.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: The Annual Update section of the 2017-20 LCAP includes 2016-17 actions, services, and estimated actual expenditures.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) and District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC) serve as the district's LCAP advisory committees.  During 2016-17, regular updates on LCAP implementation were presented to the board and public at regular board meetings, and the presentations were available to the DAC and DELAC for dissemination to their members.  The early draft 2017-20 LCAP was presented to the DAC on May 17, 2017, and to the DELAC on June 1, 2017, for review and comment.  The full document is posted on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap. 

 

BACKGROUND:  The LCAP is intended to be a comprehensive planning tool.  For school districts, pursuant to Education Code Section 52060, the LCAP must describe, for the school district and each school within the district, goals and specific actions to achieve those goals for all students and each subgroup of students in Education Code Section 52052, including students with disabilities, for each of the state priorities and any locally identified priorities.  

 

The district's local priorities (LCAP Goals) are:  

 

1. Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.  

2.  Access to Broad and Challenging Curriculum:  Students have equitable access to rigorous, well-rounded, standards-aligned curricula and access to and skill in applying technologies to leverage learning, assuring readiness for a full range of post-graduation options.

3. Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning:  Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.  

4.  Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture:  Schools provide safe and well-maintained facilities and positive learning climates and instructional practices that are culturally responsive, challenge bias, and support the academic, social, emotional, and physical needs of students.  

5. Parent and Community Engagement with Highly Regarded Neighborhood Schools that Serve Students, Families, and Communities:  Parents and community members are engaged and work within and across schools to support student learning.  Neighborhood schools are highly regarded for their quality as well as their service as centers for extended learning and enrichment opportunities, and academic and social services.  

6. Well-Orchestrated Districtwide Support Services and Communications:  All parts of the organization - personnel and infrastructure - work together to support student success through quality schools in every neighborhood.  

 

The State priorities are:  

 

1. Basic:  The degree to which teachers are appropriately assigned and fully credentialed in the subject areas and for the students they are teaching; students in the school district have sufficient access to the standards-aligned instructional materials; and school facilities are maintained in good repair.  

2. Implementation of State Standards:  The implementation of state board adopted academic content and performance standards for all students, which are:

  • English Language Arts - Common Core State Standards (CCSS) for English Language Arts 
  • Mathematics - CCSS for Mathematics
  • English Language Development (ELD)
  • Career Technical Education
  • Health Education Content Standards
  • History-Social Science
  • Model School Library Standards
  • Physical Education Model Content Standards
  • Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS)
  • Visual and Performing Arts (VAPA)
  • World Language; and
  • how the programs and services will enable English Learners to access the CCSS and ELD standards for purposes of gaining academic content knowledge and English language proficiency.

3. Parental Involvement:  Description of efforts the school district makes to seek parent input in making decisions for the school district and each individual school site, promotion of parent participation in programs for unduplicated students and students with exceptional needs.   

4. Student Achievement: Performance on statewide assessments, the Academic Performance Index, percentage of students who have successfully completed courses that satisfy University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) entrance requirements, or programs of study that align with the state board approved career technical educational standards and framework; the percentage of English Learners who make progress toward English proficiency as measured by the California English Language Development Test (CELDT); the English Learner reclassification rate; the percentage of students that pass an Advanced Placement examination with a score of 3 or higher; and the percentage of students who participate in, and demonstrate college preparedness pursuant to the Early Assessment Program, or any subsequent assessment of college preparedness.  

5. Student Engagement:  School attendance rates, chronic absenteeism rates, middle school dropout rates, high school dropout rates, and high school graduation rates.  

6. School Climate:  Student suspension rates; student expulsion rates; other local measures including surveys of students, parents, and teachers on the sense of safety and school connectedness.

7. Course Access:  Student enrollment in a broad course of study including courses described in Education Code Section 51210 and Section 51220(a)-(i), as applicable; programs and services developed and provided to unduplicated students and students with exceptional needs.  

8. Other Student Outcomes:  Student outcomes, if available, for courses described in Education Code Section 51210 and Section 51220(a)-(i), as applicable.

BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue the improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting districtwide, a new process will be implemented for streamlining Independent Contractor Agreements. The goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

 

Executive Leadership should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective Executive Leadership/Instructional Cabinet team member, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Statement 54 of the Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB 54) established accounting and financial reporting standards for all governments that report governmental funds.  GASB 54 seeks to improve financial reporting by establishing criteria for classifying fund balances and clarifies definitions for governmental fund types.  Essentially, it establishes a hierarchy of fund classifications based largely on the extent to which the government agency is restricted in the use of the funds. GASB 54 establishes the following five classifications of ending fund balances:

  • Non-Spendable Fund Balance consists of funds that cannot be spent because they are either not in spendable form (i.e. inventory and prepaid) or amounts that are legally or contractually required to be maintained intact.
  • Restricted Fund Balance consists of funds that are mandated for a specific purpose by external parties, constitutional provisions or enabling legislation.
  • Committed Fund Balance consists of funds that are set aside for a specific purpose by the district’s highest level of decision-making authority. Formal action must be taken prior to the end of the fiscal year. These committed amounts cannot be used for any other purpose unless the same formal action is taken to remove or change the limitations placed on the funds.
  • Assigned Fund Balance consists of funds that are set aside with the intent to be used for a specific purpose, but are neither restricted nor committed. Assigned fund balance includes all positive remaining amounts in governmental funds, other than the General Fund, that are not considered non-spendable, restricted, or committed. Assigned funds cannot cause a deficit in unassigned fund balance.
  • Unassigned Fund Balance consists of excess funds that have not been classified in the previous four categories.  All funds in this category are considered spendable resources.  This category also provides the resources necessary to meet unexpected expenditures and revenue shortfalls.

Governmental agencies—including school districts— are required to adopt policies and protocols to establish the processes that shall determine amounts relegated to these classifications, e.g.- through board action. Agencies are required to implement Statement 54 for fiscal years first ending June 30, 2011. The attached resolution shall establish district policy and processes as required by GASB 54.

RWP is led by Lucy Calkins, author of more than twenty best-selling books and the Robinson Professor in Literacy at Teachers College Columbia University.  The mission of RWP is to enable young people to become avid readers, writers, and inquirers.  Research has led RWP to develop state-of-the-art knowledge about the teaching of reading and writing in Grades K–8, and about methods of differentiating instruction, using formative assessments and supporting content-area literacy. 

 

RWP will provide professional development in the Lucy Calkins Units of Study in Reading for principals and teachers and the Lucy Calkins Units of Study in Writing for Grades K-8 teachers.

 

The Lucy Calkins Units of Study in Reading, one-day conferences replicate the one-day conference teachers have at Teachers College, Columbia University.  Principals and teachers at pilot schools with the Reading Units of Study will have the opportunity to attend.

 

The Lucy Calkins Units of Study in Writing four-day Institute replicates the experience teachers have at Teachers College, Columbia University. 

 

The Reading and Writing Network is returning this summer due to high interest and demand of both teachers and administrators.
