RECOMMENDATION: Approve and authorize the sale of surplus and/or obsolete instructional materials.  The term of this authorization is February 27, 2013 through June 30, 2013, with the intention to request continued board approval each fiscal year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  Revenue to the district in the amount of 100 percent of the selling price of surplus and/or obsolete instructional materials.   All proceeds shall be used to acquire basic instructional materials, supplemental instructional materials, or technology-based instructional materials as required by California Education Code section 60510.1.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On October 11, 2009, Assembly Bill 487 amended California Education Code section 60510, allowing school districts to sell surplus instructional materials and use those proceeds to purchase basic instructional materials.   The Instructional Materials Office is responsible for purchasing newly adopted instructional materials and analyzing inventories of current district adoptions to ensure sufficient instructional materials are available for students and teachers.  Newly adopted instructional materials are purchased based on course enrollment projections for the year of the new adoption.   Surpluses of district adopted instructional materials typically occur when enrollments decline in the associated courses over the life of the adoption.  Surpluses of obsolete instructional materials occur when the board approves new instructional materials to replace existing instructional materials.

 

In accordance with California Education Code section 60510, the governing board of a school district may dispose of surplus or undistributed obsolete instructional materials in the following ways:

      (a)  By donation to a governing board, county free library, or other state institution.

      (b)  By donation to a public agency or institution of any territory or possession of the United States.

      (c)  By donation to a nonprofit charitable organization.

      (d)  By donation to children or adults in the State of California, or foreign countries for the purpose of increasing the general literacy of the people.

      (e)  By sale.

 

Administrative Procedure 2805, Sale or Disposal of District Textbooks and Library Books, will be updated to reflect the revisions to California Education Code section 60510 following Board approval. Detailed documentation of any sale of surplus and/or obsolete instructional materials will be maintained by the Instructional Materials Office and made available for auditing purposes.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  No additional district positions will be required. Services will be provided by the Transitional Resources for Adult Community Education (TRACE) Department.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego County Office of Education has received grants from the San Diego Probation Department, the U. S. Department of Justice, and the San Diego Workforce Partnership and desires to contract with the district in order to implement programs and provide a variety of educational services to current or former youth offenders who have been previously involved in the juvenile justice system and have returned to district schools. These programs and services are designed to lead these students to the completion of a high school diploma or equivalent GED, and to provide work-based and school-based learning opportunities such as paid and/or unpaid internships, job shadowing and volunteer opportunities that lead to unsubsidized job placements.

 

Under the East Mesa Juvenile Detention Facility Employment Readiness Training Program, TRACE educational specialists provide three, one hour Work Readiness Training classes for students at the East Mesa Juvenile Detention facility every 18 weeks and assist with coordinating one Job Resource Fair every 16 weeks for East Mesa students who complete the Work Readiness Training classes. The district has been providing services under this Program since 2007.

 

Under the Pathways to Success Program, TRACE staff provide case management, social and recreational activities; youth development activities and also assist students in connecting to post-secondary education, advanced training, apprenticeship programs and industry-recognized training that will lead to career placement.  TRACE staff assist with coordinating Youth Resource Fairs for youth offenders and provide work readiness training at Camp Barrett Juvenile Detention on a regular basis and meet with special education students weekly to recruit students for the program.

 

Under the Re-entry Demonstration Project, TRACE staff provide educational services to special education students incarcerated at a juvenile detention facility at Camp Barrett, East Mesa, Girls Rehabilitation Facility or Juvenile Hall. Pre-release services include work readiness training, career counseling, career assessment and the development of a Life Plan before being released from a detention facility.  Once the student is released from a juvenile detention facility, TRACE staff provide post-release services including case management, social and recreational activities, youth development opportunities, vocational training, personal and professional counseling activities, job search assistance, housing and other referrals, job development, support services, mentoring activities, program referrals and follow-up services. TRACE staff also connect students to paid employment opportunities, post-secondary education, advanced training, apprenticeship programs and industry recognized training that will lead to career placement. 

 

These agreements are being submitted for ratification on this agenda due to the need for the San Diego County Office of Education to receive final approval for its funding for these Programs and Project before finalizing these agreements with the district.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. RECOMMENDATION: Approve the Title I centralized services plan, which excludes the $2 million grant program; and Option 1 for the Title I school allocations. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district’s anticipated Title I entitlement for 2013–14 is approximately $38.8 million.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: The district received approximately $43 million in Title I funds in 2012–13.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: There is no known net change in district staffing at this time; however, some staff will be funded differently upon approval of the centralized services plan. In addition, schools, via the School Site Council (SSC) decision-making process, may choose to fund positions with their categorical funds differently than they did this year.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education was consulted at its November 14 and December 19, 2012 meetings regarding the school allocation methodology that was subsequently approved by the Board of Education at its January 8, 2013 meeting. At the January 19 and February 20, 2013 meetings, the DAC was consulted regarding the centralized services plan.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages five through seventeen living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

District leadership is charged with designing a plan that meets legislative requirements while producing sustainable support that will positively impact student learning and significantly raise achievement over time during an era of diminishing unrestricted resources. Key factors that affect the school allocations are:

  • Potential addition of 33 schools to Program Improvement status
  • K-12 student enrollment at district-managed schools decreased by 1,724 students from 114,064 in 2011-12 to 112,340 in 2012-13
  • The number of free and reduced-price meal eligible students has increased over the past several years
  • Impact of the district continuing in Program Improvement

The district has provided opportunities for consultation with parents as required by Title I legislation, NCLB Section 1118(a). The DAC, at its November 14 and December 20, 2012 meetings, considered two sample funding scenarios based on example funding amounts (actual funding amounts were not available). Option 1 for school allocations continues to suspend the $2 million grant program and thus, allocates more funding across all Title I schools. Option 2 keeps the grant program and includes the support for low-performing schools and nurses.

 

Proposed Title I centralized services plans were presented to the DAC at its January 19 and February 20, 2013 meetings. The consultation at the February meeting included the plan that reflects the changes for the support of Program Improvement, low-performing schools and the supplementary nursing services.
