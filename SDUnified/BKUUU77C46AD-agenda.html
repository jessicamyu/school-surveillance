RECOMMENDATION: As a service-providing organization, the San Diego Unified School District's employees are its most valuable resource. Today's recognition includes recognition of employees with 40, 30, 20, 15, 10 and 5 years of service. Certificates and pins will be sent via school mail. The Superintendent and the Board of Education extend their most sincere gratitude to these individuals. 

 

Today, we celebrate milestones of 188 employees with a service anniversary month of September.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: As San Diego Unified celebrates progress towards meeting the inspirational goals contained in Vision 2020, we are taking time to celebrate those employees who have helped us achieve this progress. The number one predictor of student success is the presence of an experienced, high quality teacher in the classroom, according to the Learning Policy Institute. (Source: “Report: Teacher qualifications best predictor of student success,” by Amelia Harper, May 16, 2019, Education Dive). That study, which was written last year and co-authored by the Chair of the State Board of Education, found high quality teachers have helped San Diego schools do better than the rest of the state. (Source: “California’s Positive Outliers: Districts Beating the Odds,” by Anne Podolsky, Linda Darling-Hammond, Christopher Doss, and Sean Reardon, May 16, 2019, Learning Policy Institute). Educators also provide crucial services in addition to teaching. They are critical to keeping students safe at school. School staff also provide healthy, nutritious meals to children who do not have access to food at home. Nearly 100,000 schools/institutions serve school lunches to 29.8 million students each day, including 20.2 million free lunches. (Source: School Nutrition Association.) Given the clear association between the health and success of our students and the well-being and effectiveness of our workforce, we publicly recognize employees for their continuous years of service to the students, families and communities of San Diego Unified.  

 

Employees will be recognized once a month during the month their anniversary occurs. Employees and their supervisors were notified of this recognition on October 23, 2020. 

 

*This service recognition award is not used to calculate or verify the seniority date of an employee nor is the recognition part of the formal personnel processes that calculate the date for longevity pay. Both longevity and seniority dates are dictated by the Education Code as well as the applicable collective bargaining agreement. The date used to determine this service award is based on the individual's permanent status as an employee, temporary or hourly work is not used in the determination of the service award recognition. Service recognition awards will be awarded on a monthly basis. If you believe an error has been made, you may contact the Human Resource Service Division by emailing jobs@sandi.net.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2020-21 School Plans for Student Achievement (SPSAs) and Categorical Budget Summaries for 16 Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI) schools listed below.

 

School Plans for Student Achievement for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2020.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) received information and provided feedback on the timeline and components of developing the SPSA, beginning with their January 2020 meeting: January 29, 2020 (Take to Your SSCs), February 19, 2020 (SPSA Update), and September 16, 2020 (SPSA Timeline Update).

 

In addition, site principals received training regarding specific plan requirements and consulted with their school stakeholders through the School Site Council to develop these plans.  These trainings were provided by the Strategic Planning for Student Achievement (SPSA) Department beginning in September 2020, and continued through the submission of the plans: September 15, 16, 17, 21, 22, 23, 24, 28, 29, 30, and October 1, 2, 2020.

 

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with California Ed Code 64001, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a School Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds, provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan. The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. SPSAs are created under this part may serve as school improvement plans required under federal law for schools identified for targeted support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(A) of the federal Every Student Succeed Act (public Law 114-95) or comprehensive support as described in Section 1003(e)(1)(B) of the federal Every Student Succeeds Act.


The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements. Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA. 

 

Additionally, Strategic Planning for Student Achievement staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendent for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs.  Once approved by the area superintendents, these schools' SPSAs were forwarded to the SPSA Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.  The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA. 

 

The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) requires state educational agencies to determine school eligibility for Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI). Local educational agencies (LEAs) with schools that meet the criteria for CSI must partner with stakeholders to locally develop and implement a plan to improve student outcomes. The county office of education (COE) also plays a role in providing technical assistance and support to LEAs within its county that serve schools that meet the criteria for CSI.  CSI schools are identified by the state education agency as one of the lowest-performing schools in the state based mostly on students’ academic performance using the California Dashboard. Also, any high school with a graduation rate of 66 percent or less is a CSI school. 

 

SPSAs for CSI schools are required to be submitted to the California Department of Education no later than October 31, 2020. This report brings forward for board approval the 2020-21 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for the 16 identified CSI schools.   Electronic copies of each 2020-21 SPSA, which include the budget summaries and required signed Recommendations and Assurances pages are attached to this agenda item, and hard copies are available in the Board Services Offices, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231 San Diego, CA 92103. SPSAs from all remaining district schools will be presented to the board on December 15, 2020.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the 2020-21 proposed San Diego Unified English Learner Reclassification Criteria. The Office of Language Acquisition (OLA), in collaboration with the DELAC is recommending is to modify Criteria 2 by replacing course grades by using an objective and standard administered instrument. Course grades often are influenced by non-academic factors such as homework completion, class participation, and absences. These factors created barriers that prevented students from reclassifying and are not associated with language acquisition and development. There will be no change to Criteria 1, 3 and 4. 

 

 

San Diego Unified School District will continue using the following four criteria to establish Reclassification policies and procedures. 

 

1. Assessment of ELP, using an objective assessment instrument, including, but not limited to, the state test of English Language Proficiency Assessments for California (ELPAC Overall PL 4 only); and

 

2. Teacher evaluation:

 

a. Protocol for Teacher of English Learners Observation (PTEL-O), an observation protocol for language designed to evaluate the student's English language proficiency; and

 

3. Demonstration of student performance in basic skills in English compared against an empirically established range of performance in basic skill based on the performance of English proficient students of the same age (ELA SBA, FAST, Benchmark Advance Interim Assessments, Inspect ELA Interim Assessment, or DRA); and

 

4. Parent opinion and consultation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: 2020-21 Reclassification Criteria was presented at the District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC) general meeting on October 1, 2020. Members and attendees were given time to discuss and provide verbal and/or written feedback to the proposed changes. The proposed 2020-21 revised Reclassification Criteria was discussed and voted on at the Executive DELAC meeting on October 8, 2020. 

 

BACKGROUND: English learners were being denied Reclassification based on course grades that include subjective factors such as homework completion, class participation, and absences.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On August 3, 1990, George H. W. Bush, President of the United States, declared the month of November as Native American Indian Heritage Month.

 

The President authorized and called upon federal, state, and local governments, groups and organizations, and the people of the United States, to observe November with appropriate programs, ceremonies, and activities.

 

The contributions of Native American Indians to this nation’s government, history, and culture are as numerous and varied as the tribes themselves.

 

San Diego County has 19 different Native American Indian reservations, more than any other county in the United States.

 

Approximately 25,000 Native Americans live in San Diego County, most coming from four local tribes: Kumeyaay/Diegueño, Luiseño, Cupeño, and Cahuilla.

 

The San Diego Unified School District, through the Family Engagement Department, as well as through all offices and schools sites, promotes equity for all students and will continue to provide tutoring, career guidance, mental health support, cultural awareness, leadership development, and cultural enrichment programs for Native American students.

 

The commemorative month provides a platform for Native American Indians to share their culture, traditions, music, crafts, dance, and ways and concepts of life.

 

The Indian Education Program and the Native American Indian community have made and continue to provide significant and important contributions to the community and the nation as a whole.

Class 3 consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures; installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures; and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure. The numbers of structures described in this section are the maximum allowable on any legal parcel. Examples of this exemption include accessory (appurtenant) structures including fences, security doors, and security cameras. Therefore, the application of a Class 3 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. Installation of security fencing is consistent with the operation of a school. Generally, the surrounding land uses at the project site consists of residential housing, parkland, an existing school (Gompers Preparatory Academy), and Highway 94 to the north. The Proposed Project would not extend beyond the project site into these areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, existing school operations would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the construction of security fencing and infrastructure at an existing school. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. Removal of ornamental landscaping and trees may be required however, no sensitive or native plant species or trees are proposed for removal. Due to the timing of the Proposed Project, any tree removal would occur outside the nesting season (February 1 to August 31). As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. The project site is over three miles northwest of the nearest scenic highway (State Route 163 near Balboa Park). Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines section 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archaeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because major grading and trenching into native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The Human Resource Services Division launched a Position Description Review Project in the Fall of 2015, as part of the district’s obligation to maintain the classification plan and in response to requests from the bargaining units and management team to develop a process to regularly review positions. The draft position descriptions were prepared using the data collected from employees through an online survey tool as well as management feedback. The goal of this system-wide review of classified and classified management position descriptions was to ensure that position descriptions were more than a list of tasks and duties. A well-written position description gives the reader an understanding of the basic and essential functions as well as the requisite knowledge, skills, and abilities required to perform successfully in the position. By updating position descriptions, we are able to clearly define the roles and interactions, and enhance the efficiency of the organization.

 

In addition to the Position Description Review Project, in the Summer of 2016, the Human Resource Services Division, Administrators Association of San Diego, Police Officers' Association, and all three chapters of the California School Employees Association collaborated to create a joint labor-management Reclassification Committee. The Committee’s role is to accept, review, and make recommendations to the Superintendent for requests for classification review. The committee consists of one representative from each classified bargaining unit and an equal number of district representatives Reclassification Committee.

 

The classified bargaining units received the first set of draft job descriptions on April 7, 2017, and the parties have been in ongoing negotiations since that time. Approved position descriptions are brought before the Board of Education for approval on an ongoing basis as the parties reach agreement.
