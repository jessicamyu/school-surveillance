RECOMMENDATION: Approve the revised Local Educational Agency (LEA) Plan.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: No new impact.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $9,700,000 in Program Improvement Year 3 Funding

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None (one-time funding was/is used to build capacity and provide interventions).


CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Members of the District Advisory Council (DAC) for Compensatory Education, at its November 14, 2012, and December 19, 2012 meetings, were invited to participate in meetings to review and revise the LEA Plan. In addition, along with the DAC, other district and board advisory group representatives (i.e., the District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC), Community Advisory Committee for Special Education (CAC), and Gifted and Talented Education District Advisory Committee (GATE DAC)) participated in a meeting on April 15, 2013, to review and advise on the draft revisions. Subsequently, the revised draft was posted on the DAC webpage and discussed at the April 17, 2013 DAC meeting.

 

BACKGROUND: In November 2011, the State Board of Education designated San Diego Unified a Year 3 Program Improvement district. As a result, the district was required to enter into an agreement with a technical assistance provider to assist with conducting a needs assessment and revising the district's Local Educational Agency (LEA) Plan. The presentation and discussion during the first reading on April 10, 2012, focused on the needs assessment conducted by WestEd, the district's technical assistance provider, and a review of the draft LEA Plan, including the allocation of the $9.7 million that was awarded to the district in support of LEA Plan implementation. A second reading on April 24, 2012, focused on the content of the LEA Plan. Following discussion among board members and comments from the public, staff was directed to further engage district advisory committees to obtain additional feedback regarding the plan. The revised LEA Plan that was approved included, where appropriate, changes arising from the feedback obtained from advisory committees.

 

The California Department of Education requires that districts annually review their LEA Plans and update them as necessary. The updated plan included here was generated from the state's California Accountability and Improvement System (CAIS) that allows us to enter data electronically to monitor implementation. It is important to remember that our LEA Plan was the first to be given approval to depart from the state's template and instead, use the district's 12 indicators of quality schools as the structure for the plan.

 

Following the review by subject matter experts, most of the LEA Plan remains substantially unchanged given the selection of research-based strategies and the need for additional time for full implementation and measurement of outcomes. Some end dates have been adjusted where additional time is needed or duplicate tasks have been added for a second year of implementation (e.g., summer school). Two important foci of the LEA Plan continue to be improving district capacity to provide quality instruction to students in all of our schools and providing interventions to those students who are not successful during first-time instruction. These two foci are supported by also improving our capacity and support related to the other indicators, (e.g., quality leadership, professional learning, broad and challenging curriculum, and safe/supportive environment and well-maintained facilities). One indicator in particular, Valuing Diversity to Make Equity a Reality, now has additional strategies and tasks to provide more detail on how we will improve and monitor implementation.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution of the Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District in support of lower K-3 Class Sizes.

 

BACKGROUND: Keeping class sizes small at all grade levels, particularly K-3, is a research-based best practice for increasing student achievement.

 

National research, along with our own experience in this district, shows that ideal class sizes for K-3 grade levels should be fewer than 17 students per class.

 

Up until the State of California began cutting K-12 education at historic levels in 2008, students in the district benefited from the statewide class size reduction program aimed at keeping K-3 class size levels at 20:1.

 

As the state budget cutbacks deepened and continued over the last five years, San Diego Unified demonstrated its commitment to low K-3 class sizes by maintaining class sizes at lower levels than averages for many comparable large, urban districts.

 

The budget crises for public education will continue for several more years, leaving San Diego Unified facing annual deficits for at least the next three years in order to simply maintain its current staffing and class size levels.

 

Despite the improving economy and the voter passage of Proposition 30, the Governor projects K-12 funding being restored to 2008 levels only by 2020.

 

The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education seeks to bring K-3 class size levels down to levels consistent with research-based best practices as soon as possible while maintaining fiscal responsibility.

 

The Board of Education and Superintendent of the San Diego Unified School District call on all stakeholders to advocate to the Governor and State Legislature to accelerate the restoration of necessary funding to public schools consistent with the will of California's voters.
