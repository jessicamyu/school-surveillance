LAS Links Assessment--In grade-level content classes, students need to be able to understand the teacher’s explanations, discuss what is being learned, read for different purposes, and write about their learning. We call this type of language academic language, the language that is used by teacher and students for the purpose of acquiring new knowledge and skills.

 

Language is used as a functional tool for learning academic subject matter. Students learn not just the vocabulary and grammar of the content area but also learn important concepts and skills using academic language. Students learn the language functions that are important for performing effectively in the content area, such as analyzing, evaluating, justifying, and persuading. Students develop academic language skills in English through cognitively demanding activities in which comprehension is assisted by contextual supports in which scaffold instruction guides the acquisition of content. (Chamot & O’Malley, 1994)

 

LAS Links measures students’ English-language proficiency in four domains: listening, speaking, reading, and writing. LAS Links is designed to assess English-language learners (ELLs), Spanish-language learners (SLLs), and Academic English Learners (AELs). Academic English Learners sometimes known as Standard English Learners (SELs), typically speak English at home, but do not yet have enough academic language skill to succeed in the classroom.

 

LAS Links can be used for English language development and for progress monitoring by using a combination of the LAS Links assessments.  Depending on program need and intent, LAS Links provides districts with an option of measuring student progress up to five times per year.

The LAS Links standardized assessment measures Speaking, Listening, Reading, and Writing, and provides a measure of comprehension skills in academic and social English derived from the Listening and Reading domains. Each of these domains can be administered individually for monitoring specific areas, or can be administered as a whole to provide a complete picture of students' language abilities.  LAS Links blends the assessment of English proficiency with content area knowledge, using vocabulary that is needed for success in school. A wide selection of test formats, including multiple-choice and constructed-response items, are provided, and the content that is used is culturally relevant, and motivating to students. The test results from this assessment link to teaching strategies and instructional guidance for teachers. The results can also assist teachers in modifying instruction to ensure the successful achievement academic settings.

LAS Links is different from assessments that appear in core or supplemental curriculum. While those assessments have value to the classroom teacher in ensuring students are acquiring the skills being taught, they often do not prepare students for the rigors of statewide assessments, including California English Language Development Test (CELDT).  LAS Links' structure and content are similar to the content found in CELDT. The assessment offers real-time, actionable data to classroom teachers to inform instruction, as well as provides valuable aggregated information to principals and school administrators to make informed decisions about program effectiveness, student performance, and areas of need.  LAS Links is a highly reliable, high quality, research-based assessment with a proven track record.

 

Acuity Assessment--Area 1 principals and schools are deeply committed to the learning of each child and to the improvement of student achievement in their schools. The diversity of our schools and students in their academic ability has compelled each administrator to strategize about practices that give teachers and leaders the data needed to make daily instructional decisions and plan structural changes in their schools and classrooms. The use of formative assessment data in the form of benchmark exams is invaluable to the work that goes on in schools.

 

Formative assessment is a process used by teachers and students during instruction that provides feedback to adjust ongoing teaching and learning to improve students’ achievement of intended instructional outcomes (definition by the Formative Assessment for Students and Teachers State Collaborative in Assessment and Student Standards, October 2006). Formative assessment provides teachers critical information about student and classroom progress and can be used to adjust a teacher’s approach and the pace of instruction throughout the year. It is often used as a tool for uncovering opportunities for instructional intervention, and provides this data while there is still time in the school year to improve student achievement. This data is relevant and necessary for immediate changes in the academic learning for children.

 

Area 1 schools in the Lincoln and Morse Clusters (i.e. 27 schools with approximately 20,000 students), under the leadership of Dr. Brenda Campbell, have been given the opportunity to implement the Acuity benchmark assessment system. Acuity assessments are administered online to students in English Language Arts and Mathematics three times a year. These assessments use research based items aligned to state standards. Each assessment builds upon the previous one, allowing schools to monitor growth from test to test. They are based on the California Standards Test helping schools to target instruction that is in alignment to the statewide test at the end of the year. Because they provide predictive data, they help schools monitor student progress towards standards as part of school-wide improvement efforts. Results from each exam can be carefully analyzed to make instructional decisions and identify students who need targeted support. Students can receive additional instruction based on their performance on the assessment, immediately addressing areas of weakness and opportunities for enrichment. The data generated through the Acuity process provides more in depth information for teachers and administrators, making it a more valuable resource than district benchmarks for schools to do formative assessment in English Language Arts and Mathematics.

 

It is the belief of Area 1 schools that this assessment will provide critical information to support school improvement efforts that are not currently available in district benchmarks. Current district benchmark results do not allow us to measure growth or compare results from assessment to assessment. Furthermore, Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium selected CTB/McGraw-Hill to develop next generation of assessments to help schools meet new Common Core State Standards.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $81,000 (for the year 2012) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; and for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services, as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111. The purchase orders are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders.  For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. 2. Revisions to the 2012-13 Adult Education Option 2 Joint High School Diploma Graduation Requirement for Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language; BUDGETED: N/A (OE-2)

RECOMMENDATION: Approve recommended changes to the Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language graduation requirement for the Adult Education Option 2 Joint High School Diploma. This change would provide Adult School Option 2 diploma students with the option of completing one year of a Career Technical Education (CTE) course in lieu of completing one year of a Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language course, as authorized by Assembly Bill 1330.  This change would become effective for the 2012-13 school year, and the option would only be available to Option 2 diploma students enrolled in the Adult School.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School district jointly awards two Adult Education high school diplomas through the district’s Adult School and the Continuing Education (Adult Education) Division of the San Diego Community College District.  The district’s Adult School provides the young adult learner (18 to 19.11 years of age) a last chance to earn a high school diploma through two diploma options, known as Option 1 and Option 2.  Hundreds of these students earn either the Adult Education Option 1 or Option 2 diploma each year in lieu of becoming a high school drop-out statistic. Students, with counselor guidance, select the option that best meets their needs.

 

The Option 1 diploma mirrors current district requirements for a secondary high school diploma, with the exception of the physical education requirement. The Option 2 diploma model enables the district’s most at-risk students one last opportunity to obtain a diploma by completing minimum state high school diploma requirements and four additional semester credits above minimum state requirements, passing the California High School Exit Exam (CAHSEE),  maintaining a 2.0 minimum G.P.A., demonstrating computer literacy, and satisfying the Senior Exhibition requirement. This item ONLY pertains to the Adult Education Joint Option 2 High School Diploma, which was originally approved by the board on August 30, 1994, with revisions to the requirements approved by the board on May 13, 2008.  

 

On April 22, 1991, a joint meeting of the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education and the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees directed that a joint committee of faculty, staff, and administration (from both districts) be created to develop an alternative adult education high school diploma.  In February 1992, the committee began to meet on an ongoing basis and delineated its recommendations for the alternative high school diploma on May 17, 1994. Following is a summary of the committee’s findings:

 

The Adult Education high school student population is unique and distinct from the student population served in the traditional high school setting. The majority of these students have experienced failure in school, and Adult Education programs offer them a last chance to earn a high school diploma. Further, the time demands of the multiplicity of roles these young adult students have already assumed require an interdisciplinary curriculum focused on standards as opposed to credits. The recommended standards for the new Adult Education alternative joint high school diploma preserve the quality and integrity of the high school diploma while making the diploma obtainable for this special student population. This proposed alternative diploma will enable students to transition to the workplace and/or the vocational or academic programs of the community college.  Proposed alternative diploma standards are a blending of high-school level courses and other non-course graduation requirements, college-level courses, and standardized testing.

 

In May 1994, the committee recommended that the board approve the alternative requirements for the Adult Education Joint Option 2 High School Diploma through the authority delegated to governing boards as set forth in Education Code Section 51255.3, subsection (2)(b), as follows:

 

The governing board shall adopt alternative means for pupils to complete the prescribed course of study which may include practical demonstration, supervised work experience, or other outside school experience, vocational classes in high schools, courses offered by regional occupational centers or programs, interdisciplinary study, independent study and credit earned at a postsecondary institution.

 

On August 30, 1994, the board approved the alternative 22 semester credit adult education Option 2 diploma.

 

In March 2008, the aforementioned committee reviewed Option 2 diploma requirements for possible revision.  The committee agreed that the diploma had, over the years, enabled thousands of adult students to obtain the high school diploma and had helped these students successfully transition to the workplace and/or to the community college for further vocational or academic study.  Further, in order to sustain the quality and integrity of this diploma, the committee recommended that the course credit requirement be increased from 22 semester credit to 26 semester credits. The change was in alignment with Education Code requirements. (See attachment for current Option 2 diploma requirements.)

 

On May 13, 2008, the board approved the recommended credit requirement changes to the Adult Education Option 2 Joint High School Diploma by increasing semester credit course requirements from 22 credits to 26 credits.

 

At present, this report brings forward a proposed change to the current Option 2 diploma Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language requirement, as the result of the recent passage of Assembly Bill 1330.  Existing state law requires all graduating high school students to complete one year in Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language.  However, beginning with the 2012-13 school year (class of 2013), Assembly Bill 1330 authorizes local education agencies to accept the completion of one year of a  Career Technical Education (CTE) course as an optional high school graduation requirement in lieu of one year of a Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language course. 

 

In order to provide as many avenues as possible for Adult School Option 2 diploma students, we are requesting that the board approve a revision to the Adult Education Joint Option 2 High School Diploma requirement in the area of Visual or Performing Arts or Foreign Language as specified above, and authorized by Assembly Bill 1330.
