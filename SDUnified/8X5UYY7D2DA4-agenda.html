BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue the improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting districtwide, a new process will be implemented for streamlining Independent Contractor Agreements. The goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND:  The San Diego Police Foundation (SDPF), through its Cyber Safety Public Education Program (CSPEP), provides internet safety presentations at school student assemblies as well as a variety of workshops for parents, educators, professionals and concerned community members.  The goal of each presentation is to increase awareness of internet risks and to reduce child victimization resulting from online activities.  With our students' increased access to the internet, it is critical that they are provided with the necessary education for them to make appropriate decisions while in that environment.

 

In collaboration with the district's Counseling and Guidance and the Educational Technology Departments, SDPF-CSPEP will provide Cyber Safety Program Activities for students in Grades 5-12.  Training sessions will be provided to self-selected district teachers and counselors on cyber safety to prepare them for presenting Cyber Safety Program Activities to their students.  SDPF-CSPEP will also work with students on various topics, including: social networking, cyber bullying, online predators, sex and sexting, and online gaming.

 

Chief Rueben Littlejohn, School Police Services, has reviewed and supports this proposed agreement with the San Diego Police Foundation.

RECOMMENDATION:  Board Trustees, Richard Barrera and Scott Barnett, will discuss implementation of Proposition Z and propose the board take the following actions:


1) Direct the Superintendent to organize an Ad Hoc i21 committee and an Ad Hoc Environmental and Sustainability Committee.  The Superintendent will report back to the board on the composition and proposed timeline in 45 days.  Up to two board members may participate in these committees by expressing interest to the Superintendent.

2) Direct the Superintendent to develop a process and plan to ensure that Proposition Z projects are aligned with the Strategic Process and community based schools while improving operational efficiencies.   Superintendent will report back to the board with the proposed process by September 2013.

BACKGROUND:  The Passage of Proposition Z gives the district an historic opportunity to invest taxpayers’ funds which not only increase the health, safety, security and the academic growth of our students, but will allow us to fund projects which reduce impacts on the environment, improve operational efficiencies, and result in budgetary savings.  The following details the proposed board actions.

i21 Phase II and Beyond  Ad Hoc Committee

i21 Phase II should not simply be sustainability funding to replicate the current five-year i21 program over the next 15 years. When the current i21 roll out ends in 18 months, the district should be prepared to implement a more nimble and cost effective program which continues to increase academic growth, but is able to adapt to changing technology.

The Ad Hoc Committee should include key school stakeholders, but should recruit active participation by technology companies (Qualcomm, Microsoft, Apple, etc.,) as well as current vendors such as Schoolwires, etc. The committee should seek input from clusters and develop plans and recommendations within 120 days.

i21 is a nationally recognized program which has allowed access to current technology by district students and staff.

The current system of buying tens of thousands of computer devices (net books, iPads, etc.,) gives students access to critical tools for academic growth.  But we should consider ways to allow technology already owned by students to be utilized for their academic work. This not only frees up more funds for students who cannot afford these devices, but allows a student’s individual skills and personality to be in play.  In addition, students must be able to use these devices when away from school and be able to use cellular as well as Wi-Fi.

In addition, the committee should review plans and Proposition Z funding earmarked for district communications, technology, and "back of the house" IT, to ensure that we increase efficiency of both academic and business operations and meet the needs of teachers and support staff.

Comprehensive Environmental and Sustainability Planning Ad Hoc Committee

The district’s current utility bill is approximately $18 million a year. Costs of electricity, water, and waste water disposal will continue to rise. Proposition Z gives the district the opportunity to invest in technology and systems which in addition to being environmentally friendly, can reduce increases in utility costs.

The Ad Hoc Committee should include key school stakeholders, but should recruit active participation by energy and environmental technology experts and companies to explore options for solar, energy cells, grey water recycling, and re-use, wind etc.  In addition, environmental groups such as the Sierra Club, League of Conservation Voters, etc., should be included. The Ad Hoc Committee should seek input from clusters and develop plans and recommendations within 120 days.

Proposition Z and Community Based Schools

Staff should develop a process and plan to ensure that Proposition Z projects are aligned with the Strategic Process and community based schools while improving operational efficiencies.  This process should engage and include input from the Proposition S/Z oversight committee (and Ad Hoc Proposition Z and Real Estate Committees), the school realignment task force, and school cluster committees to examine how possible consolidation of school sites can better meet the goals of community based schools, and as a result, increase operational efficiencies and use of bond proceeds.

This process, including cluster and community input is anticipated to take 12-18 months.

For example: instead of simply investing $15 million - $30 million to "upgrade" 40-50 year old elementary and middle school facilities, we should explore consolidating under-enrolled schools and building NEW elementary/middle schools which will serve larger populations and be integrated into their unique communities.  These transitions and consolidations would occur in no less than 3-5 years and for the most part within 10-15 years.

8. Resolution to Increase Ending Balance and Financial Reserves to 2008 Levels in Order to Provide Financial Security and Stability for the San Diego Unified School District (Barrera and Foster)
Meeting
Feb 12, 2013 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution committing to increase ending balance and financial reserves to 2008 levels in order to provide financial security and stability for the San Diego Unified School District.

 

BACKGROUND:  Since 2008, the State of California has continuously reduced funding for K-12 education to a current level that places California nearly last of 50 states in per pupil funding.  The San Diego Unified School District has been required to cut more than $450 million from the General Fund over the last six years as a result of state funding reductions.  The District has been forced to eliminate educational programs, eliminate one week of instructional time for students, reduce overall staffing and cut supportive services to schools.  The Board of Education has placed a budget priority on preserving student teacher ratios as an essential element of maintaining quality instruction for all students. The district has also been forced to reduce available reserves in excess of the state required 2 percent of general fund as a necessary budget reduction strategy in order to maintain adequate teaching levels in classrooms.


In 2008 and in years prior, the district always maintained additional ending balance reserves in the range of $20 million to $40 million annually above the required 2 percent reserve level.  Maintaining strong ending balances and required reserve in the range of 8 to 10 percent of the Unrestricted General Fund are the average for large school districts in the state.

 

The board will direct the Superintendent to develop a district policy establishing an on-going ending balance and reserve level of 5 to 8 percent for the General Fund that would provide the district with a strong fiscal position needed to maintain high credit ratings and to respond to future state funding fluctuations.  The district expects that during the next five to seven years, state funding for K-12 will increase each year as new revenue from Proposition 30 is added to the state budget.  New Proposition 30 revenue will provide the opportunity for the district to rebuild financial reserves and to restore educational programs.


The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District is committed to incrementally restore fiscal reserves to the 2008 level by fiscal year 2016-17, and that the board will increase ending balance and reserve levels each year beginning with the 2014-15 budget year.

4. Hot Weather Temporary Air Conditioners; BUDGETED: YES, PROP Z FUNDS (OE-9)
Meeting
Feb 12, 2013 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type

  RECOMMENDATION:  Staff is providing the board two options to consider to help students when the weather is exceptionally hot.  Possible alternatives are:

  1. Install permanent individual package single classroom systems in Climate Zone 3 where feasible.  Where this is not possible, install small window/portable air conditioning units to provide relief until permanent air conditioning is installed as part of the Whole Site Modernization effort.
     
  2. Include greater weighting in the bond priority matrix for schools in hotter climate zones to expedite Whole Site Modernization projects with air conditioning.

Staff recommends option 1 above.  Regardless of the option selected by the board, staff recommends the following actions:

  1. The district advocate formally with the State Legislature to allow for hot weather days to be considered as appropriate for ADA waivers, along with the current waiver rationale (fires, earthquakes, etc.).
     
  2. The district will develop an internal methodology to determine when to shorten the school day due to excessive heat. Within this methodology, the intent would be to ensure the day would meet the requirements necessary to be counted as a minimum day, thus sustaining ADA while allowing students to go home early and avoiding the hottest part of the day.
     
  3. The board amend District Policy E-4100 a.(2) to specify that all future, new or renovated spaces in Climate Zones 2 and 3 include air conditioning as opposed to passive measures.

FISCAL IMPACT:  Depending on the option chosen, approximately $9,000,000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  On November 3, 1998, voters approved Proposition MM.  The district committed funds in Proposition MM to study, begin planning and implementation of limited thermal relief measures.  As part of this planning effort, the board approved the Phase I Climate Assessment Study on February 27, 2001, and Phase II on October 9, 2001.  The study developed heating, ventilation and air conditioning requirements by getting results from sensors in the classrooms.  Based on these results, the board approved a classroom temperature of 78 degrees to be used as the basis for determining whether a classroom is selected for thermal mitigation in the future, when funding allowed.  As a result, the district was divided into three climate zones:

 

Zone 1: Coastal

Zone 2: The central area of the district (42 schools without air conditioning)

Zone 3: The hottest inland areas (68 schools without air conditioning)

 

Zones 2 and 3 were selected for future air conditioning, when funding allowed.  The board subsequently approved final recommendations on October 9, 2001, and adopted District Policy E-4100 along with a site-ranking list.  Based on the limited funds in Proposition MM, the board directed district staff to utilize passive measures such as (ceiling fans and window tinting, for example) to provide relief to classroom spaces in the hottest areas.  Consistent with E-4100, new schools in Zones 2 and 3 were also designed and constructed to be air conditioning ready.

 

Current Air Conditioning Status

For years state funding for education has been insufficient to meet all the needs of school districts.  Funding was not allocated to install air conditioning in the hottest schools until Proposition S was approved by local voters in 2008.  Proposition S includes whole site modernization work that incorporates installing permanent air conditioning systems in 68 of the hottest schools located in Climate Zone 3, including the new schools that were designed and constructed as “air conditioning ready” as part of Proposition MM (to be implemented from 2013 – 2023).  Under Proposition S permanent air conditioning has been installed, in part of or in whole in 17 schools in Zones 2 and 3.  Twelve projects with some form of air conditioning are in construction and more that 50 projects are in pre-schematic design, Division of State Architect (DSA) review or finishing up the construction document design phase.  To date, Proposition S funding for air conditioning has been less than expected due to the decline in real estate values, tax rate limits and a focus on district-wide classroom technology. 

 

Proposition Z includes installing permanent air conditioning in schools located in climate Zone 2.  Propositions S and Z are expected to provide permanent air conditioning to climate Zone 2 and 3 schools and will cost in excess of $500 million dollars when completed (2013-2025).
