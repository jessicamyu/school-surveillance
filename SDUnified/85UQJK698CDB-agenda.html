RECOMMENDATION:  Discussion of district real property with Gary London, The London Group.  The Board of Education and the Superintendent are exploring potential revenue generating sources.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None at this time.  Depending upon future actions of the board, there may be potential, positive financial impact to the district.


PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY:  None.


IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  Unknown at this time.


BACKGROUND:  The board may decided to engage an expert in real estate strategic analysis and asset evaluation, to examine an excess property portfolio of approximately 27 properties to ascertain their value, alternative use or redevelopment potential, and create a strategic asset management and disposition strategy for the portfolio.  The purpose of this analysis would be to develop strategies to maximize the revenue from the school districts assets in order to increase revenue to support building maintenance.  The boardhas made no policy decisions regarding these properties.


If the board directs staff to proceed with further analysis, a Request for Proposals (RFP) for a professional real estate strategic analysis firm to conduct a detailed economic and feasibility analysis of the district’s asset portfolio, which includes but is not limited to school sites, adjacent land, support sites, etc. 

 

The RFP would request that companies applying must be able to complete all aspects of the following Scope of Work and must meet the following core criteria:

  1. Ability to conduct complex Economic Market Analysis that includes analyzing demographic trends, location advantages, supply and demand factors, and reconciliation of land use opportunities.
  2. Ability to conduct a Residual Land Value Analysis that calculates property value based on achievable development possibilities on the site:
    -  Site Yield Analysis
    -  Construction intensities
    -  Comparable Sales Analysis

  3. Experience specific to San Diego including local and regional real estate analysis of multiple product types that include residential, retail, office, industrial, municipal and mixed-use developments.
  4. Ability to create complex proformas that project performance measures that include but are not limited to internal rate of return, return on investment, margins on cost and revenue and other pertinent measures.
  5. Ability to compil, interpret and analyze data from multiple sources, that include but are not limited to:
  • Internal data (demographics)
  • Claritas data (demographics)
  • Multiple Listing Service (MLS) data
  • Census data
  • Tax records
  • Foreclosure data
  • CoStar data
  • LoopNet data

When applicable, the ability to assess the entitlement and rezoning issues regarding the properties and how it affects the development potential, residual land value of the property and potential project profits.

The Office of College, Career and Technical Education (CCTE) leads the district’s efforts to improve student achievement by integrating core academic subjects with relevant and challenging technical and occupational skills and knowledge. The overarching redesign initiative is based upon student and workforce development, grounded in the California Department of Education vision for Career Technical Education, the mission of the district, and rooted in the GSAs and OEs of the district, to help students make a successful transition from secondary education to college and career. Our mission, in line with that of this district, is Preparing students for their future—college and career ready.

Considered a national and state leader of the integration of core academic and applied/practical arts, CCTE includes over 230 instructors district-wide serving more than 26,000 students. The office is responsible for Career Technical Education, the Junior Reserve Officers Training Corps (JROTC), career based afterschool programs (such as 21st Century Grants and Hire-a-Youth), integrated instructional support and development, CTE industry outreach and collaboration, Work Experience Education (WEE), Proposition 1D facilities in partnership with Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC), and will be taking the lead for Small Learning Communities leadership and support for the 2010-11 school year. Pedagogical philosophies applicable to each area of responsibility are based upon the mission, vision and values of the district, CCTE office and as outlined by the California Plan for Career Technical Education. A diversified leadership model for change management is utilized to ensure a positive and persistent sense of urgency and fidelity to cause.

In California, and consistent with federal government definitions, Career Technical Education (CTE) is a program of study involving a multiyear sequence of courses that integrate core academic knowledge with technical and occupational skills and knowledge to provide students with a pathway to postsecondary education and careers. Programs of study include coherent and rigorous content aligned with challenging academic standards and relevant career and technical content. This is accomplished in a coordinated, non-duplicative progression of courses that align secondary education with postsecondary education. A program of study may also lead to an industry-recognized credential, certificate at the postsecondary level, an associate degree, baccalaureate degree, or applicable advance degree, as pursuant to career aspirations and requirements.

CCTE students are encouraged to think of their school programs of study as the beginning of a series of steps which will take them into post-secondary education programs for technical careers, community college, and/or university; each leading to a successful professional career. With course options beginning as early as elementary school, learners begin their journey of opportunity at a young age with the ability to explore numerous fields of study. However, because it is never too late to begin, foundation courses are made available through the twelfth grade. The overriding vision is to incorporate 21st century skills in K-12 education that truly prepares students to succeed in post-secondary education, the workplace and community life.

CCTE Management Structure                                    
•    2004-07: The Director of CCTE reported to the Office of Secondary School Innovation (OSSI), with the Director of CCTE often acting as the Executive Director of OSSI.
•    2007-10: The Director of CCTE reported to the Deputy Superintendent.
•    2010-11: The Director of CCTE reports to the Assistant Superintendent of ISS who in turn reports to the Deputy Superintendent of Academics.

CCTE is funded through restricted funding sources and has less than a 9 percent administrative cost to 91 percent instructor funding. This is a significant lean ratio compared with other districts that have administrative costs up to 25 percent to only 75 percent instructor funding. Limited unrestricted funds are made available to CCTE and were reduced by over 80 percent in the 2009010 school year.

CCTE Community Governance Structure                                
California State Education Code Section 8070 calls for a Career Technical Education Advisory Committee (CTE AC). This committee is currently standing and meets five times per-year. In addition, CCTE capstone courses that qualify for Regional Occupational Programs funding are required to convene an advisory board to determine if the course curriculum meets industry standards. There are additional ad hoc committees and Small Learning Community advisory boards that meet. During the 2010-11 school year this governance model is being restructured to ensure that cross-collaborative opportunities and efficient systems are being utilized with all subcommittees reporting back to the CTE AC. The CTE AC is a sub-committee of the board.

CCTE San Diego Region Workforce Study Overview (Loescher, September 2009):        
Information gathered from the California Employment Development Department by the San Diego Workforce Partnership in conjunction with Dun and Bradstreet MarketPlace published in Pathway to Prosperity Revisited 2008, indicates that the four largest industry clusters in the San Diego Region in order of size are as follows:


1.    Education
2.    Entertainment and Amusement (a sub-division of Arts, Media and Entertainment)
3.    Construction (Engineering and Design)
4.    Medical Services


These four sectors also represent the industry clusters that are predicted to post the greatest number of additional jobs between 2004 and 2014, in order of predicted postings:

1.    Education: 28,660
2.    Entertainment and Amusement: 20,191
3.    Medical Services: 19,906
4.    Construction: 10,948


Other areas of note included:

5.    Hospitality and Tourism: 6,303


Combined growth in the following areas is predicted to be 13,697 over the same period of time, in alphabetical order:

•    Biomedical Products
•    Biotechnology and Pharmaceuticals
•    Communications
•    Defense and Transportation Equipment
•    Environmental Technology
•    Software

CCTE standards-aligned programs are annually reviewed by local business and labor organizations, as called for in Section 52302.2 of California Education Code, to ensure they meet local industry needs. During these annual reviews advisory boards can make three recommendations with correlating actions:

Recommendation 1: Adopt course as presented.
Action 1: No Change

Recommendation 2: Adopt a course as amended by the committee.
Action 2: Course enters review process with instructional staff and subcommittees.

Recommendation 3: the discontinuation of a course.
Action 3: Course is brought to the ICC and board for deletion from the course of study.

A separate process is in place for exploring course additions that include labor market studies.
