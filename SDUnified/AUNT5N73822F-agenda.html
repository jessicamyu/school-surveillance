RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and approve a Resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula funds and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:

In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials. The board is required to adopt a resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable.

 

Current Williams legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12. The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core lab equipment for science laboratory courses. All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual resolution of sufficiency. Administrative Circulars 6, 7, and 8, dated August 13, 2018, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.” In September 2018, instructional materials inventory data and current PowerSchool enrollment for August 31, 2018, was compiled and entered into the “Instructional Materials Sufficiency Form” and provided to each elementary school by the Instructional Materials Office. Sites were directed to verify their instructional materials inventory and take action to remedy any insufficiencies by ordering from the Instructional Materials Office. Principals confirmed instructional materials sufficiency and returned the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 14, 2018. Secondary school principals, after completing their inventories and taking action to remedy any insufficiencies, also returned the “Principal Verification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency."

 

Twenty-five elementary schools, five K-8 schools, seven middle schools, and six senior high schools were visited by a team from the San Diego County Office of Education. All 43 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials. Additionally, all six senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

RECOMMENDATION:

The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on the status of lead in drinking water. This report will focus on implementation of the district's standard of lead content not exceeding 5 parts per billion (ppb) and the testing and remediation efforts that have been implemented since the board established the 5 ppb threshold in July 2017.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  No additional funds requested.  On July 25, 2017, the board approved a $40,000 expenditure for laboratory costs in 2018-19.  Remediation efforts will be accomplished using Physical Plant Operations (PPO) maintenance funds.  

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  $371,699 in testing and remediation efforts.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Testing and remediation to be performed by district staff.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:

Lead in drinking water became a nationwide discussion due in large part to the 2015 Flint, Michigan health crisis. California responded by requiring municipal water providers to perform sampling at the schools they served.  Testing by the City of San Diego was performed in April - June, 2017.

     

The results of the City testing were favorable with only 0.4% of the drinking water outlets tested exceeding the EPA lead standard of 15 ppb, and 5.9% of the drinking water outlets exceeding 5 ppb.  Following the results from the City testing, in July 2017 the board established an aggressive lead in drinking water threshold of 5 parts per billion (ppb) and directed the Superintendent to embark on a plan to test and remediate all drinking water fixtures throughout the district.  The lead content goal of 5 ppb is one-third of the requirement established by the EPA for municipal water providers.

 

The City test results gave the district a sense of the quality of drinking water and formed a baseline for follow-on work.  Based on the 5 ppb threshold, any location that tested above 5 ppb through the City testing was removed from service and bottled water was provided as needed.     

 

Since July, 2017 district staff have continued the lead in drinking water program, testing 1,168 water samples at 910 drinking water outlets.  All sample analysis was performed by an independent and certified laboratory.  Where the City program performed testing of a few high use drinking fountains at each site, the district staff's approach is to test all drinking water outlets at a school site, fixing schools (the worst first), based on the City’s sampling results.  Cumulative results are:

 

  • 1,877 drinking water outlets tested
  • 1,576 drinking water outlets confirmed less than 5 ppb, through initial testing
  • 266 drinking water outlets remediated and confirmation testing less than 5 ppb
  • 15 school sites with all drinking water outlets confirmed below 5 ppb

 

For any drinking water outlet where test results are over 5 ppb, there are three basic approaches to remediation:

 

  1. Plumbing repairs/plumbing component replacement
  2. Install point of use filters
  3. Remove fixture from service

 

Only after remediation efforts and confirmation testing with results less than 5 ppb is a drinking water outlet returned to service. 

 

The district continues to carry out this work in an open and transparent manner.  Water sample results are posted to the district website and school site and parent meetings are held any time an initial sample result exceeds the 15 ppb threshold. Test results can be found at: https://www.sandiegounified.org/drinking-water-sampling-1.

 

As the district continues to work through the process of testing all drinking water outlets, estimated to be more than 7,200 outlets, a flushing protocol remains in place. School sites until fully cleared, are asked to carry out daily drinking water outlet flushing.  Once all of a site’s drinking water outlets are below 5 ppb, the district still calls for a weekly flushing, conducted in the morning on the first school day of the week.  Additionally, particular emphasis is provided to school staffs to flush after school breaks.

 

Finally, as requested by the board, the experience over the last year provides some insight into achieving a higher goal of no lead content exceeding 1 ppb.  Below are some considerations for establishing a 1 ppb district plan.

 

  • Point of use filters, used in bottle filling stations and drinking fountains are the apparent way to achieve a 1 ppb goal.  Additionally, installing a filter requires new lead free plumbing from the filter outlet to the point of discharge.
  • Filters provide the added benefit of filtering other contaminants beyond lead.
  • Filters will generate a recurring maintenance and operational cost.

 

Proposition YY on the November ballot proposes $45 million in plumbing upgrades to address lead in drinking water.

NMSI’s College Readiness Program has produced unparalleled success in expanding the number of students enrolled and achieving qualifying scores of three or higher on Advanced Placement® (AP®) math, science, and English (MSE) exams, especially among economically disadvantaged and minority students.

a.       Provide forms, data, and reports needed for the Program;

b.       Advise Program Schools in recruiting AP MSE students and teachers;

c.       Advise Program Schools and the District on MSE course offerings and scheduling;

d.       Assist the Partner School Director and/or AP Coordinator in providing the best testing environment for AP students;

e.       Provide subject matter experts to support teachers and students;

f.       Assess, on an annual basis, each AP MSE teacher’s participation in and support of Program goals and expectations. District shall not use such assessment in any evaluation of teacher performance.

g.       Work directly with administrators to proactively address concerns;

h.       If necessary, develop probation plans for struggling Program Schools.

2.       Teacher Training:

a.       Funding support for Program AP MSE teachers to attend NMSI-approved CRP Summer Institutes, Fall Two-Day workshops, and Spring One-Day Trainings. 

b.       Funding support for Program AP MSE teachers and/or Grades 3 through 12 MSE teachers to attend NMSI’s Laying the Foundation Summer Institute, as approved by NMSI. 

3.       AP Course Supplies:

a.       Course supplies for AP MSE courses, subject to approval by NMSI.

4.       Student Study Sessions:

a.       Resources to conduct AP MSE student study sessions over the course of the school year;

b.       Planning and logistical support.

5.       AP Exam Fees:

a.       Reimbursement to the District for the AP MSE examination fee expenses incurred.

6.       Student Recognition:

a.       Each student enrolled in the program will receive $100 for each AP MSE exam score of 3 or higher that each student attains.

 

NMSI is using privately donated funds to cover the cost of the program at two of the schools, and partially cover the cost at the remaining two schools.  The district will be responsible for providing substitute teachers and paying teachers who attend the Saturday trainings.

RECOMMENDATION: Determine that Hoover High School Culinary Arts and Room 1152 Remodel Project is exempt from Environmental Review Under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Pursuant to CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301, 15303, 15304 and 15314; Approve the Project and Agreement with Straight Line General Contractors, Inc. to Provide Culinary Arts and Room 1152 Remodel and Path of Travel Improvements at Hoover High School (CZ19-0150-08). BACKGROUND: The Hoover High School Culinary Arts and Room 1152 Remodel Project (Proposed Project) involves an interior remodel of an existing classroom building at Hoover High School to create a state-of-the-art culinary arts instructional facility, including a restaurant/service simulation space within adjoining classrooms. Upgrades associated with the Proposed Project include new kitchen equipment including grease interceptor, gas service, dry goods storage, instructor workstations, ceilings, lighting, and finishes for two culinary classrooms. Additionally, new exterior building signage would be added above the existing patio to identify the Culinary Arts facility. The Proposed Project includes the remodel of one classroom (Room 1152) for special education, which includes new bathroom and living skills area.

 

Pursuant to Public Contract Code section 20111 (b), the district must publicly competitively bid any public project involving an expenditure of $15,000 or more. The notice calling for bids for the interior remodel of building 900 Culinary Arts Facility and Special Education Classroom 1152, including path of travel improvement, was published once a week for two successive weeks in accordance with Public Contract Code section 20112. Bids were due and opened on August 30, 2018. The district received a total of four bids from Balfour Beatty Construction, LLC; M.A. Stevens Construction, Inc.; SOLPAC Construction, Inc. dba Soltek Pacific Construction Company; and Straight Line General Contractors, Inc.

 

After evaluating the bids, staff determined Straight Line General Contractors, Inc. to be the lowest responsive and responsible bidder.

 

On May 10, 2011, the board approved a resolution in support of service-disabled veterans-owned businesses (SDVOB) and disabled veteran business enterprises (DVBE) which established a three percent required participation of DVBE’s for all district construction contracting opportunities. The recommended contractor, Straight Line General Contractors, Inc. submitted a 5.0 percent DVBE participation in its bid.

 

CEQA Analysis

The proposed activity is a project subject to CEQA. However, the Proposed Project is Categorically Exempt under §15301 – Existing Facilities, §15303 – New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures, §15304(f) - Minor Alterations to Land, and §15314 – Minor Additions to Schools. The Proposed Project is considered categorically exempt because:

Existing Facilities (Class 1) - Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

The Proposed Project includes minor alterations to the interior of an existing structure on the high school campus. Minor alterations include the installation of new kitchen equipment, gas service, dry goods storage, instructor workstations, ceilings, lighting, mechanical units, and finishes for two culinary classrooms. Implementation of the Proposed Project would involve negligible expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination.

New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures (Class 3) - Class 3 consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new small facilities or structures, installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures, and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure (CEQA Guidelines §15303).

The Proposed Project would include the installation of new small equipment to support the culinary arts instructional facility, and exterior signage would be added to the structure above the outdoor patio. In addition, the Proposed Project would include utility extensions to serve the new grease inceptor and automatic fire suppression system in the culinary classrooms. The Proposed Project would convert current general use classrooms to an instructional culinary arts facility, and only minor modifications to the exterior of the structure are proposed with the installation of new exterior building signage.

Minor Alterations to Land (Class 4) - Class 4 consists of minor public or private alterations in the condition of land, water, and/or vegetation which do not involve removal of healthy, mature, scenic trees except for forestry or agricultural purposes (CEQA Guidelines §15304).

The Proposed Project includes minor trenching for underground extension of existing gas service piping and sewer extension. All trenching would occur within previously paved and disturbed areas and would not extend off-site into any undisturbed or environmentally sensitive areas. Alteration of the land would not involve the removal of any healthy, mature scenic trees, and all disturbed areas associated with the trenching work would be restored upon completion of the Proposed Project.

Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14) - Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25% or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314).

The Proposed Project would include the installation of new kitchen equipment, gas service, dry goods storage, instructor workstations, ceilings, lighting, exterior signage, and finishes for two culinary classrooms within the existing school campus. The proposed facility upgrades are intended to serve the existing student body and would not induce any increase in student enrollment. In addition, the Proposed Project does not include the construction of any new classrooms or other facilities that would increase the student capacity of the campus by more than 25% or ten classrooms.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources including, but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of an existing high school in an urbanized community. The site has been developed as a school since 1930 and all areas on the school are either paved or disturbed, and therefore are not environmentally sensitive. Surrounding land uses consist of residential and neighborhood commercial uses. However, the Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed on the school site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the interior remodel of an existing single-story classroom building and installation of new kitchen equipment, exterior signage, and mechanical units on the campus. The Proposed Project site and surrounding neighborhood are located within an urbanized area, and no work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the proposed facility upgrades that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the demolition of existing structures or the construction of new facilities that would directly or indirectly affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the Proposed Project is not located on a site where hazardous materials or hazardous waste have been identified, nor is it located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. A Cleanup Program Site is located on the project site; however, the status of the case is “closed” as of 1994. In addition, a 100-gallon underground storage tank (UST) was previously utilized on the school site to fuel an emergency generator within the electrical transformer enclosure. The UST and associated piping were removed under the oversight of the San Diego County Department of Environmental Health on August 13, 2001 (Bestard pers. comm.). As such, no hazardous conditions exist on the site as a result of the previous Cleanup Program Site or former UST. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. The Proposed Project includes the interior remodel of an existing single-story classroom building on the Hoover High School campus, which was reconstructed in 1976, and therefore does not reach the 50-year age threshold for consideration as a potential historical resource. Therefore, the Proposed Project would not cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource as defined in Section 15064.5 of the State CEQA Guidelines, and this exception does not apply to the project.

CEQA Analysis

 

REASONS PROJECT IS EXEMPT FROM CEQA:

The proposed activity is a project subject to CEQA. However, the Proposed Project is Categorically Exempt under §15301 – Existing Facilities, §15302 – Replacement or Reconstruction, and §15314 – Minor Additions to Schools. The Proposed Project is considered categorically exempt because:

 

Existing Facilities (Class 1) - Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

 

The Proposed Project includes modernization of the existing facilities on the school campus. Minor alterations include minor food service upgrades, exterior concrete paving replacement, landscaping and irrigation improvements, modifications to perimeter security fencing/gates, and new heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems in several existing buildings. Implementation of the Proposed Project would involve negligible expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination.

 

Replacement or Reconstruction (Class 2) - Class 2 consists of replacement or reconstruction of existing structures and facilities where the new structure will be located on the same site as the structure replaced and will have substantially the same purpose and capacity as the structure replaced (CEQA Guidelines §15302).

 

The Proposed Project would include demolition of the existing classroom building and original administration building and construction of a new two-story, 26-classroom building. The Proposed Project also includes removal and replacement of the exterior concrete for an accessible path of travel and site circulation improvements. Furthermore, the Proposed Project includes upgrades to the existing food service area, including the installation of a three-compartment sink and a grease trap, for functionality and code requirements. All of these new structures and facilities would serve the same purpose and capacity as the structures and facilities being replaced.

 

Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14) - Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25% or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314).

 

Implementation of the Proposed Project would decrease the number of classrooms from 42 to 40 and would not increase enrollment capacity. Therefore, the Proposed Project does not include the construction of any new classrooms or other facilities that would increase the student capacity of the campus by more than 25% or ten classrooms.

(a)    Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. RECOMMENDATION: Ratify Resolution delegating authority to Jodie Macalos, Controller, to be the Plan Administrator for San Diego Unified School District's 403(b) and 457 Tax-Sheltered Annuity Plans, effective July 24, 2018. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The California Government Code and Education Code recognize the complexities in the day-to-day operation of school districts authorizing boards of education to delegate authority and responsibility to the superintendent and staff members to perform the many routine functions of a school district. Annually, the Board of Education has delegated authority and assigned ministerial responsibility to staff members by adopting a series of resolutions before July 1, specifying the responsibility and authority authorized by the California Government and Education Codes, and on June 19, 2018, the board adopted the annual delegation of authority resolutions for 2018-19 covering such items as signing contracts, acceptance and recording of deeds, certification of board meeting actions and minutes, rejection of claims against the district, employee mileage allowances, execution of personnel action forms, travel allowances within the amount budgeted by the board, execution of loyalty oaths, financial report documents, etc.

 

Employee requests for withdrawals of their individual tax-sheltered annuity monies require the signature of the Plan Administrator. The delegation of authority for the Plan Administrator is assigned to the Controller of the district.

 

Jodie Macalos was appointed Controller on July 24, 2018. This Resolution is necessary to delegate authority to Jodie Macalos, Controller, as Plan Administrator of the district's 403(b) and 457 tax-sheltered annuity plans, effective July 24, 2018.
