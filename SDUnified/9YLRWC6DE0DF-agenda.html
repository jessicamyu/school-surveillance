BACKGROUND: The son of migrant farm workers, Cesar E. Chavez learned about the oppression of field laborers firsthand and directed his life goals and aspirations toward educating his fellow farm workers to understand their rights to a safe workplace and a just wage.

 

In 1962, Chavez founded the United Farm Workers of America. From his studies in civic action and his study of the work of Gandhi and Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., Chavez developed and adhered to principles of non-violence in his crusade for peace and justice. César B. Chavez said, “Real education should consist of drawing the goodness and the best out of our own students. What better book can there be than the book of humanity.”

 

César E. Chavez and legions of loyal supporters dedicated themselves to the education of migrant workers and their children through the establishment of better schools, fighting to empower farm workers through voter registration, and teaching community members how to organize themselves to effectively represent their interests with government, school, and financial institutions.

 

California schools are authorized to commemorate César E. Chavez with student community service projects on Thursday, March 31, and are directed to provide instruction on the farm labor movement in the United States and the role of César E. Chavez

 

The successful leadership development of César E. Chavez Service Clubs, organized by San Diego Unified and Urban Corps of San Diego, are now being emulated in California’s most populated cities throughout the state.

 

San Diego Unified dedicated César E. Chavez Elementary School, in September 1997, to provide an education program for more than 700 students who commemorated his birthday with special activities including a community march on March 16, 2016.

BACKGROUND:  The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education recognizes the strong relationship between students’ nutrition, fitness, and health status with their school attendance, access to education and ability to learn. Good health, facilitated by both knowledge and practice of healthy eating and exercise behaviors and habits, is a major factor in helping students function at optimal levels.

 

On April 14, 2015 the board adopted a revised School Wellness Policy which is based upon the Whole School, Whole Community, Whole Child (WSCC) approach which encompasses food services, health education, and family/community involvement, among other components, to encourage nutrition education, a healthy diet, and an integrated health education curriculum to reduce or eliminate behaviors that put students’ health at risk (District Policy H-7700).

 

Diabetes, obesity, heart disease, tooth decay and other chronic diseases are disproportionate among certain ethnic and racial populations in San Diego County and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimate that 52 percent of all American adults will have diabetes or pre-diabetes by 2020. Programs that can reduce the incidence of diabetes need to be supported, expanded and covered not only to save lives but also to prevent soaring health care expenditures.

 

San Diego Unified Food Services is a recognized national leader in healthy menu options, a participant in the Farm to School program for fresh, local fruits and vegetables and includes vegetarian options on a daily basis.

 

The Chavistas (Club members) and the Cesar Chavez Service Clubs (Chavez Clubs) are beginning to develop curriculum to engage students on the health risks of sugary drinks (AKA Sugar’s Not Always Sweet) to be shared with San Diego Unified students, families, and the community.

 

The San Diego Unified Board of Education and Superintendent enthusiastically support a partnership between the Superintendent, staff from the Food Services Department, the Nursing and Wellness Department, the District Wellness Committee and the Chavez Clubs to develop and implement a program to educate students on the health risks of sugary drinks, “Sugar’s Not Always Sweet.”

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt the resolution in support for the California Department of Education (CDE) collection of data to improve services to Special Education students and opposition to federal court order to the CDE to release students' personal data.

 

BACKGROUND: In April 2012, the Morgan Hill Concerned Parents Association and the Concerned Parent Association, filed a lawsuit against the California Department of Education (CDE) alleging that CDE has violated the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) by failing to monitor, investigate, provide services to and enforce the rights of students with disabilities.

 

Although the San Diego Unified School District is not a party to the case, San Diego Unified’s past and current students are among the students whose rights the plaintiffs allege were violated.

 

In order to prove their case, plaintiffs requested that CDE disclose, subject to a Protective Order, documents contained in CDE’s databases and network drives which also contain confidential information about students, including past and current San Diego Unified students, who received or requested special education services or assessments, including names and social security numbers, which is protected from disclosure under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA).

 

The U.S. District Court ordered CDE to produce certain of this information to plaintiffs’ legal counsel, subject to a protective order which prohibits counsel from disclosing such information to the public, and requires counsel establish to the satisfaction of the court that they can maintain the information in a secure manner.

 

The court provided parents, guardians, and former students over 18 the opportunity to file an Objection to Disclosure of Student Information and Records form with the court prior to April 1, 2016. The form is available at http://www.cde.ca.gov/morganhillcase.

 

Due to the large number of objections already received by the court, the judge has ordered that the CDE maintain custody of the most sensitive of its databases, the California Longitudinal Pupil Achievement Data System (CALPADS), while running searches for the requested information.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District recognizes the importance of enforcing the rights of students, including special education students, and recognizes that sharing of the requested information may ultimately result in improved special education services throughout California. However, the Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District strongly objects to the public disclosure of any personal, identifying student information contained in the CDE’s education records.

BACKGROUND: An election was held for the district on November 4, 2008, at which a bond proposition (Proposition S) was passed by electors of the district authorizing the sale of $2.1 billion in district general obligation bonds pursuant to California Constitution Article XIII (A), to be used for the construction, reconstruction and/or rehabilitation of school facilities, including the furnishing and equipping of school facilities.  Pursuant to Proposition S, the district shall only issue bonds if it projects that the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on the proposed bonds, when combined with the tax rate necessary to pay debt service on presently outstanding bonds of the district in each year, will not exceed the rate levied in 2007-08 (being $66.70 per $100,000 of assessed valuation) to pay debt service on the presently outstanding bonds alone, referring to bonds issued under Propositions MM and S. BACKGROUND: Proposition 39 (Education Code section 47614) requires the district to offer reasonably equivalent facilities to charter schools that show that they are eligible to receive such facilities.  At its January 26, 2016 meeting, the board approved district staff recommendations of preliminary offers of facilities to eligible charter schools that made appropriate applications for facilities under the provisions of California Education Code section 47614 and California Code of Regulations Title 5, sections 11969 et seq.  Education Code section 47614 and the California Code of Regulations, Title 5 sections 11969 et seq. Subsequently, staff analyzed each of the requests in conformity with the requirements of Education Code section 47614 and the implementing regulations and developed recommendations for the final offers of facilities for eligible charter schools.  Staff is submitting this report and its recommendations for board approval and requests for the board to adopt the report as its statement of reasons.

 

The allocation of district facilities to charter schools pursuant to Proposition 39 constitutes a project for purposes of the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code section 21000 et seq.   However, the project is exempt from further environmental analysis under the General Rule clause and two categorical exemption classes as set forth below and as further described in the attached Notice of Exemption (NOE):

 

15061(b)(3). GENERAL RULE

 

The activity is covered by the general rule that CEQA applies only to projects which have the potential for causing a significant effect on the environment. Where it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that the activity in question may have a significant effect on the environment, the activity is not subject to CEQA.

The proposed project would not result in any physical changes to the affected school sites. Except for Empower Charter School, the remaining school sites already accommodate the operation of both the charter school and district-operated school. The continued co-occupancy of the charter school(s) and district-operated school would not exceed the capacity of that school site.  Therefore, no physical changes are required and it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that the continued co-location may have a significant effect on the environment.

 

15314. MINOR ADDITIONS TO SCHOOLS

 

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption.

 

The project would not add new classrooms or other facilities on the existing campuses. Additionally, the co-occupancy of the charter school and district-operated school would not exceed the existing capacity of that school site.

 

15322. EDUCATIONAL OR TRAINING PROGRAMS INVOLVING NO PHYSICAL CHANGES

 

Class 22 consists of the adoption, alteration, or termination of educational or training programs, which involve no physical alteration in the area affected or which involve physical changes only in the interior of existing school or training structures. 

 

The proposed activity would result in either the continued operation of two education programs on an existing school site or the introduction of a new charter school program at an existing district-operating school (i.e., Empower Charter School at Carver Elementary School). The combined programs on the campuses would not require any physical changes to any of the sites.

 

Categorical exemptions may be not be relied upon if the application of the exemption triggers any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2.  Staff has reviewed the sites on which the charter schools will be located and determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemptions.  Approval of this item authorizes and directs staff to file a Notice of Exemption in substantial conformance with the Notice of Exemption Attachment with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. The purchase orders, contracts, leases, or other documents are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders, contracts, leases or other documents. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 9, 2015, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

BACKGROUND: The School Climate Survey for students and staff consists of The California Healthy Kids Survey and the California Staff Climate Survey which will provide the metrics as outlined in LCAP Goal 4: Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture – With Equity At The Core and Support For The Whole Child. 

 

The Counseling and Guidance Department will collaborate with WestEd to administer the California Healthy Kids Survey (CHKS) and the California Staff Climate Survey (CSCS) which will provide school climate data for all schools.
