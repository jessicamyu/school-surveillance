BACKGROUND: In an effort to continue our improvement of procedures and processes for centralized procurement and contracting district wide, we are implementing a new process streamlining our Independent Contractor Agreements. The goal is to limit and reduce the number of contracted service items that are included in this item for approval via the ratification process. Ratification is an after-the-fact request for the board to approve services already provided or being provided at the time the Independent Contractors Agreement (ICA) is presented for approval.

Chiefs and Area Superintendents should work with requestors to reduce and eliminate the need for ratified ICA's. Effective immediately, after an ICA is submitted to and approved by the respective deputy or chief, it will then be sent to the Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Department. The ICA will include a requisition number that pre-encumbers the funding for payment of the ICA after services are completed. After board approval, a purchase order will be created and sent to the contractor.

BACKGROUND: Nonpublic Schools (NPS) and Nonpublic Agencies (NPA) provide special educational and/or related services to students with exceptional needs under the authorization of Education Code sections 56157, 56361, and 56365-56366.5 identified in the student's Individualized Education Program (IEP) or Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP). The nonpublic program services are inclusive educational services that may include a  variety of support services, vocational services, individual, group and family counseling services in addition to instruction. The nonpublic agency programs provide educational support services including tutoring, individual, group and family counseling and staff training and are provided in the home, office, or school setting. 

 

A Master Contract is developed and approved by each school district's Special Education Local Plan Area (SELPA) representative. The rates for the services identified in the Master Contract are negotiated by the San Diego County Office of Education (SDCOE) and the SELPA representatives of each school district. In July of 2010, the SDCOE distributed Master Contracts to all school districts with direction for the Governing Board of each district to approve individual agreements with each nonpublic school and/or nonpublic agency. On August 3, 2010, the board approved agreements for the term July 1, 2010 through December 31, 2010.  Shortly thereafter, the nonpublic schools and nonpublic agencies disputed terms and conditions in the Master Contract, and, as a result, chose not to execute their respective agreements. Without a new fully executed Master Contract in effect, the district and the nonpublic school and/or agency continued to provide services to students in accordance with the terms and conditions of the previous Master Contract. These terms and conditions state that, if a new contract has not been fully executed by the nonpublic school, nonpublic agency and district, then the terms of the current board approved contracts, approved on February 23, 2010, shall apply to any and all special education services provided by the NPS or NPA and the resultant payments for those services by the district. 

 

The SELPA representatives entered into ongoing negotiations with the nonpublic schools and nonpublic agencies but were not able to reach an agreement.  The San Diego County Superintendent of Schools requested intervention by the Division of Administrative Hearings. On June 1, 2011, the Judge for the Office of Administrative Hearing issued a stipulated order regarding the Master Contract between nonpublic schools (NPS) and the school districts within San Diego County. The various components of the stipulated order were subsequently incorporated into the new Master Contracts for both the 2010-11 and 2011-12 school years which were then distributed to the nonpublic schools and agencies and the SELPA representatives for finalization and ratification by the respective school boards.

BACKGROUND:  In the recent decision by the U.S. District Court, Southern District, (Thalheimer v. City of San Diego (U.S.D.C., S.D. Cal. 2012) – F.Supp.2d --, 2012 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 6563) the Court found that certain parts of the City of San Diego’s campaign contribution ordinance to be unconstitutional on First Amendment grounds.  However, the Court did approve the restriction of campaign contributions to natural persons and the ban of contributions from any other entity or organization.  Nevertheless, in writing of the special status of political parties in the First Amendment and campaign context, the Court found that the City could not exclude political parties and their state and local central committees from making direct contributions to candidates in non-partisan elections.  The Court did not set a limit, but ordered the City Council to consider a more appropriate limit in the $5,000 to $20,000 range (noting that a number of cities had no limit).  General Counsel has made revisions which will meet constitutional requirements for this district’s campaign expenditure regulation resolution – elimination of contributions by organizations and permission for political parties to contribute directly to board candidates not to exceed $5,000.

2. Potential 2012 Capital Facilities Bond Measure; BUDGETED: N/A (OE-9)
Meeting
Feb 14, 2012 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: Staff will share with the board the development of potential bond elements that could be included in a future bond measure and next steps to placing the measure on the ballot.  Information only.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The phases of work required to place a future bond measure on the ballot and estimated costs are as follows:

  • Phase I develop and evaluate potential bond elements and stakeholder outreach approximately $5,000
  • Phase II scope assessment, cost estimating of bond program alternatives for board review and approval $190,000
  • Phase III develop site specific bond project lists and budgets $110,000
  • Phase IV the cost of placing a measure on the ballot $500K to $1M  (the actual cost is driven by the number of pages in the voter guide) funding source to be determined.

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Proposition S, the capital facilities bond that was approved by voters on November 4, 2008, provided $2.1 billion in funding authorization.  Proposition S was a tax rate extension measure not intended to meet all of the facilities deficiencies identified in the district's long range facilities master plan.  On November 1, 2011, the Board of Education approved a $30,000 consulting contract with the Primacy Group to conduct a pre-bond feasibility study for a 2012 capital facilities bond; that study is underway.  Based on a review of the remaining unfunded need, and new requirements that have been identified since the passage of Proposition S, district staff has developed potential bond elements that could be included in a future bond and the next steps in moving forward to place a measure on the ballot.  These have been summarized in the attached PowerPoint presentation.
