RECOMMENDATION: Approve Resolution of the Sufficiency of Textbooks and/or Instructional Materials and certify that:

  1. the governing board met before the end of the eighth week of school and did not take place during or immediately following school hours; and
  1. ten days notice of the public hearing was posted in at least three public places (district website, Eugene Brucker Education Center, Instructional Media Center) stating time, place, and purpose; and
  1. every pupil, including English language learners, has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both, that are aligned to the content standards and cycles of the curriculum framework adopted by the State Board of Education: mathematics, science, history/social science, and English language arts; and
  1. every pupil enrolled in a world language or health course has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both, and are consistent with the content and cycles of the curriculum frameworks adopted by the state board; and
  1. laboratory science equipment is available to science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12 inclusive; and
  1. “sufficient textbooks or instructional materials” means each pupil, including English language learners, has a textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home and this does not require two sets of textbooks or instructional materials for each pupil; and
  1. beginning 2008-09 through 2014-15 fiscal years, “sufficient textbooks or instructional materials” also means that all students, who are enrolled in the same course within San Diego Unified, have standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials from the same adoption cycle; and
  1. all district schools have conducted classroom instructional materials inventories and insufficiencies have been remedied.   Principals have submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office and are available for review upon request.

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) funds and state lottery funds to purchase textbooks and instructional materials according to the state adoption cycle. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by submitting a district-adopted Core Student Instructional Materials Order Form or requesting transfers of textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System to the district’s Instructional Materials Office. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a public hearing no later than the eighth week of the school year to determine, through a resolution, whether each pupil in the district has sufficient textbooks or instructional materials, or both in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English  language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. Access to two sets of instructional materials is not required.

 

Current Williams legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12.  The Science Department and San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core lab equipment for science laboratory courses.  All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of our annual resolution of sufficiency.  Administrative Circulars 12, 13, and 14, dated August 20, 2013, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”   In September, 2013, inventory data for each elementary school was analyzed by the Instructional Materials Office and compared to Powerschool enrollment.  Action was taken to remedy any insufficiencies and a copy of their “Instructional Materials Sufficiency Survey” was provided to elementary school principals.  After confirming the accuracy of their inventories, each principal verified sufficiency and returned the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 20, 2013. Secondary school principals, after completing their inventories and taking action to remedy any insufficiencies, also returned the “Principal Verification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency”.  

 

Twenty-eight elementary schools, five K-8 schools, eight middle schools, and seven senior high schools were visited by a team from San Diego County Office of Education.  All 48 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials.  All seven senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

 

Pursuant to Education Code Section 60119, ten days notice of the public hearing was posted containing the time, place, and purpose of the hearing.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the Resolution in the Matter of Certification of Provision of Standards-Aligned Instructional Materials in order to receive funds for instructional materials from the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) and certify that:  

  1. the governing board met before the end of the eighth week of school and did not take place during or immediately following school hours; and
  1. ten days notice of the public hearing was posted in at least three public places (district website, Eugene Brucker Education Center, Instructional Media Center) stating time, place, and purpose; and
  1. every pupil, Kindergarten, (including Transitional Kindergarten) through Grade 12, has been provided with a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials in each of the following areas: mathematics, science, history/social science, and English language arts; and
  1. every pupil enrolled in a world language or health course has been provided a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials; and
  1. laboratory science equipment is available to science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12 inclusive; and
  1. instructional materials for students in Kindergarten (including Transitional Kindergarten) through Grade 8 were purchased from an approved standards-aligned state adoption list.

FISCAL IMPACT:  The district may use funding from various sources, including state funds from the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) and state lottery funds to purchase textbooks and instructional materials according to the state and local adoption cycles. Once the provision of standards-aligned instructional materials in the four core academic subject areas is determined through this resolution, IMFRP funding typically may be spent on other instructional materials pursuant to Education Code 60242.  Education Code 42605 provides further flexibility with the use of these funds for any educational purpose for the 2008-09 fiscal year to the 2014-15 fiscal year, inclusive.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: AB 1781 established the Instructional Materials Funding Realignment Program (IMFRP) that, starting with the 2002-03 fiscal year, combined three previous funding sources for instructional materials.  The program requires that districts must use funds to ensure each pupil, K (including TK)-12, is provided with a standards-aligned textbook or basic instructional materials in the four core subject areas: English language arts, mathematics; science; and history/social science.  Additionally, in accordance with State regulations, the governing board must also certify the standards-aligned basic instructional materials are provided by the beginning of the school term that starts no later than 24 months after those materials were adopted.  Education Code 60049 waives this 24 month requirement for the 2008-09 to the 2014-15 fiscal years, inclusively.  The governing board of a school district is not required to provide pupils with instructional materials by a specified period of time following adoption of those materials by the state board.  This does not relieve school districts of their obligations to provide every pupil with standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, per Education Code 1240.3.  Once the governing board certifies it has provided each pupil with standards-aligned instructional materials, the district may use 100 per cent of any remaining IMFRP funds to purchase other instructional materials consistent with the content and cycles of the curriculum frameworks.  Education Code 42605 provides further flexibility with the use of these funds for any educational purpose for the 2008-09 fiscal year to the 2014-15 fiscal year, inclusive. 

 

For the purpose of transitioning to instructional materials that are aligned with the common core academic content standards,  Education Code 60119 allows textbooks, instructional materials, and supplemental instructional materials be deemed to be aligned with the content standards, and be deemed consistent with the content and cycles of the curriculum framework adopted by the state board if the textbooks, instructional materials, supplemental instructional materials, or a combination of any such materials are aligned to the content standards adopted pursuant to Education Code 60605.

 

For students in Kindergarten (including Transitional Kindergarten) through Grade 8, the instructional materials were purchased from an approved standards-aligned state adoption list as required by CCR, Title 5, Section 9531.

 

Pursuant to Education Code Section 60119, ten days notice of the public hearing was posted containing the time, place, and purpose of the hearing.

RECOMMENDATION: Denial of the petition to establish Kieran Egan Legacy Charter School (KELCS) based on the following findings:

  1. The KELCS petitioners (“Petitioners”) are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the (“Petition”) because the Petitioners are unfamiliar with the contents of the Petition.  The Petition proposes to open an elementary school in September of 2014, with San Diego Unified as the authorizer.  However, several sections of the Petition appear to describe a different school for a different authorizer. 
    1. Page 85 of the Petition refers to holding open house community information meetings to recruit students in April 2012, a date in the past.
       
    2. Page 110 of the Petition, in describing the return rights of district employee, states that “SBCSS policies” will apply.  SBCSS may refer to the San Bernardino County Superintendent of Schools, a different authorizer located in a different county. 
       
    3. Although the Petitioners propose establishing an elementary school serving Grades K-6, page 72 of the Petition states that “Teachers represented in this attached list are California Credentialed Teachers authorized to work in secondary and/or a single subject environment.”  The references to secondary and single subject credentials indicate the Petition is describing an education program serving upper grade levels.
       
  2. The Petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the Petition because the Petitioners are unfamiliar with the requirements of the laws that would apply to the proposed charter school. 
    1. The Charter Schools Act of 1992 (Ed. Code sections 47600 et seq.) sets forth the required elements to establish a charter school in the California and was amended earlier this year to expand the pupil outcomes element in Education Code section 47605 and other areas.  The Petition does not include or address the amended language.
       
    2. The Petition includes language that is inconsistent with the requirements of the Charter Schools Act with respect to the establishment of an additional site.  Page 75 of the Petition states, “If [KELCS] moves or expands to another facility during the term of this charter, it shall not be deemed a Material Revision of this charter.”  This language conflicts with Education Code section 47605(a)(4) which states, “After receiving approval of its petition, a charter school that proposes to establish operations at one or more additional sites shall request a material revision to its charter.” 
       
  3. The Petitioners are demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program set forth in the Petition based on the lack of support for the Petition shown at the public hearing.  Aside from the lead petitioner, Robert R. Dunton, there were no teachers, other employees of the district, or parents or guardians who spoke in favor of the Petition.

The Filipino American National Historical Society (FANHS) recognizes the year 1763 as the date of the first permanent Filipino settlement in the United States in St. Malo, Louisiana, which set in motion the focus on the story of the Nation’s past from a new perspective by concentrating on the contributions that Filipino Americans have made in countless ways toward the development of the history of the United States.

It is imperative for Filipino American youth to have positive role models in their schools and communities to instill in them the importance of education, complemented with the richness of their ethnicity, and the value of their legacy.

 

The San Diego Chapter of the FANHS is an active community organization promoting understanding, education, enlightenment, appreciation, and enrichment through the identification, gathering, preservation, and dissemination of the history and culture of Filipino Americans in the United States.

 

The Filipino American community in San Diego County is the largest Asian American group in the county and the second largest in the United States with a population of approximately 3,100,000 people.

 

A portion of California State Route 54 in San Diego is officially named the “Filipino-American Highway”, in honor of the Filipino American community.

 

Bell Middle School has taught the Filipino language for the past 25 years and the school will observe the 1st Annual Filipino Heritage Celebration on October 25, 2013.

Human Resources staff was asked by the Office of the Deputy Superintendent, Business Support Services, to create the new supervisory job classification, Supervising Community Services Officer, to provide adequate supervisor-to-employee ratios within the Police Department that are consistent with national law enforcement standards, as well as Federal Emergency Management Administration (FEMA) recommendations for managing emergencies.  This job class will allow School Police Community Services Officers (CSOs) to receive better training, guidance, and supervision, while lowering risk and liability to the district.  Supervising CSOs will be tasked with overseeing regular CSOs as they perform essential safety duties at elementary schools throughout the district on a daily basis.  The Supervising CSO position also offers all CSOs a career path that was once absent within their classification.  Furthermore, the San Diego Police Department (SDPD) currently serves as School Police’s official “Keeper of Records” (they retain all School Police crime and arrest reports).  Circumstances outside the control of School Police will soon force the migration from SDPD for these services to become our own “Keeper of Records."  The Supervising CSO will be responsible for routing police reports while coordinating law enforcement sensitive data with the joint powers agency, Automated Regional Justice Information System (ARJIS) that requires the sharing of justice information among county law enforcement.
