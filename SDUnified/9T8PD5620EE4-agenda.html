3. Resolution to Reconfirm Support to Close Commercial Property Loophole in Proposition 13 (McQuary/Barrera)
Meeting
Jul 14, 2015 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action (Consent)

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution to reconfirm  support to close the commercial Property Loophole in Proposition 13.  The board supports SCA 5 (Hancock, Mitchell) currently in the State Legislature, that will establish the regular and fair reassessment of commercial and industrial property to market value while protecting small businesses and maintaining Proposition 13 protections for residential properties including homeowners and renters.

 

BACKGROUND: Voters in the state of California approved Proposition 13 in 1978; Proposition 13 created limits on the property taxes paid by residential and commercial properties.

 

Residential and commercial property values in California are reassessed upon change of ownership and on average, California residential property changes hands frequently while change of ownership for commercial property is far more complicated and therefore generates reassessments less often; commercial property owners are able to avoid reassessment of their property by limiting the portion of ownership that changes hands to ensure that no single party owns more than 50 percent.

 

Prior to 1978, property taxes represented the single, largest source of funding for public schools and Proposition 13 resulted in an over 50 percent drop in property tax collections and since its passage, the state has assumed a greater role in funding of schools.

 

Since the State of California has assumed a greater role in the funding of public schools, per-pupil support has declined from the top 10 in the nation to the bottom 10.

 

Proposition 13 is anti-competitive in that new entrepreneurs and businesses must pay fair market value for their property, while commercial property owners who have owned their property for a longer time pay disproportionately lower property taxes.

 

Public schools in California face challenges in providing an equitable and fair education for a student population with vast differences in language, poverty, parental education level, and other social, educational, and economic factors and regularly reassessing non-residential commercial property would, according to an analysis of data by researchers at USC Program for Environmental and Regional Equity, generate $9 billion in additional revenue for public schools and other public services by 2019-20.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supports SCA 5 (Hancock, Mitchell) currently in the State Legislature, that will establish the regular and fair reassessment of commercial and industrial property to market value while protecting small businesses and maintaining Proposition 13 protections for residential properties including homeowners and renters; and that tax revenues generated by modernizing how commercial property is reassessed benefit local schools and essential social services and that accountability measures be enacted so new funds are spent appropriately.

 

The Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District will communicate this position in a letter to the State Senate Governance and Finance Committee, local elected officials including members of the San Diego County Board of Supervisors, the San Diego City Council, as well as Senators Marty Block, Ben Hueso, and Joel Anderson, and Assemblymembers Brian Maienschein, Toni Atkins, Shirley Weber, and Lorena Gonzalez.

RECOMMENDATION:  On April 29, 2014, the board approved the Lincoln High School STEAM Middle College plan to begin in the 2014-15 school year.  It’s over a year later and the board is giving the Superintendent direction to bring back an update on July 28, 2015, as to the progress of the Lincoln High School STEAM Middle College implementation.

 

BACKGROUND:  The Board of Education approved on April 29, 2014, that Lincoln High School would become an early/middle college starting in the 2014-15 school year.

An early/middle college combines a high school and a community college, with high school students able to take college-credit classes, and in some cases earn associate degrees, before graduation from high school.

 

The San Diego Community College District is providing the "college" portion of the work, in collaboration with San Diego Unified staff. The emphasis at the campus is STEAM subjects: science, technology, engineering, arts and math.

 

The Lincoln plan places an emphasis on hands-on, project-based learning, with an advisory model utilizing coordinators, internships for students, student exhibitions and dual enrollment with San Diego Unified and the community college, providing students the opportunity to earn college credit.

 

The Lincoln High School STEAM Middle College plan emphasizes the following:

  • Foster an innovative learning environment through professional development and distributed leadership, enabling administrators and teachers to respond to the challenges of preparing the under-prepared (closing the achievement gap) and advancing the well prepared through mastery, competency, and project-based learning.
  • Provide the faculty with state-of-the art professional development and time for developing curriculum modules/Common Core Standards and planning instruction.
  • Infuse design and the arts into all aspects of the curriculum as a means of nurturing creativity and innovations and creating competent problem-solvers.
  • Meeting diverse students where they are academically when they enter high school, enabling all to master high school work and engage in complex real-world questions through a STEAM lens.
  • As students demonstrate key skills, academic course content, and rigor, they can enroll in college level courses and earn multiple years of college credit for free.
  • Academic, social supports and interventions (academic labs in English/math) that will help students succeed in a challenging course of study.
  • Support to ease the difficult transition between high school and college: students begin taking college courses as early as tenth grade.
  • Provide opportunities for students to obtain, in addition to a high school diploma, multiple forms of certifications and credentials in their career interests through industry-certified programs, where available.
  • Assess student performance using multiple measures, stressing exhibitions, simulations, and other performances that demonstrate skill and understanding.
  • Partnerships with local business, academic, and service communities are highly visible in curriculum choices (content, context) and in students’ opportunities for internships and research.

San Diego Unified School District Long Standing Partnership with Ocean Discovery Institute

 

Founded in 1999, Ocean Discovery Institute is a San Diego-based 501(c)3 nonprofit organization that has been providing science education for over 14 years to San Diego Unified students. The mission of Ocean Discovery Institute is to use science to engage young people from urban and underserved backgrounds and empower them to transform their lives, their community, and ultimately our world as scientific and environmental leaders.

 

On September 17, 2013, the board approved a Joint Venture Agreement for development and operation of the Living Lab, an educational facility to provide educational programs in marine science and environmental conservation to the schools, students, and community of City Heights. The Living Lab Facility will be a 12,000 square feet cutting edge facility located at the head of an urban canyon in City Heights, and will be used to provide educational opportunities to more than 10,000 youth per year who attend the 14 district schools in the vicinity.

 

The Joint Venture agreement is the overarching agreement which provides for development and operation of the Living Lab and establishes the relationship of the parties. The related agreements include a Ground Lease, by which the district will lease the project site, for a term equal to 125 percent of the anticipated useful life of the structure; and a Joint-Use Agreement, which establishes the terms and conditions for joint-use and operation of the Living Lab Facility.

This past January the partnership submitted the project to the Division of the State Architect for review and approval.

The Outdoor Learning Lab will include an amphitheater for larger classes and student presentations, storm water demonstration projects where students will conduct water quality testing, atmospheric instrumentation that will allow students to gather relevant data, rain water collection systems through which students will learn to engineer solutions for environmental challenges, plots to develop outdoor experiments, outdoor lab space for teacher professional development courses, and access to the adjacent canyon that will provide regular research opportunities for students. This Outdoor Learning Lab will offer daily educational opportunities both during and after school hours.

More than 70,000 hours of standards-based, Common Core-aligned curriculum will be provided each year during school hours. The project is authorized under California Education Code section 8760, which authorizes the district to conduct programs in outdoor science and conservation, and to acquire and maintain property for that purpose; as well as California Education Code section 17060, which authorizes the district to enter into joint venture agreements for the purposes of school facilities construction.  Construction of the Living Lab building will be paid for using Proposition Z bond funds and is authorized under Part One of the bond measure – Quality Neighborhood Schools.

Gallagher to provide the district with the expertise necessary to assist with the development of the technical document and specifications for the Request for Proposal (RFP) project to evaluate the marketplace as it relates to the district dental plans.  The agreement also provided expertise in assisting with the evaluation of proposals submitted to the district evaluation committee.  The Health and Welfare Committee made the recommendation to proceed with an RFP in order to evaluate the marketplace given that the dental benefit has not been competitively bid in some time.  This project evaluated proposals from industry carriers, Joint Purchasing Agreements (JPA), benefit trusts, etc.  This competitive bid process evaluated benefit design, premium costs, funding mechanisms, Affordable Care Act fees, etc.  Gallagher Benefit Services provides similar expertise to other districts across the United States, so is familiar with the characteristics of school district plans and provided the assistance necessary to evaluate proposals that provide the best value to plan participants with available resources.  The Health and Welfare Committee appointed a subcommittee to evaluate and score proposals, interview candidate finalists, and make a final recommendation.

 

The district, in consultation with the Health and Welfare Committee, advertised a Request For Proposals to evaluate the marketplace for high quality and cost effective dental plans in order to provide dental benefits to active and retired employees.

 

On May 8, 2015, the district received Proposals from Aetna Life Insurance Company, Alameda County Schools Insurance Group, California Schools Voluntary Employees Beneficiary Association, Cigna Health and Life Insurance Company, Delta Dental of California, Dental Health Services, Liberty Dental Plan, Metropolitan Life Insurance Company, Principal Life Insurance Company, San Diego and Imperial County Schools Fringe Benefits Consortium, Self-Insured Schools of California, United Concordia Companies, Inc., and Western Dental Services, Inc.

 

Due to the size and stability of the district's dental needs and the potential savings realized from a self-insured PPO plan, the evaluation committee recommends that it is in the best interest of the district to select a self-insured PPO plan vice fully-insured PPO plan.

 

The committee's evaluation of the written proposals determined that three proposers, California Schools Voluntary Employees Beneficiary Association, Delta Dental of California, and United Concordia Companies, Inc. should be further considered and evaluated for the self-funded PPO plan utilizing vendor interviews and that the DHMO plans remain as currently serviced by Delta Dental and Western Dental.  Of note, these DHMO plans are only available as fully-insured.

 

After further evaluation, the committee is recommending that it is in the best interest of the district to award California Schools Voluntary Employees Beneficiary Association as the district's self-insured PPO plan provider with Delta Dental service and fully-insured DHMO plan provider with both Delta Dental and Western Dental service.  With these services there is no change to the current Provider Network.
