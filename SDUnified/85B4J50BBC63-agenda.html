F. OPERATIONAL MATTERS RESERVED FOR THE BOARD
Subject
F.03. Resolution Denouncing Arizona Laws, SB 1070 and HB 2162 and Warning San Diego School Children and Their Families of the Risk of Traveling to Arizona; BUDGETED: N/A (GC-1)
Meeting
May 11, 2010 - Regular Meeting
Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution denouncing Arizona laws SB 1070 and HB 2162 and warning San Diego school children and their families of the risk of traveling to Arizona.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

BACKGROUND: On April 23, 2010, the Governor of Arizona signed into law SB 1070, permitting state and local law enforcement officials to engage in racial profiling, thereby turning the clock back on a generation of civil rights gains.

On April 30, 2010, the Governor of Arizona signed into law HB 2162 which modifies SB 1070 to prohibit racial profiling but still criminalizes unlawful presence; still requires state and local law enforcement to engage in immigration enforcement; still requires police to question people they “reasonably suspect” of being in the country unlawfully in the course of any “lawful stop, detention or arrest”; and still grants police the authority to arrest individuals without a warrant for federal civil immigration violation.

The law undermines fundamental civil rights and civil liberties, and poses a special threat to people of color who live in and travel through Arizona.

Public officials in Arizona are asserting that undocumented immigrants can be identified by the clothes they wear and the way they speak, and are using stereotypes as proxies for race which will inevitably lead to racial profiling.

The State of California and the City of San Diego prohibit the unequal treatment of its residents and, furthermore, prohibit racial profiling of any kind.

An estimated 75 percent of San Diego Unified school children are people of color and 44 percent are of Hispanic or Latino origin who potentially could be targeted and harassed by law enforcement officials in Arizona as “reasonably suspect” if they fall into a stereotype held by law enforcement officers.

The Major Cities Chiefs of the United States stated in 2006 http://www.houstontx.gov/police/pdfs/mcc_position.pdf that when police engage in immigration enforcement, community members are less apt to call them when they witness or suffer a crime, thereby undermining the ability of police to protect the community and threatening public safety.

To the extent the Arizona laws threaten public safety in Arizona , they also threaten public safety in neighboring California and could potentially undermine trust between police and the communities they serve in San Diego and in California;

Civil rights leaders, constitutional rights scholars, elected officials, and police chiefs across the country are repudiating the new law.

On May 3, 2010, the City of San Diego passed a resolution condemning the Arizona laws.

We need humane and workable solutions, not an irrational and irresponsible response to our broken immigration system, and we need solutions that help our state and our country move forward together rather than divide us apart.

THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, by the San Diego Unified School Board, for and on behalf of the school children and the families served by the school district, that the school board condemns Arizona State Laws SB 1070 and HB 2162, and urges the State of Arizona to repeal both.

BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that the San Diego Unified School Board cautions the school districts’ students and families from traveling to or spending time in Arizona due to the risk they may face in being subjected to inappropriate and unlawful scrutiny.

[Originator/Contact: Richard Barrera, Board Trustee, 619.725.5550, rbarrera1@sandi.net]

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the revised and consolidated 2010-11 Civic Center Rate Schedule and Balboa Stadium Rate Schedule. Rate schedules will be effective July 1, 2010 through June 30, 2011.

FISCAL IMPACT: In accordance with Board Policy K-4000, the Civic Center and Balboa Stadium rental rate schedules are adjusted and approved by the Board of Education annually to reflect an increase equal to the prior year’s cost of living adjustment (COLA). The rental rates were increased by 4.25 percent and rounded to the nearest dollar.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFF: None.

BACKGROUND: Under the Civic Center Act (Education Code section 38120 et. seq.) and Administrative Procedure 9205, the governing board of any school district may grant the use of facilities or grounds upon terms it deems proper. Such “civic center use” is subordinate to, and does not interfere with, the instructional program or other public school purposes.

Civic center use may be on a free use, direct cost recovery, fair rental value or commercial basis. Physical Plant Operations (PPO) - Asset Management is responsible to facilitate Civic Center use of all district facilities rentals and board approved use agreements. This responsibility includes assuring that schools serve the broader needs of the communities in which they exist, in addition to the instructional needs of the students.

In 2009, the board chose to freeze the Civic Center Rate Schedule at the 2008 rates. This year, district staff is recommending increasing the rates by 4.25 percent, the COLA, to recover the increases in nondistrict costs (e.g., utilities, custodial cleaning supplies). Staff has additionally merged the commercial and noncommercial rates for clarity.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

BACKGROUND: On December 21, 1943, the Board of Education granted permission to the City for the erection of a City recreation center on a portion of the land at Memorial Junior High School. The board allowed this with the understanding that the district would be permitted to use the recreation center during school hours. No official Agreement between the district and City has been located at this time; however, the district and City continue this partnership today.

The board revisited this item on January 15, 1946, due to a variance in the location of the building. A more formalized legal description of the building’s location was given as a part of the action; however, no official Agreement has been located.

On March 21, 1969, a letter addressed to the City of San Diego from Harold Culver, Director of Building Planning and Construction acknowledged the City’s request to use a portion of the Memorial Junior High School site for construction of a swimming pool to be utilized by both the City and the district.

On February 27, 1996, the City and district entered into a Lease Agreement for the district schools to use 11 City swimming facilities pursuant to a rate schedule established in the Agreement. It expired on December 31, 2000. On September 11, 2001, City and district entered into a second Lease Agreement for the use of the City’s 11 swimming facilities. The Agreement expired on June 30, 2006. May 9, 2006, the City and district entered into a third Lease Agreement for the district to use the City’s eleven 11 swimming facilities which will expire on June 30, 2011.

Since 1943, the City of San Diego has constructed three recreational facilities (Recreational Facilities):1) the Dolores Magdeleno Memorial Recreation Center, 2) an outdoor basketball court, and 3) the Dolores Magdeleno Memorial Pool, located at 2902 Marcy Avenue, San Diego, California 92113. These three recreational facilities encroach on approximately 13,300 square feet of district property located at 2850 Logan Avenue, San Diego, California 92113 (Memorial Preparatory for Scholars and Athletes).

Although the City and the district agree that written approvals for the development, maintenance and operation of each of the recreational facilities were given by the district, no definitive corroborating archival documents, signed by both the City and the district, can be located to establish the City’s right to occupy the district property.

To rectify this situation, the City and district have negotiated a 50-year Ground Lease to formalize the City’s right to use the district property. The proposed Ground Lease ratifies the City’s past use of the district property and allows the City continued use of the existing recreational facilities. The City may extend the term of the Ground Lease for varying periods up to a total of an additional 44 years upon written notice to district.
