RECOMMENDATION: Approve proclamation in honor of Dorothy L. W. Smith and call on all San Diegans to honor the legacy of Dorothy L. W. Smith and recognize her extraordinary efforts to lead the way towards equal education for all students in San Diego County and beyond.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: Born in Capleville, Tennessee in 1939, Dorothy L. W. Smith attended a segregated school for Black students in a small town called Martin on the Tennessee side of the Tennessee-Kentucky border. Smith was the class valedictorian in high school and attended the all-Black Philander Smith College in Little Rock, Arkansas. She married her college sweetheart, Carl, then moved to Cleveland where she graduated from Case Western Reserve University with a bachelor’s degree in English and began her education career as a high school teacher. After the couple moved to California in 1969. Smith taught at Long Beach City College, then Grossmont Community College and San Diego City College. Smith worked for 24 years as a professor at San Diego City College, where she taught English Composition and African American literature. One of the courses she designed was “Black Women in Literature and the Media,” which has now been taught in the San Diego Community College District for over 45 years. As a teacher, Smith made students believe that they could achieve, and she made them feel special even when they had been overlooked by teachers before. Elected to the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education in 1981, Dorothy Smith served for almost eight years on the Board and was elected twice as president. Smith was the first African American woman to be elected to public office in San Diego. As a board member Smith pushed to revamp high school courses at San Diego Unified, to raise the quality of instruction and curriculum, including the removal of classes that didn’t meet college entrance requirements and remedial classes that didn’t count toward high school graduation. Smith introduced school district policies to promote equity and excellence, to eliminate harmful tracking of students, and to provide a common core curriculum to better prepare all students for college and careers. In 2001, Dorothy was appointed by the mayor of San Diego as one of five inaugural members of the San Diego Ethics Commission and served two years as its president from 2003–2005. Smith was inducted into the San Diego Women’s Hall of Fame in 2019.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of imminent risk of harm to attendees of public meetings during the COVID-19 pandemic which will be effective for 30 days, commencing March 9, 2022.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: Assembly Bill No. 361 (“AB 361”) was enacted by the California Legislature and is intended to “improve and enhance public access to . . . local agency meetings during the COVID-19 pandemic and future applicable emergencies, by allowing broader access through teleconferencing options.” AB 361 permits local legislative bodies subject to the Brown Act and/or the Badgley Keene Act to hold virtual meetings, without needing to publish the physical locations of Board members or make those locations open to the public, if there is a proclaimed state of emergency (pursuant to Section 8625 of the California Emergency Services Act) and either: (i) state or local officials have imposed or recommended measures to promote social distancing, or (ii) there is a finding by the local legislative body that, as a result of the emergency, meeting in person “would present imminent risks to the health or safety of attendees.” Governor has proclaimed a state of emergency pursuant to Section 8625 of the California Emergency Services Act. Since the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, in compliance with public health orders and for the protection all involved persons, the Board of Education (“Board”) has held its meetings telephonically via Zoom and provided for public comments to be provided via teleconferencing rather than allowing in-person attendance by the public at the Education Center. The COVID-19 pandemic continues to present an imminent health risk to the public which require certain specific health and safety measures be maintained. The District’s central office facilities present specific challenges in maintaining COVID-19-related safety protocols required for the health and safety of attendees, which include, but are not limited to, adequate ventilation and security, enforcement of masking requirements, and maintenance of recommended social distancing. The health and safety risks are presented by providing public access at the remote locations from which Board members currently attend Board meetings remotely. The Board hereby finds that conducting Board and/or committee meetings in person in light of the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic presents an imminent risk to the health and safety of prospective attendees.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in support of participation in Earth Day 2022 events.

 

The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education supports and is proud of students’ leadership in demanding aggressive climate action and encourages students to plan and participate in Earth Day 2022 events. San Diego Unified School District schools will support students in planning these events and work with them to adapt as schools’ COVID guidelines evolve. The San Diego Unified School District will inform staff and administrators of this resolution and the expectation that they will support their students in organizing and leading Earth Day events so that all students may take advantage of this opportunity.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Ninety-seven (97) percent of climate scientists and organizations including the US National Aeronautics and Space Administration, American Association for the Advancement of Science, National Academy of Sciences, and the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change affirm that anthropogenic climate change is causing dire damage to the planet. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change released in 2021 their sixth and most dire assessment report, with UN Secretary-General António Guterres characterizing the report as “nothing less than a code red for humanity,” and stressed that limiting warming to 1.5°C will be impossible unless immediate, large-scale actions are taken to prevent catastrophic impacts on life on Earth. The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) has recorded a fivefold increase in the number of weather, climate, and water-related disasters over the past 50 years, which will become even more frequent and extreme as a consequence of worsening climate change. The world is far off schedule to meet the goals of the Paris Agreement, with CO2 emissions back to pre-pandemic levels and continuing to rise, and the global mean surface temperature from the past four years being at an all-time high, all according to the WMO. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration released a report on Climate Literacy, stressing that society needs comprehension of climate science and how to relate that to the current climate crisis, implementing knowledge to take action in their communities. Youth4Climate’s Eco Club Coalition unifies eco and social justice clubs in San Diego Unified to strengthen their efforts and work together to take strategic action for climate justice. The Eco Club Coalition is campaigning for Earth Day Events in schools with the goal of increasing awareness about climate change and justice issues. In 2013, the San Diego Unified Board of Education acknowledged the threat climate change poses to youth health and adopted a Resolution in Support of a Parent Teacher Association initiative to tackle climate change and established the Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee. In 2015, the San Diego School Unified District created a plan to transition to 100 percent renewable energy by 2035 in order to mitigate the effects of climate change. In 2020, San Diego Unified was recognized for its excellence in resource efficiency, health and wellness, and environmental and sustainability education by the California Department of Education. The San Diego Unified School District’s “Dream Big Solutions for a Sustainable Future” includes a Climate Action Plan that commits to 100 percent renewable energy by 2035. San Diego Unified is taking steps toward meeting its goals by retrofitting schools with more efficient air conditioning and lighting systems; and more than 29,000 lighting fixtures have been converted to LED, offsetting 1,300 metric tons of CO2 gas emissions; and projected district solar energy production by the end of 2020 will offset 8,100 tons of CO2. The San Diego School Unified School District currently has a climate action plan that reviews the ways the district can make a positive impact on climate change and how they can become an environmental leader in San Diego County. San Diego Unified students have been active in the fight for climate justice through participation in “Green Teams,” recycling clubs, “Planet Protectors,” compost installment teams, and other student groups. The San Diego Unified School District has previously supported student climate action by adopting a Resolution calling on Governor Newsom to immediately phase out offshore oil drilling. The San Diego Unified School District has previously supported youth activists by adopting Resolutions to allow students to participate in the Global Climate Walkout in 2019, online Global Climate Strike activities in 2020, and the Global Day of Climate Action in 2021, in which 3,500 San Diego County students from more than 20 different high schools participated. The San Diego Unified School District has previously supported youth action for Earth Day by resolving to support a 50th-anniversary Earth Day event on April 22, 2020 for the continued awareness of and involvement in climate justice. The San Diego Unified School District has previously committed to recognize climate change and climate justice issues, along with working to create an urgent, just transition away from fossil fuels.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Implementation of Student Vaccination Requirement Beginning with the 2022-23 School/Fiscal Year, specifically:

 

  1. All students ages five (5) and up are subject to the requirement to be vaccinated against COVID-19.
  2. Consistent with the interest of promoting and protecting the health and safety of students, individual students will not be subject to the requirement to be vaccinated against COVID-19 until the student is in an age group that has received full FDA approval for vaccination against COVID-19, and students who cannot be safely vaccinated may seek and obtain a medical exemption. The medical exemption process and criteria will be the same as they were following the implementation of the Vaccination Roadmap on September 28, 2021.
  3. For attendance in summer school/enrichment programs in the summer of 2022, students who are 16 years of age or older on or before June 21, 2022, must receive their first vaccination shot on or before June 28, 2022, and their second vaccination shot on or before the timeframe for a second shot recommended by the vaccine manufacturer and the FDA.
  4. For participation in fall, 2022 extracurricular activities, students who are 16 or older on or before July 30, 2022, must receive their first vaccination shot on or before August 6, 2022, and their second vaccination shot on or before the timeframe for a second shot recommended by the vaccine manufacturer and the FDA.
  5. For participation in in-person instruction for the fall semester, 2022, students who are 16 or older on or before August 29, 2022, must receive their first vaccination shot on or before September 6, 2022, and their second vaccination shot on or before the timeframe for a second shot recommended by the vaccine manufacturer and the FDA.
  6. For students enrolling in the District for the first time after August 29, 2022, and whose age group has been fully-approved by the FDA at the time of enrollment, the student must receive their first vaccination shot within seven (7) days of enrolling, and their second vaccination shot on or before the timeframe for a second shot recommended by the vaccine manufacturer and the FDA. Students enrolling for the first time who are foster youth, homeless, migrant, or from a military family who state they have been vaccinated but lack immediate access to records due to their circumstances will be afforded the same the timeframe for a first and second shot provided to other students to provide proof of vaccination.
  7. For students already enrolled, who become eligible for vaccination and subject to the requirement after August 29, 2022, based on full FDA approval for their age group, and to promote academic progress through educationally-sound and logical implementation dates of the vaccination requirement, the students must receive their first vaccination shot within seven (7) days of the beginning of the semester following becoming eligible, and must receive their second shot within the timeframe for a second shot recommended by the vaccine manufacturer and the FDA.
  8. For students with disabilities and subject to an individual education plan (“IEP”), who are protected by state and federal law regarding educational placement/free appropriate public education, and whose age group has been fully-approved by the FDA, the District shall comply with federal law when imposing the vaccination requirement on these students.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On February 22, 2022, the board took the following action to approve proposed revisions to the district’s Student Vaccine Mandate Plan:

 

  • ●  Implementation of the vaccine mandate for students participating in Summer 2022 programs, for students age 16 on or before June 21, 2022.

  • ●  Implementation of the vaccine mandate, following the earlier timeline, as a condition of participation in Fall athletics and extracurricular activities, for students age 16 and older, for the 2022-23 school year.

  • ●  Implementation of the vaccine mandate, as a condition of attending in-person learning and participation in extracurricular activities. for students age 16 and older, for the 2022-23 school year.

  • ●  Changes to asymptomatic testing requirements for high school students who are under the vaccine mandate age.

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. (CEQA Guidelines §15301]). The Proposed Project involves repairs to existing facilities and would not change enrollment, capacity, or existing operations. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines Section 15300.2): Section 15300.2 of the State CEQA Guidelines specifies several exceptions to the use of a categorical exemption.

  • Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the Project is to be located – a Project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant, therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the Project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies. The Proposed Project does not fall within this class of exemption. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  • Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant. The Proposed Project consists repairs to existing facilities and no cumulative impacts would occur. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  • Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. The Proposed Project consists of repairs to existing facilities where no changes in use would occur. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  • Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. There are no exterior physical improvements associated with the Proposed Project that would result in new impacts to scenic highways. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  • Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. There are no physical improvements associated with the Proposed Project that would result in new hazardous materials impacts. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  • Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource. There are no physical improvements associated with the Proposed Project that would result in impacts to existing historical resources. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
