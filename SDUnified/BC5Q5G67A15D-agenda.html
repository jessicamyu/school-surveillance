RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and approve a Resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula funds and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials. The board is required to adopt a resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable.

 

Current Williams legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12. The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core, lab equipment for science laboratory courses. All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual resolution of sufficiency. Administrative Circulars 5, 6, and 7, dated August 13, 2019, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.” In September 2019, instructional materials inventory data and current PowerSchool enrollment for August 30, 2019, was compiled and entered into the “Instructional Materials Sufficiency Form” and provided to each elementary school by the Instructional Materials Office. Sites were directed to verify their instructional materials inventory and take action to remedy any insufficiencies by ordering from the Instructional Materials Office. Principals confirmed instructional materials sufficiency and returned the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 13, 2019. Secondary school principals, after completing their inventories and taking action to remedy any insufficiencies, also returned the “Principal Verification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency."

 

Twenty-five elementary schools, five K-8 schools, seven middle schools, and six senior high schools were visited by a team from San Diego County Office of Education. All 43 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials. Additionally, all six senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

BACKGROUND:

 

There have been more than 800 confirmed illnesses tied to vaping and 13 deaths across the country and more than 22 confirmed probable vaping-related illness cases in San Diego County.

 

We support an expanded effort by our partners at the American Heart Association and SAY San Diego to educate and raise awareness of this growing health crisis of our youth, and their efforts to end all tobacco and nicotine addiction in the United States.

 

San Diego Unified Physical Education teachers have partnered with the US Department of Justice to provide students with accurate information on the risks of vaping, including recent presentations at multiple high schools.

 

After decades of decline in cigarette smoking rates we have seen a recent dramatic rise in the use of e-cigarettes by children and teens leading to a new generation of tobacco products addicting a new generation of users to tobacco and nicotine.

 

Ninety percent of smokers first try a tobacco product by age 18.

 

There are now over 5 million students using e-cigarettes and vaping products and the tobacco industry, including the e-cigarette companies, spend billions annually marketing their deadly and addictive products.

 

E-cigarette companies advertise and sell products that include flavors that are appealing to youth and can contain levels of nicotine comparable to or even higher than traditional cigarettes.

 

The short-term and long-term health risks of vaping e-cigarettes are mostly unknown and recently have been linked to multiple deaths and hundreds of cases of serious respiratory illnesses requiring hospitalization.

 

San Diego Unified is using its robust network of School Wellness Committees to coordinate vaping presentations for students and training for staff and will be including that information in their Wellness Action Plans.

 

Teenagers and young adults make up about half of those hospitalized as a result of e-cigarette use, according to the State Department of Public Health, and our vision is for all of our students to be tobacco and nicotine free.

San Diego County has 19 different Native American Indian reservations, more than any other county in the United States.

The San Diego Unified School District, through the Family Engagement Department, as well as through all offices and schools sites, promotes equity for all students and will continue to provide tutoring, career guidance, mental health support, cultural awareness, leadership development, and cultural enrichment programs for Native American students.

 

The commemorative month provides a platform for Native American Indians to share their culture, traditions, music, crafts, dance, and ways and concepts of life.

 

The Indian Education Program and the Native American Indian community have made and continue to provide significant and important contributions to the community and the nation as a whole.

RECOMMENDATIONS: Determine that the Crawford High School Career and Technical Education (CTE) Project is exempt from further environmental review pursuant to California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) Guidelines section 15314 - Minor Additions to Schools; Approve Crawford High School CTE Project; and direct staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption (NOE Attachment A Crawford CTE 15Oct19) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk and California Office of Planning and Research State Clearinghouse.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $50.00. Budget String: 9334-90403-00-6215-8500-0000-21400-8632

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: The Crawford High School CTE Project (Proposed Project) involves the modernization of the existing CTE transportation classroom (room 608), interior remodel of the existing CTE patient care classroom (room 506) and CTE computer science and game design classroom (room 607), and construction of a new, four-bay service and repair facility at the Crawford High School Campus. The proposed modernization of the existing CTE transportation classroom facility and the construction of a new, stand-alone, four-bay service and repair facility building that would address current space and safety concerns, as well as allow the growth of the CTE program.

 

The modernization of the existing 3,411 square foot (SF) wood framed CTE transportation classroom facility would consist of reconfiguring the existing areas to include a 1,233 SF classroom for 36 students with data, communications, audio visual, and HVAC systems meeting the current educational specifications of the district. Adjacent to the classroom and visible through glazed openings would be a 1,714 SF, two-bay “demonstration shop” including an area dedicated for an Engine Lab. The construction of the new, 2,956 SF, stand-alone service and repair facility would consist of four service bays, with one serving as a dedicated alignment bay. Additionally, the area surrounding the new, service and repair facility would consist of new concrete paving, an ornamental steel fence, and gates along the western perimeter, and new chain link fence with two rolling gates along the southern perimeter.

 

Finally, the interior remodel of the existing CTE patient care classroom (room 506) and CTE computer science and game design classroom (room 607) would consist of minor interior renovations, such as new paint, patching of walls, interior wall demolition, and installation of new furniture and equipment to support the respective programs of each classroom.

 

The Proposed Project includes the addition of one new classroom facility to serve the existing student population (the four-bay service and repair facility building) and would not directly result in an increase in student enrollment or capacity. Construction would take place over approximately one year ending in August 2020.

 

CEQA Analysis: Approval and implementation of the Proposed Project is subject to CEQA, however, they are exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines section 15314 – Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14). A Class 14 exemption consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or 10 classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314).

 

As described above, the Proposed Project would result in the increase in one new classroom building and would not directly increase student enrollment or capacity. Therefore, the application of a Class 14 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment, may in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
     
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
     
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
     
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
     
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
     
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. Construction and operation of the Proposed Project would be contained to the existing Crawford High School campus. Land use types adjacent to the Proposed Project site include residential uses, schools, and a healthcare center. A small open space area is located directly south of the campus situated between the existing Crawford High School softball field and a parking lot. This area is entirely surrounded by development, has no connectivity to open space, and is of limited size, which negates its ability to support sensitive species. Additionally, existing site use will not change because of the implementation of the Proposed Project. The Proposed Project will not impact environmentally sensitive areas or an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. This exception does not apply to the Proposed Project.
     
  2. Cumulative Impact. Crawford High School is in the midst of a multi-year whole site modernization (WSM) project. The district prepared a Draft EIR for the Crawford High School and Mann Middle School Athletic Facility Upgrade and Modernization (SCH No. 2013031019) project in 2015. All impacts identified in the 2015 Final EIR were determined to be less than significant except those related to noise and lighting associated with the upgraded stadium. The Proposed Project would not affect construction or operation associated with those stadium improvements. Combined with the construction of the WSM project, the Proposed Project would not result in any significant cumulative impacts. This exception does not apply to the Proposed Project.
     
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project is not considered as an unusual circumstance as these activities fulfill the demands of the existing school and include standard maintenance of building facilities and the provision of adequate classroom space. Such activities are not considered as an unusual circumstance as these activities do not include specifications that would be considered out of the ordinary. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
     
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project site is approximately one and a half miles north of State Route 94, the closest highway to the project site, and is not listed as an officially designated or eligible scenic highway. The Proposed Project will not involve activities resulting in directly, or indirectly impacting an officially designated highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway as the activities associated with the Proposed Project will occur within the existing school campus. Scenic highways deemed eligible or officially designated are not located in the immediate vicinity of the Crawford High School. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
     
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. The Proposed Project will occur within an existing, operational school. A review of federal and state standard and supplemental databases indicated that the Proposed Project site is not located within any identified hazardous material site pursuant to Government Code section 65962.5. In addition, the alteration and improvement activities will remain within the property and will not involve heavy trenching or excavation. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
     
  6. Historical Resources. Based on a review of available historic research, Crawford High School is not considered eligible for the California Register of Historical Resources and is not of historic significance. Therefore, implementation of the Proposed Project would not result in a substantial adverse change to a historical resource and impacts would be less than significant. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

Approval of this item approves the Project as described, and authorizes and directs staff to file the attached Notice of Exemption NOE Attachment A Crawford CTE 15Oct19) with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk.
