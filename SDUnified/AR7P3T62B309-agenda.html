BACKGROUND: On August 3, 1990, George H. W. Bush, President of the United States, declared the month of November as Native American Indian Heritage Month.

 

The President authorized and called upon federal, state, and local governments, groups and organizations, and the people of the United States, to observe November with appropriate programs, ceremonies, and activities.

 

The contributions of Native American Indians to this nation’s government, history, and culture are as numerous and varied as the tribes themselves.

 

San Diego County has 19 different Native American Indian reservations, more than any other county in the United States.

 

Approximately 25,000 Native Americans live in San Diego County, most coming from four local tribes—Kumeyaay/Diegueño, Luiseño, Cupeño, and Cahuilla.

 

The San Diego Unified School District, through the Family and Community Engagement (FACE) Department, as well as through all offices and schools sites, promotes equity for all students and will continue to provide tutoring, career guidance, mental health support, cultural awareness, leadership development, and cultural enrichment programs for Native American students.

 

The commemorative month aims to provide a platform for Native American Indians to share their culture, traditions, music, crafts, dance, and ways and concepts of life.

 

The Indian Education Program and the Native American Indian community have made and continue to provide significant and important contributions to the community and the nation as a whole.

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent recommends the reconfiguration of John Muir K-12 dedicated magnet school beginning in the 2018-19 school year to better serve the needs of students district-wide by phasing in a school-wide K-8 Spanish and French language immersion program that incorporates the Paideia philosophy, while phasing out the existing Grades 9-12 English-only Paideia program. This recommendation provides the district with an opportunity to serve additional students by providing families another option for language immersion and thereby increasing district enrollment.

 

Families on the existing Choice waiting list for language immersion programs will be offered the first opportunity to enroll in Grades K and 1 in the 2018-19 school year. This recommendation will also provide an opportunity for language strand pathway students at Field Elementary exiting Grade 5 to Choice to the Muir dedicated magnet to continue their Spanish language pathway in Grades 6-8.

 

Upon Board approval of the recommended reconfiguration, next steps include a phased-in approach:

 

2017-18:

  • Phase out Grades 9-12. Provide individualized assistance to the existing 31 high school students to select the high school in which they wish to enroll (14 students in Grade 9, nine students in Grade 10, and eight students in Grade 11). Parents of students in Grades 9-11 have already been notified of the potential change, which was discussed at community and parent meetings held in 2016-17, as noted in the attached Planning and Engagement timeline.
  • Develop a marketing plan to increase public awareness of and enrollment opportunities in the district's specialized language immersion programs.
  • Assemble a central office support team to ensure appropriate support services are in place to facilitate a smooth transition.
  • Develop a plan for exiting Field Elementary students to transition to Innovation Middle for language pathway continuation beginning in 2019-20, as Muir will not be ready to accept Grade 6 Spanish immersion students until 2023-24.

 

2018-19:

  • Open three Kindergarten classes (one French and two Spanish), and two Grade 1 classes (one French and one Spanish).
  • Begin the school re-naming process (e.g., "John Muir Language Academy").
  • Continue to serve existing Muir students in Grades K-8 in the current English-only Paideia Program, and continue to offer Spanish enrichment to existing Muir students.
  • Convene Design Task Force for Whole Site Modernization.

 

2019-20:

  • Add two Grade 2 classes (one French and one Spanish).

 

2020-21 through 2025-26:

  • Add two Grade 3 classes (one French and one Spanish) and additional grades each year thereafter up to Grade 8.

 

2026-27:

  • Growth to Grade 8 complete.
  • The first class of 8th graders in the language immersion program will exit to high school. Students and families will be advised as to options to continue world language instruction at district high schools (e.g., San Diego High School, Mission Bay High School and Madison High School have strong language programs).

CONSULTATION WITH STAKEHOLDERS: Discussion of potential reconfiguration of Muir began in November 2016, as shown on the attached Planning and Engagement timeline. Throughout the Winter and Spring of 2016-17, conversations with parents, community, educators, and others about the district's desire to increase the number of language immersion options resulted in the suggestion that Muir become a language immersion dedicated magnet school. District staff brought several scenarios to the Muir community for review, including moving the current program to another location to create physical space for a new language immersion program. Based on feedback from the Muir community, the district revised the scenarios to include Muir remaining at its current location, eliminating Grades 9-12 due to severe under-enrollment, and incorporating the existing Paideia philosophy into a new language immersion program.

 

The Muir Site Governance Team (SGT) met on September 12, 2017, to discuss the proposal to reconfigure grade levels (from K-12 to K-8) and program design (English-only Paideia program to French and Spanish language immersion Paideia program). The results of the vote were that staff are in support of the reconfiguration of grade level (K-12 to K-8) and program (language immersion).

 

DISTRICT DESIGN TEAM: Integrated district-wide central office support will provide for a seamless reconfiguration process. The integrated approach developed and implemented in the Muir reconfiguration process serves as a best practices model for future school, program and facility redesign projects. The roles and responsibilities of each department are outlined below.

  • Area Superintendent - Provide guidance and support to site principal throughout the reconfiguration, and facilitate collaboration among principals of language immersion programs to ensure the highest quality services are offered district-wide.
  • Facilities Planning and Construction - Ensure whole site modernization project is designed to support the site's instructional program in alignment with Vision 2020 and LCAP goals and is completed on time and on budget.
  • Family and Community Engagement - Provide support to site principal in communicating with parents and community throughout the transition process.
  • Human Resource Services - Recruit and hire qualified staff for the expansion of the language immersion programs (specific credentials required).
  • Labor Relations -  Ensure provisions of collective bargaining agreements are honored in staffing the new program.
  • Neighborhood Schools and Enrollment Options - Assist families in enrolling in the new language immersion program and update Enrollment Guide to include information on the new program.
  • Office of Language Acquisition - Provide guidance and support to site principal in developing and implementing the language immersion program.
  • Physical Plant Operations - Plan and prepare for emerging needs in Food and Nutrition, Custodial, Landscape, and Transportation Services.
  • Public Information Office - Develop language immersion schools marketing and communications plan.

 

BACKGROUND: The district's 2015 Concepts to Action included expansion of successful programs. Vision 2020 and LCAP Goal 5.5. call for aligning and strengthening district pathways, including model language immersion programs. Each year, through the Choice Enrollment process, hundreds of students seek access to the Language Academy and Longfellow's dual language immersion programs, but are not served due to facility limitations. For the 2017-18 school year, 1,158 Choice applications were received for language immersion programs (Language Academy and Longfellow), and only 287 students were seated, resulting in a waiting list of 871 students for language immersion programs.

 

As outlined in the June 6, 2017 LCAP Board workshop, the district offers two settings for world language instruction; school-wide language immersion magnet programs and language pathways within resident schools/clusters.

  • Successful existing school-wide K-8 language immersion programs are offered at Language Academy and Longfellow magnet schools. "John Muir Language Academy" would offer a third dedicated magnet option to students district-wide.
  • Cluster dual language strand pathway opportunities are available in ten clusters (Crawford, Henry, Hoover, Kearny, Lincoln, Madison, Mission Bay, Morse, San Diego and Serra). The strand pathway offers students the opportunity to participate in language programs from elementary to middle to high school, within the student's school/cluster of residence.

 

Keeping the Paideia Philosophy

John Muir School offers a unique teaching strategy within San Diego Unified, a holistic approach to education in which students are representative members of a democracy and are citizens of the world, which are in alignment with developing fluency in world languages. Staff recommend keeping the existing Paideia philosophy throughout the school's conversion to a language immersion program and beyond. Paideia is active lifelong learning - engaging students through thought-provoking Socratic seminars, growth of intellectual skills, and mastery of information. Benefits of Paideia for students and educators include:

  • Increased motivation to learn, because of the excitement of generating, articulating, and developing their own thoughts.
  • Improved self-confidence, because teachers and classmates listen to them with interest and respect.
  • Increased participation of children who tend to be more quiet, because they know that their teachers and classmates will pay attention.
  • Improved communication skills by speaking, listening and responding to each other.
  • Improved reading, writing, speaking and listening, as ideas are developed through conversation.
  • Increased engagement, because students have the opportunity to be more active and expressive.
  • Improved academic achievement, because students are more interested in learning.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of support for adoption and implementation of Community Choice Aggregation in the City of San Diego.

 

BACKGROUND: At the July 14, 2015 meeting, the Board of Education of the San Diego Unified School District supported the development of a San Diego Unified Climate Action Plan (CAP) in alignment with the overall goals of the City of San Diego CAP to achieve 100 percent renewable energy by 2035, and to provide local control and build-out of citywide clean energy programs.

 

The results of the Feasibility Study of Community Choice Aggregation (CCA) determined that CCA is feasible for San Diego and will allow local and publicly accountable control of energy decisions.

 

Electricity rates have increased 61 percent since 2009 and continue to rise, adding significant costs to San Diego Unified's budget which inhibits our ability to spend funds on other critical projects.

 

CCA is structured as a nonprofit entity whose primary responsibility is to serve the public interest and, according to the technical study, will be able to stabilize rates and offer competitive or reduced costs.

 

The technical study validates that CCA will enable the City of San Diego to reach its goal of 100 percent renewable energy.

 

CCA can source an increasing share of power from local, clean sources, supporting local jobs and local economic development reducing greenhouse gas emissions, and providing cleaner air.

 

San Diego Unified continues to work on solar energy installations, and recognizes that CCA will generate revenue that can be reinvested into the community for rooftop solar energy and other innovative energy programs.

 

CCA can promote social equity by collaborating with community organizations to prioritize reinvestment of revenue in local clean energy resources and programs that serve disadvantaged communities.

 

The San Diego Unified Climate Mitigation Adaptation Plan includes adopting CCA as a strategy to access 100 percent of our electric power requirements from renewable sources by 2035.

 

San Diego Unified could consider advocating locally for reasonable rates by requesting a seat on the CCA governing board to represent the interests of San Diego Unified.

Prior Background Provided for the First Reading:

 

With careful attention to student outcomes and equity, the District has set forth new fiscal and operational models to ensure the organization is financially healthy and situated to serve current and future generations of students while remaining responsible fiscal stewards of taxpayer funds. The Board of Education and District staff are continuously evaluating current practices and forecasting based on all data available while paying special attention to variables expected to create fiscal impact. These forecasts and projections indicate significant opportunities for student achievement coupled with laws and policies out of the District’s control that will impact the District’s future operations. The Board of Education has accounted for these variables, risks, and opportunities and is taking strong action.

Therefore, the Board of Education is establishing the following guiding principles to achieve Vision 2020 through the implementation of LCAP goals, create meaningful learning experiences for all students and maintain long-term fiscal stability. The guiding principles serve as the essential ideas and ideological anchors for all financial processes and decisions system-wide. The principles are listed below along with practical examples of their application to specific processes intended to improve student outcomes and preserve current and future fiscal resources.

 

Our Principles:

We will:

 

1. Ensure each financial decision is rooted in district supports, structures, and capacity development that accomplish Vision 2020, Local Control Accountability Plan, and future plans to improve outcomes for students.

 

2. Develop, maintain and communicate long-term strategies that ensure strong fiscal health for the District.

 

3. Use a disciplined approach to make financial decisions and hold ourselves accountable for each transaction as it relates to student outcomes and sound economic strategies aligned with Vision 2020 (i.e. decisions made with consideration to projected revenue and long-term obligations).

 

4. Regularly and openly have conversations with the public about fiscal projections, activities, accomplishments and engage key groups and stakeholders in an input process. (i.e.board meetings, LCAP input process, yearly school site budgeting process, yearly review of the organization, etc.).

 

5. (New) Utilize creative and entrepreneurial approaches to seek funds through legislative advocacy at the local, state, and federal levels along with grants, partnerships and/or other funding mechanisms to support each and every student.

 

We are committed to implement processes and controls that ensure long-term fiscal stability and focus on achievement for all learners (example applications of the principles below)

  • Controls and Audits
    • Vigilant adherence to internal controls such as a fraud hotline and account auditing
    • Consistent use of professional experts and external auditors to ensure compliance and maintenance of proper controls
  • Spending Freeze:
    • Evaluation of discretionary expenses at all levels to confirm support for student success and the responsible stewardship of taxpayer dollars
    • Reduction or suspension of all non-essential travel and purchases
  • Position Management / Position Review Committee
    • Prioritize positions that fill critical needs for both school sites and the required business of the district
    • Ensure all positions are properly funded
    • Consistent evaluation of all positions to ensure they are optimized and providing meaningful support to the District’s mission

These processes serve as initial examples, and the Board of Education’s adoption will result in continual addition, refinement and application of processes to ensure the District can continue to unlock the genius for current and future generations of students.

BACKGROUND: Transworld Services Inc. provides collection services for aged receivable accounts for the Pupil Transportation Department on uncollectible student fee accounts.

On November 19, 2013, the board approved the ratification of Agreement with Transworld Systems Inc. to continue providing collection services for the Pupil Transportation Department from November 19, 2013 through November 18, 2015.

This new Agreement will continue providing two-years of continued collection services for up to 700 accounts for the Pupil Transportation Department on uncollectible student fee accounts.

 

California Government Code section 53060 grants the district the authority to contract with and employ persons for the furnishing to the district of special services if such persons are specially-trained, experienced, and competent to perform the special services requested.
