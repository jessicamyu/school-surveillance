RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of recognizing May 22, 2021, as Harvey Milk Day. The Board of Education encourages students and staff to virtually recognize Harvey Milk’s contributions by flying the rainbow flag and reading and discussing Milk’s speech “The Hope,” delivered in 1978 at a public celebration of the California Gay Freedom Day, that encouraged the LGBTQ+ community and all other discriminated minorities to fight for their rights as a people. Resources for teaching and learning about Harvey Milk and the LGBTQIA+ community’s positive contributions are available at http://culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com/harvey-milk.html. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Harvey Milk was born May 22, 1930 in Woodmere, New York. Harvey Milk served in the United States Navy during the Korean War as a diving officer aboard the submarine rescue ship USS Kittiwake and was later transferred to Naval Station, San Diego to serve as a diving instructor with the rank of Lieutenant. Harvey Milk was a tireless advocate of fairness for all Americans, both as a candidate for office and as a San Francisco Supervisor. In 1977, Harvey Milk was elected to the San Francisco Board of Supervisors, becoming the first openly gay man to be elected to public office in California. This milestone achievement gave hope to millions of gays and lesbians across the country that a day would come when they could live their lives openly and honestly without fear of discrimination. As a Supervisor, Harvey Milk worked with others to secure passage of San Francisco’s landmark Gay Rights Ordinance. This ordinance extended employment protections to gays and lesbians in San Francisco, and it became a model for anti-discrimination legislation throughout California and the nation. In 1978, Harvey Milk traversed the state to campaign against Proposition 6, which was known as the Briggs Initiative. Had it passed, Proposition 6 would have required California school districts to fire openly gay and lesbian teachers solely because of their sexual orientation. The proposition was defeated in the November 1978 election in part because Harvey Milk successfully appealed to Californians’ basic sense of fairness. Although Harvey Milk’s life was cut short, he was a hero and role model, and his legacy of hope, tolerance, and equality lives on.

6. Resolution in the Matter of Support for Zero-Emissions School Buses (Beiser/Whitehurst-Payne)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of support for zero-emissions school buses and urge the Congress and the President of the United States to approve federal legislation that funds a full transition towards clean electric school buses and charging stations.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In the United States, school buses transport millions of children to and from school every day and the American School Bus Council estimates that 480,000 buses carry 26 million children daily, making it the largest form of mass transit in the country. It has been estimated that on average, each school bus carries 54 children per day for 180 days of the year, traveling 12,000 miles per year. In California, school districts provide transportation between home and school for about one in eight students. Emissions from diesel engines may contribute to respiratory illnesses in children, studies have found, and have been linked to poor academic performance. Transitioning to all-electric, zero-emission school bus fleet will prevent the release of an estimated 5.3 million tons of greenhouse gas emissions each year, and protect our students’ lungs by keeping diesel fumes out of the air inside and outside of the buses. The Strategic Plan for Asthma in California, 2015-2019, states that compared to non-Hispanic Whites, asthma prevalence among African-Americans is 40 percent higher; rates of emergency room visits and hospitalizations are about four times higher; California's American Indian/Alaska Native, Pacific Islander, and Filipino populations also are more adversely affected by asthma compared to non-Hispanic Whites; low-income is a significant risk factor for asthma morbidity in California; and household income below $20,000 is associated with poor asthma control and higher rates of asthma hospitalizations and emergency room visits. Transitioning our school bus fleet to zero-emission vehicles is an essential aspect of building equitable, sustainable infrastructure and is a wise investment in our children, our environment and our future. By investing in zero-emission school buses we can create cleaner, healthier, more sustainable school communities where our children don’t just survive but actually thrive. The conversion of one school bus to an electric vehicle would save approximately 20,000 pounds of oxides of nitrogen and more than 350 pounds of diesel particulate matter over a 12-14 bus lifetime, the equivalent of taking 27 new cars off the road. According to the Environmental Law and Policy Center, electric school buses have zero fuel costs and a lower operating and maintenance costs than diesel school buses. Electric school buses also have the potential to create energy storage options for utilities through vehicle-to-grid technology, which will help California reduce emissions and could position school districts for possible future additional revenue from their utility.

7. Resolution in Support of Pathways to Advance Educator Workforce Diversity and Strengthen Career Pathways for CTE and Adult Educators (Barrera/Whitehurst-Payne)

Type
Action (Consent)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in support of pathways to advance educator diversity and strengthen career pathways for CTE and adult educators.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: California’s students need a stable, experienced, and highly qualified workforce of educators who reflect the diversity of the student body to help them reach their full potential. Existing career pathways for educators in California remain incomplete and current funding levels in California, including as provided by the Adult Education Block Grant and the Career Technical Incentive Grant, are insufficient to provide professional development to meet student and workforce needs. These insufficiencies result in barriers to diversification of the educator workforce and to establishment of core competencies in technical, vocational and adult education. According to the Learning Policy Institute, 80% of school districts reported having a teacher shortage and 70% drop in enrollment in teacher preparation programs over the last decade, contributing to a severe teacher shortage and the need to strengthen career pathways for educators. A set of legislative proposals currently pending before the California legislature, including AB 320, SB 488 and AB 388, would begin to advance educator workforce diversity and strengthen pathways for CTE and adult education teachers, when enacted along with full funding to meet student and workforce needs. AB 320 would define an institution with regional accreditation as an institution of higher education that has been accredited by a regional accrediting agency, or an institution of higher education that has a status of pre-accreditation at the time of degree conferral, provided that full accreditation was achieved within five years, and would thus allow for accreditation of credentialed teachers who would otherwise be denied a credential due to an accreditation timeline. SB 488 would authorize candidates who have been unable to take the Reading Instruction Competence Assessment (RICA) due to the COVID-19 pandemic to take a Commission on Teacher Credentialing (CTC)-approved assessment in reading instruction; would require the CTC to update the Teaching Performance Assessment to replace the RICA by July 1, 2025; and would require the CTC to revise their teacher preparation program standards and teaching performance expectations for literacy. AB 388 would allow adult education and ROCP teachers to gain permanent status after successfully completing a probationary period.

The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.

  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the Agreement to remove and replace electrical service at Chesterton Elementary School and Mason Elementary School (Proposed Project) is subject to CEQA, however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities. Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the project involves negligible or no expansion of an existing use. Examples cited by the CEQA guidelines relevant to the Proposed Project include interior or exterior alterations including interior partitions, plumbing, and electrical conveyances. The Proposed Project involves the replacement and repair of existing facilities and would not result the expansion of the existing use at the school site. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.

RECOMMENDATION: Ratify Agreement with IVC Media, LLC, to provide development, maintenance, project management, and an optimized and interactive website for district families and students to support the summer academic and summer enrichment programs (PS21-0934-21). Approve any documents necessary to effectuate this Agreement. The term of this Agreement is March 24, 2021 through December 31, 2021.  

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Total not-to-exceed amount: $87,250.00. Budget String: 5707-00000-00-5100-2100-0000-01000-0000. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On March 9, 2021, the board approved $5,000,000.00 to fund the 2021 Summer School Program and Extended School Year (ESY) plan. This plan approved funding to provide district students the opportunity to enroll in various programs with community-based non-profit organizations that will support instructional opportunities for expanded enrichment activities.

 

On March 23, 2021, the board approved an agreement with The San Diego Foundation to assist the district, by acting as a fiscal agent, in providing a vast reach of activities for district students at various locations with various community partners to provide these summer enrichment opportunities. In collaboration with our summer enrichment partner, The San Diego Foundation, and the district, IVC Media is filling an immediate need.

 

IVC Media possesses the professional skills and has the immediate staffing to build and maintain an optimized and interactive website dedicated to the summer enrichment programs. This website will allow the district to continually inform and update the public as it relates to these new opportunities in a timely manner and allow easy access to all summer offerings.
