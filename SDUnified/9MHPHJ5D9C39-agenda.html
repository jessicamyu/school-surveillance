RECOMMENDATION: This is a First Reading of the proposed 2015-16 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district.  This report presents historical information on the 2015-16 Title I eligibility ranking and school participation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) had preliminary discussion of the Title I ranking student counts on November 19, 2014, and reviewed the proposed site allocations at its meeting on December 17, 2014. The information is scheduled to be discussed at the January 21, 2015 DAC meeting. 

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized by the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Title I, one of several programs under NCLB, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards. The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty. Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary. The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The Proposed 2015-16 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2014-15 Free and Reduced Lunch percentage of 40 percent or higher. The total Projected 2015-16 Title I Entitlement is $37,328,217. The Title I budget includes required reservations, services and supports (Attachments 2 and 3). Of the total projected entitlement, the proposal recommends allocating $15,400,000 to schools (Attachment 1).  Staff will incorporate any adjustments in the Proposed 2015-16 Title I budget based on continued input from the board, staff, District Advisory Council, parents, and various stakeholder groups prior to scheduled board action on January 27, 2015.

Subject
2. Resolution in the Matter of Support of Federal Impact Aid Funding and Urge the U.S. Congress to Maintain Pre-Sequester Funding (Beiser/Evans)
Meeting
Jan 13, 2015 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in support of the federal Impact Aid funding and urge the U.S. Congress to maintain pre-sequester funding.

 

BACKGROUND: Impact Aid is the oldest elementary and secondary federal education program administered by the U.S. Department of Education and is designed to reimburse more than 1,300 local educational agencies across the United States for the loss of traditional revenue due to the presence of tax-exempt property or federal activity.

 

Payments are allocated directly to local educational agencies for military bases, Native American reservations, low-rent housing, and other federal property in lieu of local tax dollars to assist with the basic educational needs of the thousands of students served by those local educational agencies; and existing property owners in affected local agencies already bear a significant burden in financial support of local educational agencies due to federal acquisition of private lands.

 

Impact Aid funding is of critical importance in providing quality educational opportunities for the more than 100 school districts and charter schools in California which apply for and receive Impact Aid in support of the more than 53,300 federally connected students educated in California schools.  Sixteen school districts in California are estimated to receive $16 million in Impact Aid Section 8002 funding as federal property payments, and 100 local educational agencies in California are estimated to receive approximately $60 million in Impact Aid Section 8003 funding for basic support payments.

 

San Diego Unified is comprised of 117 elementary schools, 25 middle schools, 24 high schools, and 11 alternative or atypical schools that service over 109,000 students and approximately 12 percent of the student population is federally connected.

 

The $8 million in federal Impact Aid funding that the San Diego Unified School District is estimated to receive for Fiscal Year 2014 will be used for staffing and support services for regular education, special education and to support general operating costs across a wide range of expenditures to include salaries, utilities, technology, and facilities maintenance, among others.

 

The Budget Control Act of 2011, established caps on discretionary spending through Fiscal Year 2022, and in Fiscal Year 2013, there were sequestration cuts to discretionary programs across-the-board, and these cuts eliminated funding to the U.S. Department of Education by over $4 billion – the largest cut ever to education programs.  The cut to Impact Aid for Fiscal Year 2013, was over $65 million across the country; and the recession and sequestration forced school districts to make difficult decisions like eliminating positions and professional development, cutting academic programs, reducing transportation, increased class sizes, and reduced funding for art, music and afterschool programs; and this created an overall environment of financial uncertainty.

 

Fiscal Years 2014 and 2015 received a two-year reprieve from sequestration; and school districts are now concerned about the viability of Impact Aid funding in Fiscal Year 2016 through Fiscal Year 2022, if the U.S. Congress does not find a balanced alternative to the Budget Control Act as maintaining Impact Aid funding levels will become increasingly difficult based on strict caps for discretionary programs; it is the federal government’s obligation to rectify the negative financial impact its presence has caused.

 

San Diego Unified requests the California Legislature and the U.S. Congress support federally impacted school funding by advocating for an immediate full-year appropriations bill, as well as long-term fiscal plan that truly supports the nation’s children, and strongly opposes sequestration as created by the Budget Control Act of 2011, and its negative effect on the Impact Aid Program and other federal programs; and advocates for the continuation of funding for this federal obligation to alleviate the local taxpayers burden caused by federal land ownership as has been the standard for more than 60 years.  San Diego Unified supports the Senate Fiscal Year 2015 funding bill which (1) maintains pre-sequester funding levels for Impact Aid, and (2) funds Impact Aid Federal Properties which was eliminated in the Administration’s budget request. 

 

San Diego Unified requests the U.S. Congress recognize the importance of the Impact Aid Program in ensuring that federally connected children receive a high-quality public education by fully funding the Impact Aid Program; and requests the U.S. Congressional members to co-sponsor the bipartisan stand-alone Impact Aid reauthorization bill: Local Taxpayer Relief Act: House – H.R. 2296, Senate – S. 1108.  This bill is similar to the Impact Aid language adopted by the House of Representatives and the Senate HELP Committee.

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $84,100 (for the year 2014) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and for contracts, leases or other documents no greater than $84,100 (for the year 2014) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111. The purchase orders, contracts, leases, or other documents are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders, contracts, leases or other documents. For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 18, 2013, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

 

Thus, the district has met the requirements of Education Code section 17605 with this process of reporting out the specified purchase orders under $84,100 (for the year 2014) that are not associated with any previously-approved board contracts.
