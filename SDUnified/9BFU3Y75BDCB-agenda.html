4. Continue Discussions with Jacobs Center for Neighborhood Innovation (JCNI) to Plan and Implement the Final Year of Diamond Community Summer Readers-Future Leaders Summer Learning Camp; BUDGETED: N/A
Meeting
Feb 11, 2014 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: Approve continued discussions with the Jacobs Center for Neighborhood Innovation (JCNI) to plan and implement the final year of the pilot for the Diamond Community Summer Readers-Future Leaders Summer Learning Camp at Chollas/Mead Elementary.

 

An agreement with the Diamond Educational Excellence Partnership (DEEP) may be brought forward, for board approval, at a later date.  A funding source will need to be identified for the district contribution toward the final pilot year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None at this time.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $47,750.00

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: No additional staffing is needed.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On March 26, 2013, the board approved a resolution to partner with the Diamond Community Education Working Group to implement a community-based summer learning camp at Chollas/Mead Elementary School. A subsequent agreement with Jacobs Center for Neighborhood Innovation (JCNI), approved by the board on April 23, 2013 (item E.3., attached), specified the camp components: literacy acceleration and enrichment, literacy linked to the arts, science and service learning, connecting to community literacy resources, building strong bodies and characters, family engagement, and community engagement; responsibilities of the district; responsibilities of JCNI; and the project budget. The purpose of the camp was to increase students' motivation to read, reduce summer reading loss, improve social-emotional development and physical health, and engage in experiential inquiry-based educational and service-learning activities linked to the restoration of natural resources. Chollas/Mead Elementary was selected as it is the only Diamond Community elementary school operating on a traditional-year calendar that feeds solely into Lincoln High School. Additional community partners participating in the project included the University of California San Diego (UCSD) California Reading and Literature Project, Young Audiences, Groundwork San Diego, UCSD Science Project, Malcolm X Library, READ/San Diego, UPforEd, and the Jackie Robinson YMCA. The district provided JCNI with $47,750 from Program Improvement Year 3 funds for the first year of this pilot program. The camp took place from June 17, 2013 through July 19, 2013, for 90 rising second and third grade students.  A final evaluation of the 2013 camp implementation is attached.

 

2013 Camp Components:

 

Literacy Acceleration and Enrichment: Students  participated in lessons designed to promote literacy acceleration and enrichment based on their reading proficiency levels. Certificated teachers (1:10 student ratio) from the district provided literacy instruction aligned with district and the new Common Core standards, but delivered in ways that made the summer experience distinct from the typical school year. Instruction was designed based upon the assessed needs of the participating students.

 

Literacy Linked to the Arts: With the professional services support of Young Audiences, afternoon activities, twice weekly engaged students in instructional units that integrated the arts with reading to improve both reading motivation and literacy skills.

 

Science and Service Learning: Students, along with their teachers and parents, participated in professional services experiential learning opportunities at Groundwork San Diego’s four-acre outdoor classroom or EarthLab located in the Chollas Creek Watershed. These activities, which  took place in the morning hours twice weekly, involved students rotating among five science-focused demonstration stations. Students and their teachers learned from professional service provided by the San Diego Science Project. Students were expected to keep scientific journals in which they used academic vocabulary to document their experiences and findings.

 

Connecting to Community Literacy Resources: For five Fridays, students walked with their teachers to the Malcolm X Library. At the library, they participated in local programs, checked out books, and participated in a summer reading program. Both the Malcolm X Library and READ/San Diego provided students with incentives for active engagement in reading. READ/San Diego provided each student with six books for their home libraries.

 

Building Strong Bodies and Characters: Five afternoons per week, students participated in swim-and-gym and character development programming at the Jackie Robinson YMCA. Camp activities included access to a swimming pool, basketball gym, outdoor basketball courts, soccer arena, and baseball, and t-ball fields. Character development included small group facilitation focused on understanding the core values of caring, honesty, respect, and responsibility.

 

Family Engagement: Opportunities for engaging families in children’s summer learning were woven into each camp area. In lieu of a camp fee, families were expected to participate in arts performances, science labs, fitness or literacy events.

 

Community Engagement: The Diamond Educational Excellence Partnership (DEEP) convened camp partners weekly throughout the summer to discuss camp activities, progress, and challenges. Additionally, partner organizations participated in two presentations to the full membership of the DEEP during the summer so that this community leadership group could deepen its understanding of the supports being provided to students and the progress being made. DEEP members, with connections to the local business community, actively sought business involvement in the camp program through the provision of incentives to students for strong attendance.

 

Next Steps for 2014:

 

District staff are in preliminary discussions to continue the final year of the pilot for the Diamond Community Summer Readers-Future Leaders Summer Learning Camp, with intent to phase back in an elementary summer program in the future. The Summer Readers Future Leaders program may serve as a model for future summer elementary program offerings.   A funding source will need to be identified for the district contribution toward the final pilot year.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve recommendation to delegate decision making authority to district staff related to Early Admission to Kindergarten.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Beginning with the 2014-15 school year, a child must have his or her fifth birthday by September 1 to be admitted to Kindergarten.  This represents a shift in the age eligibility requirements which were phased in over the course of three years from December 2, to November 1, to October 1, and finally to the current date.  Students who turn five between September 2 and December 2, will be eligible to enroll in a Transitional Kindergarten program. 

 

California Education Code - EC 48000(b) allows districts the discretion to provide early admission to Kindergarten, on a case-by-case basis:

“The governing board of a school district maintaining one or more kindergartens may, on a case-by-case basis, admit to a kindergarten a child having attained the age of five years at any time during the school year with the approval of the parents or guardian, subject to the following conditions:

(1) The governing board determines that the admittance is in the best interests of the child.

(2) The parent or guardian is given information regarding the advantages and disadvantages and any other explanatory information about the effect of this early admittance.”

 

Proposed process

Currently, each individual case requesting early admission to kindergarten is presented to the Board of Education for consideration in closed session.  The proposed process provides parameters for early admission to kindergarten and authorizes district staff to act on behalf of the governing board in deciding individual requests for Early Admission to Kindergarten.  The proposed process and guidelines are outlined below.

 

Consideration for Early Admission would require all of the following:

  1. The student will have his or her birthday by December 2, and
  2. The parents/guardians of the student request Early Admission, and
  3. A determination is made at the elementary school that Early Admission to Kindergarten is in the best interest of the student.   The district elementary school principal and staff:
    1. provide information regarding the advantages and disadvantages of early admission, in addition to information regarding the school’s Kindergarten and Transitional Kindergarten programs.
    2. review information and data to determine that Early Admission to Kindergarten is in the best interests of the student and that they are prepared to successfully meet the standards of kindergarten in one year.  Such information and data may include (but not limited to): Pre-school experience, recommendation from current/previous school/teacher; interview of the student; commitment from parents to provide support needed for success in kindergarten.

Documentation of these three criteria are signed by parents, principal, and area superintendent with copies files in the student’s cumulative file and given to the parents.

 

In the event that a review of information and data do not indicate that placement in Kindergarten is in the best interests of the student (e.g. readiness to successfully complete and accomplish the standards of kindergarten in one year is in doubt) and criteria 1 and 2 are in place, the student will be allowed admittance to Transitional Kindergarten.  Documentation of the request and rationale for the decision will also be made available to parents/guardians and placed in the student’s cumulative file.

 

The Office of Teaching and Learning will develop forms and guidance documents based on the proposed process and provide support to schools and parents.

On October 11, 2011, the board approved a First Amendment to Agreement to increase the consideration by an additional $80,000 for a revised total of $200,000.

15. Grant Acceptance from the Department of Defense (DOD), Program for Construction, Renovation, Repair, or Expansion of Public Schools Located on Military Installations, for Whole Site Modernization (WSM) Projects at Hancock and Miller Elementary Schools ; BUDGETED: YES PROPOSITION Z FUNDS

RECOMMENDATIONS: Approve:

  1. acceptance of estimated $34 million in grant funding provided by the U.S. Department of Defense (DOD) Program for Construction, Renovation, Repair or Expansion of Public Schools located on military installations for Whole Site Modernization (WSM) projects at Hancock and Miller elementary schools.
     
  2. delegating signatory authority to allow the Executive Director, Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC), to sign the grant documents, binding the district to the terms and conditions of the grant, and to approve and sign additional grant documents involving funding requests or changes to the scope of work. 

A Request to Develop a Proposal (RDP) form is being routed for approvals under separate cover, as required, and in accordance with Administrative Procedure 1320: Grants/Projects Supported by Outside Funding Sources.

 

This DOD grant will leverage approximately $8.6 million in existing Proposition Z bond funds, to perform work valued at $42.6 million. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $34 million in federal DOD grant funding requires $8.6 million in local match using Proposition Z bond funds ($4.6 million had previously been allocated to Hancock Elementary School Whole Site Modernization (WSM) project, and $4.0 million previously allocated to Miller Elementary School WSM project).  No fiscal or schedule impact to other bond projects.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

  

BACKGROUND: On April 15, 2011, the Department of Defense and Full-Year Continuing Appropriations Act was signed into law.  Under section 8109 of the law, Congress appropriated $250 million to construct, renovate, repair, or expand elementary and secondary public schools located on military installations in order to address capacity or facility condition deficiencies. Subsequent congressional appropriations have provided additional funding.  In 2012, San Diego Unified was invited by the DOD to submit Project Proposals and Preliminary Engineering Information for Hancock and Miller elementary schools.     

 

The district's Facilities Planning and Construction (FPC) staff worked with the DOD over the past 18 months to develop a scope of work consistent with the district’s Long Range Facilities Master Planning, Proposition's S and Z bond scope, and federal facilities requirements. This coordinated effort resulted in a scope which includes replacement of portable classrooms with new permanent construction, renovation of open, loft-style classroom buildings including installation of permanent walls to separate classrooms, installation of turf playing fields, upgrades to existing air conditioning systems, new information technology infrastructure upgrades, and other modifications.

 

We are happy to report that on Friday, January 10, 2014, we received letters inviting San Diego Unified to submit an application for grant funding.  This means that the federal review team has validated the proposed scope and its responsiveness in addressing capacity and facility condition deficiencies at the two schools.  A grant award will be based on our complete application for federal funding.

 

These federal matching grants will allow the district to leverage an estimated $8.6 million in bond funding already allocated to the two WSM projects to receive an additional $34 million in Federal funds ($18 million for Hancock and $16 million for Miller).  The availability of this federal funding will allow the district to replace more than 20 portable classrooms with district-standard permanent construction, convert existing open "loft style" classroom buildings, and otherwise expand the scope of work at these two schools beyond what was originally planned for the WSM project, without spending additional district funds.  This will have a significant positive impact, supporting the initiative to provide quality neighborhood schools.  Use of bond funding to fulfill grant requirements has proven to be effective, as in the case when FPC has taken advantage of federal E-Rate funding, State Office of Public School Construction funding, and other grant opportunities to improve facility conditions.

 

The current bond construction program plan anticipates construction at the two schools starting in late 2015. The schools will remain in operation while construction is being performed.  Construction phasing and other strategies will be employed to minimize disruption so that teaching and learning are not compromised.
