1. Public Hearing and Resolution in the Matter of Certification of Sufficient Standards-Aligned Textbooks and/or Instructional Materials

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and adopt a Resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System, and/or providing student access to digital versions of Board approved instructional materials using a computer.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code section 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. Per Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must be provided access to a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials. The materials may be in a digital format as long as each pupil, at a minimum, has and can access the same materials in the class and to take home, as all other pupils in the same class or course in the school district and has the ability to use and access them at home. The board is required to adopt a Resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable.

 

Williams Settlement Legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12. The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core, lab equipment for science laboratory courses. All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation also requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual Resolution of Sufficiency. Administrative Circulars 1, 2, and 3, dated August 26, 2021, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency." Principals were directed to complete an instructional materials inventory, remedy any insufficiencies, and confirm instructional materials sufficiency by completing and returning the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 17, 2021.

 

Twenty-five elementary schools, four TK-8 schools, six middle schools, and six senior high schools were visited by a team from the San Diego County Office of Education. All 43 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials. Additionally, all six senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

RECOMMENDATION: Review the Local Indicators Required for the California School Dashboard.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: As part of California's school accountability system aligned with the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) and the Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP), accountability results are reported via the California School Dashboard (Dashboard). In addition to several state indicators (e.g., student scores from the Smarter Balanced assessments, English learner progress, graduation rates), Local Educational Agencies (LEAs) are required to report outcomes on five local indicators that apply to LEAs only, not schools:

 

  1. Appropriately Assigned Teachers, Access to Curriculum-Aligned Instructional Materials, and Safe, Clean and Functional School Facilities (State Priority 1)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress in meeting the Williams Settlement requirements at all of its school sites, as applicable, and promptly addresses any complaints or other deficiencies identified throughout the academic year, as applicable.
  2. Implementation of State Academic Standards (State Priority 2)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress implementing state academic standards.
  3. Parent Engagement (State Priority 3)
    • Standard: LEA annually measures its progress in (1) seeking input from parents in decision-making and (2) promoting parental participation in programs.
  4. School Climate (State Priority 6)
    • Standard: LEA administers a local climate survey at least every other year that provides a valid measure of perceptions of school safety and connectedness, such as the California Healthy Kids Survey, to students in at least one grade within the grade span(s) that the LEA serves (e.g., K-5, 6-8, 9-12).
  5. Access to a Broad Course of Study (State Priority 7)
    • Standard: Local educational agencies (LEAs) annually measure their progress in the extent to which students have access to, and are enrolled in, a broad course of study that includes the adopted courses of study specified in the California Education Code for Grades 1-6 and Grades 7-12 , as applicable, including the programs and services developed and provided to unduplicated students and individuals with exceptional needs.

Prior to finalizing information reported via the Dashboard, LEAs are required to have their governing boards review the information at a regularly scheduled public meeting. The information in the attachment to this item presents information that will be included on the Fall 2021 Dashboard that will be released in December 2021. Given state flexibility allowing use of local assessments in Spring 2021 in lieu of state assessments, and other situations attributable to the COVID-19 pandemic, limited data will be published on the Dashboard. Instead, the California Department of Education will post many related reports, if valid and reliable data are available, on its DataQuest online site.

 

The three typical performance levels for the local indicators (Met, Not Met, and Not Met for Two or More Years) will not be used on the Dashboard this year. This agenda item fulfills this year's requirement that the district has collected and reported performance data for each local indicator.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution in the Matter of Recognizing the Second Monday of October as Indigenous Peoples' Day. The district encourages staff to utilize this day as an opportunity to teach the truth about US history and the impact that colonialism has had on Indigenous people. The district commits to celebrating the thriving cultures and values of the Kumeyaay Nation, to reflect upon the ongoing struggles of the Indigenous people of this land, and to stand in solidarity with Indigenous peoples everywhere.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District recognizes that the Indigenous peoples of the lands that would later become known as the Americas have occupied these lands since time immemorial. The district recognizes that the arrival of European explorers, colonists, and missionaries led to the genocide of Indigenous peoples of the land through slavery, violence, coercion, and disease. The district recognizes the fact that San Diego is built upon the unceded territory of the Kumeyaay Nation, without whom the building of the City would not have been possible. The district values the many contributions made to our community through Kumeyaay knowledge, labor, technology, science, philosophy, arts and the deep cultural contribution that has substantially shaped the charter of the City of San Diego. The district has a responsibility to oppose the systematic racism towards Indigenous people in the United States, which perpetuates high rates of poverty and income inequality, exacerbating disproportionate health, education, and social crises. The district must seek to combat prejudice and eliminate discrimination stemming from colonization and to promote awareness, understanding, and good relations among Indigenous peoples and all other segments of our district. And the district promotes the closing of the equity gap for Indigenous peoples through policies and practices that reflect the experiences of Indigenous peoples, ensures greater access and opportunity, and honors our nation’s indigenous roots, history, and contributions. California State Assembly Bill 738, approved by the Governor on October 9, 2017, requires the state to develop a model curriculum that encourages school districts to offer a standards-based Native American studies curriculum. The San Diego Unified School District seeks to join the hundreds of other municipalities, school districts, and universities that recognize the second Monday of October as Indigenous Peoples’ Day to be observed as a day in solidarity with Indigenous peoples.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of imminent risk of harm to attendees of public meetings during the COVID-19 pandemic which will be effective for 30 days, commencing October 12, 2021.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Assembly Bill No. 361 (“AB 361”) was enacted by the California Legislature and is intended to “improve and enhance public access to . . . local agency meetings during the COVID-19 pandemic and future applicable emergencies, by allowing broader access through teleconferencing options.” AB 361 permits local legislative bodies subject to the Brown Act and/or the Badgley Keene Act to hold virtual meetings, without needing to publish the physical locations of Board members or make those locations open to the public, if there is a proclaimed state of emergency (pursuant to Section 8625 of the California Emergency Services Act) and either: (i) state or local officials have imposed or recommended measures to promote social distancing, or (ii) there is a finding by the local legislative body that, as a result of the emergency, meeting in person “would present imminent risks to the health or safety of attendees.” Since the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, in compliance with public health orders and for the protection all involved persons, the Board of Education (“Board”) has held its meetings telephonically via Zoom and provided for public comments to be provided via teleconferencing rather than allowing in-person attendance by the public at the Education Center. The COVID-19 pandemic continues to present an imminent health risk to the public. The District’s central office facilities present specific challenges in maintaining COVID-19-related safety protocols, including adequate ventilation and security, and maintenance of recommended social distancing.

CEQA ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW: The approval of the proposed Joint Use Agreements and License Agreement with the City of San Diego for continued recreational use by the City and district (Proposed Project) is subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), however, it is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines Sections 15301 (Class 1) – Existing Facilities and 15323 (Class 23) – Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings.

 

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

 

The Proposed Project includes the approval of new joint use agreements and a license agreement at facilities where existing similar use by the City and the district occurs. Expansion of recreational use at the above mentioned facilities would not occur and approval of these agreements would not result in any operational changes compared with the existing use. Therefore, the application of a Class 1 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Class 23 consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose. Past history is defined as a similar kind of activity that has been occurring for at least three years and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323).

 

The Proposed Project includes the approval of new joint use agreements and a license agreement to replace existing, expired agreements to support recreational uses by students and residents. The sites have operated as recreational facilities for over 10 years. Therefore, the application of a Class 23 Exemption is appropriate.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may, in a particularly sensitive environment, be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may have an impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not applicable.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of existing school sites, existing joint use areas, and existing recreational centers. The Proposed Project would not extend into any offsite or open space areas that may be classified as an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with operation of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Operation of the joint use fields and recreation centers is consistent with existing use and would not contribute to a cumulatively considerable impact. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes approval of joint use agreements and a license agreement for existing recreational uses. No construction activity is proposed on- or off-site that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. As such, there is nothing unusual about the existing school sites, joint use areas, recreation centers, or surrounding areas. Operation of the joint use areas would be consistent with existing use. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. The Proposed Project would not result in the construction of new facilities that would directly, or indirectly, affect an officially designated scenic highway or scenic resources near a scenic highway. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, none of the project sites are located on any list compiled pursuant to Section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. The Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment at the project site is anticipated with the implementation of the Proposed Project. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the Project.

The Fencing and Single Point of Entry Project at Cabrillo Elementary, Longfellow K-8, and Ross Elementary Schools are subject to the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), however, the project is exempt from further environmental review under CEQA Guidelines section 15303 – New Construction or Conversion of Existing Structures (Class 3). Class 3 consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures; installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures; and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure. The numbers of structures described in this section are the maximum allowable on any legal parcel. Examples of this exemption include accessory (appurtenant) structures including fences, security doors, and security cameras. Therefore, the application of a Class 3 categorical exemption is appropriate for the Proposed Project.

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2, and summarized below, prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

Environmental Review: The following provides review of the potential environmental issues associated with the Proposed Project to ensure that the above exceptions are not relevant to the project.

  1. Location. The Proposed Project would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. Installation of security fencing and other security infrastructure is consistent with the operation of a school. Generally, the surrounding land uses at the project site consists of residential housing and the Proposed Project would not extend beyond the project site into open space areas. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  2. Cumulative Impact. There are no known future projects proposed at the project site or in the project vicinity that, when combined with the construction of the Proposed Project, would result in a cumulatively considerable effect on the environment. Once the Proposed Project has been constructed, existing school operations would remain unchanged compared with existing conditions. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  3. Significant Effect. The Proposed Project includes the construction of security fencing and infrastructure at an existing school. No work is proposed offsite that could result in impacts to sensitive vegetation or other environmentally sensitive areas as a result of the Proposed Project. Removal of ornamental landscaping and trees may be required however, no sensitive plant species of are proposed for removal. Due to the timing of the Proposed Project outside the nesting season (January 15 to August 31), impacts to nesting birds would not occur. As such, there is nothing unusual about the project site or surrounding area. Furthermore, there is nothing unusual about the design of the Proposed Project that could result in a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. Therefore, there is no reasonable possibility that the Proposed Project would have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances, and this exception does not apply to the project.
  4. Scenic Highways. There are no designated scenic highways visible from any District school site. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. According to the California State Water Resources Control Board Geotracker database, the project site is not included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code. Therefore, this exception does not apply to the project.
  6. Historical Resources. According to the CEQA Guidelines regarding historical resources, a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource amounts to a significant impact on the environment (Guidelines section 15064.5(b)). Accordingly, a substantial adverse change means demolition, destruction, relocation, or alteration of the resource or its immediate surroundings resulting in the significance of the resource being materially impaired. Because the Proposed Project does not require the demolition of existing historical era buildings or structures, the significance of a potentially historical resource will not be materially impaired, and no substantial adverse changes to the significance of a historical resource or significant impacts to the environment is anticipated. Additionally, the likelihood of impacting either prehistoric or historic archaeological resources is negligible for the current undertaking due because major grading and trenching into native soils is not proposed. This exception does not apply to the project.
