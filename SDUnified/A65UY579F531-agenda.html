1. Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood - Report on Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All (LCAP Goal 1)
Meeting
Nov 8, 2016 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All (Vision 2020 Quality Indicator and corresponding LCAP Goal 1).  This report will focus on early learning, pre-Kindergarten literacy, and pre-school opportunities for all students.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destination:  Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate the significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring tht every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gap goals are implemented.  All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly, or indirectly, to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.

 

LCAP Goal 1:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:

  • 1.2.a.1.  The district will develop and implement a well-articulated pathway from pre-school through Grade 3, including language instruction (e.g., English Learner [EL] grade level goals, biliteracy and dual language instructional programs).  
  • 1.2.b.1.  Lower class size will be implemented in Transitional Kindergarten (TK) - Grade 3.  
  • 1.2.b.2. TK-3 students will receive differentiated instruction, to ensure reading proficiency by Grade 3.  
  • 1.2.b.4. Professional development for teachers will be provided on key topics including student centered teaching, early intervention and support, effective teaching practices, and parent communication.  Training by cluster, and cross-cluster and out of district visits to observe best practices will be included.   
  • 1.2.b.6. Communication and recruitment strategies will be provided to families to ensure all eligible students participate in Pre-K and TK.

BACKGROUND:  At each regular, public board meeting, Superintendent Marten provides a report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation. The complete LCAP document is available on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

2. Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood - Report on Positive School Environment, Climate, and Culture, with Equity at the Core and Support for the Whole Child (LCAP Goal 4)

Information

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Positive School Environment, Climate and Culture, with Equity at the Core and Support for the Whole Child (Vision 2020 Quality Indicators and corresponding LCAP Goal 4).  This report will introduce the district's plan for an integrated multi-tiered system of support and reconnection.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destinations:  

  • All employees have knowledge and skills necessary to make equity a reality as they support and serve the learning needs of diverse student populations.  Students demonstrate the knowledge and skills, and act thoughtfully and respectfully in order to learn collaboratively with, and value the contributions of, students of different backgrounds.  Multiple cultural perspectives and experiences are valued and people feel respected and welcomed.  Schools challenge and eliminate practices that encourage bias.  Leaders are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.  
  • The school site is a hub connecting families to wrap-around services (including academic and social services) and/or resources needed by students and communities to ensure equity.  Services can impact students' academic development directly, such as tutoring or after school programs, or indirectly such as services to address physical or emotional growth.  
  • All schools are clean, safe, and well-organized and have a positive climate that supports the academic, emotional and physical needs of students through appropriate supervision, positive behavior intervention and support and customer service.

LCAP Goal 4:  Schools provide safe and well-maintained facilities and positive learning climates and instructional practices that are culturally responsive, challenge bias, and support the academic, social, emotional, and physical needs of students.

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:  

  • 1.3.b.6. Additional high school innovation centers will be established to reconnect students on a pathway to meaningful graduation that meets a continuum of individual needs including enrichment, acceleration, and intervention.  
  • 1.3.b.7. Mentoring programs will be expanded at identified middle and high schools.  
  • 1.5.2. Rigorous online learning opportunities will be provided.  
  • 1.5.5. The mentor program for at-risk youth to monitor attendance, behavior, and school performance will be expanded.  
  • 1.5.7. All schools, site administrators and area superintendents will provide support in the development and implementation of quality Response to Instruction and Intervention (RTII) programs.  Professional development will be provided to ensure staff are prepared to implement the RTII program with fidelity.     
  • 1.10.6. The district will expand restorative justice and Positive Behavioral Interventions and Support (PBIS) and provide professional development to promote effective and positive behavior support strategies at each school.  
  • 1.10.7.  A robust mentoring program that engages district staff, advocacy groups, and other community members to serve as trained mentors to increase the academic and social success of African American and Latino students will be developed.  
  • 1.10.8.  The district will support the expansion and development of student advocacy groups at all secondary schools where students' cultures and identities can be explored and nurtured.  These advocacy groups will also serve as mechanisms for students to connect to organizations and events in the broader community.  
  • 4.1.a.1. Counseling, psychological and guidance services that address student social and emotional needs will be provided.
  • 4.1.a.2. Support systems for improved student attendance and alternative placement opportunities will be provided.  
  • 4.1.b.2. Support, case management, and referrals for ongoing mental health treatment and support will be provided.  
  • 4.1.b.3. Behavior support resources will be provided.  
  • 4.1.b.4. Restorative justice and Positive Behavioral Intervention and Support (PBIS) will be expanded to more schools.  
  • 4.1.b.5. Additional, focused support for students with disabilities, including mental health and behavior support services, and PBIS collaboration will be provided.  
  • 4.1.b.7. Professional development opportunities will be provided for all teachers and classified staff to learn and utilize effective strategies to create a proactive method of addressing behavioral challenges, such as restorative circles and conferences, Positive Behavioral Intervention and Support (PBIS), trauma informed care practices, development of learning partnerships, and increased support from school counselors and other support staff. 
  • 4.3.1. The district will provide coordinated and collaborative support services to youth in transition (homeless, foster, probation, refugee, military), including resource teachers to work alongside area superintendents and supporting departments to monitor academic progress, attendance rates and behavioral data of youth in transition (homeless/foster) and to develop a tiered level of support and resources at school sites with high concentrations of youth in transition.
  • 4.3.2. A Children and Youth in Transition (CYT) resource teacher will coordinate and collaborate with the area superintendents, supporting departments and school sites to develop tiered interventions to address site and student needs, as well as issues impacting students identified as being in transition.  Interventions may include or address safety, attendance, basic needs, and barriers to a student's education or academic interventions.  
  • 4.3.3. Central office and school personnel will coordinate and collaborate to develop and refine processes to ensure that students in transition are identified at school sites and provided with the necessary supports and resources for educational success.  Resources and supports may include linkages or access to tutoring, mental health, medical, basic needs (i.e., clothing, food), school supplies, and transportation to school of origin.
  • 4.3.4. Mentor teachers will be located at five high schools (Crawford, Hoover, Lincoln, Morse and San Diego) and paired with at-risk youth identified as homeless, foster and/or probation to monitor attendance, behavior, school performance and progress toward graduation.  
  • 4.3.5. Trauma informed care professional development will be offered to all district staff as a first level of support, awareness and knowledge that is in alignment with the district's implementation of Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS), Response to Instruction and Intervention (RTII), and restorative practices.  Professional development in the area of trauma informed care will be developed in collaboration with supporting departments for teachers and school staff to assist them in the understanding and development of how to effectively support students whose lives have been affected by trauma and the implementation of PBIS, RTII, and restorative practices.  
  • 4.3.7. Children and Youth in Transition (CYT) staff will collaborate with district departments, community agencies and government agencies to provide linkages to resources and supports for youth in transition.
  • 4.3.8. Professional development will be provided on laws and topics pertaining to foster and homeless youth (e.g., AB 490, McKinney Vento Act, graduation requirements, trauma informed care resources).
  • 4.4.1. Districtwide efforts will be continued to provide safe inclusive environments for all students to thrive, specifically Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender and Questioning, Intersex, and Asexual (LGBTQIA) students by:  Continuing LBTQIA awarness and advocacy trainings at school sites and departments; developing an ongoing method to survey and report results of the inclusive climate in secondary schools; and continuing to support Gay-Straight Alliances (GSA) at our secondary schools as a robust and viable source of student empowerment.   

Related LCAP Metrics:  School Climate Survey (safety, protective factors, caring relationships, high expectations, meaningful participation), suspension rates, number of expulsions, student attendance rates, chronic absentee rates.

BACKGROUND:

 

Proposed 2017 Summer School Program

 

It is proposed that a 2017 Summer School program be offered to general education students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects) and a one-day Bridging Program be offered for incoming 9th grade students to help them transition from middle to high school.

 

A proposed six-week program will be offered at an estimated nine high school sites to be located district-wide, for students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects).  Historical data indicates that approximately 5,000 students in Grades 9-12 receive F grades in core subjects at the first semester, which will make them eligible for the program.  Proposed program dates are June 19, 2017 – July 28, 2017.  Transportation will not be provided.

 

A proposed one-day bridging program would be offered for incoming Grade 9 students.  The program would be offered at every high school to help students transition from middle to senior high school.  Approximately 7,200 students entering Grade 9 in fall 2017, will be eligible.  Transportation will not be provided.

 

2017 Summer School Cost Estimate and Proposed Funding Source

 

The approximate cost of the 2017 Summer School program described above is $1,794,000.00 which includes $1,602,000.00 for teacher, counselor, librarian, and administrator salaries, office/clerical, security, custodial and technical support, and supplies for the summer credit recovery program; $190,000.00 for the one-day bridging program; and $2,000 for the summer graduation ceremony for seniors who complete their graduation requirements during summer school.

 

This cost estimate is based on the maximum number of students eligible for the program, as indicated by fall 2016 data.  Should the number of eligible students decline during the spring 2017 semester, or if fewer eligible students actually attend summer school, teachers will be released at the end of the first program week and classes will be consolidated to reduce costs. Title I Funds have been identified for the summer school program. 

 

Proposed 2017 Summer School/Extended School Year Sites

 

It is proposed that summer programs for both general education students and students with disabilities be held at districtwide sites. Tentative high school sites are: Garfield, Hoover, iHigh, Lincoln, Madison, San Diego, SCPA, Twain, and University City.  Due to major construction projects in the district, all final host summer school and ESY sites have not been selected at this time. Final sites will be determined after working with PPO/Facilities to ensure no conflicts, and in collaboration with Student Services, the Area Superintendents, and in consultation with the Physical Plant Operations and Transportation Departments; taking into consideration actual geographic patterns of ESY eligible students. 

 

Proposed 2017 Extended School Year (ESY) Program for Students with Disabilities

 

The California Code of Regulations Section 3043(f) requires that Extended School Year (ESY) services be provided for each individual with exceptional needs who has unique needs and requires special education and related services in excess of the regular academic year.  Such individuals shall have disabilities which are likely to continue indefinitely or for a prolonged period, and interruption of the pupil’s educational programming may cause regression, when coupled with limited recoupment capacity, rendering it impossible or unlikely that the pupil will attain the level of self-sufficiency and independence that would otherwise be expected in view of his or her disabling condition.  The lack of clear evidence of such factors may not be used to deny an individual an ESY program if the student’s Individual Education Program (IEP) team determines the need for such a program and includes ESY in the IEP. 

 

Transportation services will be provided.

 

2017 Extended School Year Cost Estimate for Students with Disabilities and Proposed Funding Sources

 

The approximate cost of the 2017 Summer School Program described above for students with disabilities is $3,200,000.  The estimate includes program services for infant/early childhood (birth-5 years old), elementary, middle, high school, and the TRACE Program (transition program for 18-22 year olds). 

 

These cost estimates are based on the maximum number of students eligible for the program, as indicated by fall 2016 data.  Should the number of eligible students decline during the spring 2017 semester, or if fewer eligible students actually attend summer school, teachers will be released at the end of the first program week and classes will be consolidated to reduce costs. Special Education Extended School Year (65005) and Special Education Transportation (72400) funds have been identified for the program. 

 

Background/History

 

Through 2007, the San Diego Unified School District offered a summer school program to a percentage of general education students in Grades 1-8 who performed below and significantly below grade level based on teacher judgment, instructional level, and assessment data; general education students in Grades 9-12 that needed to make up core courses to graduate; and to students with disabilities whose Individual Education Program (IEP) were found eligible for “Extended School Year.” Transportation services for all students were included in the program.

 

In 2009 and 2010, summer school was offered to general education students in Grades 9-12 for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8, per the district’s retention policy.  Transportation was no longer provided for general education students.  The Special Education department began funding summer school and providing transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2011, summer school was offered only to Grade 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8, per the district’s retention policy. Transportation was not provided for general education students.  The Special Education department continued to fund summer school and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2012, summer school was offered only to Grade 11 and 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8, per the district’s retention policy. Transportation was not provided for general education students. The Special Education department continued to fund summer school and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2013, summer school was offered only to Grades 9- 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8, per the district’s retention policy. The Special Education department continued to fund summer school and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

 

In 2014, 2015, and 2016, summer school was offered only to Grades 9- 12 general education students for credit recovery. The general education program was paid for out of Title I Funds. Transportation was not provided for general education students. In 2014, we had an average 3,511 high school students attend summer school; in 2015, we had an average of 3,837 high school students attend summer school; and in 2016 we had an average of 3,907 students attend summer school.  The Special Education department continued to fund summer school and provide transportation for students with Extended School Year (ESY) eligibility on their Individual Education Program (IEP).

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution recognizing November 17, 2016, as National Parent/Family Involvement Day.  The board acknowledges the importance of National Parent/Family Involvement Day and its goal of increasing overall student achievement through family engagement.

 

BACKGROUND: In 2012, the San Diego Unified Council of PTAs initially introduced the first National Parent Involvement day for San Diego Unified School District.

 

The San Diego Unified School District, the San Diego Unified Council of PTAs, and the broader education field recognize there are many significant caregivers in children’s lives not adequately represented by the term “parent,” therefore the term “family” is also used to celebrate this day.

 

The San Diego Unified School District and the San Diego Unified Council of PTAs partner to recognize National Parent/Family Involvement Day at district schools and all of the existing avenues for family engagement.

 

The San Diego Unified School District and San Diego Unified Council of PTAs are catalyzed by 40 years of research confirmed by experience that strong family, school, and community partnerships to support student learning, both at school and at home, increase overall student achievement, attendance, credit acquisition, graduation rates, life outcomes, and school effectiveness.

 

The mission of PTA is to make every child’s potential a reality by engaging and empowering families and communities to advocate for all children.

 

The California State PTA School SMARTS Parent Academy Program provides parents with information and resources to help advocate for their children using art and was introduced to San Diego Unified School District in the fall of 2013; is currently in the process of being introduced or continued into eight schools.

 

Schools will host an event on National Parent/Family Involvement Day, Thursday, November 17, 2016, to honor the powerful contributions that families make to support the success of students and to reinforce awareness of the invaluable role that families play in supporting student learning and public education, and honor their contributions to student success.

 

In addition to recognizing the role families play as invaluable allies in student success, secondary goals of National Parent/Family Involvement Day include identifying family and community leaders whose efforts will help schools increase family engagement, building strong partnerships linked to student learning.
