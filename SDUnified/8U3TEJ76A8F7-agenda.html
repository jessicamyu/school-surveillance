BACKGROUND:  Communications Crossroads, Inc. will provide 30 full-day professional development workshops during the term of this agreement to school principals, general and special education teachers, paraprofessionals, occupational therapists, speech-language therapists, psychologists and family members.  Each workshop will include a critical review of the contemporary neuroscience in Autism Spectrum Disorders and the impact of these “hard–wired” learning differences on active engagement and behavior in the classroom setting.  Longitudinal and developmental research will then be reviewed to ensure that multidisciplinary teams are confident in selecting IEP goals that are predictive of positive long-term outcomes correlated with achievements in academics, social-emotional well-being, and vocational success.

 

Participants in each workshop will also be introduced to the Social Communication Emotional Regulation Transactional Supports (SCERTS) Model, a comprehensive, multidisciplinary educational approach designed for children with Autism Spectrum Disorders (ASD).  This model is not exclusive of other treatment approaches and methodologies, but rather provides a framework for those who are seeking guidelines for implementing a comprehensive educational plan that is based on our knowledge of the core developmental challenges faced by children with ASD, family-centered care, and our knowledge of the recommended tenets of educational programming.  The framework was designed to provide guidelines for helping children progress through the stages of becoming a competent social communicator.  It was also designed to provide families and educational teams with the help they may need to feel successful in supporting the child and to prevent behavior that interferes with learning and engagement. 

 

Video examples will be integrated to illustrate how the SCERTS practice principles can be applied to ensure that curriculum modifications and learning supports are implemented to ensure that students are: 1) maintaining active engagement in everyday activities, 2) making smooth and independent transitions across natural routines, and 3) expressing their emotion and using coping strategies in a conventional manner.

 

Emotional regulation and social communication are the core challenges for children with autism spectrum disorders, and for their teachers and families.  Teachers and other service providers who select and systematically use a range of evidence-based and effective instructional and interpersonal supports in the classroom significantly enhance access to the curriculum as they relate to the educational achievements and academic success for these students. 

 

Emily Rubin, MS, CCC-SLP is the director of Communication Crossroads, a private practice in Carmel, California. She is a speech-language pathologist specializing in autism, Asperger’s Syndrome, and related social learning disabilities. As a former adjunct faculty member and lecturer at Yale University, she served as a member of their Autism and Developmental Disabilities Clinic.  She has also served as an instructor for the Communication Sciences and Disorders Department of Emerson College in Boston, Massachusetts where she has developed courses to prepare graduate level students for addressing the needs of children with autism and their families. In addition to co-authoring the SCERTS Model, her publications have focused on early identification of autism, contemporary intervention models, and programming guidelines for high functioning autism and Asperger’s Syndrome.  She recently participated as a member of the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association’s Ad Hoc Committee on Autism Spectrum Disorders, a committee charged with developing guidelines related to the role of speech-language pathologists in the diagnosis, assessment, and treatment of ASD.  She lectures internationally and provides consultation to educational programs serving children and adolescents with autism and related developmental disorders.

In order to ensure that naming recognition rights in the PHAME Center are efficiently processed through a single entity working to benefit the PHHS Cluster and the district, the district under this agreement, agrees that CMCF shall have the exclusive right as a fundraising organization to solicit and negotiate naming recognition rights to the PHAME Center and its related facilities, grounds and programs.  Under the agreement, the Board of Education and PHHS will retain final approval of any particular naming recognition associated with the PHAME Center for exterior facilities, grounds, areas and programs (as identified in Exhibit B), and CMCF agrees that donations for such exterior facilities or programs may be solicited by CMCF but not accepted without the approval of the district.

 

Administrative Procedure 3300 outlines the district policies and procedures for naming schools and school facilities. The procedure states that anyone may submit a suggestion for a school or facility name. The requestor must provide evidence of support from the community and school staff for the naming, get approval from the family if the naming request is for a deceased individual, and submit a brief biography and description of why the individual is deserving of having a school/facility named after him or her.  No corporate donors (e.g., Pepsi, Coca Cola, and Nike) shall be able to purchase naming rights at any district school or facility. The information provided by the requestor(s) is reviewed by the 12-member School Names Committee, which makes a recommendation to approve or not approve the request.
