RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution calling for full and fair funding of California's Public Schools.

 

 

BACKGROUND: California has the sixth largest economy in the world, and the largest Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of any State in the nation. Despite California’s leadership in the global economy, the State falls in the nation’s bottom quintile on nearly every measure of public K-12 school funding and school staffing. California ranks 45th nationally in the percentage of taxable income spent on education, 41st in per-pupil funding, 45th in pupil-teacher ratios and 48th in pupil-staff ratios. K-12 school funding has not substantially increased, on an inflation-adjusted basis, for more than a decade. Under Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF), State funding for K-12 schools has only recently returned to levels predating the Great Recession of 2007. The modest revenue increases since the implementation of LCFF have been eroded by rapidly increasing costs for health care, pensions, transportation and utilities. 58 percent of California’s public school students are eligible for free and reduced-price lunch – 13 percent above the national average – and 23 percent of California students are English Learners, more than twice the national average. California’s investment in public schools is out of alignment with its wealth, its ambitions, its demographics and the demands of a 21st Century education. In 2007, a bipartisan group of California leaders commissioned a report titled Getting Down to Facts, which stated it would take an additional $17 billion annually to meet the State Board of Education achievement targets for K-12 schools. In 2016, a California School Boards Association (CSBA) report, California’s Challenge: Adequately Funding Education in the 21st Century, updated the Getting Down to Facts data and determined that, adjusting for inflation, an additional $22 billion to $40 billion annually would be required to provide all public school students with access to a high-quality education. California funds schools at roughly $1,961 per student less than the national average, which translates to approximately $3,462 per student with adjusted for California being a high-cost State. California trails the average of the top 10 States by almost $7,000 in per-pupil funding. In Robles-Wong v. State of California, a group of plaintiffs led by CSBA argued that California’s school funding system violated Article IX of the State Constitution by denying all students access to an education that prepares them for economic security and full participation in our democratic institutions. The California Supreme Court declined to hear the case by a 4-3 margin, prompting Justice Goodwin H. Liu to write, “It is regrettable that this court, having recognized education as a fundamental right in a landmark decision 45 years ago Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584], should now decline to address the substantive meaning of that right.” In order to prepare our students for participation in a democratic society and an increasingly competitive, technology-driven global economy, California must fund schools at a level sufficient to support student success. Despite its vast wealth, California has consistently underfunded public education while widening its scope, adding new requirements and raising standards without providing appropriate resources to prepare all students for college, career and civic life. If California is to close opportunity and achievement gaps and create a public school system that offers consistently high levels of education, the State must provide schools with the resources to meet the needs of their specific populations.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the proposed 2019 Summer School Program and Extended School Year as described below. 

 

It is proposed that a 2019 Summer School program be offered to general education students in Grades 9-12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects).The program will be offered at an estimated nine high school sites, to be located Districtwide. Approximately 5,000 students in Grades 9-12 receive F grades in core subjects at the first semester, which would render them eligible for the program. Proposed program dates are June 17, 2019 through July 26, 2019. Transportation will not be provided.

 

The California Code of Regulations Section 3043(f) requires that Extended School Year (ESY) services be provided for each individual with exceptional needs who has unique needs, and requires special education and related services in excess of the regular academic year. Such individuals have disabilities, which are likely to continue indefinitely or for a prolonged period; interruption of the pupil’s educational programming may cause regression, when coupled with limited recoupment capacity, rendering it impossible or unlikely that the pupil will attain the level of self-sufficiency and independence that would otherwise be expected in view of his or her disabling condition. The lack of clear evidence of such factors may not be used to deny an individual an ESY program if the student’s Individual Education Program (IEP) team determines the need for such a program and includes ESY in the IEP. The proposed program for high school students is June 17, 2019 through July 26, 2019, and for elementary and middle school students is July 24, 2019 through August 18, 2019. Transportation services will be provided.

 

It is proposed that summer programs for both general education students and students with disabilities be offered at Districtwide sites. Tentative high school sites are: Clairemont, Crawford, Garfield, iHigh, Lincoln, Morse, San Diego High, Serra, and Twain. Due to major construction projects in the district, all final host Summer School and ESY sites have not yet been selected. Final sites will be determined after working with Physical Plant Operations and Facilities, Planning and Construction to ensure there are no conflicts, in collaboration with the Area Superintendents, and in consultation with the Physical Plant Operations and Transportation Departments, taking into consideration actual geographic patterns of ESY eligible students.

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning (Vision 2020 Quality Indicator and corresponding LCAP Goal 3). This report will focus on English Learners and Students with Disabilities.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destination:
Quality Teaching:   Effective teaching requires careful crafting of learning communities built on trust and respect, as well as routines, expectations, resources, and strategies that support diverse students' intellectual engagement in learning.  Effective teaching happens through thoughtful planning focused on defined outcomes for student learning and leveraging of teachable moments. Effective teaching is revealed in the ways in which teachers respond to the strengths and needs of individual students while engaging and supporting all students in their learning.  Effective teaching is a form of inquiry wherein teachers use evidence and analysis of students' strengths and struggles, and their own performance, to guide their practice in support of student learning. Effective teaching relies on engagement within a professional learning community. Teachers in California have a professional responsibility to provide students with safe and caring learning environments, where students' differences are celebrated and supported, and they acquire the knowledge, skills, strategies, and concepts they will need for successful participation in an increasingly technological and global society. Teachers are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.

 

Quality Leadership:  Schools have a clear rationale, vision, and high expectations for achievement and immediacy for change in a positive and productive learning environment.  School leaders collaborate with and motivate the school community providing support, sharing leadership, paying attention to the work and magnifying best practices using the appreciative inquiry lens.  Schools know students' learning needs and can recognize if instruction is improving student learning through classroom visits and analyzing data focused on student achievement. School leaders monitor belief systems and teach the importance of embracing diversity and respecting all cultures.  The school community understands why change is important and collectively acts to ensure that all students have a quality education. Leaders positively influence the attitudes of students, staff, parents, and the community in setting high expectations for all students. Leaders bring systemic change towards a culture that embraces diversity, respects all cultures, and ensures the development and implementation of educational programs that maximize academic achievement of all students.  Leaders are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.

Professional Learning:  Schools hold professional learning among their top priorities for the school community.  They recognize that universal high expectations for all students require ambitious and continual improvements in curriculum, instruction, assessment, leadership practices and support systems.  These improvements require effective professional learning to expand educators' knowledge, skills, practices, and dispositions. Schools demand effective professional learning focused on substantive results for themselves, their colleagues, and their students.  Schools artfully combine a deep understanding of and cultural responsiveness to the community they serve with high expectations and support for adult learning in order to achieve school and school system goals. They embed professional learning into the organization's vision by communicating that it is a core function for improvement and by establishing and maintaining a public and persistent focus on educator professional learning.

 

 

LCAP Goal 3:  Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.
 

BACKGROUND: At regular, public board meetings, Superintendent Marten provides a report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services, and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation. The complete LCAP document is available on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

RECOMMENDATION: Accept and approve the external audit of the District's Propositions S and Z Financial and Performance Audit Reports for fiscal year 2017-18, as prepared by Crowe Horwath, LLP.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: On November 15, 2018, the audit results were shared with the Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee (ICOC).

 

BACKGROUND: On November 4, 2008, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition S, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,100,000,000.00. On November 6, 2012, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition Z, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,800,000,000.00.

 

The passage of Proposition 39 in November 2000, amended the California Constitution to include accountability provisions. Specifically, the District must conduct an annual independent performance audit to ensure that funds have been expended only on the specific projects listed in the bond project list, as well as an annual independent financial audit of the proceeds from the sale of the bond until all of the proceeds have been expended for facilities projects.

 

The results of the Fiscal Year 2017-18 Performance and Financial Audits were shared by Crowe Horwath, LLP staff with the full Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee on November 15, 2018.

 

In its financial audit, Crowe Horwath, LLP found that the District’s financial statements "present fairly, in all material respects, the respective financial position of the San Diego Unified School District Proposition S General Obligation Bonds and Proposition Z General Obligation Bonds activity as of June 30, 2018, and the respective changes in financial position thereof for the year then ended in accordance with accounting principles generally accepted in the United States of America." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on the financial statements of a school bond program.

 

In its performance audit, Crowe Horwath, LLP found that "in all significant respects, San Diego Unified School District met the objectives listed . . . and expended Proposition S and Z General Obligation Bond funds for the year ended June 30, 2018, only for the specific projects developed by the District's Governing Board and approved by the voters, in accordance with the requirements of Proposition 39, as specified by Section 1(b)(3)(C) of Article XIIIA of the California Constitution." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on an operational performance of a school bond program.

 

For the tenth consecutive year, the District's capital bond program has received the highest opinion auditors can offer for both its financial and performance audits. For the fifth consecutive year, the auditors made no adverse findings, a significant achievement for a bond program of this size and complexity.
