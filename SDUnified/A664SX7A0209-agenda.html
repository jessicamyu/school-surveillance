1. Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood - Report on Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning (LCAP Goal 3)
Meeting
Dec 13, 2016 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 P.M.
Type
Information

RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning (Vision 2020 Quality Indicator and corresponding LCAP Goal 3).  This report will focus on implementation of the teacher pipeline, teacher effectiveness, leadership development and teacher career pathways.

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destinations:

  • Quality Teaching:   Effective teaching requires careful crafting of learning communities built on trust and respect, as well as routines, expectations, resources, and strategies that support diverse students' intellectual engagement in learning.  Effective teaching happens through thoughtful planning focused on defined outcomes for student learning and leveraging of teachable moments.  Effective teaching is revealed in the ways in which teachers respond to the strengths and needs of individual students while engaging and supporting all students in their learning.  Effective teaching is a form of inquiry wherein teachers use evidence and analysis of students' strengths and struggles, and their own performance, to guide their practice in support of student learning.  Effective teaching relies on engagement within a professional learning community.  Teachers in California have a professional responsibility to provide students with safe and caring learning environments, where students' differences are celebrated and supported, and they acquire the knowledge, skills, strategies, and concepts they will need for successful participation in an increasingly technological and global society.  Teachers are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.  
     
  • Quality Leadership:  Schools have a clear rationale, vision, and high expectations for achievement and immediacy for change in a positive and productive learning environment.  School leaders collaborate with and motivate the school community providing support, sharing leadership, paying attention to the work and magnifying best practices using the appreciative inquiry lens.  Schools know students' learning needs and can recognize if instruction is improving student learning through classroom visits and analyzing data focused on student achievement.  School leaders monitor belief systems and teach the importance of embracing diversity and respecting all cultures.  The school community understands why change is important and collectively acts to ensure that all students have a quality education.  Leaders positively influence the attitudes of students, staff, parents, and the community in setting high expectations for all students.  Leaders bring systemic change towards a culture that embraces diversity, respects all cultures, and ensures the development and implementation of educational programs that maximize academic achievement of all students.  Leaders are personally aware of the role race plays in perpetuating a system of bias, prejudice, and inequity.  
     
  • Professional Learning:  Schools hold professional learning among their top priorities for the school community.  They recognize that universal high expectations for all students require ambitious and continual improvements in curriculum, instruction, assessment, leadership practices and support systems.  These improvements require effective professional learning to expand educators' knowledge, skills, practices, and dispositions.  Schools demand effective professional learning focused on substantive results for themselves, their colleagues, and their students.  Schools artfully combine a deep understanding of and cultural responsiveness to the community they serve with high expectations and support for adult learning in order to achieve school and school system goals.  They embed professional learning into the organization's vision by communicating that it is a core function for improvement and by establishing and maintaining a public and persistent focus on educator professional learning.

LCAP Goal 3:  Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:

 

3.1.  The size, stability, and quality of the teacher applicant pool will be increased through improved outreach and support for multiple pathways into teaching, by:  

  • Human Resource Services staff attending national recruitment events hosted by historically black colleges and universities and bilingual educators events.
  • Strengthening university partnerships to ensure early access to high-quality, diverse graduates and intern-eligible students and hosting and participating in local hiring events with university partners to interview and hire these candidates.  
  • Offering early employment contracts to high-quality, diverse candidates in hard-to-staff areas (Math, Science, and Special Education).  
  • Implementing hiring processes that ensure the provision of employment information to candidates in a timely manner.  
  • Continuing to build partnerships with military institutions to hire veterans and their spouses into the fields of teaching and educational support.  
  • Exploring partnerships and programs that support the development of apprenticeship and internship opportunities for district students to enter the field of education.  
  • Regularly monitoring potential barriers to initial and continued employment of diverse candidates.  
  • Continuing to convene the Teacher Pipeline Advisory Committee to monitor and analyze data to determine needs of candidates in all stages and programs of the pipeline; identify data tools and collection responsibilities; collaborating with outside partners on input and analysis; disseminating and using data to improve recruitment efforts.  
  • Engaging in partnerships with other large urban districts to promote the field of education and work toward national solutions to the teacher shortage and developing a purposeful plan to market education as a premier profession.  
  • Collaborating with district departments to build early interest in, and capacity for, teaching by strengthening existing pre-collegiate programs and recruiting district students to the field of education.  
  • Working with universities to assign student teachers to inner-city schools.

3.2.1. District support for beginning teachers and interns, and peer assistance to effectively teach diverse student groups will be provided.  

3.2.2. A beginning teacher induction program will be provided.  

3.2.3. A Peer Assistance and Review (PAR) program will be provided.  

3.2.4. The district will collaborate and partner with universities for developing teachers including interns and student teachers. 

3.4.5. The district, in collaboration with the San Diego Education Association (SDEA) will develop a professional growth system for educators.

  • During the 2016-17 school year, schools will have the opportunity to prototype different aspects of a growth system.  
  • Principals and association representatives will work together to study growth models, and communicate with and support staff as the new system is created.  

Related LCAP Metrics:  Percentage of fully credentialed teachers, rate of teacher miss-assignments.

 

BACKGROUND:  At each regular, public board meeting, Superintendent Marten provides a report that includes the alignment of Vision 2020, the 12 Quality Indicators, and supporting LCAP actions, services, and baseline data.  Following the Superintendent's Vision 2020/LCAP report, cluster and advisory groups have the opportunity to engage in dialogue on the report and provide input and feedback on current LCAP implementation.  The complete LCAP document is available on the district website at https://www.sandiegounified.org/what-lcap.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: On November 17, 2016, the audit results were shared with the Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee (ICOC).

 

BACKGROUND: On November 4, 2008, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition S, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,100,000,000. On November 6, 2012, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition Z, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,800,000,000.

 

The passage of Proposition 39 in November 2000 amended the California Constitution to include accountability provisions. Specifically, the district must conduct an annual independent performance audit to ensure that funds have been expended only on the specific projects listed in the bond project list, as well as an annual independent financial audit of the proceeds from the sale of the bond until all of the proceeds have been expended for facilities projects.

 

The results of the fiscal year 2015-16 Performance and Financial Audits were shared by Crowe Horwath with the Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee on November 17, 2016. 

 

In its financial audit, Crowe Horwath found that the district’s financial statements "present fairly, in all material respects, the financial position of the San Diego Unified School District Proposition S General Obligation Bonds and Proposition Z General Obligation Bonds activity as of June 30, 2016, and the respective changes in financial position for the year then ended in accordance with accounting principles generally accepted in the United States of America." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on the financial statements of a school bond program.

 

In its performance audit, Crowe Horwath found that "in all significant respects, San Diego Unified School District met the objectives listed . . . and expended Proposition S & Z Bond funds for the year ended June 30, 2016 on for the specific projects developed by the District's Governing Board and approved by the voters, in accordance with the requirements of California Proposition 39, as specified by Section 1(b)(3)(C) of Article XIIIA of the California Constitution." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on an operational performance of a school bond program." 

 

For the seventh consecutive year, the district's capital bond program has received the highest opinion auditors can offer for both its financial and performance audits.  For the third consecutive year, the auditors made no adverse findings, a significant achievement for a bond program of this size and complexity.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  San Diego Unified School District in partnership with UCSD Extension will begin promoting and awarding achievement by issuing digital badges. These badges will recognize accomplishments like UC a-g completion, Dual Enrollment, College, Career, and Technical Education Pathway completion, Passing Advanced Placement (AP) and Smarter Balanced Exams, Grade Point Average, Attendance, and much more. Schools and courses of study (e.g. Biology, Algebra, or Automotive) will also be able to create badges to capture behaviors, skills, and competencies that are not always apparent in a letter grade. For example, these badges or micro credentials can indicate community service, or proficiency in CPR, biomedical lab techniques, automotive repair knowledge, etc. The badging system will be backed by UCSD, reviewed by local business partners and certain badges (micro-credentials) will be recorded on UCSD extension transcripts. We believe these badges will support students as they enter the post-secondary world of college and career by capturing what students can do, as well as what they know.

 

Badges are digital tokens that appear as icons or logos on a web page or other online venue. Awarded by institutions, organizations, groups, or individuals, badges signify accomplishments such as completion of a project, mastery of a skill, or marks of experience. Proponents suggest that these credentials herald a fundamental change in the way society recognizes learning and achievement—shifting from a traditional books-and-lecture pedagogy to a model with multiple knowledge streams, including new media, collaboration, interest-based learning, and project-based learning.  As records of achievement, badges can recognize the completion of projects within a traditional college program or acknowledge experience gained through community efforts, online learning venues, or work-related projects.

BACKGROUND: The City of San Diego is implementing a new technology to combat gun violence with the use of ShotSpotter. This is a gunshot detection system that the San Diego Police Department (SDPD) would like to use to improve safety in the communities of southeastern San Diego, which include communities where district schools are located and where many district students live.  ShotSpotter utilizes a network of specialty sensors to detect the sound of a gunshot, accurately identify the location of the gunshot, and quickly communicate this information to police patrol vehicles.

 

SDPD is requesting to locate six ShotSpotter sensors on district facilities to improve the coverage area of the ShotSpotter system.  The locations of the sensors will be determined in further discussion with SDPD.  The sensors are small and unobtrusive, with negligible impact to district facilities. The sensors are acoustic devices that are tuned to, and only activate by, loud impulsive sounds.  The system does not infringe upon personal privacy as there is no live listening nor the opportunity to listen in on or record conversations.

 

The ShotSpotter system is for use by SDPD and there is no connection or feed to the San Diego Unified Police Department.  In the event of a gunshot near a school, school police would be notified by SDPD through normal police communication channels, just as they are now.
