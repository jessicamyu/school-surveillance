BACKGROUND: Through 2007, the San Diego Unified School District offered a Summer School Program to a percentage of general education students in Grades 1-8 who performed below and significantly below grade level based on teacher judgment, instructional level, and assessment data; general education students in Grades 9-12 that needed to make up core courses to graduate.

 

The summer school budget was based on an estimate of the cost of operating the planned program and an estimate of the revenue that could be generated through student attendance.  Summer school funds came to the district as a reimbursement after summer school was completed. 

 

In 2008, the state changed its funding structure for supplemental hourly programs and no longer “reimbursed” districts for summer school based on hourly student attendance.  Districts began receiving Tier III supplemental hourly program funding based on their 2007-08 hourly earnings, less approximately 20 percent. 

 

Since 2009, when the state suspended all restrictions on Tier III funding and allowed districts to use the funds for any educational program or purpose, funds that had in previous years been earmarked for summer school have been absorbed into the district’s general operating fund to help balance the budget. 

 

In 2009 and 2010, summer school was offered to general education students in Grades 9-12 for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.  The general education program was paid for using a combination of Title I and American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) categorical funding. Transportation was no longer provided for general education students. 

 

In 2011, summer school was offered only to Grade 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy

 

In 2012, summer school was offered only to Grade 11 and 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.

 

In 2013, summer school was offered only to Grades 9- 12 general education students for credit recovery and to general education students in Grades 1, 3, and 8 per the district’s retention policy.

 

In 2014 and 2015, summer school was offered only to Grades 9- 12 general education students for credit recovery. The general education program was paid for out of Title 1 Funds.

 

Changes which led us to not offer summer school to students at grades 1, 3, and 8:  In 2013-14, changes to the state assessment system with the passage of AB484 and the transition to the Common Core State Standards, created a situation where CST and district benchmark data required to make an informed Promotion/Retention determination were not available. Coupled with the limited funds available for a summer program, summer school at the retention Grades of 1, 3 and 8 was not offered during the 2014 summer school and will not be offered during the 2016 summer school. The office of Leadership and Learning will meet with secondary principals and head counselors to ensure they are identifying those students who are at risk and working to provide supports they will need to be successful in the high school setting.

 

Proposed 2016 Summer School Program:  It is proposed that a 2016 Summer School program be offered to general education students in Grades 9 through 12 for credit recovery (D and F make-up in core subjects). We estimate approximately 5,000 students will attend summer school. 2015 we had approximately 4923 students attend summer school. 2014 we had approximately 3511 students attend summer school. Transportation will not be provided.

 

A proposed one-day bridging program would be also be offered for incoming Grade 9 students.  The program would be offered at every high school to help students transition from middle to senior high school.  Approximately 7,217 students entering Grade 9 in fall 2016 will be eligible.  Transportation will not be provided.

RECOMMENDATION:  This is a First Reading of the proposed 2016-17 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district.  This report presents historical information on the 2016-17 Title I eligibility ranking and school participation.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,400,000.00.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  The District Advisory Council (DAC) General Membership and Executive Board.  The DAC had preliminary discussion of the Title I ranking student counts on November 18, 2015, and reviewed the proposed site allocations at its meeting on December 16, 2015.  The DAC requested the opportunity to review additional ranking scenarios to be developed and shared at the January 20, 2016, DAC meeting.  In preparation for the January 20, 2016, DAC meeting, additional ranking scenarios were brought forth at the January 8, 2016, DAC Executive Board meeting.

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965, was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA).  Title I, one of several programs under ESSA, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards.  The law intends that the funds go to the districts and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty.  Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of “children in poverty” residing within the district boundary.  The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The Proposed 2016-17 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2015-16 Free and Reduced Lunch percentage of 40 percent or higher.  The total Projected 2016-17 Title I Entitlement is $36,507,059.00.  Of the total projected entitlement, the proposal recommends allocating $15,400,000 to schools (See Attachment 1).

BACKGROUND: California Education Code section 17604 allows the Board of Education to delegate certain powers to a designee. To allow for efficiency in the administration of a large urban school district, California Education Code section 17605 specifically allows a school district to delegate limited purchasing power, i.e., for purchases no greater than $86,000 (for the year 2015) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services; for purchases less than $15,000 for construction services; and for contracts, leases or other documents no greater than $86,000 (for the year 2015) for supplies, materials, apparatus, equipment and non-construction services as long as each transaction is below the competitive bidding threshold under Public Contract Code section 20111. The purchase orders, contracts, leases, or other documents are binding on the district once the employee delegated by the board has entered into those purchase orders, contracts, leases or other documents. For this reason, these types of transactions must be reviewed by the board within 60 days of the date of the transaction under Education Code section 17605. Pursuant to the authority delegated to the district's Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer on June 9, 2015, these purchase orders have been released to the respective vendors and are being submitted for review by the board at this time.

 

Thus, the district has met the requirements of Education Code section 17605 with this process of reporting out the specified purchase orders under $86,000 (for the year 2015) that are not associated with any previously-approved board contracts.
