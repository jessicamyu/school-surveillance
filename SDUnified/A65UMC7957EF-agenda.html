1. Adoption of Mathematics Placement Policy in Accordance with Senate Bill 359; BUDGETED: N/A
Meeting

Type
Action
Fiscal Impact
No
Budget Source

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt a Mathematics Placement Policy in accordance with Senate Bill 359, California Mathematics Placement Act of 2015.  SB 359 requires the governing board of a school district that serves students entering Grade 9 to adopt a fair, objective, and transparent mathematics placement policy before the beginning of the 2016-17 school year.  The policy must include the following:  

  • Systemically take into consideration multiple objective academic measures of student performance such as statewide mathematics assessments, placement tests that are aligned to state-adopted content standards in mathematics, classroom assignments and grades/report cards.  
  • Include at least one placement checkpoint within the first month of the school year to ensure accurate placement and permit re-evaluation of individual student progress.  
  • Examination of aggregate student placement data annually to ensure that students who are qualified to progress in mathematics courses based on their performance on objective academic measures are not held back in a disproportionate manner on the basis of their race, ethnicity, gender or socioeconomic background.  
  • Offer clear and timely recourse for each student and his/her parent/guardian who questions the student's placement.

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  In 2014, the district began full implementation of the Common Core State Standards that required a shift in how mathematics instruction was implemented.  In 2015, input was gathered through site conversations, cluster level and department conversations, and collaboration with a respected mathematics consultant to strategize next steps and improve the math placement process. As a result of the work in 2015, the district revised the math pathways, identified multiple measures that a site could use to determine student course placement, and created Math Readiness Assessments that would align with Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium (SBAC) and district assessment practices. The procedures and criteria put in place in 2015, provided useful guidelines for all schools and the procedures will continue for 2016-17 with only one modification—the addition of a Readiness Assessment for Advanced Integrated Math II. This additional assessment is required to bring the district math placement procedures into compliance with SB359 which requires assessments for all students entering 9th grade math.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution designating September 2016, as Attendance Awareness Month. The Superintendent and other appropriate staff will continue to provide assistance to raise public awareness on the importance of improved attendance and hereby commit to focusing on reducing chronic absenteeism to give all children an equitable opportunity to learn, grow, and thrive academically, emotionally, and socially.

 

BACKGROUND: The board recognizes that good attendance is essential to student achievement and graduation, and we are committed to enhancing the connection between school attendance and academic achievement.

 

Children who are chronically absent in preschool, kindergarten, and first grade are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade, and students who cannot read at grade level by the end of third grade are four times more likely than proficient readers to drop out of high school.

 

Chronic absence becomes, by 6th grade, a leading indicator that a student will drop out of high school, and closing the achievement gap requires a serious and sustained focus on chronically absent students due to the correlation between absenteeism and test scores, graduation, and dropout rates.

 

San Diego Unified is committed to supporting positive school attendance, as well as monitoring and addressing attendance at the first signs of chronic absence.

 

The district recognizes the importance of partnering with schools, families, and communities to create and promote a positive culture of daily attendance for all students and significantly, thus reducing chronic absenteeism by understanding and removing barriers that keep children in grades kindergarten through twelve from getting to school.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the resolution in support of divestment from thermal coal/fossil fuel companies.  The board supports STRS and PERS divestment from thermal coal, fossil fuel companies and strongly urges them to instruct their asset managers to stop any new investment in fossil-fuel companies and ensure none of its directly held or comingled assets include holdings in fossil-fuel public equities or corporate bonds; and each should provide and release quarterly updates, available to the public, detailing progress made towards full divestment, as required by Senate Bill 185 (SB 185).

 

BACKGROUND: SB 185, relating to public retirement systems was approved by the Governor on October 8, 2015; and prohibits boards of the California State Teachers' Retirement System (CalSTRS) and of the California Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS) from making new investments or receiving investments of public employee retirement funds in a thermal coal company.

 

The climate crisis is a serious threat to current and future generations here in California and around the world, and almost every government in the world has agreed that any warming above a 2 degree Celsius (3.6 degrees Fahrenheit) rise would be unsafe. We have already raised the temperature 0.8 0.87 degrees Celsius, and that has caused more damage than most scientists expected. One-third of summer sea ice in the Arctic is gone, the oceans are 26 percent more acidic, and since warm air holds more water vapor than cold, the likelihood is high for both floods and drought.

 

Proven coal, oil, and gas reserves of the fossil-fuel companies, and the countries that act like fossil-fuel companies, equals about 2,795 gigatons of carbon dioxide, or five times the amount we can release to maintain 2 degrees of warming.

 

California teachers as members of CalSTRS and state and school district employees as members of CalPERS contribute a collective wealth in the tens of billions of dollars, and that collective wealth should not be used to undermine the future of the very students and citizens they devote their professional lives to.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve proposed 2017-18 instructional calendars as recommended by the Superintendent and Instructional Calendar Committee.  The proposed calendars include 180 instructional days, with a pre-Labor Day start date of August 28, 2017.  The recommendation of only one school year (2017-18) is in alignment with the district’s conversion to a common instructional calendar which is scheduled to be complete in fall 2018. When all schools are on the same calendar (with none on year-round schedules) many options can be considered regarding instructional calendar configurations. In fall 2016, the Instructional Calendar Committee will begin work on developing the 2018-19 and 2019-20 calendar recommendations, and plans for community and stakeholder engagement.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEES: The district's Instructional Calendar Committee includes voting representatives from the Community Advisory Committee on Special Education (CAC), District Advisory Council on Compensatory Education (DAC) and the District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC). 

 

CONSULTATION WITH ADDITIONAL STAKEHOLDER GROUPS:  The district's Instructional Calendar Committee includes voting representatives from the Associated Student Body (ASB), Cluster Representatives, Parent Teacher Association (PTA), and all bargaining units as per contract language (Administrators Association San Diego [AASD], Operations Support Services [OSS], Office-Technical and Business Services [OTBS], Paraeducators [PARA], Police Officers Association [POA], and the San Diego Educators Association [SDEA]). The Instructional Calendar Committee consists of 32 voting members, as shown on the attached roster, with equal representation of students/parents and staff. The vote on June 29, 2016, was conducted with 24 voting members present.  The result of the vote was 19 for pre-Labor Day start of school in 2017-18, and five for post-Labor Day start.

 

BACKGROUND:  The Calendar Committee considered many factors in making its 2017-18 recommendation, including:

  • The number of students taking AP classes and whether the start date of school has an impact on AP testing (starting earlier means more days to prepare)
  • Attendance did not drop in 1998-99 (the last time school started pre-Labor Day)
  • Of the 42 school districts in San Diego County, only three began post-Labor Day in 2015-16 (Borrego Springs, Mountain Empire, and San Diego Unified)
  • Alignment of earlier school start with fall athletics season
  • Alignment of earlier school start with San Diego Community College District
  • Weather – there are hot days in both August and September – and the district’s continuing air conditioning installation
  • Transcript and grade processing timelines are improved when school ends earlier
  • It would be confusing to parents and stakeholders to revert to a post-Labor Day start in 2017-18, since the 2016-17 start date is pre-Labor Day

Previous Board Action:

  • On October 8, 2013, the board directed staff to consistently schedule Spring Break to the week that includes March 31. This is in alignment with the San Diego Community College District.
  • On October 28, 2014, and December 9, 2014, the board heard as a first and second reading the Calendar Committee’s and Superintendent's recommendation for the 2015-16 and 2016-17 Instructional Calendars.  The Calendar Committee’s and Superintendent's recommendation was to begin both the 2015-16 and 2016-17 school years pre-Labor Day. This recommendation was made in part because Labor Day in 2015 was very late (September 7). The board rejected the recommendation and voted to begin the 2015-16 school year post-Labor Day (September 8, 2015) and directed that staff conduct additional public review of the proposal to begin the 2016-17 school year pre-Labor Day (August 29, 2016).  This action was in response to public feedback that the district had not provided sufficient notification to families about the recommendation to begin pre-Labor Day in 2015-16.  The district has started school post-Labor Day for over 20 years, with the exception of the 1998-99 school year. The board also voted to ensure that the last instructional day of the school year is not on a Monday. 
  • On June 9, 2015, information was provided to the board on the outreach efforts that had transpired over the previous six months to ensure that stakeholders had the opportunity to provide input on the district’s proposal to begin school pre-Labor Day in the 2016-17 school year.  District staff engaged the community to ensure that stakeholders were informed and understood the rationale behind the proposed change, made presentations to stakeholders including district advisory committees, cluster groups, the San Diego Unified Council of PTAs, Project Ujima, and others.  The information was also available on individual school websites, on the Principals’ Page and was included in the district’s electronic newsletter, Unified Vision.  Stakeholders were asked to provide comments on feedback forms that were collected at meetings and online. While not a formal survey, this feedback mechanism provided mixed results, with no clear preference for starting pre- or post-Labor Day being identified. The board approved the Calendar Committee’s and Superintendent's recommendation that school begin pre-Labor Day in 2016-17 (August 29, 2016).

BACKGROUND:

 

Proposed Project

In alignment with the recommendations of the district’s Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee, photovoltaic (PV) system installation projects have been initiated to create an energy efficient and renewable power source, delivering a significant cost savings to the district. Eight project sites have been selected and design-build contractors have been selected.

 

The Project involves the installation of a solar power generation system on the existing school site. The solar power generation system would consist of photovoltaic (PV) arrays mounted onto canopy structures in existing graded and disturbed areas within the campus, such as parking lots and decomposed granite areas.  All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. The Project would be designed to ensure that all existing Americans with Disabilities Act accessible paths of travel are maintained or reconfigured as prescribed by code.  Electrical transmission lines would be installed in an underground conduit and would extend from the combiner to the main electrical service in one of the existing school buildings.  All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

 

New lighting would be installed under the proposed canopies to provide a similar amount and quality of lighting as the existing lighting on the campus. All new lighting would be down-shielded to prevent any light spillover offsite in accordance with City of San Diego regulations on glare and outdoor lighting (Municipal Code sections 142.0730 and 142.0740). In addition, the new lighting would be connected to an existing lighting timer, which extinguishes all non-essential lighting on the campus generally by 10:00 p.m.

 

Environmental Review

The Project is subject to the CEQA review.  Based on the findings of an Initial Study, staff determined that the Project is exempt from further environmental analysis under four categorical exemption classes as set forth below and as further described in the attached NOE:

 

15303(e). New Construction or Conversion of Small Structures (Class 3)

Class 3 consists of construction and location of limited numbers of new, small facilities or structures; installation of small new equipment and facilities in small structures; and the conversion of existing small structures from one use to another where only minor modifications are made in the exterior of the structure. The numbers of structures described in this section are the maximum allowable on any legal parcel.

 

15303(e) Accessory (appurtenant) structures including garages, carports, patios, swimming pools, and fences.

 

The proposed canopy structures would be located in the existing developed or previously disturbed areas of the campus, and would serve as carports/shade structures if installed in existing parking lots and/or decomposed granite areas on the campus.

 

15304(f). Minor Alterations to Land (Class 4)

Class 4 consists of minor public or private alterations in the condition of land, water, and/or vegetation which do not involve removal of healthy, mature, scenic trees except for forestry or agricultural purposes, including grading on land with a slope of less than ten percent and minor trenching and backfilling where the surface is restored.

 

15304 (f)  Minor trenching and backfilling where the surface is restored.

 

Construction of the Project would potentially require minor trenching for the electrical transmission lines, which would be backfilled and restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.    

 

15311. Accessory Structures (Class 11)

Class 11 consists of construction, or placement of minor structure accessory to (appurtenant to) existing commercial, industrial, or institutional facilities.

 

The proposed canopy structures would be located in the existing developed or previously disturbed areas of the campus, and would serve as carports/shade structures if installed in existing parking lots and/or decomposed granite areas on the campus.

 

15314. Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14)

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption.

 

The Project would not add new classrooms or other facilities on the existing campus and would therefore not increase the existing capacity of the school site.

 

Categorical exemptions may not be relied upon if the application of the exemption triggers any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2. Based on the Initial Study, staff has determined that no exception applies to prohibit application of the categorical exemptions. Approval of this item approves the Project and authorizes and directs staff to file the NOE with the San Diego County Recorder/Clerk and California Office of Planning and Research State Clearinghouse.

BACKGROUND:

 

Proposed Project

In alignment with the recommendations of the district’s Environmental Sustainability Advisory Committee, photovoltaic (PV) system installation projects have been initiated to create an energy efficient and renewable power source, delivering a significant cost savings to the district. Eight project sites have been selected and design-build contractors have been solicited.

 

The Project involves the installation of a solar power generation system on the existing school site. The solar power generation system would consist of PV arrays mounted onto canopy structures in existing graded and disturbed areas within the campus, such as parking lots and decomposed granite fields. All proposed work would occur entirely within the boundaries of the existing school property. The solar PV arrays would generally be supported by metal cantilever frames anchored in concrete pier foundations. The individual solar PV panels would be constructed of dark-colored (usually blue or black) materials and covered with anti-reflective coatings. The Project would be designed to ensure that all existing Americans with Disabilities Act accessible paths of travel are maintained or reconfigured as prescribed by code. Electrical transmission lines would be installed in an underground conduit and would extend from the combiner to the main electrical service in one of the existing school buildings. All affected hardscape and/or landscape areas would be restored to pre-project conditions once construction is complete.

 

New lighting would be installed under the proposed canopies to provide a similar amount and quality of lighting as the existing lighting on the campus. All new lighting would be down-shielded to prevent any light spillover offsite in accordance with City of San Diego regulations on glare and outdoor lighting (Municipal Code sections 142.0730 and 142.0740).

 

Environmental Review

The Project is subject to CEQA review.  Based on the findings of an Initial Study, staff determined that the Project is exempt from further environmental analysis under one statutory exemption and four categorical exemption classes as set forth below and as further described in the attached NOE:

 

Public Resources Code section 21080.35

Public resources Code section 21080.35 provides the following statutory exemption that would be applicable to the installation of any solar photovoltaic arrays within existing parking lots.

 

     (a) Except as provided in subdivision (d), this division does not apply to the installation of a solar energy system on the roof of an existing building or at an existing parking lot.

 

(b) For the purposes of this section, the following terms mean the following:

(1) “Existing parking lot” means an area designated and used for parking of vehicles as of the time of the application for the solar energy system and for at least the previous two years.

(2) “Solar energy system” includes all associated equipment. Associated equipment consists of parts and materials that enable the generation and use of solar electricity or solar-heated water, including any monitoring and control, safety, conversion, and emergency responder equipment necessary to connect to the customer’s electrical service or plumbing and any equipment, as well as any equipment necessary to connect the energy generated to the electrical grid, whether that connection is onsite or on an adjacent parcel of the building and separated only by an improved right-of-way. “Associated equipment” does not include a substation.

 

(c) (1) Associated equipment shall be located on the same parcel of the building, except that associated equipment necessary to connect the energy generated to the electrical grid may be located immediately adjacent to the parcel of the building or immediately adjacent to the parcel of the building and separated only by an improved right-of-way.

(2) Associated equipment shall not occupy more than 500 square feet of ground surface and the site of the associated equipment shall not contain plants protected by the Native Plant Protection Act (Chapter 10 (commencing with Section 1900) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code).

 

(d) This section does not apply if the associated equipment would otherwise require one of the following:

(1) An individual federal permit pursuant to Section 401 or 404 of the federal Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Sec. 1341 or 1344) or waste discharge requirements pursuant to the Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act (Division 7 (commencing with section 13000) of the Water Code).

(2) An individual take permit for species protected under the federal Endangered Species Act of 1973 (16 U.S.C. Sec. 1531 et seq.) or the California Endangered Species Act (Chapter 1.5 (commencing with Section 2050) of Division 3 of the Fish and Game Code).

(3) A streambed alteration permit pursuant to Chapter 6 (commencing with section 1600) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code.

 

(e) This section does not apply if the installation of a solar energy system at an existing parking lot involves either of the following:

(1) The removal of a tree required to be planted, maintained, or protected pursuant to local, state, or federal requirements, unless the tree dies and there is no requirement to replace the tree.

(2) The removal of a native tree over 25 years old.

 

(f) This section does not apply to any transmission or distribution facility or connection.

  3. The Project does not require an individual federal permit pursuant to section 401 or 404 of the federal Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. Sec. 1341 or 1344) or waste discharge requirements pursuant to the Porter-Cologne Water Quality Control Act (Division 7 (commencing with Section 13000) of the Water Code);
  4. The Project does not require an individual take permit for species protected under the federal Endangered Species Act of 1973 (16 U.S.C. Sec. 1531 et seq.) or the California Endangered Species Act (Chapter 1.5 (commencing with section 2050) of Division 3 of the Fish and Game Code);
  5. The Project does not require a streambed alteration permit pursuant to Chapter 6 (commencing with section 1600) of Division 2 of the Fish and Game Code;
  6. The Project does not require the removal of any trees that were planted, maintained, or protected pursuant to local, state, or federal requirements, nor does the Project require the removal of any native tree over 25 years old; and
  7. The Project does not include the construction of a transmission or distribution facility or connection.

BACKGROUND:  The district provided transportation service for a student from the Polinsky Children’s Center to Jefferson Middle School, located in the Oceanside Unified School District.  In situations where a homeless student resides in one district and attends school in another, the two school districts are to share the expense of transporting the student.  This is typically done by each district providing a one-way trip.  In this case Oceanside Unified School District was not able to provide transportation and requested that San Diego Unified provided the transportation both to and from school.  Oceanside Unified School District agreed to compensate San Diego Unified for their share of the transportation.  

 

In accordance with the Title VII-B of the McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act, as amended by the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001, the state educational agencies (SEAs) and its local educational agencies (LEAs) must adopt policies and practices to ensure that transportation is provided, at the request of the parent or guardian (or in the case of the unaccompanied youth, the liaison) to and from the school of origin.  If the homeless child or youth continues to live in the area served by the LEA in which the school of origin is located, then that LEA must provide or arrange for the child’s or youth’s transportation to or from the school of origin. However, if a homeless child or youth transfers to a school served by another LEA, then the new LEA must agree to equally share the cost of transportation with the original LEA school of origin.
