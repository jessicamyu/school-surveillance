RECOMMENDATION: Conduct public hearing and adopt Partnership Agreements SV-17-0165-99, SV-17-0166-99, SV-17-0167-99, and SV-17-0168-99 with City College, Mesa College and Miramar College, San Diego Community College District, in alignment with AB 288 for community college courses offered on high school campuses for the 2016-17 school year.  The first reading of the partnership agreements was held on June 14, 2016.

 

In alignment with the provisions of AB 288, as approved by the Governor on October 8, 2015, community college and K-12 districts offering college courses on high school campuses must enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships that include specific language regarding the total number of high school students to be served and the total number of full-time equivalent students (FTES) projected to be claimed by the community college district for those students.  Agreement SV-17-0166-99, for classes offered by Miramar College on the campuses of Mira Mesa, Scripps Ranch, Serra, and University City High Schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreements SV-16-1543-13, SV-16-0045-14, SV-15-0789-13, SV-15-0776-13, SV-17-0094-99, and SV-17-0095-99.  Agreements SV-17-0167-99 and SV-17-0168-99, for classes offered by Mesa College on the campuses of Clairemont, Henry, Kearny, La Jolla, Madison, Mission Bay, Morse, Point Loma, Serra, and University City high schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreements SV-16-1066-99 and SV-16-1067-99.  Agreement SV-17-0165-99, for classes offered by City College on the campuses of Crawford, Garfield, Hoover, Lincoln, Morse and San Diego high schools, will supersede and replace Educational Partnership Agreement SV-15-0712-13.

 

The first amended master Memorandum of Understanding SV-14-0291-13 between the San Diego Community College District and the San Diego Unified School District remains in effect and is applicable to programs in which San Diego Unified students participate in classes on the Miramar, Mesa, or City College campuses.

 

The San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees conducted a first reading of the CCAP Agreements at its May 12, 2016 meeting and adopted the agreements at its June 9, 2016 meeting. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  AB 288 authorizes community college and K-12 districts to enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships with the goal of developing seamless pathways from high school to community college for career technical education or preparation for transfer, improving high school graduation rates, or helping high school students achieve college and career readiness.  Through dual enrollment partnerships, school districts and community college districts can create clear pathways of aligned, sequenced coursework that allows students to more easily and successfully transition to for-credit, college-level coursework leading to an associate degree, transfer to the University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) systems, or to a program leading to a career technical education certificate.  The intent of AB 288 is to offer or expand dual enrollment opportunities for students who may not already be college bound or who are underrepresented in higher education.  AB 288 was supported by both the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees and the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  The District Advisory Council (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements during the 2015-16 year.

 

BACKGROUND: As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA).  The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.  The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle.

 

The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA.  Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.  Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with its site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate its SPSA.

 

Additionally, the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process.  Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review.  Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs.

 

Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Financial Planning, Monitoring, and Accountability department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.  The local governing board must review and approve each school's SPSA.  This report brings forward for board approval the 2016-17 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 176 schools.

 

A copy of each 2016-17 SPSA, which includes the budget summaries and required signed Assurances page is available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

The Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) is an essential element of California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF).  It is a three-year, district-level plan that is updated annually.  The plan describes the school district's key goals for students as well as the actions (with expenditures) the district will take to achieve the goals and the measures used to indicate progress.  The LCAP also includes an Annual Update, which reflects on implementation of the current year's LCAP (2015-16).  The San Diego Unified School District has aligned its six LCAP goals and supporting actions and expenditures to the board-adopted Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood. 

 

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and adopt the district's proposed 2016-19 Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP).  The 2016-19 LCAP was presented to the Board of Education as a public hearing and first reading on June 14, 2016.  This is the second and final reading of the district's proposed 2016-19 LCAP.

 

SUMMARY OF CHANGES TO LCAP SINCE FIRST READING: The following changes to narrative which are included in the attached final documents, have been made to the LCAP since the First Reading on June 14, 2016.  These changes are based on stakeholder feedback and preliminary review of the document by the San Diego County Office of Education.  There are no changes to expenditures or dollar amounts.

  • Homeless students have been included in services for subgroups in Goal 1.1.b,1.2.b, 1.3.b, 1.4.b, 1.5.b, and 1.6.  
  • Professional development opportunities for paraeducators are included in Goal 1.7.3, 3.3.3, 3.5.2, and 3.5.7.

  • An additional metric has been added to Goal 5 to establish a baseline for measuring functionality of School Site Councils (SSC).

  1. Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.
  2. Access to Broad and Challenging Curriculum:  Students have equitable access to rigorous, well-rounded, standards-aligned curricula and access to and skill in applying technologies to leverage learning, assuring readiness for a full range of post-graduation options.
  3. Quality Leadership, Teaching and Learning:  Effective leadership and teaching is evident system-wide with a unifying vision that equips and empowers all stakeholders to provide optimal student learning opportunities and outcomes.
