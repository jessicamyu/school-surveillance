Related LCAP Goal 4 Actions and Services:

  • 4.1.a.1.   • 4.1.a.5.  All nurses will attend Mental Health First Aid training.

  • 4.2.a.1. The district will continue to provide health and wellness services to students.  
  • 4.2.a.2. The district will continue to implement its comprehensive wellness policy and action plan.
  • 4.2.a.3.  The district will continue to provide additional focused support for students with disabilities and significant health needs.

RECOMMENDATION: In alignment with AB 288, conduct a public hearing, second reading and adoption of the 2017-18 College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) agreements with the San Diego Community College District for college classes conducted on San Diego Unified School District high school campuses.  AB 288 requires community college and K-12 districts offering college courses on high school campuses to enter into CCAP partnerships that include specific language regarding the targeted number of high school students to be served and the total number of full-time equivalent students (FTES) projected to be claimed by the community college district for those students.  

  1. SV-18-0137-99 San Diego City College - for various classes at Crawford, Garfield, Hoover, Lincoln, Morse, San Diego high schools, and the School of Creative and Performing Arts (SCPA)  
  2. SV-18-0138-99 Mesa College Accelerated College Program (ACP) - for Mathematics and Political Science classes at Clairemont, Henry, La Jolla, Madison, Morse, Point Loma, and University City high schools
  3. SV-18-0139-99 Mesa College - for various classes at Clairemont, Henry, Kearny, La Jolla, Madison, Point Loma, Twain, and University City high schools  
  4. SV- 18-0140-99 - Miramar College - for various classes at Mira Mesa, Scripps Ranch, Serra and University City high schools 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. The San Diego Unified School District is eligible for average daily attendance (ADA) revenue for the periods each day that students are enrolled in  San Diego Unified courses, and the San Diego Community College District is eligible for apportionment for the period that students are enrolled in City, Mesa, or Miramar College courses.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  AB 288 authorizes community colleges and K-12 districts to enter into College and Career Access Pathways (CCAP) partnerships with the goal of developing pathways from high school to community college for career technical education, or preparation for transfer, improving high school graduation rates, or helping high school students achieve college and career readiness.  Through dual enrollment partnerships, school districts and community college districts can create clear pathways of aligned, sequenced coursework that allow students to more easily and successfully transition to for-credit, college-level coursework leading to an associate degree, transfer to the University of California (UC) or California State University (CSU) systems, or to a program leading to a career technical education certificate.  The intent of AB 288 is to offer or expand dual enrollment opportunities for students who may not already be college bound, or who are underrepresented in higher education.  AB 288 was supported by both the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees and the San Diego Unified School District Board of Education.  The 2017-18 CCAP agreements were presented to the San Diego Community College District Board of Trustees for first reading on June 8, 2017, and adopted on July 20, 2017.  The 2017-18 CCAP agreements were presented to the San Diego Unified Board of Education on July 11, 2017, and are submitted for final reading and adoption.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt new district-wide action level for lead in drinking water, develop a remediation plan to meet the new standard, and establish an on-going water sampling program.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $200,000 for laboratory testing over 5 years, with anticipated $40,000 expenditure in 2017/18. Remediation efforts will be accomplished using PPO maintenance funds. Budget String: 5686-81500-00-5100-8100-0000-01000-0000. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $58,000 in remediation efforts.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Testing and remediation to be performed by district staff.  

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:

In providing a status update on the lead in drinking water testing at the April 25 school board meeting, the Trustees directed the superintendent to develop a plan to address lead in drinking water samples which were above the detection limit of 5 parts per billion (ppb), but below the action level of 15 ppb, as established by the State Water Resource Control Board, which follows the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's (US EPA) rule. 

 

As a result of December 20, 2016 permit action by the State Water Resources Control Board Division of Drinking Water, all community water systems are required to test for lead in drinking water at K-12 schools when requested by the school district. This testing is to be done by collecting and analyzing up to five water samples from drinking water fountains and water faucets at a school. Our water is provided by the City of San Diego Public Utilities Department. From April 2017 to June 2017, the City carried out testing of 967 water samples from 193 school sites. All test results have been posted on the district web page at: https://www.sandiegounified.org/drinking-water-sampling-1.

 

City sampling resulted in only four samples exceeding the action level of 15 parts per billion (ppb) and only 57 samples in total with levels of lead above the detection level of 5 ppb.  The district responded to the school sites with the sample results over the action level, with remediation complete at the Emerson Elementary campus, and remediation work underway and soon to be resolved at the Birney Elementary campus.

 

In addition, the district is in the process of testing locations not authorized under the Division of Drinking Water direction, which only included K-12 schools. District staff, with the support of an independent laboratory, is testing areas not tested by the City including child development centers, preschools and administrative sites. To date, district testing identified one sample from the Montezuma Child Development Center that exceeded the action level, and remediation efforts are underway.

 

The State Water Resources Control Board, US EPA, and the Centers for Disease Control acknowledge that there is no safe level of lead in drinking water and this proposed action will align the district with US Food and Drug Administration's standard for lead in bottled drinking water, which is significantly below the EPA's lead action level. This item recommends the district establish the following long term objective for lead in drinking water:

No drinking water outlet will have a lead content above the laboratory detection level of 5 parts per billion (ppb).

 

This objective is:

  • One third of the current action level of 15 ppb;
  • Supported by our current testing – It is the detection limit for reporting as established by the State Water Resources Control Board, Division of Drinking Water;
  • Supported by our infrastructure.  In general, the City of San Diego water quality and distribution systems as well as district plumbing infrastructure, are sound;
  • 5 ppb is the bottled drinking water threshold (Food and Drug Administration).

In addition, San Diego Unified intends to remain at the forefront of this issue. As methods to identify and remediate lead continue to improve with time, staff will bring the Board — no later than 2020 — a variety of options to allow for the district action level to be lowered to 1 part per billion for possible board action.
 

To carry out this objective, staff recommends the following five actions:

  • Follow through on city test results, remediating any water outlet over 5 ppb;
  • Test all remaining water outlets across district;
  • Remediate water outlets identified by district testing;
  • Maintain flushing protocol until all testing is complete;
  • Perform quality assurance testing of water outlets into the future.

 

Follow-Through on City Testing

 

For the start of the 2017/18 school year, all water outlets with City test results over 5 ppb will be out of service or remedied. Temporary bottled water stations will be provided for those water outlets that are out of service, as needed.

 

Schools with lead detected by the 2017 City of San Diego sampling will be prioritized for remediation efforts. Sites with the younger students and with the greater number of positive samples will be first for remediation.

 

Remediation is considered complete when test results show 5 ppb or less. Remediation work includes the water outlet tested and other related outlets (located in same building, similar type of construction, etc.), so as to leave the entire campus operating with expected lead concentrations at 5 ppb or less. 

 

Testing All Remaining Water Outlets

 

There are approximately 7,200 drinking fountains and kitchen sinks used for food preparation throughout the district.

 

Perform testing of those water outlets not tested by the city. Testing to follow state protocol (same as city testing) which requires “tagout” of water outlet the night before testing to ensure water remains in line a minimum of six hours before testing. Samples will be analyzed by an independent state certified laboratory.


Because the SWRCB specifically regulates the duration of water stagnation before a water sample may be taken, the district agrees to engage with the SWRCB and CALPIRG in a review of the SWRCB’s policies and protocols.  

 

In order to produce sampling results that would reveal worst-case scenarios as CALPIRG has requested, the district will continue to tag out fixtures the day before a water sample is taken, ensuring that all samples that are taken are done so in the worst conditions available, that is water left stagnant for at least six hours. District staff will be instructed to sample, when feasible, water left stagnant for 12 hours.

 

SWRCB protocol allows for repeat and confirmation sampling for an initial result that is above the threshold, and only after the repeat/confirmation sample results are known, would the water outlet be required to be removed from service.

 

The district did not use this method in carrying out the city testing.  The district reacted and responded to the initial test results above threshold, specifically immediately removing the unit from service.  The district only retested and returned outlets to service when physical repairs were made.   

 

The district will continue this practice in this plan, remediating outlets where more than 5 ppb of lead is found in the first sample analyzed, using the remediation method most appropriate for that fixture or site. 

 

This follow-on testing process is planned for accomplishment by existing district staff (planning, managing and sample taking, with analysis by certified laboratory).  It is anticipated to take three to five years to complete. Laboratory fees are expected to be approximately $200,000 over the course of this effort. 

 

Prior to the start of city testing, the district notified parents of water quality issues when there was a risk of student exposure. Moving forward, parents will be notified if district water sampling identifies lead above the new district standard of 5 ppb. This notification requirement is three times below the notification standards proposed in new legislation being considered by Sacramento.   

 

Remediate Water Outlets Identified by District Testing

 

The City testing of 967 water outlets resulted in 57 water outlets with sample results exceeding 5 ppb. This equates to 5.9% of the results. There are no apparent correlations between the type of plumbing fixture, the age of the facility, and the level of identified lead contamination. Assuming these results are representative, testing the remaining 6,200 water outlets across the district will yield another 366 +/- water outlets with results over 5 ppb (beyond the 57 already identified by the city).  Because there is no trend in plumbing fixture or construction type where lead has been identified, the remediation effort will be a combination of:

  • Investigate and repair, replace, or decommission water outlet. Make repairs to or replace fixture and plumbing components to remove lead-bearing components from water system.
  • Installation of point of use water filters where repairs are not feasible. Installing filters creates a recurring maintenance effort and cost, which should be considered.
  • Consolidate - Fewer water outlets of a higher quality - Installation of strategically placed filtered fountains/bottle filling stations throughout San Diego Unified campuses.  Centrally located in common areas of middle and high schools. Keep drinking fountains in elementary classrooms. 

 

Maintain Flushing Protocol Until All Testing is Complete

 

Lead leaches into drinking water while water is stagnant in the pipe. Flushing the water outlet after periods of non-use is a simple method to reduce potential lead exposure. Since lead was identified in some drinking water early on in the City testing, the district implemented a drinking water flushing program as a temporary proactive measure. The program calls for daily flushing (1 minute) at the start of the school day. The drinking water outlet flushing will continue until water sampling confirms lead levels are 5 ppb or less.  Specifically, flushing will continue until all drinking water outlets on a site have a test result of 5 ppb or less. The district will coordinate our follow-on testing to assist sites in being released from daily flushing.

 

To minimize the risk of exposure to lead that might leech into stagnant water over the weekend, the district will implement a permanent district-wide flush of all water outlets used for drinking and food preparation in the morning of the first day of each week, prior to first use.

 

Flushing remains a good practice when encountering longer periods of non-use, such as returning to school after breaks, and should remain a part of our program going forward.  School sites will be reminded of the need to flush as schools return from summer break.

 

Perform quality assurance testing of water outlets into the future

 

Conduct quality assurance testing to ensure drinking water continues to meet the established goal and that our remediation efforts are effective. After initial testing is complete, randomly sample 2% of water outlets annually. The district would increase testing if results indicate levels higher than initial testing.

BACKGROUND:  In an effort to provide more park space in San Diego neighborhoods, the District, in collaboration with the City, plan to construct and operate three new joint use facilities:

  1. Gage Elementary:  7.05 acre joint use facility with fields, walking track, hardcourts, parking lot, and a drinking fountain. 
  2. Linda Vista Elementary:  2.27 acre joint use facility with fields, walking track, hardcourts, and a drinking fountain.
  3. Marvin Elementary:  3.87 acre joint use facility with fields, walking track, parking lot, and a drinking fountain.

The District will construct the Joint Use Projects.  The City will operate and maintain the joint use fields for the contract term, while the district will continue to maintain landscaped areas outside the school, all perimeter fencing, and hardscape areas. District and City staff coordinated the facility designs with each school and their community.

 

Usage of each joint use area is as follows, with any modifications being mutually agreed upon by District and City staff to accommodate each site’s specific needs:  

  • Each day school is in session, the District has exclusive use of the area 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day until 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • Each day school is in session, the City will have use of the area up to 30 minutes prior to the beginning of the school day and 30 minutes after the dismissal bell.
  • City shall have exclusive use of the area on holidays, weekends, and other days when school is not in session.

     

ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW
 

These joint use facilities (proposed project) are subject to CEQA, Public Resources Code section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The proposed project, however, is exempt from expanded environmental review as described below and, more specifically described in Attachment 4.

 

Existing Facilities (Class 1)

Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination (CEQA Guidelines §15301).

  • The proposed project includes minor alterations at each of the existing school sites, and includes the conversion of existing decomposed granite (DG) play areas to natural turf fields, installation of stabilized walking tracks, installation of drinking fountains, and the replacement and construction of new fencing. At Gage and Marvin Elementary, existing parking areas will be included in the joint use. Residential use of the facilities outside of school hours will be similar to current school usage of the fields during school hours; therefore, would be considered a negligible expansion.

     

Minor Additions to Schools (Class 14)

Class 14 consists of minor additions to existing schools within existing school grounds where the addition does not increase original student capacity by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms, whichever is less. The addition of portable classrooms is included in this exemption (CEQA Guidelines §15314).

  • The proposed project does not cause an increase in student enrollment. In addition, the proposed project does not include the construction of any new classrooms or other facilities that would increase the student capacity of any campus by more than 25 percent or ten classrooms.

     

Normal Operations of Facilities for Public Gatherings (Class 23)

Class 23 consists of the normal operations of existing facilities for public gatherings for which the facilities were designed, where there is a past history of the facility being used for the same or similar kind of purpose. Past history is defined as a similar kind of activity that has been occurring for at least three years, and that there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity would not represent a change in the operation of the facility (CEQA Guidelines §15323).

  • The proposed project includes minor alterations at each of the existing school sites and includes the conversion of existing DG play areas to natural turf fields, installation of stabilized walking tracks, installation of drinking fountains, and the replacement and construction of new fencing. At Gage and Marvin Elementary, existing parking areas will be included in the joint use. The proposed facilities have been used for recess at each site for several decades. Recess typically involves students playing several types of games and recreational activities, including basketball, soccer, and other sporting activities. The future recreational use of these areas by the general public would be substantially similar to that which currently occurs at each campus. Furthermore, the project does not represent an operational change, as each site will continue to operate as a school with the  facilities available for public recreational uses outside of school hours. Therefore, there is a reasonable expectation that the future occurrence of the activity at each site would not represent a change in the operation.   

As set forth in Attachment 4, staff has determined that no exception applies to defeat application of the categorical exemption, and approval of the proposed project would not compromise the environmental quality of the sites and surrounding areas.

BACKGROUND: The district has been grappling with a variety of structural challenges including declining enrollment, pension contribution increases, labor market pressures, and mandates from the state and federal governments. Stability can be achieved, but it requires efficiently using available resources in order to focus on student achievement; one of these efficiencies is in the creation of the Chief Business Officer. The Chief Business Officer (CBO) is responsible for planning, organizing, integrating, controlling, and directing all business services functions for Finance, Operations, IT and Facilities Planning & Construction. The CBO serves as a member of the Superintendent's Cabinet and as the District's Chief Fiscal Officer. Additionally, the incumbent will develop information and recommend policies and procedures for immediate and long-range financial, business management, and planning.
