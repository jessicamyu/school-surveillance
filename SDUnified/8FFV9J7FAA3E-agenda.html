In November 2009, the district was awarded $40 million in funding to provide technology upgrades at 76 district schools.  For Fiscal Year 2011-12, district staff applied for an additional $18 million in funding to provide technology upgrades at 25 additional district schools which now qualified for E-rate funding.

BACKGROUND: The  Jacobs & Cushman San Diego Food Bank foundation was founded in 1977 to act as the safety net for the hungry in San Diego County. The “Food 4 Kids Program” was started in the 2006-07 school year to help feed the chronically hungry children in the elementary schools of San Diego County on the weekends. 

 

The program started with just one school and 150 children.  It has since grown to 29 schools and 1,125 children.  Qualifying schools choose the 25 most chronically hungry children in the school to participate.  The schools that qualify to participate in the program are schools where at least 85 percent of the children participate in the low-fee or no-fee feeding program funded by the USDA.  If the school qualifies, then the school’s administrative staff, typically the School Nurse, choose the 25 children to participate based upon the criteria for qualification.

 

Once these children are identified, backpacks are delivered full of child-friendly and nutritious food on Friday afternoon to take home to supplement their food supply and help them through the weekend until they return to school on Monday morning.  Upon return to school on Monday, they are fed through the school cafeteria feeding program.  When they return on Monday morning, they deliver the empty backpacks to be refilled for distribution the following Friday.  The names of those participating in the program are confidential.  The backpacks are all different so that the backpacks cannot be identified with the program.  Without this program, most of these children would not have another meal after they leave school on Friday until they return to school the following Monday morning.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Each school's School Site Council (SSC) works with the site instructional leadership team and district staff to develop, implement, monitor, and evaluate their Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA).

 

BACKGROUND:  As a result of SB 374, the state holds each district school accountable for developing and implementing a Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Site principals, SSC chairpersons, SSC members, and the District Advisory Council for Compensatory Education (DAC) received training regarding specific plan requirements.

Additionally, Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department staff provided individual support to principals and site teams to discuss Title I expenditures and the SPSA development process. Sites then developed their SPSAs, had them approved by their SSCs, and submitted them to their area superintendents for review. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their SMART goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

The local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2011–12 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 169 schools.

Copies of each SPSA, budget summary, and required, signed assurances page are available in the Board Services Office, Eugene Brucker Education Center, 4100 Normal Street, Room 2231, San Diego, CA 92103.

BACKGROUND:  Thousands of schools across the country participate annually in No Name-Calling Week, a week of educational activities designed to address bullying and name-calling of all kinds.  A project of the Gay, Lesbian, and Straight Education Network (GLSEN), Simon & Schuster Children's Publishing, and more than 50 participating organizations, No Name-Calling Week has become one of the most used and celebrated bullying prevention programs in the country. Eliminating name-calling is a key component of reducing bullying and violence escalation.  No Name-Calling Week includes educational activities aimed at ending name-calling of all kinds and providing schools with the tools and inspiration to launch an ongoing dialogue about ways to eliminate bullying in their communities.

LAS Link Assessment--In grade-level content classes, students need to be able to understand the teacher’s explanations, discuss what is being learned, read for different purposes, and write about their learning. We call this type of language academic language, the language that is used by teacher and students for the purpose of acquiring new knowledge and skills.

 

Language is used as a functional tool for learning academic subject matter. Students learn not just the vocabulary and grammar of the content area but also learn important concepts and skills using academic language. Students learn the language functions that are important for performing effectively in the content area, such as analyzing, evaluating, justifying, and persuading. Students develop academic language skills in English through cognitively demanding activities in which comprehension is assisted by contextual supports in which scaffold instruction guides the acquisition of content.  (Chamot & O’Malley, 1994)

 

LAS Links measures students’ English-language proficiency in four domains: Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing. LAS Links is designed to assess English-language learners (ELLs), Spanish-language learners (SLLs), and Academic English Learners (AELs). Academic English Learners sometimes known as Standard English Learners (SELs), typically speak English at home, but do not yet have enough academic language skill to succeed in the classroom.

 

LAS Links can be used for English Language Development and for Progress Monitoring by using a combination of the LAS Links assessments.  Depending on program need and intent, LAS Links provides districts with an option of measuring student progress up to five times per year.

The LAS Links standardized assessment measures Speaking, Listening, Reading, and Writing, and provides a measure of comprehension skills in academic and social English derived from the Listening and Reading domains. Each of these domains can be administered individually for monitoring specific areas, or can be administered as a whole to provide a complete picture of students' language abilities.  LAS Links blends the assessment of English proficiency with content area knowledge, using vocabulary that is needed for success in school. A wide selection of test formats, including multiple-choice and constructed-response items, are provided, and the content that is used is culturally relevant, and motivating to students. The test results from this assessment link to teaching strategies and instructional guidance for teachers. The results can also assist teachers in modifying instruction to ensure the successful achievement academic settings.

LAS Links is different from assessments that appear in core or supplemental curriculum. While those assessments have value to the classroom teacher in ensuring students are acquiring the skills being taught, they often do not prepare students for the rigors of statewide assessments, including CELDT.  LAS Links structure and content are similar to the content found in CELDT. The assessment offers real-time, actionable data to classroom teachers to inform instruction, as well as provides valuable aggregated information to Principals and School Administrators to make informed decisions about program effectiveness, student performance, and areas of need.  LAS Links is a highly reliable, high quality, research-based assessment with a proven track record.

 

Acuity Assessment--Area 1 principals and schools are deeply committed to the learning of each child and to the improvement of student achievement in their schools. The diversity of our schools and students in their academic ability has compelled each administrator to strategize about practices that give teachers and leaders the data needed to make daily instructional decisions and plan structural changes in their schools and classrooms. The use of formative assessment data in the form of benchmark exams is invaluable to the work that goes on in schools.

 

Formative assessment is a process used by teachers and students during instruction that provides feedback to adjust ongoing teaching and learning to improve students’ achievement of intended instructional outcomes (definition by the Formative Assessment for Students and Teachers State Collaborative in Assessment and Student Standards, October 2006). Formative assessment provides teachers critical information about student and classroom progress and can be used to adjust a teacher’s approach and the pace of instruction throughout the year. It is often used as a tool for uncovering opportunities for instructional intervention, and provides this data while there is still time in the school year to improve student achievement. This data is relevant and necessary for immediate changes in the academic learning for children.

 

Area 1 schools, under the leadership of Dr. Brenda Campbell, have been given the opportunity to implement the ACUITY benchmark assessment system. ACUITY assessments are administered online to students in English Language Arts and Mathematics three times a year. These assessments use research based items aligned to state standards. Each assessment builds upon the previous one, allowing schools to monitor growth from test to test. They are based on the California Standards Testing Blueprint helping schools to target instruction that is in alignment to the statewide test at the end of the year. Because they provide predictive data, they help schools monitor student progress towards standards as part of school-wide improvement efforts. Results from each exam can be carefully analyzed to make instructional decisions and identify students who need targeted support. Students can receive additional instruction based on their performance on the assessment, immediately addressing areas of weakness and opportunities for enrichment. The data generated through the ACUITY process provides more in depth information for teachers and administrators, making it a more valuable resource than district benchmarks for schools to do formative assessment in English Language Arts and Mathematics.

 

It is our belief that this assessment will provide critical information to support our school improvement efforts that is not currently available in district benchmarks. Current district benchmark results do not allow us to measure growth or compare results from assessment to assessment.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution regarding the district’s Stabilization Agreement (PSA) for Proposition S School Construction Repairs and Renovation and ratify the attached side letter agreement(s) extending the term of the PSA project for the life of the Prop S program and expand its scope to cover all projects paid for in whole or in part with state bond funds; direct staff to transition labor compliance to shift enforcement of prevailing wage laws back to the state for all projects with contracts executed on or after January 1, 2012. 


FISCAL IMPACT: Approximately $1.65 million in savings due to elimination of staff augmentation. 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $718,000.00.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: A plan to transition the program from the  district to the state will need to be developed that addresses the close out of current labor compliance projects before the impact to district staffing can be quantified for the current fiscal year; however it is anticipated there will be no net loss of district staff.


CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On July 28, 2009, the board adopted a Project Stabilization Agreement (PSA) for Proposition S School Construction, Repairs, and Renovation.  Since the adoption of the PSA, a third party study has confirmed that the PSA has met the intent of the agreement to promote the use of local workers and provide quality training for the local workforce.  Recent legislation allows the district to enter into an agreement such as the PSA provided it meets the minimum criteria set forth therein (Public Contract Code sections 2500-2502).  These minimum criteria require the PSA have provisions prohibiting discrimination on the basis of race, national origin, religion, sex, sexual orientation, political affiliation, or membership in a labor organization; permitting all qualified contractors to bid for and be awarded work regardless of whether otherwise parties to a collective bargaining agreement; providing an agreed-upon protocol for substance abuse testing of employees working on site; guarantees against strikes, work stoppages, lockouts and similar disruptions; and provides that disputes between the parties will be resolved by a neutral arbitrator. 

 

New legislation also creates a state Compliance Monitoring Unit (“CMU”), effective January 1, 2012, to ensure payment of prevailing wage to all employees working on projects paid for in whole or in part with state bond funds (Labor Code sections 1771.3, 1771.7 and 1771.75.)  School districts are exempt from paying a fee to the state enforcement agency for enforcement of prevailing wage laws if the projects are covered by a collective bargaining agreement, such as the PSA, which binds all of the contractors performing work on the project and that includes a mechanism for resolving disputes about the payment of wages. (Labor Code section 1771.3.)  Enforcement of prevailing wage laws on these and other district projects is currently accomplished through the district’s Labor Compliance program, which has been in operation 2000.  

 

The attached resolution makes the required findings under Public Contract Code section 2500 that the PSA meets the minimum criteria described above, and authorizes ratification of a side letter agreement with the signatories to the PSA that extends the term of the PSA for the duration of the Prop S program and extends its scope to cover projects paid for by state bond fund. The latter enables to shift prevailing wage enforcement back to the state without having to pay a fee.  The current staffing budget for fiscal year 2011-12 is $1.635 million for staff augmentation consultants brought on this year to deal with a backload of labor compliance audits.  However, in lieu of operating its own program, the district will shift enforcement back to the state through the CMU; thereby eliminating the need for staff augmentation.  Labor Compliance program will continue to operate with existing district staff where required by statute, with respect to those projects for which contracts were executed prior to January 1, 2012, until such time as such projects are completed.

Gartner Inc. is the IT industry leading consulting group that reviews IT solutions and provides guidance to several government agencies and fortune 500 companies about emerging technology and its impact on IT decisions that they are faced with today.

 

Gartner has completed its comprehensive review of our plans and support the direction the district is headed.  Attached is a modified version of the plan based on new timelines and minor recommendations from Gartner analysts.

 

District ERP Background

 

In 2002, the Board of Education made an investment to replace the legacy applications with Oracle’s PeopleSoft Enterprise Resource Planning system and the Zangle Student Information System (SIS). Over time other primary systems were replaced including a transportation system, PPO work order system, and in progress is a new Special Education IEP tracking and Response to Intervention system.

 

Current challenges include:

  • The district currently does not have easy drill down access to our data across the core student, HR, Finance and other business systems
  • The version of Oracle PeopleSoft that the district is currently running on is nearing its end of supportable life and will require significant upgrades starting in 2012
  • The district’s current SIS provider (C-Innovation – Zangle) has gone out of business and the district now needs to consider a replacement system
  • Most Non-Instructional computers at school sites and central offices are outdated, out of warranty and unfixable as they fail.

Information Technology Support Services (ITSS) recommended solutions to address these challenges are as follows:

  1. Oracle Business Intelligence Build-Out: It is recommended that the district ‘build out’ the Oracle Business Intelligence Enterprise Edition (OBIEE) platform via rapid deployment of Board of Education and Superintendent priority reports/dashboards providing real-time key metrics. Additionally, after the system is built out, fund the upgrade of the Oracle BI system from 10gR3 to 11gR1.
     
  2. PeopleSoft Upgrades: It is recommended that the district stay on the PeopleSoft suite of business applications and to upgrade the applications as new releases become available. To maintain an Oracle supported platform and ensure that core business systems are up to date and current through 2017 will require approving and funding a multi-year upgrade of the PeopleSoft Human Capital Management, Finance, and Supply Chain Management systems from version 8.9 to 9.1/9.2. This upgrade also accounts for the integration with Primavera and Contract Manager that will assist FPC in managing financial aspects of Proposition.

    As part of the upgrade, ITSS is recommending the approval and funding of one Enterprise Business Software Coordinator type position to manage the complex PeopleSoft upgrade and related tasks.
     
  3. Zangle Replacement: It is recommended that the district replace the current, non-supported SIS (Zangle) to provide a modern and supported platform for student records to be maintained and reported from. In May of 2011, the district put out an RFI to allow staff to better understand what is available today, specifically looking for features such as an online student registration module, an informative parent portal, and comprehensive school site reporting tools. In July a diverse team of stakeholders will review the top responders and assist in developing an RFP that included all of the essential features identified by the team.

    As part of the replacement project, ITSS is recommending the approval and funding of one Enterprise SIS Software Coordinator type position to manage the complex SIS replacement and related tasks.
     
  4. Non-instructional Computer Replacement: It is recommended that the district replace the outdated and out of warranty DWA/Non-Instructional computers and provide a structure to establish a budgeted four year replacement schedule past year five. This strategy is geared to align with both warranty duration (four years) and the maximum useful life of computers procured today. An ITSS coordinated deployment will guarantee that school sites and departments have current and warrantied computers thereby improving staff efficiency and minimizing downtime due to computer hardware failure, lack of parts availability, incompatibility, or general performance (slow).

Funding--The initial system purchase in 2002 and upgrades to other districtwide systems have traditionally been funded through the State Schools Facilities Fund (SSFF). Since this is such a large project and due to the current budget situation we are recommending that SSFF be considered as a funding source for this critical project.
