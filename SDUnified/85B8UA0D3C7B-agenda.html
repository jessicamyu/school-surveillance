RECOMMENDATION: Approve Memorandum of Understanding between San Diego Unified School District and San Diego Education Association (SDEA) regarding Special Education Certification Program.

FISCAL IMPACT: Up to a maximum of $648,000, all from federal IDEA Stimulus Funds. The cost will in all likelihood be lower, as the figure stated is based on maximum participation (40 SDEA bargaining unit members), at the higher cost participating university, with 100 percent program completion. Cost is expected to range from $10,386 to $16,200 for each successful participant.

BACKGROUND: The district is facing a significant shortage of special education teachers due to the significant number of special education teachers who have accepted the Supplemental Early Retirement Program (SERP). The number of potential openings will be increased by additional teachers who choose to take leaves of absence for the 2009-2010 school year. At the same time, the district faces an excess number of K-6 general education teachers. There are 40 spaces available in the program, with an enrollment period ending on July 1, 2009. Participation is strictly voluntary.

The details of the program are:
  • Teachers will enroll in coursework at a local university leading toward a Master’s Degree in Special Education.
  • The entire cost of tuition, fees and books necessary toward obtaining the Master’s degree in Special Education will be paid by the district, using federal IDEA stimulus funds.
  • Teacher’s who complete the Master’s program will agree to be available to teach in a special education assignment for three consecutive school years, beginning with the 2009-10 school year. None of the cost of tuition, fees and books paid by the district will be subject to full or partial repayment if this condition is met.
  • The teacher and the district will cooperate to obtain an Intern Special Education Credential from the Commission on Teacher Credentialing.
  • Participating teachers will receive salary schedule advancement credit for all completed coursework in the program.

RECOMMENDATION: Approval of request for indemnity by members of Independent Citizens Oversight Committee, for actions within the course and scope of their duties as a member of the ICOC, to be subsequently incorporated into Bylaws of the ICOC.

FISCAL IMPACT: None

BACKGROUND:

At a meeting of the ICOC, staff was asked whether the District would indemnify members of the ICOC in the event they were sued, collectively or individually, based on actions and decisions as members of the ICOC.  District General Counsel informed the members that he believed they would be covered based on their status as members of a statutory advisory committee appointed by the Board of Education.  A review of the Memorandum of Coverage (MOC) with the District’s excess insurance carrier confirmed that an indemnity provision for ICOC members would fall within the scope of the District’s existing coverage, and the ICOC was informed of this information.  Additionally, as “officers” appointed by the Board to a statutorily-mandated committee, the provisions of California law providing for defense and indemnification are arguably applicable to ICOC members even without express action to indemnify.  Finally, staff research has revealed that numerous districts in the state have affirmatively agreed to indemnify members of their Proposition 39 bond oversight committees.

The ICOC recently took formal action to request that the Board of Education provide the ICOC with proof of indemnification for their actions as ICOC members.  This matter is therefore on the agenda for Board consideration of action to confirm indemnity coverage for ICOC members acting within the course and scope of their duties as members of the ICOC.

F.04. Plan for Comprehensive Study of Demographics, Location of Neighborhood Schools, and Transportation--First Reading (Evans)
Meeting
Jun 23, 2009 - 06/23/2009
Type
Information, First Reading
RECOMMENDATION: Board member discussion of a Plan for Comprehensive Study of Demographics, Location of Neighborhood Schools and Transportation: First Reading.

BACKGROUND:
Problem: Decisions about the location of schools, school capacity, types of schools available and school closures have too often been made due to immediate need and short-term financial considerations. The board is often faced with decisions regarding school closures and transportation issues without long term data. San Diego Unified now has insufficient school capacity in areas where many children live and excess capacity in neighborhoods with fewer kids. Many students are bussed great distances to correct the facilities imbalance.

Some neighborhood schools are perceived as inadequate, which results in lower enrollment and continued deterioration of quality. While parents and students should have some choices to meet the unique educational needs of each student, parents should not have to choose another school based solely upon an inadequate neighborhood school.

Solution: Stronger neighborhood schools will result in better connections with students, parents and community. School capacity needs to be developed where students live. At the same time we need to promote diversity throughout the entire San Diego community. We also need to offer varied educational opportunities based on the needs of students. There are many fine programs within the district that offer choice, special curricular emphasis and promote diversity. These programs need to be fine tuned to meet our changing needs from 2009 to 2020.

As a district, we need to develop criteria for the long term development of our schools that will also affect our short term decisions. Such questions include, but are not limited, to the following: What is the proper size range for our schools? What is a reasonable amount of transportation for students within the district? What type of high schools do we want and where should they be located? How do magnet schools supplement neighborhood schools and promote diversity and special programs? If we support neighborhood schools, how far should a child have to travel to reach a “neighborhood school?” How do we lessen the capacity of bus schedules on individual school schedules? How do we support neighborhood schools and diversity at the same time? How do we support communities in developing their own schools and the stable flow from elementary to middle to high school? How can new technologies bring new educational opportunities to all of our schools regardless of location? Key question: How do we want our schools to be configured by the year 2020?

Determination of the above criteria needs to be made by the Board of Education with input from staff and from the community. It is proposed that this long term planning (minimum of 10 years into the future) be accomplished through the following steps:
  1. Board workshop to develop the criteria for school type, capacity and location of schools
    Proposed time frame: July to September 2009
  2. Board workshop with staff input
    Proposed time frame: July to September 2009
  3. Presentation to the public of criteria for school development (without listing specific schools) for possible modification
    Proposed time frame: October 2009
  4. Direction to staff to perform demographic study of where children live vs. where they attend school, reasons for variability, current birth rates and housing development. All of this needs to factor in the school needs for each community and the financial and time considerations for re-aligning our schools.
    Proposed time frame: November 2009 to January 2010
  5. Adoption of 10 year plan
    Proposed time frame:     January 2010
  6. Plan for implementation working with communities for closure, school change, increase to full capacity or increased capacity
    Proposed time frame: January to March 2010
  7. Finalization of plan for 2010-11 and approval of school changes.
    Plans could include means to utilize full capacity of schools and changes in programs. Any school closures at this point would be based upon those who received early notification in Summer 2009 and ongoing determination that it is appropriate at this point.
    Proposed time frame: March 1, 2010
  8. Finalization of plans for 2011-12
    This will result in 18 months to implement any changes.
    Proposed time frame March 1, 2010


Review of the long term plan and possible modification
October of each year.

Specific changes would be approved no later than 18 months prior to the start of the proposed September changes.

RECOMMENDATION: Modification of action previously taken regarding the renewal charter petition for Holly Drive Leadership Academy, as follows:

The Board of Education renews the Holly Drive charter petition for a one-year five-year term beginning July 1, 2009 and expiring on June 30, 2010 2014, so long as the following conditions are achieved by June 30, 2010 and maintained for the duration of the term. Failure to meet conditions 3 through 9 would result in the immediate revocation of the charter petition:
  1. The school achieve API growth targets for the next three consecutive years;
  2. The school receive a statewide ranking above the minimum rank of 4 as required in Education code 47607(b) the next three consecutive years;
  3. The school change its status from a dependent (arm-of the-District) charter school to a non-profit public benefit corporation with a 501c3 non-profit tax exemption from the Internal Revenue Service;
  4. The school maintains a sufficient cash reserve in the County Treasury to reimburse the district within 30 days of receiving an invoice for expenses incurred on behalf of the charter school and for fees and services owed to the district;
  5. The school’s expenses will not exceed the earned revenue for the fiscal year in order to generate an operational surplus each year for the term of the charter;
  6. The school generates a sufficient operational surplus to obtain and maintain a positive ending fund balance at the end of each fiscal year;
  7. The school achieves the district required reserve for economic uncertainty of $50,000 or 3 percent of expenses and maintains the reserve requirement for the term of the charter;
  8. The Holly Drive Board must all have current terms, receive appropriate Brown Act training, develop charter school By-laws, and conduct all board meetings only when quorums are present; and
  9. The school must possess a Conditional Use Permit by September 1, 2009.


FISCAL IMPACT: None

BACKGROUND:

At the June 2, 2009 Special Meeting, staff presented the board with three options related to the renewal petition for Holly Drive Leadership Academy: 1) Charter School Non-Renewal; 2) Approve Charter School Renewal with Conditions; or 3) Approve Charter School Renewal without Conditions. The Board adopted the second option, with two modifications of the staff recommendation: 1) that there be a ninth condition added related to the obtainment of a Conditional Use Permit by September 1, 2009; and 2) that the term of the charter renewal be for one year rather than five years. The latter modification was offered and approved based on erroneous information provided by General Counsel – although there is a general provision in the Charter Schools Act stating that charter petitions may be granted for a period of “up to five years,” a more specific provision states that charter renewals will be for a five year period. This matter is being brought back to amend the prior action taken to bring it into conformity with the Charter Schools Act. The recommendation is intended to conform with the board’s prior action in that it: 1) is based on the fact that grounds currently exist to deny the renewal petition; 2) the conditions for approval notwithstanding these grounds apply during the entire term of the charter; and 3) failure to meet any conditions during the term of the charter will result in revocation.

RECOMMENDATION: Each school in San Diego Unified School District would keep the same bell time in 2009-10 as in 2008-09 (with the exception of the Creative and Performing Arts (CPMA) magnet school which will start at 9:15 a.m., due to high transportation costs).

This means that the nine schools (Alcott, Bayview Terrace, Encanto, Ericson, Gage, Hawthorne, Hearst, Marvin and ALBA) approved on February 17, 2009, for a change to an earlier time would keep their 2008-09 start time. It also means that the proposal to move all schools 15 minutes later next year (approved by the board on June 2, 2009) would not occur. Any potential changes in bell times for 2010-11 would have to be approved by the board no later than March 15, 2010.

BACKGROUND: We have approved major changes in our transportation system saving approximately $4 million by changing the minimum ridership from 8 to 15 students. This will result in consolidated bus routes.

The savings in the bus schedule and bell time changes are relatively minor in comparison to the above savings with the exception of one school, CPMA, which saves us $1.0 million by a major change in the bell time. (CPMA is a magnet school totally dependent upon bus transportation with 26 buses.)

We currently face serious problems in our current school schedules with many schools starting before 7:30, including some as early as 7:15 a.m., and bus pick-up times as early as 5:50 a.m. These early times are a great hardship and often result in high school students and others not getting enough sleep. However, start times after 9:00 a.m., cause difficulties with late arrival and late departure.

Therefore, next year each school (with the exception of CPMA) would retain its current 2008-09 schedule. Any changes for 2010-11 would have to be approved by the board by March 15, 2010.

The following steps will be taken during the 2009-10 school year to plan for the following year:

1) Assessment of the effects of the above changes in ridership rules once the new school year patterns stabilize. Besides an increased number of students per bus (fewer buses), an undetermined number of students may choose to not ride the bus, which will result in even greater flexibility for district buses.
Proposed timeline: September 8 to October 15, 2009

2) Other possible reductions in district bussing that could result in the elimination of extremely early and/or late start times without changing other schools’ schedules. (Investigate also feasibility of charging students who are able to pay. Such payments could allow funds for additional routes delivering students at the same, reasonable time.)
Proposed timeline: September 8 to October 15, 2009

3) Study of alternatives, including on-site flexibility in teaching schedules at certain schools that have a small number of students riding the bus. They may be able to accommodate slightly different hours. This should be done at a school site level and on a cluster level. This would be done in consultation with parents, administrators, teachers and support staff. Also, district central staff will provide education to communities on the role of bussing, including its role in keeping certain schools open.
Proposed timeline: November 1, 2009 to January 15, 2010

4) Presentation to board of recommended Comprehensive Transportation and Bell Schedule for 2010-11
