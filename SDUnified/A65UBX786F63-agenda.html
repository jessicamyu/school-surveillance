1. Resolution in the Matter of Reaffirming the Values of Peace, Tolerance, and Respect for Multiple Perspectives (Barrera/Evans)

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution in the matter of reaffirming the values of peace, tolerance, and respect for multiple perspectives.

 

BACKGROUND: San Diego Unified is committed to providing each and every child with a world-class education, and this commitment requires removing all social and emotional barriers to instruction.

 

The fears felt by some district students following the recent presidential election—fears that their friends, family, or loved ones are at risk of being harmed—pose significant barriers to their well-being and ability to learn.

 

The ability of San Diego Unified to support students who feel vulnerable will help both those individual students and will help create safer schools for all students we serve.

 

Working to protect the rights of all students and their families is consistent with core district values of peace, compassion, tolerance, diversity, and inclusion.

 

Support for vulnerable students is consistent with San Diego Unified’s long history of advocating for tolerance and assuring the safety of all students in our care, including most recently the introduction of online reporting mechanisms to stamp out bullying.

 

Standing in solidarity with students and their families is unrelated to partisan politics and no single political party or leader has a monopoly on virtue, and the values of peace and tolerance belong equally to all Americans.

 

Following the recent presidential election, the School Superintendents Association (AASA), the American School Counselor Association, GLSEN, the National Association of Elementary School Principals, the National Association of Secondary School Principals, the National PTA, the National School Boards Association, and the National Association of School Psychologists have urged all school district across the nation to reaffirm the inclusive values that are the foundation of healthy learning cultures.

 

Immediately following the national election last month, the Superintendent and the leader of the district teacher’s union issued a joint letter to more than 17,000 San Diego Unified staff members reaffirming district schools must remain safe places for all students and families with respect and civility the guiding principles on every campus.

 

San Diego Unified students have reacted with grace and compassion and support for their peers following the recent election.

 

The San Diego Unified Family and Community Engagement Office is organizing an event to support the right of all students, regardless of race, color, national origin, immigration status, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, disability, or religion to be educated in an environment free from fear, violence, and intimidation.

 

The event to support all students has been named a Celebration of Light and scheduled for Wednesday, December 14, at 4:00 p.m. at the Ballard Parent Center in Old Town, located at 2375 Congress St, San Diego, California 92110.

 

We urge the entire county of San Diego to join the students, staff, and families of San Diego Unified at the Celebration of Light in Old Town on December 14, 2016.


We urge the San Diego City Council to reaffirm its 2013 resolution supporting the rights of immigrant families to live without fear as full and equal members of a civic community.
 
We urge those immigrant families who are eligible for citizenship, but have not yet applied, to do so before the pending cost increase on December 23, 2016.
 
We urge the Superintendent to continue working collaboratively with all community partners who share the commitment of San Diego Unified to safe and open school communities, including but not limited to the PTA, SDEA, CSEA, POA, AASD, and the United Way, Alliance San Diego, the ACLU and the internal accountability committees like CAC, DAC, and DELAC.

 

We direct the Superintendent, teachers, counselors, and staff to take whatever steps necessary to maintain the safety and openness of the school community and to document these steps in an action plan to be presented to the Board within 90 days.

 

We direct district General Counsel to issue a legal memorandum to all students and staff affirming the protections afforded immigrant students and their families under the 1982 Supreme Court decision in the matter of Plyler v. Doe, affirming the rights of undocumented students to access the same education as all students, and the 2011 memorandum from the Department of Homeland Security classifying schools as sensitive locations where enforcement actions are prohibited.

 

We direct the Superintendent to work with district General Counsel to create a staff directive reminding all district personnel of their obligations under the California Trust Act of 2014, and the aforementioned federal case law and administrative procedures.

 
We urge the Immigration and Customs Enforcement Agency to affirm that schools are now and will forever be sensitive locations where they will not seek to detain and deport immigrant families who pose no threat to community safety.

 

We call on President-Elect Trump to continue the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) program and to clearly articulate the rights of all students to attend school without the fear of detention and deportation.

 

We urge President Obama to expand the Department of Justice prohibitions against racial profiling to include all agencies within the Department of Homeland Security, including the Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agency.

RECOMMENDATION:  Deny America’s Finest Charter School’s proposed revisions to its charter to add Grades 9-12 and add a second site at 4481 Estrella Avenue, and adopt the below findings.

  1. The charter revision does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of the educational program because it is vague with regard to the dual enrollment partnership with San Diego State University (SDSU). The charter revision describes a high school program that is substantially defined by students participating in “dual enrollment” in partnership with SDSU for their math, history, fine arts, and foreign language courses (see pages 5, 12, 18, 32, and 33 of the charter revision). However, there are no SDSU documents or details (for example, a proposed credit articulation agreement). The budget submitted by America’s Finest Charter School does not include the estimated expenses related to the SDSU partnership.
     
  2. The charter revision does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of the qualifications to be met by the individuals to be employed by the charter school. Page 18 of the charter revision describes the high school as having two “Deans” who will also serve as the “College Counselors.” Each Dean will be responsible for overseeing one of the two Academies, the Engineering Academy or the International Studies Academy, as well as for working with SDSU to determine course sequencing and ensuring students receive the support they need. The charter revision does not contain any information as to the qualifications to be met by the individuals to be employed by America’s Finest Charter School serving in the Dean/College Counselor positions (for example, the minimum years of teaching, counseling, and/or administrator experience; a pupil personnel services credential, teaching credential, and/or administrative credential; bachelor’s degree and/or master’s degree in engineering or world languages).
     
  3. The charter revision does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of the educational program because it is not supported by the financial plan with regard to administrator salaries. Page 9 of the charter revision states the charter school will have one Executive Director, one Principal for Grades K-8, and one Principal for Grades 9-12, for a total of three administrators. However, the budget for fiscal years 2017-18 through 2020-21 contains salaries and benefits for only two administrator positions.
     
  4. The charter revision does not contain a reasonably comprehensive description of the educational program because it is vague with regard to the physical education partnership with the YMCA. The educational program is described as having a physical education partnership with the “Copley YMCA” (see page 12 of the charter revision). However, there are no YMCA documents or details. The budget submitted by America’s Finest Charter School does not include the estimated expenses related to the YMCA partnership.
     
  5. America’s Finest Charter School is demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program described in the charter revision because it is unfamiliar with the applicable requirements of law with regard to the Brown Act. As supporting documentation for the charter governing board’s approval to purchase the proposed site at 4481 Estrella Avenue, America’s Finest Charter School submitted its meeting agenda and minutes for August 17, 2016. These documents describe a teleconference meeting, but they do not demonstrate compliance with the Brown Act open meeting law requirements, which include, but are not limited to, the address(es) from which the board member(s) were calling from and documentation of a roll call vote.
     
  6. America’s Finest Charter School is demonstrably unlikely to successfully implement the program described in the charter revision because the operational plan with regard to the proposed facility is unrealistic due the small size of the site and classrooms within the site. America’s Finest Charter School proposes serving approximately 320 students in Grades 9-12. Two separate floor plans were submitted for review. The original floor plan shows six classrooms, ranging from 799 to 951 square feet in size per classroom, along with a future gymnasium, with a maximum capacity to serve 149 students. In response to the district’s inquiry for how the site would accommodate the proposed enrollment of 320 students, America’s Finest Charter School submitted a revised floor plan showing eight classrooms, with one classroom being 679 square feet and the remaining seven classrooms ranging in size from 288 to 350 square feet. While acknowledging the charter revision on page 5 describes small class sizes, neither the original or revised floor plan presents a realistic facility design.

Below is an overview of historical enrollment and retention patterns in charter high schools.
     
    1. In 2015-16, Urban Discovery Academy enrolled 28 eighth graders. In the following year, 2016-17, Urban Discovery enrolled just 20 ninth graders, a retention rate of 71 percent.
       
    2. When it opened in 2007-08, Arroyo Paseo Charter High enrolled 53 ninth graders. After nine years of operation, Arroyo Paseo now serves a total of 12 ninth graders, a 77 percent enrollment decline for ninth grade.
       
    3. When it opened in 2009-10, King-Chavez Community High enrolled 207 ninth graders. Four years later, King-Chavez enrolled just 149 twelfth graders, a 72 percent retention rate. After six years of operation, King-Chavez currently serves 146 ninth graders.
       
    4. When it opened in 2013-14, e3 Civic High enrolled 176 ninth graders. Four years later, e3 Civic High enrolled just 113 twelfth graders, a 64 percent retention rate. e3 Civic High currently serves 108 ninth graders.
       
    5. From 2015-16 to 2016-17, the School of Entrepreneurship and Technology (formerly Coleman), dropped from 73 ninth graders to just 40 ninth graders, a 45 percent enrollment decline.
       
    6. In 2014-15, City Heights Prep enrolled 28 eighth graders. In the following year, City Heights Prep reported only 22 ninth graders (79 percent retention rate). In 2015-16, City Heights Prep enrolled 32 eighth graders. In the following year, City Heights Prep reported only 19 ninth graders (59 percent retention rate).

RENEWAL VERSUS REVISION

 

A petition to open a new charter school can be approved by the district for up to a five-year term. Near the end of the term, a charter school applies for “renewal” of its charter to continue operating for another five-year term. Education Code section 47605 requires the review and approval process for new and renewing charter petitions to take place over two board meetings: (i) public hearing, and (ii) action to grant/deny.

 

Within a five-year term, a charter school may request the district’s approval to revise its existing charter pursuant to Education Code section 47607. Proposed revisions may include a change in the school’s location, reassignment of special education responsibilities, expansion of grade levels, or other significant revision.

When considering a petition for renewal, pursuant to Title 5 of the California Code of Regulations section 11966.4(b)(1), the district governing board shall consider the past performance of the school’s academics, finances, and operations in evaluating the likelihood of future success, along with future plans for improvement if any.

 

Staff has visited Health Sciences High and Health Sciences Middle annually. ACADEMICS

 

As the charter granting authority, pursuant to Education Code section 47607(a)(3)(A), the district shall consider increases in student academic achievement for all groups of students served by the charter school as the most important factor in determining whether to grant a charter renewal.

 

A charter school shall meet at least one of the following criteria before receiving a renewal:

  1. Attained its Academic Performance Index (API) growth target in the prior year or in two of the last three years both schoolwide and for all groups of pupils served by the charter school. 
  2. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API in the prior year or in two of the last three years. 
  3. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API for a demographically comparable school in the prior year or in two of the last three years.  
  4. The entity that granted the charter determines that the academic performance of the charter school is at least equal to the academic performance of the public schools that the charter school pupils would otherwise have been required to attend, as well as the academic performance of the schools in the school district in which the charter school is located, taking into account the composition of the pupil population that is served at the charter school.  
  5. Qualified for an alternative accountability system pursuant to Education Code section 52052(h).

Assembly Bill (AB) 484 amended Education Code sections 52052(e)(2)(F) and 52052(e)(4) to allow schools that do not have an API calculated in 2013–14 and 2014–15 to use one of the following criteria to meet legislative and/or programmatic requirements:

  1. The most recent API calculation;
  2. An average of the three most recent annual API calculations; or
  3. Alternative measures that show increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils schoolwide and among significant groups. 

The decision to use one of these criteria may be made on a program by program basis and is a local decision.

2/28/14 – 2/28/17

 

With regard to facilities, as noted above, Albert Einstein Academy Charter Elementary operates on a district site at 3035 Ash Street. On June 7, 2016, following the completion of a feasibility study and master plan, the school submitted a project proposal application to the Charter School Facility Committee which held a publicly noticed meeting to hear a presentation, review the proposal and voted to recommend the project to the Board of Education for funding. The Board of Education approved $26,434,098 in Proposition Z bond funds for the removal of portable classrooms and construction and modernization of classrooms, office and sport facilities at current site. This project is scheduled to receive the first round of funding for design in 2021.

 

District staff is reviewing the charter renewal documents submitted by Albert Einstein Academy Charter Elementary and will bring a recommendation to grant or deny the renewal of the charter at a subsequent date. The recommendation will include information about the school's LCAP, academic outcomes as compared to the district schools that the students would have otherwise attended based on their residence, the population served by the school, and finances.
