RECOMMENDATION:  Conduct a public hearing and approve the application to the State Board of Education to waive the specified 2012-13 Quality Education Investment Act (QEIA) requirements at:

  • Edison Elementary School—Highly Qualified Teacher (HQT) [EC 52055.740 (a)(3)];
  • Mann Middle School—teacher experience index (TEI) [EC 52055.740 (a)(4)] and highly qualified teacher (HQT) [EC 52055.740 (a)(3)] requirements;
  • Montgomery Middle School—highly qualified teacher (HQT) [EC 52055.740 (a)(3)] and class size reduction (CSR) EC 52055.740(a)(1)(C)(i)(ii)] requirements;
  • Roosevelt Middle School—highly qualified teacher (HQT) [EC 52055.740 (a)(3) and class size reduction (CSR) EC 52055.740(a)(1)(C)(i)(ii)] requirements.

FISCAL IMPACT: Absent approved waivers, the four schools will not meet their 2012-13 QEIA requirements for participating in QEIA funding; as a result, QEIA funding will be discontinued beyond the 2013-14 school year.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: For 2012-13, schools received QEIA funding in the amounts of:

  • Edison, approximately $365,400;
  • Mann, approximately $878,400;
  • Montgomery Middle School, approximately $393,300;
  • Roosevelt Middle School, approximately $780,300.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: Loss of QEIA funding, mostly used for reducing class sizes, will result in the reduction of teaching positions as follows:

  • Edison, approximately 3.91 FTE;
  • Mann, approximately 9.5 FTE;
  • Montgomery, approximately 4.15 FTE;
  • Roosevelt, approximately 9.86 FTE.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable. However, as a condition of the waiver, the San Diego Education Association (SDEA) bargaining unit has been asked to take a position on the waivers: support, neutral, or opposed. The SDEA has indicated that it has taken a position of neutral on the waivers.

 

BACKGROUND: In 2007-08, seven district schools began participation in the eight year, Quality Education Investment Act (QEIA) program that provides low performing schools with funding to reduce class sizes and provide additional professional development. Each year, QEIA schools must meet six performance targets to continue to be eligible for funding. Specific information regarding each of the schools is provided below:

  • Edison will receive approximately $369,200 in 2013-14. One of the targets is achieving 100 percent employment of highly qualified teachers. For 2012-13, Edison had one teacher who did not possess documentation to show compliance. The teacher possessed the correct credential and certification for her assignment as a biliteracy teacher. She needed to pass the California Subject Examination for Teachers (CSET) for Multiple Subjects. However, she did not pass the exam. 
  • Mann will receive approximately $828,900 in 2013-14. One of the targets is a TEI calculated on average years of teaching experience. However, QEIA places a cap of 10 years experience for its calculations. Therefore, a teacher with 25 actual years of experience is assigned a value of 10 when determining the QEIA TEI. For 2012-13, Mann’s TEI is 6.2 years, which is below the required 6.6 years. The requested waiver would suspend the requirement for last year, and with one more year of experience for Mann’s current teachers, the school will likely meet the TEI target for this year. For HQT, Mann had one teacher in the Fall CALPADS report listed as not HQT for teaching Arabic. This was an input error as the teacher had completed the required coursework and is certified to teach Arabic. CALPADS was changed to correct the error.
  • Montgomery will receive approximately $408,600 in 2013-14. One of the targets is achieving 100 percent employment of highly qualified teachers (HQT). For 2012-13, Montgomery had two teachers entered into the CALPADS system in the fall as not meeting requirements for HQT. Subsequently, both teachers have been determined to be HQT by examination. For 2012-13, Montgomery’s class size exceeded the QEIA target for no core class over 27 students. During the 2012-13 school year, 26 classes exceeded 27 and 8 were still above 27 at the end of the year. It is anticipated that Montgomery will meet its other QEIA targets this year, and with the HQT and class size requirements waived, Montgomery will continue to be eligible for funding.
  • Roosevelt will receive approximately $837,900 in 2013-14.  One of the targets is achieving 100 percent employment of highly qualified teachers (HQT). For 2012-13, Roosevelt had two teachers reported as not HQT in the Fall CALPADS report. One of those teachers was a co-teacher in a class to support special education teachers. This co-teacher did not need to meet the requirements for HQT because the co-teacher was placed in a classroom with a teacher who was HQT. The second teacher was HQT and qualified by examination but the documentation had not been entered by the Human Resources Department when the CALPADS report was submitted. Regarding the class size requirement, at the end of the 2012-13 school year, Roosevelt had five classes that were over the QEIA required limit of 27 students. QEIA class size reduction records are kept by the district’s office of Pupil Accounting and data are pulled from the student information system.  Special education students are counted in general education classes and may exceed the limit of 27 if there is a co-teacher assigned. All five classes had co-teachers. It is anticipated that Roosevelt will meet its other QEIA targets this year, and with the HQT and class size waived, Roosevelt will continue to be eligible for funding.

Upon board approval, required information will be available so that relevant data can be entered into the state’s online waiver system.

BACKGROUND: Taiko, though primarily known as a modern Japanese art form, contains both an ancient and a modern history. Taiko, which literally means "drum" in Japanese, stems back over 1000 years in Japan. They were originally used as military instruments and as a time keeper for daily village life. Modern Taiko, as it is known today in its ensemble (Kumi-daiko) art form, dates back only half a century to the post war Jazz musician Daihachi Oguchi. Oguchi was the first artist to bring Taiko together in an ensemble format, using multiple drums and rhythms within a single arrangement.

 

Since its origins 40 years ago, North American Taiko has taken off as a popular art form, fusing traditional drumming with many modern styles of music. Its power and dynamism has inspired the establishment of hundreds of groups across the continent including those on college and university campuses.

 

In January 2008, with the support of the Taiwanese American Community Center, collegiate Taiko alum Diana Wu organized an open workshop for the local San Diego community as a means to introduce and teach the basics of Taiko and formed the first Taiwanese American Sponsored Taiko drumming group, Naruwan Taiko. The Naruwan Taiko drumming group has become a part of the Barnard Asian Pacific Language Academy family through their regular performances on the campus.

1. Resolution Regarding Positive Behavior Interventions and Support Strategies as Alternatives to Out of School Suspensions and Expulsions to Increase Student Success (Foster/Beiser)
Meeting

Type
Action
Fiscal Impact

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt Resolution Regarding Positive Behavior Interventions and Support Strategies as Alternatives to Out of School Suspensions and Expulsions to Increase Student Success.

 

BACKGROUND: The San Diego Unified School District Board of Education endorses proactive positive support strategies to create a school climate that has alternatives to out of school suspensions and expulsions to increase student success overall and directs the Superintendent to take to scale a plan that promotes and implements proactive behavior strategies and supports as the preferred discipline method across San Diego Unified as the alternative to out of school suspensions and expulsions.

 

The board has a critical role to play in reshaping classroom, district, and community discipline practices and policies to create safe and supportive learning environments for students in line with the Board’s Vision 2020 Quality Schools in Every Neighborhood.

 

These environments should reduce out-of-school time, provide better support to teachers, staff and administrators to address disciplinary challenges, and engage parents and students in the development and implementation of more educationally sound and equitable policies and practices while fostering relationships with community-based organizations.

 

Nationally, African American students are over three and one-half times more likely to be suspended or expelled than their Caucasian peers. Furthermore, in districts that reported expulsions under zero-tolerance policies, Hispanic and African American students represent 45 percent of the student body, but 56 percent of the students expelled under such policies.

 

In 2013, San Diego Unified African American students comprised 10 percent of enrollment, but 25 percent of suspensions and 21 percent of expulsions, Hispanic students accounted for 46 percent of enrollment, but 55 percent of suspensions and 60 percent of expulsions.

 

It has been proven that higher absenteeism has a negative effect on academic performance, and students who are suspended or expelled are almost three times more likely to have contact with the juvenile justice system.

 

The Obama administration is now encouraging school administrators to end zero tolerance policies and instead encourage more training in classroom management, conflict resolution and approaches to de-escalate disruptive behavior.

 

Daniel A. Domenech, Executive Director of the School Superintendents Association, acknowledged that students of color were being suspended and expelled in disproportionate numbers, and said, “Superintendents recognize that out-of-school suspension is outdated and not in line with 21st Century education."

 

Willful defiance and minor disruption are broad behavioral categories that are not clearly defined but result in a large number of suspensions and expulsions, and it is recognized that a proactive tiered behavior approach would mediate many of the incidents.

 

Alternative disciplinary strategies such as Restorative Justice, Social-emotional Skill Curricula, and Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS) have shown promising results. These approaches encourage accountability and restore relationships, help students proactively learn and practice empathy, stress management and problem solving, and improve behavior and school climate.

 

Smarter school discipline strategies can also be more cost-effective. Limiting the overuse of suspensions and expulsions through smarter preventative steps will increase ADA and help San Diego Unified retain and maximize Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) dollars, close the opportunity gap, and avoid paying for extra years of schooling.

 

The focus should be shifted to creating a supportive school climate with community engagement, school partnerships, a strengthening of human capital and a careful integration of student support services.

 

In 2008, San Diego Unified began to implement additional positive school climate strategies at 15 sites, which has grown to 80 sites, such as Response to Instruction and Intervention process, and created a Uniform Discipline Policy in 2012, in an effort to address the concern that students are not learning if they are not in school.

RECOMMENDATION:  Approve proposed revisions to the district protest process for school impact fees, “Protest Process for San Diego Unified School District Fees on Development Projects” to reflect the current statute (Education Code 17620 et. seq.) and make the district procedure consistent with the requirements of state law. 

 

If approved, a residential project will no longer be able to appeal the superintendent's decision to the board.  Commercial and industrial projects will continue to have the opportunity to appeal to the board.

 

FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING:  Minimal - Staff will no longer prepare documentation for residential appeals to be heard by the board.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On February 3, 1987, the Board of Education adopted an appeal process for fees on development.  The process was last revised on October 6, 1987 and September 10, 1996.

 

The Legal Services Office has conducted a review of the process and has determined that it provides an appeal for residential projects than is not required by statute.

 

Under the current appeal process, an individual or developer may challenge the imposition of school impact fees by filing a protest (or claim) for either residential and commercial/industrial projects with the superintendent.  Recently, most protests have challenged the manner in which the City of San Diego calculates assessable space for residential projects.  The superintendent either grants or denies the claim. 

 

Past practice has allowed the claimant to appeal to the board for both residential and commercial/industrial projects.  Education Code 17620 et. seq. does not require the governing board to provide a process for the appeal of the superintendent’s decision for residential projects.  Education Code 17621(c)(2) states that for commercial or industrial development projects, the governing board shall provide a process that permits the party an opportunity for hearing to appeal that imposition; there is no requirement to hear residential appeals.

 

The proposed revisions to the district appeal process are being brought forward at this time to correct references to the appropriate Education Code sections and to make the procedure consistent with the requirements of state law.

 

If approved, a residential project will no longer be able to appeal the superintendent's decision to the board. Commercial and industrial projects will continue to have the opportunity to appeal the superintendent's decision to the board.
