RECOMMENDATION: Approve expansion of the October 6, 2009, eligibility criteria for charter schools to enter into Multiyear Facilities License Agreements (MFLAs) by allowing a charter school that is co-located with a district school to be considered for an MFLA under a limited exception.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: On October 6, 2009, the Board of Education adopted criteria to determine eligibility of charter schools, beginning with the 2010-11 school year, to enter into an MFLA for facilities on district sites.  Among other items, that criteria specifically stated that a charter school which shares a site with a district-run school would not be eligible for an MFLA.

 

Ten years later, district staff have re-evaluated the criteria and recommend that we allow a limited exception for a charter to be eligible for an MFLA as follows: "Charter school is not sharing a site with a district-run school or use, except where the construction of a new classroom building, through a charter school project approved by the Board of Education, has reached substantial completion. Site is defined by the small scale plot plans produced by the district’s Facilities Planning and Construction Department. A charter is eligible under this limited exception when the district is not projecting over the five-year term to expand its use of the same site into spaces allocated by the MFLA to the charter school."

 

District staff also proposes removing the eligibility criteria for an MFLA that “Charter school will have received an acceptable annual performance review by district staff in the last two years of operation" because it is obsolete.

 

Attachment 1 is a redline version of the current eligibility list, which was approved on October 6, 2009. Attachment 2 provides an updated version of the MFLA eligibility criteria, which includes the proposed modifications. The eligibility list is Exhibit A to the MFLA, a sample of which is included for reference as Attachment 3.

 

The rationale for allowing a co-located charter school to be eligible for an MFLA in this limited exception is to save the time and expense for both charter and district that would otherwise occur through the Proposition 39 process every year, to request use of the same newly constructed building on the same site.  An MFLA in this instance would also provide the charter school with a sense of permanency in the newly constructed building that the Board of Education already approved as a charter school project.

 

Thus far, the Board of Education has considered and approved a small number of co-located, new charter classroom building projects, which include San Diego Global Vision on the Adams Elementary campus, which is substantially completed, and Kavod Charter School on the Schweitzer Elementary campus, which is a Measure YY Planning and Project Application. The modified criteria would permit the district to consider offering an MFLA to these charter schools co-located with a district school or program, when the spaces included in the proposed MFLA were constructed as a charter new classroom project, previously approved by the Board of Education, and has achieved substantial completion, within the district’s discretion on a case by case basis as stated above.

 

Therefore, staff proposes that the expanded criterion be applied prospectively to future eligible applications, where the board determines that the district is not projecting over the five-year term to expand its use of the campus to include space allocated to the charter school in the MFLA. Staff also proposes the elimination of the obsolete criteria regarding annual performance review be applied prospectively.

 

The proposed modification of the eligibility criteria for charter schools to enter into MFLAs is not subject to review under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA) because the activity is not a project as defined by Section 15378(b)(5) of the State CEQA Guidelines. Approval of any future MFLAs are considered under CEQA as projects, and would be required to undergo CEQA environmental review subject to the requirements of the Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000, et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et seq., as amended).

BACKGROUND: On November 4, 2008, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition S, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,100,000,000.00. On November 6, 2012, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Proposition Z, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $2,800,000,000.00. On November 6, 2018, the voters of San Diego County approved, by more than the required 55 percent favorable vote, Measure YY, authorizing the issuance and sale of general obligation bonds, not to exceed $3,500,000,000.00.

 

The passage of Proposition 39 in November 2000, amended the California Constitution to include accountability provisions. Specifically, the district must conduct an annual independent performance audit to ensure that funds have been expended only on the specific projects listed in the bond project list, as well as an annual independent financial audit of the proceeds from the sale of the bond until all proceeds have been expended for facilities projects.

 

The results of the Fiscal Year 2018-19 Performance and Financial Audits were shared by Crowe LLP staff with the full Independent Citizens' Oversight Committee on November 21, 2019

 

In its financial audit, Crowe LLP found that the district’s financial statements "present fairly, in all material respects, the respective financial position of the San Diego Unified School District Proposition S General Obligation Bonds, Proposition Z General Obligation Bonds, and Measure YY General Obligation Bonds activity as of June 30, 2019, and the respective changes in financial position thereof for the year then ended in accordance with accounting principles generally accepted in the United States of America." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on the financial statements of a school bond program.

 

In its performance audit, Crowe LLP found that "in all significant respects, San Diego Unified School District met the objectives listed . . . and expended Propositions S and Z and Measure YY General Obligation Bond funds for the year ended June 30, 2019, only for the specific projects developed by the district's Governing Board and approved by the voters, in accordance with the requirements of Proposition 39, as specified by Section 1(b)(3)(C) of Article XIIIA of the California Constitution." This is the highest opinion that can be bestowed on an operational performance of a school bond program.

 

For the eleventh consecutive year, the district's capital bond program has received the highest opinion auditors can offer for both its financial and performance audits. For the sixth consecutive year, independent auditors made no adverse findings, a significant achievement for a bond program of this size and complexity.

CEQA ANALYSIS: The acquisition of 9330 Balboa Avenue, San Diego, CA, 92123 (Project Site) and subsequent 18-month leaseback of the property to Jack in the Box, Inc., to continue existing operations (Proposed Project), is considered a project under CEQA and is subject to the requirements of the Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000, et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15300.2 et seq., as amended). However, the Proposed Project is exempt from further environmental analysis consistent with CEQA Guidelines Section 15301 – Existing Facilities (Class 1). Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The key consideration is whether the Proposed Project involves negligible or no expansion of use.

 

The acquisition of the project site and 18-month leaseback to Jack in the Box, Inc., would not result in a change in land use, or the expansion of the existing use, when compared with existing conditions. Therefore, it can be seen with certainty that there is no possibility that Proposed Project would result in a significant environmental effect, and the application of a Class 1 exemption is appropriate.

 

The suggested environmental impacts of potential future development of the project site by the district are simply not reasonably foreseeable at this time, and evaluation of the impacts would be wholly speculative because no such development plan has been prepared or approved by the Board of Education. Prior to the implementation of future development of the project site by the district, a project specific environmental analysis, consistent with the CEQA Guidelines, will be undertaken and considered by the board for approval.

 

Exceptions to Use of a Categorical Exemption (CEQA Guidelines section 15300.2): The Proposed Project does not have the potential to trigger any of the exceptions identified in CEQA Guidelines §15300.2 prohibiting the use of a categorical exemption.

  1. Location. Classes 3, 4, 5, 6, and 11 are qualified by consideration of where the project is to be located – a project that is ordinarily insignificant in its impact on the environment may in a particularly sensitive environment be significant. Therefore, these classes are considered to apply in all instances, except where the project may impact on an environmental resource of hazardous or critical concern where designated, precisely mapped, and officially adopted pursuant to law by federal, state, or local agencies.
  2. Cumulative Impact. All exemptions for these classes are inapplicable when the cumulative impact of successive projects of the same type in the same place, over time is significant.
  3. Significant Effect. A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.
  4. Scenic Highways. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project which may result in damage to scenic resources, including but not limited to, trees, historic buildings, rock outcroppings, or similar resources, within a highway officially designated as a state scenic highway. This does not apply to improvements that are required as mitigation by an adopted negative declaration or certified EIR.
  5. Hazardous Waste Sites. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project located on a site that is included on any list compiled pursuant to section 65962.5 of the Government Code.
  6. Historical Resources. A categorical exemption shall not be used for a project that may cause a substantial adverse change in the significance of a historical resource.

BACKGROUND: These transactions are submitted to the board for review pursuant to Education Code sections 17604 and 17605 and under its delegation of authority to the district Strategic Sourcing and Contracts Officer.
