Teachers who hold a bachelor’s degree and completed an approved teacher preparation program leading to the issuance of a regular education credential or special education credential, but did not hold an English Learner authorization, qualify for an Emergency Crosscultural Language Academic Development (CLAD) Permit to cover them in their assignments while completing the requirements to obtain a Clear CLAD Certificate.

Pursuant to CTC leaflet CL-622 (9/10), Emergency CLAD permits may be reissued to an applicant four times authorizing a maximum of five years of service to complete the requirements for the English Learner/CLAD Certificate.

As a result of SB 374, the state holds each school accountable for developing and implementing a SPSA. The SPSA identifies the instructional needs of students and specifies how school funding, including categorical funds provided through the Consolidated Application, will be used to accomplish the goals outlined in the plan.

The SPSA is developed or modified through an annual planning cycle. The School Site Council (SSC), in collaboration with the site instructional leadership team, is responsible for developing, implementing, monitoring, and evaluating the SPSA. Area superintendents reviewed plans to ensure schools identified student needs and aligned their smart goals with appropriate courses of action to address those needs. Once approved by area superintendents, the plans were forwarded to the Monitoring and Accountability Reporting Department where staff reviewed each plan and provided individual assistance to schools to ensure that their SPSAs met state requirements and that the plans and associated categorical budget allocations were in alignment.

Prior to implementation, the local governing board must review and approve each school’s SPSA. This report brings forward for board approval the 2010–11 SPSAs and categorical budget summaries for 24 schools.

BACKGROUND: The downtown library school project has two main components.  The first component is the 40-year, $20 million pre-paid lease with the City of San Diego (City) to acquire two consecutive floors (sixth and seventh) of approximately 71,800 square feet in the downtown library.  The second component is the design and construction of the tenant improvements with an estimated cost of $10 million.

 

The City approved the lease agreement at its meeting on April 19, 2010, and the board approved the lease on April 27, 2010. The board had previously approved a non-binding Letter of Intent with the City on June 23, 2009, for a proposed charter school, which formed the basis for the lease. The lease payment was originally anticipated to come from a $20 million allocation of Proposition S funds which had been programmed into the bond to support new educational facilities in the downtown area.

 

Lease payments schedule

San Diego Unified will issue three payments of $5 million each at the beginning of construction, at 25 percent, and at 50 percent completion dates ($15 million). The district will then pay $3 million at 75 percent completion and $2 million at the end of construction for a total of $20 million.

 

Funding

On March 30, 2010, the board authorized the first $7 million of the lease payments to come from City Centre Development Corporation (CCDC) redevelopment funds in lieu of Proposition S. These payments are projected to be paid in the 2010-11 fiscal year. The $13 million balance of lease payments is planned to come from Proposition S in 2011-12. However, that amount will be offset by CCDC funds allocated to Proposition S capital projects at San Diego High School in 2012-13 and 2013-14 fiscal years.

 

The board subsequently approved an action item on July 13, 2010, setting aside $10 million of CCDC redevelopment funds in fiscal years 2011-12 and 2012-13 to cover the cost of the educational program tenant improvements and other non-lease costs associated with the development of the charter school within the library. This amount includes $500,000 to be deposited with the city for necessary building modifications made during construction of the library.

 

Funding Option

As previously stated, the original intent when the lease was being approved in April 2010, was for Proposition S to pay for the $20 million lease cost and CCDC redevelopment funds to pay for the $10 million tenant improvement costs. The March 30, 2010 board action shifted the lease funding from Proposition S to CCDC funds, freeing up Proposition S funds for future land acquisition, new school development, or capital improvements in the downtown area.

 

The board has the option to approve this item and shift all funding away from CCDC funds and back to Proposition S as follows:

 

Shift the initial $7 million dollars in CCDC lease payments to Proposition S in fiscal year 2010-11. Eliminate the $13 million in CCDC offsets to Proposition S capital projects in fiscal years 2012-13 and 2013-14.  Shift the $10 million dollars in CCDC educational program tenant improvement costs to Proposition S in fiscal years 2011-12 and 2012-13.

 

Fiscal Impact

The current favorable bidding climate has resulting in approximately $32 million in cost avoidance to deliver the required scope for projects that have been awarded to date. This under expenditure would typically be held in the program to offset potential future bid escalation during the balance of the program. The board can elect to use this under expenditure to help offset the schedule impact of shifting the downtown library funding in full to Prop S. This action would also fully complete the commitment to construct classrooms and schools in the downtown area under Prop S.  The effect of this funding shift on the schedule will be felt beginning in fiscal year 2013-14 and could result in a 3 month delay in program awards at that time, based on current cash flow projections. This action does not preclude the acceleration of the four track and artificial turf football field installations and the 11 whole site modernizations submitted for approval as a separate board item (I.3). BACKGROUND: At its January 25, 2011 and February 1, 2011 meetings, the board approved district staff recommendations for preliminary offers of facilities to eligible charter schools that made appropriate applications for facilities under the provisions of California Education Code section 47614 and California Code of Regulations Title 5, sections 11969 et seq.

 

Education Code section 47614 and the California Code of Regulations, Title 5 sections 11969 et seq. (“Regulations”), require that on or before April 1, districts must submit final offers of facilities to eligible charter schools, and on or before May 1, charter schools must respond to the final offers if they are to receive district facilities for the upcoming year.

 

District staff met with the eligible charter schools to discuss and refine the preliminary offers, and the final offers of facilities space are contained in this report.

 

Proposition 39 (Education Code section 47614) requires the district to offer reasonably equivalent facilities to charter schools that show that they are eligible to receive such facilities. Staff has analyzed each of the requests in conformity with the requirements of Education Code section 47614 and the implementing regulations and has developed recommendations for responding to each of the requests, as well as for the final offers of facilities for eligible charter schools. Staff is submitting this report and its recommendations for board approval and requests the board adopt the report as its statement of reasons.

BACKGROUND: On March 1, 2011, the board re-adopted the Board Governance Policies.  There were no changes to OE-10, Asset Protection.  On March 16, 2010, the board  approved the interpretations and indicators for OE-10, Asset Protection.  OE-10, Asset Protection provides that the superintendent will assure that all organizational assets are adequately protected, properly maintained, appropriately used and not placed at undue risk.

 

Last year, on April 27, 2010, the board approved the monitoring report on OE-10, Asset Protection, as compliant.

 

It is the board's responsibility to consider the monitoring report as evidence of district performance in this area of operations.  The board policy provides two criteria against which they are to judge the report: whether the board is satisfied that --

  1. the district and the superintendent are in compliance with the provisions of the policy or have disclosed procedures to establish compliance; or
  2. the information is sufficient to allow the board to decide.
