BACKGROUND: North Park Main Street, a 501(c)(6) non-profit organization, is proposing a new assessment district called the North Park Business & Improvement District (PBID).  In this type of assessment district, property owners pay an annual fee that funds public improvement and beautification services within the assessment district's boundaries.

 

The proposed PBID includes Jefferson Elementary School within its boundaries.

 

Proposed services to the entire area include increased lighting, landscape maintenance, graffiti abatement, tree planting, and sidewalk cleaning.

 

If approved, the PBID is anticipated to take effect in January 2015.  After five years, the PBID will automatically expire unless it is renewed by a new petition and affirmative vote of property owners.

 

Formation of the PBID is a multi-step process. At this stage, the board is being asked to approve a resolution in support of the PBID and authorize staff to execute an affirmative signature on a petition to form the PBID. If the property owners who will pay more than 50 percent of the assessments proposed sign the petition, then the proposed PBID may be taken to the City Council to adopt a resolution expressing intention to form a district. If the city council approves the resolution, a ballot will be sent to property owners in the boundaries for a formal vote to form the PBID.

 

Staff expects to receive a final Management Plan in April or May that outlines more specifically the services and benefit to be provided to the district. If the petition effort is successful, staff will complete an analysis of the plan and ask the board to consider a yes or no vote on the ballot.

 

The PBID would be administered by North Park Main Street and governed by an elected board with open meetings subject to the Brown Act. If the PBID is approved by property owners, there will be an election in fall 2014 for new voting members to serve on the governing board.

 

This resolution is being brought forward now at the request of North Park Main Street so that the board can provide input and feedback as the Management Plan is being developed.

 

Attached to this report are background documents provided by North Park Main Street, including a sample petition, timeline of activities, and general scope of proposed services.

BACKGROUND:  The district is preparing for the development of its 2014-15 Budget.  Fiscal assumptions with regard to 2014-15 revenues and expenditures are made based on historical trends, future commitments, the Governor’s Proposed Budget updated from January, and direction from the SDCOE.

 

On January 9, 2014, Governor Brown released his proposed budget for the 2014-15 fiscal year.

 

The Governor's 2014-15 Budget continues implementation of the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) with an infusion of $4.5 billion of additional Proposition 98 revenues. The new formula, adopted beginning with the 2013-14 state budget, provides funding to move all school districts and charter schools toward a system of school finance that allocates similar amounts per ADA as base grants within four grade spans, and provides additional percentage increases or "weights", for class-size reduction (CSR) in Grades Transitional Kindergarten-3, career-technical education (CTE) for Grades 9-12, and supplemental/concentration grants on behalf of students that are not English language proficient, who are from low-income families, or who are in foster care.

 

School districts and charter schools are entitled to supplemental increases equal to 20 percent of the base grant plus CSR/CTE adjustments for the percentage of enrolled students who are English learners, eligible for the Free and Reduced-Price Meals program, or in foster care. An additional 50 percent per-pupil increase is provided as a concentration grant for each eligible student enrolled that exceeds 55 percent of total enrollment.

 

LCFF dramatically changes the state's accountability system; moving us away from a system of rule compliance measured by audits and enforced through penalties to a system of accountability based upon local needs and measured by progress towards annual goals. The cornerstone of the accountability system is the Local Control Accountability Plan (LCAP). Governance and Operations:  Staff has visited Harriet Tubman Village Charter School annually. The annual site visit includes document review, classroom observations, and interviews of the charter faculty and leadership. During the annual site visit, as well as throughout the year, the areas of academics, governance and operations, and finance are monitored as part of the district’s oversight duties. 

 

Finances: Based on the Harriet Tubman Village Charter School budget, location, grade levels, and programs offered, the enrollment projections have been evaluated and appear to be within normal range compared to other charter schools and district schools.  The estimated California Department of Education (CDE) funding rates and Cost of Living Allowance (COLA) factor, provided by School Services of California’s Financial Projection Dartboard, have been used to estimate revenue for Harriet Tubman Village Charter School based on the projected Average Daily Attendance (ADA).  This revenue projection has been compared to the projected revenue included in the charter school petition budget and no significant variances have been found.  Other expenses unique to charter schools have also been examined and found to be comprehensive and appropriate.  Over the last four years, Harriet Tubman Village Charter School has addressed all required annual financial audit and reporting to the state and the district. 

 

Based on the projected enrollment, revised projected budget, expenses, and cash flow submitted in the renewal documents, Harriet Tubman Village Charter School has been found to be financially viable.  In addition, the district has examined whether Harriet Tubman Village Charter School maintains an adequate reserve for economic uncertainty greater than 3 percent of expenses or $50,000, whichever is greater, as another consideration of the school’s financial viability.  According to the financial documentation provided in its charter, Harriet Tubman Village Charter School has maintained an adequate reserve for economic uncertainty. At the end of 2012-13, per the school’s audit report, the ending fund balance of $946,051 exceeded the reserve requirement. 

 

Attached to this report is a table that compares the budget to actual expenses for each year of the charter.  The school’s actual expenses for each of the three years of operation (2010-11, 2011-12, and 2012-13) were all greater than budgeted amounts. The actual expenses for 2009-10 were less than the budgeted amounts. In summary, Harriet Tubman Village Charter School is financially sound.
