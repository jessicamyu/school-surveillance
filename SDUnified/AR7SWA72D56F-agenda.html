RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in support of Climate Science Literacy.

 

BACKGROUND: San Diego Unified is committed to protecting the environment for our families today and the future of the over 100,000 children we serve.

 

In 2015, the Board of Education adopted the Climate Action Plan, the major goals being zero net waste, 100% renewable energy by 2035, and quality schools in every neighborhood, so students can walk or bike their way to receive a world-class education.

 

The Board of Education has further expressed its determination that San Diego Unified should lead on climate change issues by approving 76 joint-use parks with the City of San Diego, increasing greens pace across our city, and approving 60 solar projects across the district, which allow us to produce more than 7.4 million kilowatt hours of clean, renewable electricity each year – a number we expect to double.

 

A firm understanding of the science behind climate change is essential to our students’ ability to take action to protect the planet and their own futures.

 

Climate literacy will also prepare our students for future academic success now that the Environmental Principles and Concepts (EP&Cs) of California are included in the 8th grade and High School California Science Test (CAST).

 

San Diego Unified has placed significant emphasis on early adoption of the Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS) because of their emphasis on climate literacy and advanced scientific concepts.

 

San Diego Unified is in year two of its three year NGSS transition and has 78 K-8 teachers that have been trained deeply in regards to content and pedagogy shifts for NGSS through the Early Implementation Initiative.

 

San Diego Unified has organized capacity-builder trainings to focus specifically on climate change and vertical instruction of climate change in secondary schools and these trainings have been attended by lead teachers at nearly all middle and high schools.

 

San Diego Unified has created a district science resource site with outlines of instructional materials to support teachers regarding NGSS climate standards and is in the process of writing instructional materials for NGSS.

 

Confronting climate change is central to our work as leaders of the second largest school system in the state and the eighth largest city in the nation.

Project SWELL (Stewardship: Water Education for Lifelong Leadership) is a completely free, standards-aligned K-6 science curriculum about the importance of San Diego County’s water and how their participation can help improve the health of our ocean and waterways.

In recent years, an increasing number of San Diego Unified students and teachers have participated in hand-on experiments, watershed models, marine debris activities, and storm drain field trips, as well as receiving Project SWELL presentations in classrooms throughout the District.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of support for Assembly Bill 1951: Pathways to College Act.

 

BACKGROUND: Under current law, students in grades 3-8 and 11 are assessed through the California Assessment of Student Performance and Progress (CAASPP) system.

 

In an effort to increase college-attendance rates, over 30 school districts in California administer college entrance exams to 11th grade students free of charge.

 

Administering the exam during the school day removes a barrier to college attendance for students who may not otherwise have access to the exam or take it on their own.

 

More studies across the nation are showing that providing assessments such as ACT or SAT at no cost to students during the school day helps set the conditions for overall increases in students enrolling in a 4-year college.

 

Federal law allows states, beginning in the 2017-18 school year, to authorize local educational agencies (LEAs) to administer a nationally recognized high school assessment in place of the state’s high school assessment.

 

AB 1951 provides the opportunity for more students to take college admissions tests thereby connecting more underrepresented students to scholarships, free test practice tools, and college application fee waivers.

 

The San Diego Unified School District places equity and opportunity at the core of our mission and AB 1951 would provide a choice which we believe will help break down barriers and reduce inequity in educational attainment.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve resolution in the matter of support for AB 2808: Local Control Funding Formula Funding Increase.

 

BACKGROUND: On 2013‑14, the state eliminated most categorical programs, replacing previous program-specific funding formulas with the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF).

 

LCFF is general purpose funding, which provides flexible funding to schools to address their district’s needs.

 

According to the United States Census Bureau, California’s state and local per-student spending is far below the national average.

 

In 2014, the publication EdWeek provided an estimate that found California’s per-student spending was $8,694 which is $3,462 below the national average of $12,156.

 

The governor proposes to fully fund the LCFF targets in 2018-19, a $2.9 billion increase over 2017-18 levels.

 

AB 2808 would increase per-student funding above the National average.

2. 2018-19 Civic Center Rate Schedule with Annual Incremental Rate Increases for an Additional Five Years; BUDGETED: N/A (Civic Center Cost Recovery)
Meeting
May 29, 2018 - Regular Meeting, 5:00 p.m.
Type
Action

RECOMMENDATION: Approve the 2018-19 Civic Center Rate Schedule (Attachment 1), which reflects the results of the Real Estate Department’s analysis (Attachment 2), which will adjust rates over the current rate schedule by a 22 percent (approximate) increase in cost recovery. The current Civic Center Rate Schedule only recovers approximately 54 percent of eligible costs. The new rates go into effect July 1, 2018 through June 30, 2019, with no changes applied to Free Use groups. Additionally, where appropriate, the District will incrementally increase rates at the beginning of each fiscal year over the next five years to achieve accurate full cost recovery by July 1, 2023. A new rate study is recommended every five years, beginning in 2023, to ensure fair and reasonable civic center rates that achieve full cost recovery.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: Current rates produce $3,518,754.00 in cost recovery. Approval of this item will result in approximately $1,440,024.00 of additional cost recovery over the previous year, if rental activity remains the same. Previously, the Civic Center Rate Schedule was adjusted and approved by the Board of Education periodically to reflect an increase equal to the Consumer Price Index (CPI). The last CPI adjustment took effect July 1, 2015.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: Historically, San Diego Unified’s (District) Civic Center Rate Schedule has been periodically adjusted to keep up with general cost inflation by applying a CPI factor, which is one of the most common approaches to maintaining fees and charges for local governments throughout California. Use of the CPI is a conservative approach to keeping up with cost inflation, as the costs of labor for local public agencies typically outpaces the CPI.

 

The District’s Real Estate Department retained NBS, a professional services firm with extensive experience in fee and rate analysis, to complete a comprehensive analysis of its Civic Center rates to understand the District’s direct costs related to Civic Center activity and to support the rate schedule. After extensive research and investigation, the NBS study produced the proposed attached rates within the Civic Center Act (Education Code, Section 38130-38139) allowable maximum rates.  

 

District staff is not recommending any change to Free Use groups. Free Use permits are subsidized by the District, but eligible for direct cost recovery in accordance with the Civic Center Act. Current Civic Center rates recover approximately 54 percent of what is eligible for recovery per the allowable guidelines of the Civic Center Act. The proposed 2018-19 Rate Schedule will increase cost recovery to the District by approximately $1,440,024.00 annually, if rental activity remains the same.

 

As shown in the NBS study (Attachment 2), the District is justified in charging more for some facilities. The staff-recommended Civic Center Rate Schedule indicates rates lower than the NBS allowable maximum rates to keep rental activity stable, and continue to make District sites accessible as community centers when not in use by District students.

 

If this item is approved, staff recommends new pricing go into effect July 1, 2018. Any rental permits issued from July 1, 2018 through June 30, 2019 will be at the new rates. Approval of this item will allow the District to annually increase the Civic Center Rate Schedule incrementally over the next five years, where appropriate, to achieve full cost recovery up to the next rental rate study, anticipated in 2023.
