RECOMMENDATION: As a service-providing organization, the San Diego Unified School District's employees are its most valuable resource. Today's recognition includes recognition of employees with 40, 30, 20, 15, 10 and 5 years of service. When the district returns to in-person work, each employee will receive a certificate and pin. The Superintendent and the Board of Education extend their most sincere gratitude to these individuals. 

 

Today, we celebrate milestones of 1,418 employees. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: As San Diego Unified celebrates progress towards meeting the inspirational goals contained in Vision 2020, we are taking time to celebrate those employees who have helped us achieve this progress. The number one predictor of student success is the presence of an experienced, high quality teacher in the classroom, according to the Learning Policy Institute. (Source: “Report: Teacher qualifications best predictor of student success,” By Amelia Harper, May 16, 2019, Education Dive). That study, which was written last year and co-authored by the Chair of the State Board of Education, found high quality teachers have helped San Diego schools do better than the rest of the state. (Source: “California’s Positive Outliers: Districts Beating the Odds,” by Anne Podolsky, Linda Darling-Hammond, Christopher Doss, and Sean Reardon, May 16, 2019, Learning Policy Institute). Educators also provide crucial services in addition to teaching. They are critical to keeping students safe at school. School staff also provide healthy, nutritious meals to children who do not have access to food at home. Nearly 100,000 schools/institutions serve school lunches to 29.8 million students each day, including 20.2 million free lunches. (Source: School Nutrition Association.) Given the clear association between the health and success of our students and the well-being and effectiveness of our workforce, we publicly recognize employees for their continuous years of service to the students, families and communities of San Diego Unified.  

 

*This service recognition award is not used to calculate or verify the seniority date of an employee nor is the recognition part of the formal personnel processes that calculate the date for longevity pay. Both longevity and seniority dates are dictated by the Education Code as well as the applicable collective bargaining agreement. The date used to determine this service award is based on the individual's permanent status as an employee, temporary or hourly work is not used in the determination of the service award recognition. Service recognition awards will be awarded on a monthly basis. If you believe an error has been made, you may contact the Human Resource Service Division by emailing jobs@sandi.net

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution recognizing October 2020, as Filipino American History Month for all schools and students in San Diego Unified. Filipino History Month is a time of reflection and remembrance, and provides an opportunity for all people to learn and appreciate Filipino Americans’ historic contributions with various activities and events to bring awareness of the significant role Filipinos have played in American history. Resources for teaching and learning about Filipino American History are available at http://fanhs-national.org/filam/.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None. 

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None. 

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: The celebration of Filipino American History Month in October of each year commemorates the first recorded presence of Filipinos in the continental United States, which occurred on October 18, 1587, when “Luzones Indios” came ashore from the Spanish galleon Nuestra Senora de Esperanza and landed at what is now Morro Bay, California. In 1992, the late Dr. Fred Cordova, along with his wife, Filipino American National Historical Society (FANHS) Founder Dr. Dorothy Laigo Cordova, first introduced October as Filipino American History Month with a resolution from the FANHS National Board of Trustees, and in 2009, U.S. Congress recognized October as Filipino American History Month in the United States. Filipino Americans are the second largest Asian American group in the nation and the third largest ethnic group in California, after Latinx and African Americans; and 5,251 students in the San Diego Unified School District are Filipino-American. In September 1988, Bell Junior High School was the largest junior high school west of the Mississippi, and had the largest concentration of Filipino students in the San Diego Unified School District. In 1989, the Filipino community rallied to advocate the offering of Filipino language classes at Morse High School and Bell Junior High School. Filipino language classes are currently offered at Challenger Middle School and Mira Mesa High School as well. Filipino Americans have contributed greatly to the fine arts, music, dance, literature, business, journalism, education, science, technology, government, politics, fashion, health care, the armed forces, and many other fields that enrich the landscape of the country; and efforts should continue to promote Filipino American history and culture, because the roles of Filipino Americans and other people of color have been overlooked in the writing, teaching, and learning of United States history.

RECOMMENDATION: Adopt resolution recognizing October 2020, as National Bullying Prevention Month in San Diego Unified and uses this opportunity to highlight our ongoing commitment to promote an improved school environment, enforce positive interactions between students, and raise awareness for teachers if a student is being bullied. The San Diego Unified School District offers lesson plans, video resources, and print resources through the Digital Cultural Awareness Center at culturalawarenesscenter.weebly.com and information about Ally Action Programming, bullying prevention, and bullying intervention at youthadvocacy.us. All elementary, middle, and high schools coordinate anti-bullying activities at the beginning of each new school year to be held in the month of October, during a week or days of their choosing.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

BACKGROUND: The National Bullying Prevention Month campaign was founded in 2006. San Diego Unified School District's Vision 2020 calls for all schools to be safe, inclusive neighborhood learning centers, and each quality neighborhood school to provide a “supportive environment that values diversity in the service of students.” School district leadership is committed to actively addressing bullying by utilizing updated and revised protocols and procedures, participating in innovative training, and effectively mitigating incidents of bullying and/or intimidation for all involved. San Diego Unified has partnered with the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and their No Place for Hate program which provides participating schools with anti-bias and anti-bullying resources to help create sustainable change that leads to an improved school environment. San Diego Unified has implemented programming to support schools in building positive school climates and a culture of upstanders through "Ally Action,” bullying prevention efforts, and student-led capacity building for peers as well as school site staff. The San Diego Unified Council of ASB Presidents fully supports October as National Bullying Prevention Month in which the schools may conduct anti-bullying activities within the month which are also conducted throughout the year. Students’ participation in anti-bullying activities provides opportunities for the following to occur:

  • Allow students to speak honestly about their experiences with bullying and find solace in the knowledge that others have been in similar situations.

  • Allow students to learn of the various forms of bullying which people may experience including, but not limited to, cyber, verbal, physical, and exclusion;

  • Help students become aware of bullying on campus and the consequences of bullying.

  • Support students to come up with solutions to stop bullying and enforce positive interactions between students.

  • Engage teachers and parents in raising awareness if a student is being bullied.

  • Connect with larger community and civic efforts to confront bullying.

  • Help empower students in the recognition of the role everyone plays in addressing and reducing bullying with the goal to eradicate bullying on all school campuses.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: The District Advisory Council (DAC) and the District English Learner Advisory Committee (DELAC) serve as the district's LCP advisory committees. The DAC Executive Committee was consulted on September 2, 2020, and the DELAC General Meeting was conducted on September 3, 2020 but did not reach quorum. Changes Since First Read:
The First Reading of the LCP was conducted on September 8, 2020. Following further stakeholder engagement efforts with district advisory groups as well as survey’s received, the district strengthened existing language within the plan as well as added additional language that was not included in the initial draft. 

Language has been added that addresses supports for underperforming students who do not receive targeted funding within the Supports for Pupils with Unique Needs section. Language has been added to address questions about students not utilizing their cameras during virtual class time. Information regarding Phase 1 planning is included in the plan addressing the question about TK and K starting sooner. (DAC)

Specificity has been added regarding online learning for students with disabilities, families having access to general education instructional material at home, flexibility regarding online learning schedules to best meet the needs for students and families. (CAC)

 

BACKGROUND:

 

Senate Bill (SB) 98 established that the Local Control and Accountability Plan (LCAP) and an annual update to the LCAP are not required for the 2020–21 school year and that the California Department of Education (CDE) shall not publish the California School Dashboard in December 2020 based on performance data on the state and local indicators. SB 98 establishes California EC Section 43509 and the Learning Continuity and Attendance Plan (Learning Continuity Plan) requirements for the 2020–21 school year.

Amplify Science 6-8 supports a model of continuous improvement through feedback to students provided through both formative and summative assessment tasks. These tasks are designed to measure what students know and are able to do and provides guidance for teachers on how to use scoring rubrics and interpret assessment results to guide instruction supports.

 

The materials recommended for adoption were selected from the state adoption list, and they were piloted by some middle schools during the 2019-20 school year through research grant funding. Teachers in the schools piloting the materials responded favorably to the resources provided and spoke highly of the alignment to NGSS and the ease of use for students. Amplify materials are designed to function completely in a digital environment; however, in order to support the transition to new curriculum, the current adoption includes both print and digital resources for year 1. Teacher guides are also provided in print and digital format. In subsequent years of the adoption schools that elect to continue to use print resource have the option of purchasing them.

 

Middle level science curriculum materials were last adopted in 2008 and the older materials are not aligned to NGSS. In order to advance achievement in science and to achieve continuity, integration, vertical alignment, and articulation of the curriculum, it is important to move forward with newly-adopted instructional materials aligned to the Next Generation Science Standards. The approval of the recommended instructional materials assures that teachers and students are provided with high quality instructional materials that meet both state and district standards.

 

All legal requirements have been met in accordance with California Education Code sections 60002, 60040-60048, 60052, 60200, 60210, and Board Policy and Administrative Regulation 6161.1.
