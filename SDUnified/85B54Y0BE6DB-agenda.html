A.03. Technology and Curriculum Workshop; BUDGETED: N/A (OE-8)
Meeting
Apr 6, 2010 - - Board Workshop, 9:00 A.M.
Type
Information
RECOMMENDATION: Staff will provice a technology integration update on the Proposition S Technology Integration Proposed Five-Year Implementation Plan. This i21 model is designed to systematically integrate technology in every classroom throughout the district by the end of the 2013-14 school year.

FISCAL IMPACT: This program will be fully funded through the use of Proposition S bond funds. The costs are estimated at approximately $30,000.00 per classroom and were previously approved by the Board.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: 2009-10 is the first year of the five-year program implementation.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: N/A

DISCUSSION: The board workshop will present an overview of the accomplishments in the first year of the i21 Technology program and highlights of other technology initiatives. We will discuss lessons learned through survey data, steering committee meetings, and recommendations for year two's implementation.

BACKGROUND: The 21st Century (i21) Interactive Classroom is an engaging interconnected learning environment designed to optimize student access and participation by integrating mobile computing, audio, visual and formative assessment technologies across the curriculum. The i21 classroom is both relevant and advanced in technology implementation that maximizes flexibility and provides just-in-time functionality for student learning.

The essential tools of the i21 classroom include the pairing of Interactive White Board (IWB) technology with student Netbooks to increase the ability to teach with technology and to optimize student access and engagement. These tools allow the teacher to configure the learning environment according to the context of the student-centric work at hand to meet state standards and learn 21st century skills as thinkers, creators, designers and builders.

The 21st Century (i21) Interactive Classroom Initiative is being implemented in the district using a multi-phased grade level approach that will allow students beginning in grades 3 and 6 to access a consistent and equitable 21st century learning environment as they move from one grade to the next. The implementation plan in Grades 9-12 uses a department by department model that will provide all students access to technology rich learning environments beginning the first year.

To ensure the successful integration of this technology investment into district classrooms, every professional development opportunity offered in the district will contain a thread on the use of 21st century classroom tools. As each grade level or department is equipped, teachers and administrators will receive training on all features of the new technology tools to ensure they are integrated into daily classroom teaching. Funding for professional development will be provided through “Enhancing Education Through Technology” formula funds and other training dollars available such as the stimulus program and Title 1. A detailed professional development plan has been developed with input from teachers, principals and other stakeholders.

A Multiple Pathways Program for students in the areas of information technology and computer science is also being developed as part of this program. The district’s Integrated Technology Support Services Department will assist in the development of this part of the program and will provide technical support for the student portion of the network.

RECOMMENDATION: Approve proposed organizational restructuring of the Research and Evaluation Department (RED) into the Integrated Technology Division.

FISCAL IMPACT: None, the Research and Evaluation Department remains as it is currently organized. There are no staffing reductions or increases proposed in this restructuring.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None, the Research and Evaluation Department remains as it is currently organized. There are no staffing reductions or increased proposed in this restructuring.

BACKGROUND: On March 16, 2010, the board discussed and approved with modifications the organizational structure for the district effective July 1, 2010. Part of the board's discussion centered around the belief that the Research and Evaluation department needed to be independent of the Deputy Superintendent for Academics. This item brings forward for board consideration and action alignment of the functions and data reports prepared and managed by Research and Evaluation with those data reports and systems managed by the Integrated Technology Division.

The merging of RED and ITSS has several positive outcomes and synergies, including the ability to increase the reliability of our data systems and data elements that are used to report on both the operations and academic functions of the district. Both departments already work very closely together in coordinating many aspects of information and assessment reporting, including CALPADS/CBEDS and other state and federal reporting, the implementation and support of Data Director, NWEA MAP and other disparate multiple-measure data sources, as well as ad-hoc data requests.

The attached memo further outlines the realignment and addresses concerns about direct line of reporting and safeguarding the staffing and focus of the RED team.

A.06. Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA) Progress Reporting Discussion and Board Direction on Next Steps, BUDGETED: N/A (OE-2)
Meeting
Apr 6, 2010 - - Board Workshop, 9:00 A.M.
Type
Information
RECOMMENDATION: Board members will discuss with the Interim Superintendent, Principals, School Improvement Officers, the Executive Director for Federal and Special Programs and staff how to best evaluate and assess progress made towards schools' Plans for Student Achievement.

The report to the board is in response to a request by the Board of Education members to provide a mid year update on the progress made towards meeting the educational goals outlined in the Single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA) at all district schools. The report will bring forward findings of an analysis of different assessments measuring progress made towards student achievement. Based on staff's initial findings, it is proposed that all schools use a minimum of specific and consistent common assessments to monitor student academic progress toward California’s Annual Measurable Objectives (AMOs) identified in the Local Educational Agency Plan (LEA) and the single Plan for Student Achievement (SPSA). In order for schools to monitor student progress and respond appropriately to student need, and for the district to monitor districtwide progress, it is recommended that a committee be established to review options and suggest specific universal measurement tools and goals for the SPSA. This districtwide committee should include central office staff and school principals from elementary and secondary levels.

FISCAL IMPACT: None.

PRIOR YEAR EXPENSE HISTORY: None.

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

BACKGROUND: California legislature requires schools that participate in state and federal categorical programs funded through the Consolidated Application process to create a Single Plan for Student Achievement. Its purpose is to “improve the academic performance of all students to the level of the performance goals, as established by the Academic Performance Index. [CA Ed Code Section 64001(a)]

Additionally, Annual Yearly Progress (AYP) is required under Title I of the federal No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001. Schools, LEAs and the state must meet all AYP criteria in order to meet federal NCLB accountability requirements.

The Board of Education approved the 2009-10 SPSAs on June 9, 2009. During this meeting a discussion was held about accountability in the monitoring of progress using strategies identified in the schools’ SPSA plans. Board members asked for a mid-year monitoring progress update on how schools are implementing their plans.

Each site provided a progress report using different assessments measuring progress made towards students meeting proficiency levels in English Language Arts (ELA) and Mathematics.

Elementary Schools utilized Safe Harbor which is an alternate method of meeting the AMOs.

K-8 and Middle Schools utilized data charts to report the SPSA SMART goals identified in the SPSA for ELA and Mathematics. These goals are measured by quarterly benchmarks to show the overall gain for schoolwide and all significant subgroups populations.

High Schools provided a mid-year progress report narrative to report progress towards student achievement.
