RECOMMENDATION:  This is a First Reading of the proposed 2017-18 Title I Eligibility Ranking for the district.  This report presents 2017-18 Title I Eligibility Ranking and school participation. There are fourteen schools that no longer meet the forty percent eligibility threshold. Staff recommend these fourteen schools be grandfathered for the 2017-18 school year.

 

FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: $15,500,000.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  The District Advisory Council (DAC) discussed the Title I ranking on November 16, 2016 and December 14, 2016.  Any updated information will be shared at the January 18, 2017 DAC General meeting. 

 

BACKGROUND: The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 was reauthorized as of December 10, 2015, as the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA).  Title I, one of several programs under ESSA, provides funds to schools to help disadvantaged children meet challenging academic standards.  The law intends that the funds go to the district and schools that have the highest concentrations of poverty.  Title I requires funding to be allocated to a district based on the number of "children in poverty" residing within the district boundary.  The federal census is used to identify children ages 5 through 17 living in poverty who are included in the calculation of Title I entitlement.

 

The proposed 2017-18 Title I Ranking report allocates Title I funding to all district schools with a 2016-17 meal eligibility percentage of 40 percent or higher and the fourteen additional schools recommended to be grandfathered.  The total 2016-17 Title I Entitlement is $37,937,634. Due to changes in ESSA guidelines and poverty decline based on 2015 California census data, the 2017-18 Title I Entitlement for San Diego Unified could potentially decrease. The projected 2017-18 Entitlement should be known in March 2017.

ACADEMICS

 

As the charter granting authority, pursuant to Education Code section 47607(a)(3)(A), the district shall consider increases in student academic achievement for all groups of students served by the charter school as the most important factor in determining whether to grant a charter renewal.

 

A charter school shall meet at least one of the following criteria before receiving a renewal:

  1. Attained its Academic Performance Index (API) growth target in the prior year or in two of the last three years both schoolwide and for all groups of pupils served by the charter school. 
  2. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API in the prior year or in two of the last three years. 
  3. Ranked in deciles 4 to 10, inclusive, on the API for a demographically comparable school in the prior year or in two of the last three years.  
  4. The entity that granted the charter determines that the academic performance of the charter school is at least equal to the academic performance of the public schools that the charter school pupils would otherwise have been required to attend, as well as the academic performance of the schools in the school district in which the charter school is located, taking into account the composition of the pupil population that is served at the charter school.  
  5. Qualified for an alternative accountability system pursuant to Education Code section 52052(h).

Assembly Bill (AB) 484 amended Education Code sections 52052(e)(2)(F) and 52052(e)(4) to allow schools that do not have an API calculated in 2013–14 and 2014–15 to use one of the following criteria to meet legislative and/or programmatic requirements:

  1. The most recent API calculation;
  2. An average of the three most recent annual API calculations; or
  3. Alternative measures that show increases in pupil academic achievement for all groups of pupils schoolwide and among significant groups. 

The decision to use one of these criteria may be made on a program by program basis and is a local decision.

The PBID will be formed pursuant to a state law that took effect in January of 1995. The “Property and Business Improvement District Law of 1994,” Streets and Highways Code sections 36600-36671.  In this type of assessment district, property owners subject to the assessment pay an annual fee that funds public improvement and beautification services within the assessment district's boundaries. If approved, the PBID is anticipated to take effect in January 2018.  After five years, the PBID will automatically expire unless it is renewed by a new petition and affirmative vote of property owners.

 

Formation of the PBID is a multi-step process.  If the property owners who will pay more than 50 percent of the assessments proposed sign the petition, then the proposed PBID may be taken to the City Council to adopt a resolution expressing intention to form a district. If the city council approves the resolution, a ballot will be sent to property owners in the boundaries for a formal vote to form the PBID.

 

The PBID boundaries generally include all parcels fronting 30th Street from Polk Avenue to Upas Street, along with all parcels fronting University Avenue from Arizona Street to 32nd Street. Blocks bound by University Avenue, Granada Avenue, North Park Way, and Herman Avenue are also included in the boundaries. The block bound by Lincoln Avenue, Ohio Street, University Avenue, and 30th Street is included along with the block bound by Lincoln Avenue, 32nd Street, University Avenue, and Iowa Street. In addition, a few outlying parcels have been included where the commercial core reaches past the fronting parcels. These parcels have been included in the district boundaries based upon their commercial nature. The proposed PBID includes Jefferson Elementary School within its boundaries.  Jefferson Elementary principal, Francisco Morga supports the proposed PBID.  See attached letter of support.

 

North Park Organization of Businesses, doing business as North Park Main Street (NPMS), a 501(c)(6), will serve as the Owners’ Association for the PBID.  Although NPMS is a private organization, the Owners’ Association is subject to government regulations relating to transparency, namely the Ralph M. Brown Act and the California Public Records Act, when conducting PBID related business and keeping PBID related records. Thus, meetings of NPMS’ Board and committees wherein PBID business is heard or conducted must be held in compliance with the public notice and other requirements of the Brown Act. With few limited exceptions as authorized by the Brown Act, meetings held pursuant to the Brown Act must be open to the public, including property owners. The Owners’ Association is also subject to the record keeping and disclosure requirements of the California Public Records Act for district-related records.

BACKGROUND:

 

Optional Area Phase-Out

At the April 21, 2015 board workshop (Logistics and Implementation of Quality Neighborhood Schools in Every Neighborhood), one of the “Concepts to Action” presented involved analyzing the district’s optional attendance areas through the lens of providing clear articulation patterns within clusters to strengthen neighborhood schools.

 

In most cases, the district’s neighborhoods are assigned to one elementary school, one middle school, and one high school.  However, there are a number of small areas which have more than one school assignment at one or all three school levels; these areas are called “Optional Areas.”  Some of these optional areas were created 40 to 50 years ago, and the reasons for their creation are sometimes unclear. 

 

The map in the attached report shows the location of optional areas in the district.  The maps shows that the phase out of optional areas has been a process: in 2012-13, there were 25 optional areas (counting each level - elementary, middle and high - as a separate option), and as of this year, there are 19. This report proposes phasing out eight of the remaining optional areas, beginning in 2017-18.  The remaining optional areas (in green on the map) will be analyzed for potential phase-out in future years, depending on enrollment trends in those areas and capacity considerations, among other factors.

 

The optional areas that are being proposed for phase out are those where one option is being chosen overwhelmingly.  Phasing out the less popular option will therefore result in a very small impact on school enrollments.  It is also proposed that any existing students in the optional areas in question can continue to attend the option school they have chosen, as can any upcoming siblings who would attend school with the older child concurrently; therefore, the phase-out will only affect new students.  

 

In most cases, the lesser option is being chosen by around 10 students.  New students who wish to attend the phased-out option in the future can apply through the district’s Choice program, and for 2017-18, given the shortened application timeline, any new residents who wish to choose the phased-out option will be able to do so by submitting a Choice application.  It is very likely that any student submitting a Choice application will be able to attend the phased-out school, given the small numbers involved.

 

Staff attended scheduled cluster meetings of the schools affected by the phase-out: Crawford Cluster - November 28, 2016; Point Loma Cluster - December 5, 2016; Hoover Cluster - December 5, 2016; Lincoln Cluster - November 14, 2016, with a follow-up presentation on December 12, 2016; and San Diego Cluster - November 14, 2016.

 

The major reason for phasing out optional areas include giving neighborhood schools clear feeder paths within clusters, equity for neighborhood schools so that all families have equal opportunity when applying through the Choice program, and focusing on optional areas where very small numbers of students were choosing of the options offered.

 

Grade Reconfigurations

There are only two district-run elementary schools which operate as K-6 schools, Emerson/Bandini and Webster.  They are unique within their respective clusters in that they are the only schools whose students do not have access to a three-grade-level, middle school education, entering Memorial Preparatory Academy and Millennial Tech Middle School respectively 7th grade, while other cluster elementary schools feed into the middle schools at 6th grade, accessing a number of electives that are only available at middle school.

 

Staff has met with both school communities, and as a result of the feedback received at these meetings, is recommending that Emerson/Bandini be reconfigured from Grades K-6 to Grades K-5 in 2017-18, and Webster be reconfigured from Grades K-6 to Grades K-5 in 2018-19.

The proposed activity is a project under the California Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), Public Resources Code (PRC) section 21000 et. seq., and its implementing Guidelines (California Code of Regulations, Title 14, Division 6, Chapter 3, sections 15000 et. seq., as amended). The project, however, is categorically exempt from expanded environmental review under Section 15330 – Minor Actions to Prevent, Minimize, Stabilize, Mitigate or Eliminate the Release or Threat of Release of Hazardous Waste or Hazardous Substances. On October 30, 2014, a Notice of Exemption for the project was filed with the Office of Planning and Research, State Clearinghouse and is included as Attachment A.
