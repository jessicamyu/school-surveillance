RECOMMENDATION: The Superintendent will provide information to the board and the public on Closing the Achievement Gap with High Expectations for All (Vision 2020 Quality Indicator and corresponding LCAP Goal 1).  This report will focus on outcomes for the Class of 2016, ensuring all members of the Class of 2017, are on track to graduate, and plans and support for the Class of 2020 including master schedule development and advisement to ensure success, and access to rigorous curriculum. 

 

Vision 2020 Quality Indicator Destination:  Every school is strongly committed to maintaining high expectations for all students and to eliminating persistent disparities in achievement and performance among student groups based on race, ethnicity, language, or disability.  These disparities are understood as unacceptable and a focused plan of support is in place to eliminate the significant achievement gaps in order to realize academic proficiency for all students.  All relevant policies and practices reflect the goals of eliminating achievement disparities and attaining academic proficiency, explicitly and emphatically.  A districtwide focus and effort supports the advancement of high levels of student achievement by ensuring that every school is culturally competent, ensures uniformly high expectations, promotes rigorous curricula, differentiates instruction, and maximizes access for all students to high-level educational opportunities.  The district actively seeks to hire a diverse cadre of educators and administrators, as well as ensure proper emphasis on culturally responsive practices and regularly examines and monitors student achievement, policies, programs, tools and written documents to ensure that achievement gap goals are implemented. All staff members understand how their respective positions contribute, directly or indirectly, to the implementation of the achievement gap goals.  Staff members understand and actively seek to learn the necessary knowledge and skills to serve diverse students and families.  Staff members assume responsibility for implementing plans that will address achievement disparities.  District and community stakeholders engage students and families, the private sector, faith-based groups, and higher education institutions to support all district students in achieving academic proficiency.

 

LCAP Goal 1:  All levels of the organization work to improve student achievement and close the achievement gap for all underperforming student groups.

 

Related LCAP Actions and Services:  

  • 1.3.a. All middle and high school students will have 6- or 4-year plans, respectively, that clearly articulate the courses students will complete to meet district graduation/'a-g'  requirements, and be prepared for college, career and community.
  • 1.3.b.1. Counselors and support staff will monitor student progress toward graduation and access to advanced pathways, with emphasis on English Learners (EL), students with disabilities, youth in transition, and African American and Latino students.  
  • 1.3.b.2. Central office and secondary school staffs will work collaboratively to identify and implement best practices that improve the outcomes for all Grade 9 students at every high school.  
  • 1.3.b.3. Schools with high percentages of students off-track to graduate will continue to have additional Grade 9 and 10 counselors to support students to remain on track and develop a college-going culture.  
  • 1.3.b.4. Increased counselor allocations will continue to be provided to all middle and high schools.  
  • 1.3.b.5. Area superintendents, principals, the Office of Secondary Schools, and counselors (along with the Special Education Department and Office of Language Acquisition [OLA] will work in partnership to ensure that appropriate structures, supports, and resources are in place for all students to access rigorous 'a-g' coursework, support and interventions, stay on track to graduate, and are prepared for college, career and community.  Well-articulated pathways for Grades 6-12 will be developed and designed to meet the continuum of needs for students to graduate ready for college, career and community.  
  • 2.1.1. Coherent and well-articulated programs and course pathways will be provided within and across all grade levels that lead to meaningful graduation for all students, in preparation for college, career and community.  
  • 2.1.2. The district will monitor master schedules to ensure access to 'a-g' courses for all high school students and all student groups.
  • 2.1.3. School staffs will support students and families to plan and monitor courses to prepare them for graduation and post-graduation opportunities (e.g., 4- and 6-year plans, Naviance).  
  • 2.2.1. All students will have access to advanced courses and learning opportunities (e.g., Advanced Placement [AP], International Baccalaureate [IB], Honors, college courses, and Gifted and Talented Education [GATE].

RECOMMENDATION: Conduct a public hearing and approve a resolution certifying that the district has provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks, instructional materials, or both. 

 

FISCAL IMPACT: The district may use funding from various sources, including state Local Control Funding Formula funds and state lottery funds to purchase standards-aligned textbooks and instructional materials. Newly adopted materials are provided based on actual enrollments the first year of an adoption. Schools are responsible for maintaining sufficiency during the remaining years of core adoptions by ordering from the district's Instructional Materials Office, using an Instructional Materials Order Form or by ordering textbooks and instructional materials using the Destiny Textbook Management System.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT: None.

 

IMPACT TO DISTRICT STAFFING: None.

 

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE: Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND: In accordance with Education Code 60119, each school district’s governing board must hold a noticed public hearing, no later than the eighth week of the school year, to determine whether each pupil in the district has sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials, or both, in the following subjects: mathematics, science, history-social science, and English language arts. The governing board must also determine whether each student enrolled in a foreign language or health course has been provided sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials. According to Williams Legislation, sufficient textbooks or instructional materials means that each pupil, including English language learners, must have a district-adopted textbook or instructional materials, or both, to use in class and to take home. However, the district does not have to provide each pupil with two sets of instructional materials.  The board is required to adopt a resolution setting forth its determination that there are sufficient standards-aligned textbooks or instructional materials as applicable. 

 

Current Williams legislation also requires that science classrooms have available basic science laboratory equipment for students in science laboratory courses offered in Grades 9 to 12.  The San Diego County Office of Education developed a science laboratory equipment worksheet to identify basic core, lab equipment for science laboratory courses.  All schools conducted inventories and principals verified and submitted a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”

 

Williams Settlement Legislation requires districts and schools to closely examine student access to textbooks and instructional materials and gather evidence to use as the basis of the district's annual resolution of sufficiency.  Administrative Circulars 9, 10, and 11 dated August 17, 2016, were posted notifying school sites (not subject to a Williams site visit) of the need to conduct a school site inventory and complete a “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency.”  In September 2016, instructional materials inventory data and current PowerSchool enrollment for September 2, 2016, was compiled and entered into the “Instructional Materials Sufficiency Form” and provided to each elementary school by the Instructional Materials Office.  Sites were directed to verify their instructional materials inventory and take action to remedy any insufficiencies by ordering from the Instructional Materials Office.  Principals confirmed instructional materials sufficiency and returned the “Principal Verification Statement of Instructional Materials Sufficiency” to the Instructional Materials Office by September 16, 2016. Secondary school principals, after completing their inventories and taking action to remedy any insufficiencies, also returned the “Principal Verification of Instructional Materials Sufficiency." 

 

Sufficiency has been verified prior to the eighth week of school. Because of the cancellation of the October 11, 2016 board meeting and the requirement for a ten-day Notice of a Public Hearing, the next available board meeting date is October 25, 2016.  Board certification at this meeting, in the ninth week of the school year, is in violation of Education Code 60119.  The California Department of Education was contacted for direction and verified the violation, further stating that the district showed due diligence in complying with the legislation and if audited, would most likely be found to have an audit exception in this case.  Education Code 41344.4 states that districts are no longer subject to monetary fines or withholding of state funds when audit exceptions are corrected or a plan has been implemented.  Approval of this resolution demonstrates correction of an audit exception within four days of the violation.

 

Twenty-five elementary schools, five K-8 schools, seven middle schools, and six senior high schools were visited by a team from San Diego County Office of Education.  All 43 schools were found to have sufficient instructional materials.  Additionally, all six senior high schools were found to have adequate laboratory equipment for the science laboratory courses offered.

BACKGROUND:  On October 14, 2014, the board approved an agreement with Follett Software Company to provide the software license, implementation services, maintenance, support, peripherals, and hardware for its Destiny Resource Management Solution.  The district has relied on three distinct components of Destiny: Library Manager, Textbook Manager, and Media Manager:

  • Library Manager:  Destiny manages print and digital library and instructional resources in all district site libraries. Library Manager supports the instructional program by giving students, staff, and parents the access to each school’s print library collection. As an instructional tool, Library Manager provides equitable access to quality digital resources (ProQuest, World Book Online, Teen Health and Wellness, TeachingBooks.net, Safari Montage videos, etc.) and online reputable websites. Library Manager is a one-stop portal for students and staff to access authentic, rigorous research and reference information.
     
  • Textbook Manager:  Destiny Textbook Manager provides the district with an efficient and effective way to reduce instructional materials costs, circulate and track textbooks, and ensure every student has the materials needed to meet the requirements of the Williams legislation.  Follett has customized the delivery and transfer features within Textbook Manager to support the district's unique standards and requests.
     
  • Media Manager:  This media management system makes it convenient for district teachers to easily schedule the delivery of instructional media from the Instructional Media Center (IMC) to their classrooms. Media Manager keeps track of inventory to help reduce the amount of lost materials. It also features tools to create shared instructional resource lists.

The Digital Student Device Inventory Manager has been added for tracking student devices.  This new service is needed to efficiently track digital student devices, i.e tablets, laptops, iPads, etc.  With the district's 1: 1 initiative to provide students with digital devices to use in the classroom and take home, this component has become critical to effectively track and circulate digital student devices, and provide accurate reporting capabilities.

 

On May 31, 2016, the district received one Request for Proposal (RFP) submission to provide the required districtwide library, textbook, media, and digital student device inventory management system.  The RFP was received from Follett School Solutions, Inc., which was determined to be a responsive and responsible Proposer.  The district's evaluation committee, consisting of Toren Allen, Applications Director; Rose Gustafson, Material Control Manager; Martha Karanopoulos, Supervising Instructional Materials Specialist; Anne Mealiffe, Instructional Materials Specialist; Kelly O'Brien, Central Office Resource Teacher; Vicki Ranes, Cataloging Specialist; Chris Woehler, Manager Instructional Materials; and Jessica Youngs, Systems Development Coordinator, recommends that it is in the best interest of the district to award Follett School Solutions, Inc. as the district's library, textbook, media, and digital student device inventory management system provider.

FISCAL IMPACT:  Total not-to-exceed amount:  $13,500.00.  Budget string:  5388-90622-00-5801-2150-0000-01000-4240.

 

PRIOR YEAR FISCAL IMPACT:  None.

CONSULTATION WITH BOARD ADVISORY COMMITTEE:  Not applicable.

 

BACKGROUND:  A.M.A.S. - Mindfulness, Adaptability, and Sustainability in American Sign Language (ASL) provides Mindfulness training to support students' emotional and academic intelligence.  Mindfulness can help to lower stress and build resilience.  Deaf students live in a hearing world where it is a constant struggle to access communication both in academic and social settings.  The on-site training uses research-based practices delivered in ASL.  Benefits of the training include: increased attention, cooperation, positivity, empathy and compassion and a decrease in stress, acting out behaviors, aggression, anxiety and impulsivity.  Mindfulness Based-Stress Reduction (MBSR) provides healthy coping tools and skills for students, teachers and staff to utilize in and out of the classroom to better manage their lives and increase their daily feeling of well being. 

 

This Agreement will include approximately 37 high school students attending Madison High School as well as training for teachers and support staff at the site.  All sessions will be conducted in American Sign Language.  The training will be funded through the Buchanan Parsons Trust (BPT) which must approve all expenditures under the trust. The proposal for these services was approved by the BPT Committee on June 6, 2016.

A Restorative Community Conference (RCC) is a face-to-face, problem-solving meeting between people who were harmed (victims), the people who caused that harm, and other affected community members.  Led by trained and experienced RCC facilitators, affected persons discuss the harm caused, ways to repair the damage, and actions to prevent further harm.  The responsible youth is held accountable by having to face his/her victim(s).  Together, the group creates an action plan for the youth to repair the damage done to the victim, the community, the family, and themselves.  Once the youth fulfills the RCC plan in a timely manner, charges are dismissed.
