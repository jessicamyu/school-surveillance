June 1, 2015 – Work Session No. 120

 

 

Subject:

Advanced Academic Programs Advisory Committee (AAPAC) Annual Report

 

School Board Action Required:  Discussion

 

Related To:                   State Requirement

 

Key Points:

 

AAPAC did a careful analysis on the number of students identified for Level IV services and the reason for the growth in the program over the past ten years with the following findings:

  • The combined impact of population growth and increased pressures on teachers to differentiate to meet the needs of a broad spectrum of students has led to an increase in the number of students referred for Level IV services.
  • The percentage of students found Level IV center eligible has been remarkably consistent.
  • The increase in numbers is largely attributed to three key items for consideration:
  1. Community recognition of the value of the Level IV advanced academic curriculum and the teaching pedagogy,
  2. A demand for more advanced academic services by parents referring their students for additional screening, and
  3. Greater awareness of the program by previously underserved communities. 

AAPAC collaborated with Facilities Planning Advisory Committee (FPAC) to develop a decision framework with criteria for establishing new Level IV programs.

  • AAPAC used the framework to analyze the Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) and make recommendations.
  • AAPAC recommends that FCPS use this framework for determining when a new Level IV center should be established.

Critical success factors when opening a new center include:

  •  Professional development and teacher qualification
  •  Fidelity of Implementation (FOI) of the AAP curriculum
  • An academic cohort of students and teaching professionals trained in the AAP pedagogy.

The committee will address the School Board charge and present recommendations from the committee as documented in the attachments.

Subject:

Advisory Council for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) Report

 

School Board Action Required:  Discussion

 

Key Points:

 

School Board Annual Charge – Executive Functioning Skills

 

Examine current practices in FCPS regarding life skills training for all students with high or low incidence disabilities. The emphasis should be on how schools are teaching Executive Functioning skills and aligning their curriculum/course work/classes/electives with Student Achievement Goal 2, so students are prepared for post-secondary education and/or employment opportunities and can live independently with success.

 

Recommendations – Subcommittee on Low Incidence Students with Disabilities

  1. FCPS mandate social workers to attend the ARC Transition series and then do the turnaround training for the school staff with a primary focus on waivers, SSI, and guardianship.
  2. FCPS enhance its outreach efforts to parents of low incidence students in an attempt to make sure the parents are made aware of available resources, including Career Transition Services, as early as possible in their child’s education. In addition, include a parent resource fact sheet that describes the suggested timing of services as well as community based resources. This would be distributed at IEP meetings no later than when the child is in 6th grade.
  3. The management of Davis and Pulley Centers establish a working group to share ideas and educational best practices to foster information sharing and collaboration among the staff. This would also promote consistency in services between the centers.
  4. Based upon the noted effectiveness of the use of mindfulness with respect to self-regulation and its subsequent effect on Executive Functioning, it is requested that FCPS work with/advise its current vendors of Mindfulness programs to adapt their curriculum to meet the needs of the low incidence population and to extend its availability to such programs to all low incidence special education students.

Recommendations – Subcommittee on Middle Incidence Students with Disabilities

The ACSD recommends that FCPS implement a robust Executive Functioning curriculum based upon and using the program adopted at Mountain View in at least one high school, one middle school, and one elementary school in each pyramid, with the plan to expand the program to other schools, using the initial schools as models.

 

Recommendations – Subcommittee on High Incidence Students with Disabilities

  1. FCPS disseminate a matrix of available Executive Functioning programs and curriculum to educators and parents and ensure access to Executive Functioning programming to all students who could benefit.
  2. Parents and caregivers are most effective as partners with educators when they have the information and tools to be the best informed advocates possible. As the Committee witnessed with the public dissemination by FCPS of the “descriptions of specialized reading programs” for students with high and low incidence disabilities, all partners benefit when they have a shared understanding of available interventions.
  3. FCPS offer workshops and educational seminars to train its staff on the effectiveness of Executive Functioning education and to ensure continuity of service among teachers, programs, grades and schools.
  4. FCPS leadership provides clear and explicit guidance to schools on how to embed and integrate Executive Functioning curriculum and skill development into existing school and classroom intervention strategies and avoid a separate initiative approach.
  5. FCPS employ on-line and assistive technologies (AT) to support improvement of Executive Functioning skills for all students and build the capacity for every IEP team to independently consider AT at every IEP meeting.
    1. As noted above, there are a wide variety of online and other technologies available to support Executive Functioning instruction and reinforcement for students, staff and families alike. It is critical that assistive technologies that support so many students with disabilities in accessing academic curricula be exploited for their benefit in developing Executive Functioning skills.
    2. One specific recommendation is that FCPS create a High Incidence Accessible Technology (HIAT) collaborative team that applies the principles of universal design for learning to support school teams to meet the needs of all students, with a mission to provide training, resources and consultation to build the capacity of classroom environments to incorporate technology options for all students.
  6. FCPS explore the creation of a target Executive Functioning program for high functioning learners who have autism spectrum disorders and to students with attention deficit hyperactivity disorders (ADHD).
    1. FCPS should examine proven programs that tightly focus on the unique needs of students with average and above-average abilities, but for whom Executive Functioning and related social skill deficits serve as a monumental barrier to post-school success.
