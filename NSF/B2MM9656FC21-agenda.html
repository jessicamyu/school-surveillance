Read Across America Day – March 2, 2019

 

Education Association's (NEA) Read Across America, now in its 20th year, is an annual reading motivation and awareness program that calls for every child in every community to celebrate reading on or around Dr. Seuss's birthday. This year’s celebration is based on the theme, “Grab your Hat and Read with the Cat!" to encourage students to delve into books to learn as much as possible while becoming lifelong readers. In cities and towns across the nation, teachers, teenagers, librarians, politicians, actors, athletes, parents, grandparents, and others develop NEA's Read Across America activities to bring reading excitement to children of all ages.

 

 

National Foreign Language Week, March 3-9, 2019

 

The week of March 3-9, 2019, has been designated as National Foreign Language Week. The Alpha Mu Gamma foreign language honor society has sponsored National Foreign Language Week since 1957, and the theme for 2019 is “Languages are the Key to the World”. Schools are encouraged to use this opportunity to highlight the value of learning world languages and learning about other cultures, and to celebrate the successes of world language students and teachers.

Black History Month

February 2019

 

 WHEREAS, the month of February is the time that we as a nation recognize Black History Month; and the Fairfax County School System is an educational institution designed to further the education of children from kindergarten through high school preparing them for adulthood, productive and healthy lives; and

 WHEREAS, the first Africans arrived on the shores of Virginia 400 years ago, some of whom were indentured servants who established a community and began contributing to the founding of this nation and all children benefit from gaining a full knowledge of the contributions of their fellow Americans; and

 WHEREAS, the institution of slavery in this continent commenced out of economic convenience to gain work from those of African descent without compensation; and the institution of slavery was codified into Virginia law arising from such lawsuits as Johnson v. Parker, which allowed an employer to convert the status of an African indentured servant to that of a slave; and

 WHEREAS, the history of African Americans in Virginia and the nation is currently discussed largely in terms of slavery; and African American contributions are an integral part of Virginia History; Africans came to the shores of Virginia having developed skills that were used to enrich our state and our Nation; and these Africans came from rich cultures, traditions and loving families; and

 WHEREAS, African Americans have participated in the founding of this nation and its culture despite the obstacles of slavery, discrimination, and institutionalized patterns of disparity, including such great Americans as Crispus Attucks, who was one of many African American patriots who fought in the Revolutionary War; Phillis Wheatley a well-known early American poet; Benjamin Banneker, a mathematician, astronomer, and an early architect of our nation’s capital; Charles Drew a doctor who established the procedure for the removal of plasma and blood transfusions; as well as, Mary Jackson, a native Virginian who worked as a mathematician and aerospace engineer. Ms. Jackson was featured in the movie Hidden Figures and is credited with helping to launch the first American satellite; and

 WHEREAS, those of African descent have lived in and contributed to the development and advancement of Fairfax County since its inception; as evidenced by the establishment of communities and organizations as early as 1833 including:

  1. The Tinner Hill Heritage Foundation which was established to preserve the rich cultural heritage of pioneering African Americans who fought racial segregation and established the nations’ first rural branch of the NAACP in a Northern Virginia neighborhood where Africans bought land and worked to build a positive community environment for all of its citizens; and,
  2. The Gum Springs community established in 1833 by West Ford, a former slave who obtained land to provide a sanctuary for free and self-emancipated Africans who then built their school, homes, and their church; and

 WHEREAS, Fairfax County is home to such great African Americans as Supervisor Catherine Hudgins who was elected in 1999 and has been recognized for her outstanding dedication and pioneering initiatives to foster and build inclusive, quality communities for all;

 NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County Public Schools hereby officially recognizes Black History Month in appreciation for the contributions of great Americans who have worked tirelessly and often without recognition for the advancement of our State and Our Nation.

National Social Work Month - March 2019

 

March is National Social Work Month and School Social Worker week is March 4th-8th. This year’s National Association of Social Workers theme is “Elevate Social Work”. School Social workers are trained to examine and address life’s problems in a holistic way. They empower students and families, giving them the ability to solve problems, cope with personal roadblocks and access the resources they need to succeed.

 

Social workers tackle some of the toughest challenges facing our society. In every city and every community they develop solutions to increase access to both mental health and physical health care, reduce poverty, eliminate injustices and protect vulnerable children from harm. The services of school social workers are needed now more than ever as they invest their time and expertise to promote equity and address barriers to learning. School social workers play a vital role in helping students meet the academic, social, and emotional challenges on the road to becoming successful and productive young adults.

 

School social workers are valuable members of school multidisciplinary teams along with providing a wide range of services to students, parents and faculty. School social workers are professionally trained to understand the needs of students who present with difficulties that interfere with learning and have expertise in many areas including mental health, human development and behavior, trauma and family systems. They increase student engagement by fostering positive relationships and creating hope and opportunity for youth and families in need.

 

School Social workers in Fairfax County Public Schools use their training, experience, and skills to provide services to children, families, and school staff members in an effort to remove personal and environmental obstacles for optimal student academic performance. They are committed to helping every child achieve, promoting academic success and mental health wellness for all.

National Athletic Training Month - March 2019

 

March is National Athletic Training Month and this year’s theme is “ATS Are Health Care,” an appropriate slogan reflecting the important role certified athletic trainers employed by Fairfax County Public Schools play in keeping our students safe. Through educational programs for students, parents and coaches, pre-season sports screenings and emergency planning, these nationally certified and state licensed medical professionals are dedicated to promoting the health and safety of our student-athletes before, during and after practices and competitions.

 

The athletic healthcare services provided by certified athletic trainers include the prevention, recognition, evaluation, treatment, and rehabilitation of athletic injuries and illness. FCPS continues to be recognized as a leader in adolescent injury epidemiology research and sports-related concussion management for high school athletes. Our athletic trainers work collaboratively with parents, school staff and community medical providers to promote safe participation for over 27,000 athletic participants each year. In fact, during the 2017-18 school year, over 9,600 individual students received care from an FCPS Athletic Trainer, accounting for over 78,000 total visits to our 25 athletic training facilities.

 

FCPS is nationally recognized as a leader among high school athletic training programs. In 2017, each FCPS high school was once again designated as a 1st Team Safe Sports School through the National Athletic Trainers’ Association, the second time all 25 schools have been recognized in this fashion.

RESOLUTION HONORING WOMEN’S HISTORY MONTH AND THE INTERNATIONAL WOMEN’S DAY

 

WHEREAS, the struggle for voting rights for women, or suffrage, began in 1848 at a women’s rights convention held in Seneca Falls, New York, and was led by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott and spread across the country while facing opposition and jail time for supporters, some of whom were incarcerated in Lorton, Virginia; and

 

 

WHEREAS, the Lorton corrections facility, known as the Occoquan Workhouse, housed seventy-two women suffragists, many of whom were force-fed as hunger strikers, and would go on to set the course of the nation, as they marched in Washington, D.C., demanding the vote, despite public outrage, ridicule, imprisonment, and abuse; and

 

 

WHEREAS, heroes like Susan B. Anthony, who traveled the country giving speeches to persuade people to support women’s right to vote; Alice Paul, who led demonstrations, hunger strikes, and picketed the White House and was jailed for her efforts, but whose leadership was instrumental to the passage of the 19th Amendment in 1920, giving women the right to vote, and Ellen Glasgow, a Pulitzer Prize-winning novelist from Virginia whose writings promoted women’s right to vote, all played an integral role in our history; and

 

 

WHEREAS, the Turning Point Suffragist Memorial will be built in Occoquan Regional Park, Fairfax County, Virginia, part of the historic prison grounds where the infamous Occoquan Workhouse was located to honor the scores of Silent Sentinel suffragists aged 19 to 73 who were unjustly imprisoned there, were humiliated, kept in inhumane conditions, locked in solitary confinement and severely beaten; and

 

 

WHEREAS, the centennial anniversary of the passage of the 19th Amendment is approaching in 2020; and March is Women’s History Month, with March 8 recognized as International Women’s Day; this is a time to honor the struggles of the brave women who dedicated much of their lives to gaining the right to vote in this country;

 

 

NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board, on behalf of the Fairfax County Commission for Women and all its residents, hereby proclaims March 2019 as Women’s History Month, and a time to remember and honor the countless women who fought relentlessly for the rights of all women in our great Nation.

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

FCPS implemented the new strategic plan, Ignite, during school year 2014-15. Since that time, refinements have been made to increase the clarity of the plan’s elements. During school year 2017-18, a variety of stakeholders (School Board, Leadership Team, principals, teachers, students, and the community) provided input to prioritize the outcomes in each of the four goal areas.

 

The result of this work is a streamlined Strategic Plan with eight areas of focus for school years 2018-2020. These areas of focus have specific strategic actions to move the Division toward its goals. Each strategic action has targeted metrics to show the Division’s current baseline data, an interim target to achieve by school year 2020, and an aspirational statement that describes what success would look like for students, families, employees, and the community.

 

At this work session, the Board will have an opportunity to review enhancements that have been made to the Strategic Plan since the November 19, 2018, School Board meeting.
