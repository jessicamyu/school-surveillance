National Dyslexia Awareness Month, October 2015

 

October is National Dyslexia Awareness Month.  Dyslexia is a language-based learning disability that results in people having difficulties learning to read, spell, write and pronounce words. Recognizing the markers of dyslexia and intervening early helps to provide opportunities for future success. This month and next, the Office of Special Education Instruction will be offering several opportunities to help raise awareness about dyslexia.  On Tuesday, October 20 and Friday, November 13, parent workshops will be held on "Strategies to Support Secondary Students with Dyslexia" and “Strategies to Support Elementary Students with Dyslexia”.  These workshops will be held at the Parent Resource Center and will highlight strategies to support reading, writing and math at home; ways to promote effective study skills; and strategies to promote self-advocacy skills. Please check our FCPS web site for information http://www.fcps.edu/dss/osp/prc/index.shtml.

 

The School Board will formally recognize National Dyslexia Awareness Month at the October 22 Regular School board Meeting.

 

 

National School Lunch Week, October 12-16, 2015

Using the theme School Lunch Snapshot, the 2015 National School Lunch Week will encourage school-based staff members to share the best, real images of school lunches today, including fresh fruits and veggies, whole grains, milk, and happy students. The 2015 event will encourage school food professionals to use social media to spread the word about healthy school lunches.  The National School Lunch program is dedicated to the health and well-being of our nation’s children, and has been joined through the years by many other excellent child nutrition programs. There is a continued need for nutrition education and awareness of the value of school nutrition programs and the School Board encourages all residents to become aware and concerned about their children's, and their own, nutrition habits in hopes of achieving a more healthful citizenry for today and the future.

 

Teen Read Week, October 18-24, 2015

 

 Teen Read Week encourages teens to go beyond the traditional book and explore poetry, audiobooks, books about music, and more at their school and local library.  The 2015 theme Get Away@your library encourages teens to escape their daily routines and pick up something fun to read – fantasy novels, science fiction, beach reads, travel books, or tales of adventure.  Teen Read Week has been observed since 1998 in an effort to engage teens in reading for pleasure and fun and to use the services at the library.  Research shows that teens who read for fun have better test scores and are more likely to succeed in the workforce.  The School Board encourages all FCPS teenage students to take advantage of the resources in their school and public library and to become lifelong readers and learners.

 

Mental Health Awareness Week, October 4-10, 2015

 

During the first full week of October, the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) and participants across the country are bringing awareness to mental illness.  Each year NAMI works to fight the stigma of mental illness, provide support, educate the public,  and advocate for equal care.  NAMI believes that these issues are important to address year round, but highlighting these issues during Mental Health Awareness Week provides a time for people to come together and display the passion and strength of those working to improve the lives of the tens of millions of Americans affected by mental illness.  This year’s theme is I am stigma free. Being Stigma Free means learning about and educating others on mental illness, focusing on connecting with people to see each other as individuals and not a diagnosis, and most importantly, taking action on mental health issues.  The hashtag for the theme is #IAmStigmaFree.

National School Bus Safety Week, October 19-23, 2015

 

 The week of October 19-23 has been designated as National School Bus Safety Week. Based on the theme Be Smart—Be Seen, I wait in a safe place!  the week focuses on student safety when waiting for and boarding a school bus.  School bus safety is an issue for all citizens of Fairfax County, who need to be aware of laws requiring them to stop and wait behind a school bus when it is loading and unloading student riders.

The major responsibility for school bus safety in Fairfax County rests firmly in the hands of our school bus drivers and attendants, who daily transport more than 110,000 students in over 1,500 school buses on more than 6,500 separate routes traveling nearly 17 million operational miles each year.

Approve adjustments to school attendance areas, effective for the 2016-17 school year in order to balance enrollment and utilization of school facilities pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.7, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings.

 

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  October 8, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 5

 

Subject:                  Proposed Freedom Hill/Vienna Expedited Boundary Adjustment

 

School Board Action Required:  Action

 

 

Background:

 

An expedited boundary study for Freedom Hill and Vienna Elementary Schools has been initiated by the Region 1 and 2 Assistant Superintendents and the Department of Facilities and Transportation Services.  The purpose of the boundary study is to align the feeder pattern of students who live in the Town of Vienna to the Madison High School Pyramid to maximize efficiency.  Guidance has been provided to give careful consideration to minimizing impact on demographics, specifically English Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) and the Free and Reduced Meals (FRM) programs.

 

Key Points:

 

The study area is a section of the Freedom Hill ES attendance area that is situated in the Town of Vienna.  Students in this study area attend Freedom Hill ES which is located in the Marshall HS pyramid, and then they attend Thoreau MS and Madison HS in the Madison HS pyramid.

 

As of September 30, 2014, 26 students reside in this Freedom Hill ES study area.  Seven of these students attend Vienna ES as a result of a student transfer.  Five of these students attend Westbriar ES and Colvin Run ES to access the Advanced Academic Program (AAP) Centers at these sites.  Five of these students are designated as English Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) and five of these students are designated as receiving Free and Reduced Meals (FRM).

 

Freedom Hill ES has 640 students and is overcapacity by 28 students. According to the Spring 2015 student enrollment projections, Freedom Hill ES is projected to be overcapacity by 142 students by SY2020-21.  Vienna ES has 427 students and is overcapacity by six students.  According to the Spring 2015 student enrollment projections, Vienna ES is projected to be overcapacity by 35 students by SY2020-21.

 

 

Discussion:

 

In June 2015, staff proposed four options to the community.  Option 1 proposed the reassignment of students in the study area from Freedom Hill ES to Vienna ES in September 2015.  Sixth grade students would remain at Freedom Hill ES.  Option 2 proposed “status quo” which involved no adjustment of attendance areas.  Option 3 proposed a phase-in process, beginning with rising kindergarteners in September 2015, along with flexibility for school assignments of siblings.  Option 4 proposed the reassignment of students in the study area from Freedom Hill ES to Vienna ES in September 2016.

 

In general, staff found that the community was supportive of the reassignment of students in the study area from Freedom Hill ES to Vienna ES effective September 2016.  Feedback was received about students who live in the study area who receive Local Level IV (school-based) AAP services at Freedom Hill ES.  Feedback was also received about AAP Center-Eligible students who are assigned from Freedom Hill ES to the newly established Westbriar ES AAP Center, which is being phased in grade by grade, year by year from Colvin Run ES.  Vienna ES students who are AAP Center-Eligible are assigned to Louise Archer ES. 

 

Families in the study area from Freedom Hill ES who currently receive School Age Child Care (SACC) services will continue to receive these services at Vienna ES when the boundary change is made.

 

The middle and high school assignments of students in the study area would remain the same.  This boundary adjustment would align students in the study area to the Madison High School Pyramid.

 

Capacity relief will be needed at Vienna ES through interior modifications.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve adjustments to school attendance areas, effective for the 2016-17 school year in order to balance enrollment and utilization of school facilities pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.7, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings.

Subject:                       Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

 

Key Points:

 

The Fairfax County Board of Supervisors adopted on July 1, 2015, the current version of the Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution (“Purchasing Resolution”), following legislative changes resulting from the 2015 session of the General Assembly. Historically, the Fairfax County School Board also has annually adopted the newly revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, following its adoption by the Board of Supervisors.

 

Code Changes

 

  1. Cooperative Procurement. Authorizes use of contracts solicited and established by the Metropolitan Washington Council of Governments. This increases the number of cooperative purchasing agreements available to FCPS, providing greater access to competitively-priced goods and services. Page 5, Section 5.

 

  1. Eliminates the requirement to create a written justification for the file prior to using competitive negotiations. Prior to this change, a written justification was required before using competitive negotiations to purchase goods and services. As a result of this change, written justifications are no longer required. Page 14, Section 2 – Methods of Procurement.

 

  1. Request for Proposal Scoring. The numerical scoring system and weight assigned to individual components of a proposal must be included in the Request for Proposal.  Prior to this change, this information was normally developed after Request for Proposals were issued and prior to the evaluation of proposals. This change is designed to provide offerors an opportunity to better structure and align their proposals with the critical factors that will be used to evaluate proposals. This change also requires that the weight or point values be included in the Request for Proposal or publicly posted prior to the due date and time for receipt of proposals. Page 14, Section 2 – Methods of Procurement.

 

FCPS Conditions to the Purchasing Resolution:

 

As was done in connection with the School Board’s adoption of the 2009, 2010, 2011, 2012, 2013, and 2014 Purchasing Resolutions, school system staff recommends that the School Board adopt the 2015 Purchasing Resolution, subject to the following two conditions:

  1. The “FCPS Construction Exception,” which is set forth in Article 1, Section 3(A)(2.) and Article 3, Section 1(2.) of the Purchasing Resolution and which is by definition an exception to the Purchasing Resolution, shall be revised to read as follows:

“The Fairfax County School Board shall be responsible for construction, related architectural and engineering services, related consulting services, maintenance, repair, and related services in connection with building, furnishing, equipping, renovating, maintaining, and operating the buildings and property of the school division in accordance with §22.1-79 of the Code of Virginia.  The Fairfax County School Board’s procurement, award, and execution of contracts under this provision shall be in accordance with the policies and regulations of the school division and with the Code of Virginia, including the VPPA.” 

 

[Note:  The following text is presently contained in the above-referenced Articles of the Purchasing Resolution adopted by the BOS, but this text would be excluded from the School Board’s adoption because it inaccurately describes the Superintendent’s authority:  “The school division’s Superintendent or his designee shall have the same authority as the County Purchasing Agent to execute and administer contracts.  Execution of contracts under this section shall be conducted under the rules and regulations established by the Fairfax County School Board in accordance with the mandatory sections in the Code of Virginia.”]

  1. The Fairfax County School Board reserves the right to procure and award contracts for goods, services, and insurance pursuant to its own policies and regulations and pursuant to the Code of Virginia, including the VPPA, where such is in the best interest of the school division.

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopts the newly revised 2015 Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, previously adopted by the Board of Supervisors, subject to the conditions detailed in the agenda item.

Meeting Category: October 8, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 5

 

Subject: Virginia Retirement System (VRS) Resolution

 

School Board Action Required: Information 

 

Related to: Legal Requirement

 

 

 

Key Points:  

 

Currently, employees who participate in the VRS Hybrid Retirement Plan direct both the mandatory and voluntary portions of the defined contribution (DC) component to the VRS deferred compensation plan.    

 

With the 2015 legislation, Senate Bill 1162 and House Bill 2178, the School Board has an option to create an employer-sponsored plan for the voluntary contributions.  The participants can then choose to direct their voluntary contributions to this newly created employer-sponsored plan.  The legislation requires that the School Board adopt a resolution (delivered no later than October 30, 2015) to either:   

 

  • Allow eligible employees the option to direct the voluntary portions of their contributions to an employer sponsored hybrid 403(b) plan; or
  • Not offer this option, so that both the mandatory and voluntary contributions continue to be directed into one plan, the VRS Hybrid 457 Deferred Compensation Plan

 

This election can be revisited on an annual basis.

 

Should FCPS elect to offer an employer-sponsored plan, the following are some of the challenges:

 

  • Meeting a November 1, 2015 deadline
  • Challenging communication requirements
  • Administering multiple 403(b) participant groups (hybrid vs. non-hybrid)
  • Overpaying VRS employer defined benefit contribution with lagged reimbursement
  • Increased exposure with additional fiduciary liability
  • Increased resource requirements for a small portion of our employee population.

 

Additionally, employees who elect to participate in an employer-sponsored plan will:

  • Be required to research an additional vendor to make an election to direct the voluntary contributions to the employer-sponsored plan or to remain with the VRS deferred compensation plan. This is also an annual election for employees, requiring an annual open enrollment activity for this plan alone.
  • Be subject to additional fees and complexity as the FCPS plan election would be in addition to, and not replace, the VRS defined contribution component
  • Lose access to a consolidated view of their primary retirement benefit

 

At this time, we do not recommend an employer-sponsored plan due to the additional complexity and expense of creating another level of investment under the VRS Hybrid Retirement Plan.  This allows the current process to be in place where both the mandatory and voluntary portions continue to be directed to the VRS deferred compensation plan.

 

Recommendation: 

That the School Board pass the resolution not to elect using an employer-sponsored hybrid 403(b) plan.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
