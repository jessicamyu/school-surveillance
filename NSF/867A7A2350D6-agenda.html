National Career and Technical Education Month - February

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from registered nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (office administration and technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (merchandising, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle school, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

National School Counseling Week - February 2-6, 2009

National School Counseling Week 2009, "School Counselors: Making a Difference," will be celebrated from February 2 -6 to focus public attention on the unique contribution of professional school counselors within U.S. school systems. School counselors are employed in public and private schools to help students reach their full potentials, and school counselors help parents focus on ways to further the educational, personal, and social growth of their children. School counselors are actively committed to helping students explore their abilities, strengths, interests, and talents as these traits relate to career awareness and development. School counselors also seek to identify and use community resources that can enhance and complement comprehensive school counseling programs and help students become productive members of society.

5.03 School Board Policy - Adopt Policy 1375, Charity Drives, as reviewed and discussed at the Policy work session (S; work session held 1/12/09; presented as new business 1/22/09)
Meeting
Feb 5, 2009 - Regular Meeting No. 13
Category
5. Consent Agenda - 11 p.m.
Type

Staff Contact: Anne Murphy, Division Counsel

Meeting Type: February 5, 2009 - Regular Meeting No.13

SUBJECT: School Board Policy

TYPE: Action

RELATED TO: Goal 3

BACKGROUND:

At the October 23, 2008 forum, Ms. Wilson recommended the amendment of Policy 1375 to facilitate the approval of student-initiated charity fundraising, including international charities. Other Board members requested information about the charity fund-raising practices of area local jurisdictions. They also requested guidelines that staff could use to assess charities prior to approving them for fundraising drives.

KEY POINTS:
    • The attached revisions to Policy 1375 clarify that student initiated fund-raising relating to Goal 3 will be permitted. They also authorize fundraising for international as well as local organizations.
    • The on-line policies of Montgomery, Arlington, Loudon, Alexandria and Prince William schools had no or very little information on this topic, e.g. Arlington and Alexandria permit charity drives with the approval of the principal, but provide no other guidance. A nation-wide email survey of NSBA in-house lawyers also produced limited responses. A few jurisdictions that permitted charity fund-raising had had problems with insuring compliance with accounting rules, actual fraud, or aggressive recruiting of students and staff by non profits.
    • Consistent with the Board’s request, staff investigated potential guidelines for schools’ use in assessing the bona fides of potential recipient charities. Examples of such guidelines included section 501(c)(3) status under the Internal Revenue Code, accreditation under the Better Business Bureau’s charity accountability guidelines, inclusion on the “approved” list of a designated charity umbrella organization such as United Way or Combined Federal Campaign, and/or exclusion from the U.S. Department of State list of terrorist organizations.
    • Leadership Team members, however, and particularly principals, recommended against requiring all recipient charities to comply with such guidelines, for several reasons. First, some potential recipients of school fund-raising efforts (such as needy families, area disaster victims, or smaller nontraditional charities) are unlikely to qualify under such guidelines. Moreover, students are increasingly taking the lead to identify and develop their own relief projects and the proposed recipients may be nontraditional entities who will not qualify under federal and related guidelines. Maintaining Policy 1375’s existing approval system will allow principals to determine whether 501 (c)(3) and/or accredited charity status is important to a given fund drive, while also providing them the flexibility to approve or disapprove fund-raising efforts for non-traditional recipients.

OPTIONS:
    1. Leave policy as is
    2. Approve amended policy (see attachment)
    3. Amend policy to provide more detailed guidance and/or to reflect other School Board priorities

RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board approve Policy 1375.3, Charity Drives, as reviewed and discussed at the Policy work session.

Attachmentment: Policy 1375.3, Charity Drives

Staff Contact: Dan Storck, Chairman and Mount Vernon District

Meeting Type: February 5, 2009 – Regular Meeting No. 13

SUBJECT: Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee

TYPE: New Business

BACKGROUND:

The Chairman of the School Board and the Vice Chairman of the Board of Supervisors have recently had discussions about the establishment of a joint County/School Board committee that would pursue cost savings and/or improved County services by streamlining, combining, or consolidating separate County/School functions. As a result of the grim budget situation, there is an increasing need and demand for public services with far fewer funds available to provide them, and the situation is likely to worsen. There has not been any joint Board-level review of combining or streamlining functions in more than ten years, and both Boards have a strong commitment to shared values. A 4 to 6 member joint Board of Supervisors /School Board committee would be appointed to investigate and recommend to both Boards any immediate and long-term actions to better align governmental responsibilities and resources.

The charge of this joint committee would include: 1) Review existing County and School operations and identify immediate and longer-term combinations or improved alignments of resources, responsibilities or programs; 2) Review County and School equipment and facilities and identify immediate and longer-term options for streamlining, consolidations or integration; and 3) Be bold in identifying policy options where changes have the potential for improved services or availability or decreased costs.

Potential opportunities for change would include SACC/middle school after-school program, school health and safety (including public health nurses, clinic aides, SRO, and crossing guards), community schools, and transportation

RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board appoint School Board members Brad Center, Dan Storck, and Janie Strauss to the newly-established joint Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee, which will begin meeting by the end of January 2009 and will report back to a joint meeting of the Boards on opportunities and next steps for any immediate and long-term actions to better align governmental responsibilities and resources.
