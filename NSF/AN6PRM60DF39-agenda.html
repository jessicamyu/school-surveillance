Food Allergy Awareness Week - May 13-19

 

Created in 1998 by the Food Allergy Research & Education Inc., Food allergy awareness week shines a spotlight on the seriousness of food allergies and helps to improve public understanding of this potentially life threatening medical condition.  By increasing awareness, we can encourage respect, promote safety, and improve the quality of life of all those affected by food allergies and anaphylaxis.  Food allergies affect up to 15 million Americans, including nearly 6 million children, roughly two in every classroom.  Nearly 30 percent of children with food allergies are allergic to more than one food.  Every three minutes, a food allergy reaction sends someone to the emergency room in the U.S. You can learn more about food allergies and anaphylaxis by visiting the Food Allergy Research and education website at www.foodallergy.org.

RESOLUTION NAMING THE OBSERVATORY AT MCLEAN HIGH SCHOOL

"DEAN HOWARTH OBSERVATORY"

 

 

   WHEREAS, Dean Howarth, after graduating from Florence Township Memorial High School in Florence, New Jersey in 1983, attended Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University, where he earned a Bachelor of Science in Education while majoring in Physics, graduating from Virginia Tech in 1987; and

 

   WHEREAS, Mr. Howarth had a remarkable teaching career from 1987 until his retirement in 2018, with each of his 3 1 years spent teaching Physics and Astronomy at McLean High School, while also serving as a Junior Varsity Softball Coach and the sponsor of the Astronomy Club, Project Enlightenment, and It's Academic; and

 

   WHEREAS, Mr. Howarth served as a guide and mentor for students by consistently challenging them to think analytically about the world around them, its past, and its future as sponsor of McLean's historical reenactment club Project Enlightenment, a group that provided students an opportunity to share multiple perspectives on the past with members of the public both young and old; and

 

   WHEREAS, Mr. Howarth was consistently regarded with esteem by his students and colleagues for his regular use of captivating interactive labs and demonstrations that engaged students while allowing their creativity to flourish; and

 

   WHEREAS, Mr. Howarth worked tirelessly and joyfully to perform the "behind the scenes" work required to ensure the success of the McLean High School astronomy program, including the development of the curriculum for the Astronomy course, acquisition of funds for the maintenance & repair of the observatory, and orchestration of community-wide observation nights;

 

   NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board names the observatory at McLean High School the "Dean Howarth Observatory" in honor of this exemplary educator, colleague, friend, citizen, and mentor.

On behalf of the Fairfax County School Board, it is an honor for me to recognize the Fairfax County Alumnae Chapter (FCAC) of Delta Sigma Theta Sorority.

 

Founded in 1913, Delta Sigma Theta Sorority, Inc. is a private non-profit organization dedicated to providing services and programs that promote human welfare including economic development, educational development, international awareness and involvement, political awareness and involvement, and physical and mental health.   The Fairfax County Alumnae Chapter (FCAC) of Delta Sigma Theta Sorority, chartered in 1993, provides services and programs that enhance the quality of life and social welfare of Fairfax County residents.  FCAC will celebrate its 25th year Anniversary on May 23, 2018.  Since its chartering, FCAC has implemented many impactful service projects for students across Fairfax County to ensure their academic success.

 

Some of the ways FCAC has been actively involved in our community include: Road to Success Luncheons for High School Students, which prepare students for academic, social, and financial transition from high school to college; various mentoring programs to provide students with tools and experiences to enrich and sharpen their educational skills and to prepare them to be leaders; partnerships with local organizations such as Reston Community Center to provide prom dresses and accessories for high school students at no cost, and the Fairfax County Department of Family Services Parenting Education Program to provide dinners and babysitting services; and participation in numerous school backpack drives and donation efforts. Since 1993, FCAC has awarded $320,000 in scholarships to Fairfax County High School Senior Girls.  On May 20, 2018, at its 25th Anniversary Luncheon, FCAC we will award an additional $27,000 in scholarships to 9 Fairfax County Senior girls.

 

FCAC is proud to have former and current Fairfax County Public School Teachers and Administrators as members of this chapter. Congratulations on your 25th year anniversary!

Recognition of National Adult and Continuing Education Month - May 2018

The Adult and Community Education (ACE) program of Fairfax County Public Schools began with an action by the School Board in 1955 and, in the over 60 years of the program’s existence, thousands of adults have learned essential skills and completed their high school educations.

Through the years, adults have learned new languages, acquired job skills, transitioned to new careers, and grown as workers, citizens, and parents. The Fairfax County community has looked to ACE to help develop our workforce, provide solutions to community problems through education, and create a culture of lifelong learning for residents of all ages.

Adult and Community Education has made major contributions to the school system as a laboratory of innovation for the piloting of new programs and services in support of PreK-12 education through family literacy, summer programs, extended day programs, enrichment courses, and driver education.

Through creative programming and targeted advocacy for the adult learner, as well as providing enrichment opportunities for youth beyond the school day, ACE has helped to create a dynamic learning community for all generations in Fairfax County.

On behalf of the Fairfax County School Board, it is an honor for me to recognize Chantilly Academy - a Governor’s STEM Academy - team for winning Virginia’s first place award in the Open Division of CyberPatriot, the Air Force Association’s National Youth Cyber Defense Competition.

 

Led by coaching staff  Davi Anson and George Bennett, the Chantilly Academy team is made up of Adam Batori, Jay Ghosh, Min Jun Kim, Jonathan Lu, Ian Shiff, and Shaun Wu, all of whom attend Chantilly High School. The team was praised for demonstrating outstanding teamwork, critical thinking skills, and technical knowledge, traits acknowledged by CyberPatriot as key to a successful career in cybersecurity. It was named a Platinum Tier team, representing the highest scoring team in its division.

 

The National Youth Cyber Defense Competition is designed to excite, educate, and motivate students toward careers in cyber security and other (STEM) disciplines. The teams in this competition are challenged to find and resolve cybersecurity vulnerabilities in simulated environments.

 

This achievement is a testament to your talent, dedication, and hard work. We wish you continued success for the remainder of the school year and all the best in your future endeavors!

Ignite Link: Goal 4- Resource Stewardship

 

Summary/Background (Key Points): FY 2019 Approved Budget

 

FY 2019 Advertised Budget

On February 8, 2018, the School Board adopted the FY19 Advertised Budget that included a requested increase of $97.1 million, or 4.9% in the county transfer. Overall, the FY19 Advertised Budget showed a net increase of $118.9 million or 4.3%.

 

Impact of State Budget to FCPS

The State budget process has not yet concluded. FCPS state revenue projections have been updated since the Advertised budget by increasing State aid $8.7 million and an increase of $5.0 million in sales tax. Overall, the FCPS FY19 State revenue projections are consistent with those seen in the Governor’s Introduced Budget and almost all increases are due to enrollment, re-benchmarking and recalculation of the LCI. Thus, it is unlikely we would see any reductions to State revenue. Once the State budget process concludes, any additional State funds allocated to FCPS will be brought back to the School Board at a quarterly review as a revision to the FY19 Approved Budget.

 

Impact of County Budget

On May 1, 2018, the County Board of Supervisors adopted their FY19 Budget that included an increase in the transfer to the School Operating Fund of $84.74 million or 4.3% over the FY18 Approved Budget. The County acknowledged the FCPS increase in State aid of $8.7 million and the increase of $5.0 million in the Sales tax assumption. These changes allowed the County Board of Supervisors to reduce the transfer by $12.4 million. Thus, the FY19 Approved Budget is fully funded.

 

May 1, 2018 Budget Work Session

As presented at the May 1, 2018 work session, there is no shortfall in the FY19 Budget, after accounting for the State and County budget adjustments. Note that the revisions to State revenue total $13.7 million coupled with a County reduction of $12.4 million result in a $1.3 million revenue surplus. The revenue surplus will cover the net increase in other adjustments, discussed at length at the May 1, 2018 work session, and detailed below. The other adjustments primarily include the spring enrollment forecast and other minor increases and decreases in selected programs.
