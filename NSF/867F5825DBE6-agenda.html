SUBJECT: Planning for FY 2009 Budget Development & Deficit Approach

TYPE: Discussion

BACKGROUND:
      On July 30, 2007, FCPS presented an updated FY 2009 Fiscal Forecast to the School Board and the County Board of Supervisors based upon an assumption of a zero percent county transfer increase which was included in the FY 2009 budget guidelines advanced by the Board of Supervisors. The fiscal forecast accounted for estimated baseline costs associated with increased costs for health and retirement, inflation, worker’s compensation, insurance, GASB, and other commitments earmarked for program expansion. It also included a 3.0 percent market scale adjustment for all employees resulting in a deficit of $145.2 million once all liabilities were considered.

      At the October 15 School Board Work Session, the chief financial officer provided revised estimates on the costs for health, retirement, insurance, and projected year-end balances. The total adjustment to the original forecast amounted to $40.2 million which decreased the forecasted deficit to $105.0 million. Eliminating the 3.0percent market scale adjustment and the planned commitments for the replacement of the student information system (SASI) and the expansion of programs such as Teacher Leadership, Foreign Language in the Elementary Schools (FLES), and Full-Day Kindergarten (FDK) would reduce the deficit to $22.9 million.

      The Superintendent then introduced various budget reduction proposals that would potentially offset the remaining deficit and sought the Board’s guidance on what core values to consider in approaching the FY 2009 budget reduction exercise.

KEY POINTS:
      At the October 15 work session, the School Board identified a number of key priorities that should be protected from adverse budget reductions: SASI, FLES, FDK, maintaining core class sizes in our schools, and providing a market scale adjustment (COLA) for employees. Funding SASI ($5.7 million), FLES ($2.1 million), Full-Day Kindergarten ($5.7 million), and a systemwide 3.0percent COLA increases the deficit to $98.8 million.

      In addition, the FY 2009 student membership projection increased significantly over the FY 2008 approved level requiring $10.0 million in increased funding. It is also important to note that other baseline budget requirements and unknown variables for enrollment may impact on the final FY 2009 Proposed Budget.

      In order to eliminate the budget gap, the Superintendent, Leadership Team, and school principals across all levels engaged in a deliberative and thoughtful process to solve the looming deficit and recommended a number of budgetary proposals for consideration during the budget process.

OPTIONS:
    For the purposes of this discussion, we are seeking guidance from the School Board regarding the following options the Superintendent is considering in preparation of his proposed FY 2009 budget:
    1. Fund SASI, FLES, FDK, and a 3.0percent COLA (Attachment 1)– Revised budget assumptions would fund a 3.0 percent systemwide COLA for all employees ($52.4 million), provide funding for staffing to support projected enrollment growth ($10.0 million), and provide expansion funding for SASI ($5.7 million), FLES ($2.0 million), and FDK ($5.7 million) increasing the deficit from $22.9 million to $98.8 million.
    2. Move forward with $9.3 million in Departmental Budget Reductions (Attachment 2) – The Superintendent and Leadership Team identified $9.3 million in central/departmental reductions that will be implemented to offset the FY 2009 deficit. Many of these reductions will be implemented immediately (FY 2008). However, the value of the FY 2008 reductions has yet to be determined. All other reductions would become effective in FY 2009. Several of the proposed cuts would affect the availability of services at the school level. The Leadership Team was extremely mindful of this fact and tried to protect school level services as much as possible. However, virtually all of the services departments provide support local schools so it is challenging to make reductions without impacting schools.
    3. Concur with the identification of an additional $30.0 - $40.0 million in reductions (Attachment 3) – The Superintendent has engaged both the Leadership Team and school principals to develop possible budget cuts for FY 2009 that would not adversely affect our core mission. While none of the options are painless, each stakeholder group developed a series of reductions to consider for satisfying the budget condition. The listing of cuts prepared by each group will be taken into consideration when developing the FY 2009 Proposed Budget. However, it was quickly determined that if reductions were required to achieve the full target of $98.8 million, then many harmful strategies would need to be employed which would certainly affect classroom services and offerings.
    4. Assume a 3.0 - 4.0 percent increase in the County transfer- The FY 2009 budget proposal will include $40 - 50 million in budget reductions. However, the Superintendent will propose that the School Board seek the remaining balance from the County to fully fund the COLA and targeted program expansion, which amounts to an estimated 3.0 - 4.0 percent increase in the County transfer.

RECOMMENDATION:

Affirmative of the aforementioned options.

    3. Principals Reduction Options- This document is a summary matrix of the recommended budget reduction option submitted by school principals across all levels. This list will be taken into consideration when determining final budget cuts to offset the deficit.

Meeting Manager Roles and Responsibilities
    Overview
    The Chairman shall appoint two Board Members to be meeting managers for each of the Board’s monthly work sessions. These “Meeting Managers” are responsible for ensuring that the meetings materials have been sufficiently prepared for Board consideration, with appropriate attachments, including having clear objectives or desired outcomes, respond to likely Board Member questions and are posted to BoardDocs in a timely manner.

    Process/Checklist
    1. The Chairman shall inform the two Board Members of the Work Session they are to manage at least 1 month before the scheduled meeting.

    2. These “Meeting Managers” should review with the Chairman the purpose or need for inclusion of these items on the agenda and the outcome expected at the Work Session.

    3. Meeting Managers should meet with appropriate Leadership Team members to discuss the topics for the Work Session. They should discuss the following items with staff:
        a. Purpose and expected outcome of the agenda item e.g., informational only, action required,
        b. The information and attachments on the BoardDocs cover sheet
        c. The time required to present the Agenda Item
        d. Likely questions or concerns of the Board that staff should be aware of
        e. Who will be presenting the material
    4. Meeting Managers should review all material to be presented and make suggestions to improve the material that will further the Board’s ability to make a decision or understand the material presented.

    5. Meeting Managers should contact the Chairman to advise if any concerns, particularly the time set aside for each agenda item.

    6. Meeting Managers should develop a communication plan for contacting Board Members as soon as the Work Session material is posted to BoardDocs, which should be the Friday ten days before the Work Session. Meeting Managers should contact all Board members prior to the meeting to solicit questions and concerns. Additionally, Meeting Managers should advise board members of the objective or outcome expected for the agenda item, which should also be posted on the BoardDocs Summary Sheet.

    7. Meeting Managers, prior to the Work Session, will determine which Manager will facilitate each section and which will record the summary notes to be reviewed by the Board at the end of each section.

    8. Meeting Managers role at the Work Session is to:
        a. Facilitate each topic to ensure that discussion is meaningful, broad-based, respectful and outcomes-focused.
        b. Refrain from advocating a particular position while in the role of facilitator
        c. At the end of each discussion, the Manager not facilitating the topic should be prepared to summarize the actions agreed upon by the Board including the follow-up required, person(s) responsible and due date(s).

Linkage: Parental Involvement

Background
It is the parent’s or guardian’s responsibility to educate their child. This is not removed when the parent delegates the instructional element to the public school system. For an optimum process the parents and the public school work as a team to ensure that the child gets the instruction he or she needs to be competitive in the world. As with any team, there should be defined roles and responsibilities along with the mechanism for each member to carry out his or her part of the process. The extent to which the parents participate in the process varies from none to practically doing everything – to include doing the child’s homework. The preferred range of parental involvement is in the middle of this range. However, there is little guidance beyond the minimum legal requirements as to the desired (expected) or optional roles and responsibilities of parents. The lack of parental involvement is a frequent buzz phrase that comes up when student achievement is discussed. There is little guidance as to what that parental involvement is, much less how it directly impacts a students learning. It is much more than reading to an early-grade elementary student and attending Back-to-School-Nights. It should include advocating for their child’s needs – be it special education services or a plan to get their child up to grade level or getting the child into more challenging classes.

Purpose
The purpose is to interact with the community to:
        • Find the limits of what parents believe are their roles and responsibilities.
        • Establish a spectrum of parental involvement roles and responsibilities and specific tasks within those roles.
        • Determine the best methods to reach parents to expand the understanding of the minimum, desired and optional roles and responsibilities, and the impact they have on student achievement.
        • Determine the barriers to parents fulfilling the desired roles and responsibilities.
        • Determine means to overcome those barriers.
        • Determine the extent to which schools encourage, support and enable parents to fulfill their roles and responsibility.
        • Determine new ways that schools could enable parents in fulfilling their desired roles and responsibilities.
What: Cluster Meetings

When: Two per Month Starting in February through May

Audience: Parents and family members

Content: Board and OCR to develop talking points for open discussions – need staff to help record sessions but seen as an interactive session with Board Members as the facilitators.
Linkage: 21st Century Learning
Background: The new School Board mission, vision and goals are what everyone is defining as 21st Century Learning. The following is the Framework for 21st Century Learning developed by the Partnership for 21st Century Learning. This was the group that presented at the NSBA conference.

21st Century Core Subject Areas include:
        • Global awareness
        • financial, economic, business and entrepreneurial literacy
        • civic literacy
        • health and wellness awareness

Additionally 21st Century Skills emphasize thinking skills such as:
  • critical thinking and problem solving skills
  • Communication skills
  • Creativity and innovation skills
  • Collaboration skills
  • Contextual learning skills
  • Information and media literacy skills

Information and communications technology (ICT) literacy is the ability to use technology to develop 21st century content knowledge and skills, in the context of learning core subjects. Students must be able to use technology to learn content and skills so that they know how to learn, think critically, solve problems, use information, communicate, innovate and collaborate.

Purpose:
Achieve an understanding among parents and the community about what students need to learn to be successful and how parents and schools together can help students achieve the goals

What: Four meetings, each meeting combining two clusters.

When: April/May 2008

Audience: Parents and interested community members

Content: Board and staff develop a presentation that:
        • Describes 21st Century Learning Skills and their importance.
        • How this relates to the new FCPS mission, vision, goals.
        • Update on where FCPS is in implementing our goals and accountability measures. A discussion on how parents and schools can help children achieve the goals.
Linkage: Transitions

Background: Fairfax County Public Schools does a good job of preparing students academically for post secondary opportunities. But do we prepare them in a “real world sense” for those same opportunities? Do students know how to access the tools they need to move forward? Students graduating from high school have the following options available to them (not necessarily an all inclusive list):
    • Work
    • Military
    • Marriage/parenthood
    • 2 year college
    • 4 year college
    • Technical school
    • Internship

Purpose: To find out how much parents and students know and to then tailor our curriculum an future engagements to help meet the need.

Some of the questions which should be asked are:
        • Do students and parents have the necessary information to move forward? For example, do students know the minimum requirements for entrance into the military?
  • Do they understand the cost of college, and where that money will come from?
  • What the benefits of two year versus four year colleges?
  • How do students apply for a loan, rent an apartment, apply for needed social services?

Methodology: Large meetings could be used as the mechanism to generate interest. At these meeting School Board members would introduce the problem and then provide a survey for parents to fill out. Breakout sessions could then be used to generate further discussion. Data would then be synthesized and analyzed.

When:
        • Questionnaire development from December to January.
        • Large county-wide meetings could be held February and March.
        • Data analysis would occur from April to May.

Audience: Parents and family members
