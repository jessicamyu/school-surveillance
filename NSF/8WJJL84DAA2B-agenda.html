School Nurse Day - May 8, 2013

School Nurse Day was established in 1972 to foster a better understanding of the role of school nurses in the educational setting.  This annual event recognizes the contributions that school nurses make every day to improve the health and success of our nation’s children.  This year’s theme, Advocacy, Access, Achievement: Leading the Way, captures the significant role of a school nurse in improving students’ health and academic outcomes.

School nursing is a specialized practice of professional nursing that advances the health and well-being of students and is an integral part of a quality school health program. School nurses promote health, safety, prevention, and wellness;  intervene with actual and potential health problems; and provide case management services.  They are an invaluable bridge connecting health care providers, public health, and families.

The school system works closely with the Fairfax County Health Department to provide quality services to the children of Fairfax County Public Schools and recognized May 8 as School Nurse Day in Fairfax County.

The winner of the 2013 Distinguished School Resource Officer award is Master Police Officer Rudy Coffield, Key Middle School.


Fairfax County Public Schools and the Fairfax County and Fairfax City Police Departments have established a mutually beneficial relationship for more than a decade. [Updated 5/9/13 - Motions and Voting Below]

 

Staff Contact:         Jeff Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  May 9, 2013 – Regular Meeting No. 19

 

Subject:                 Fairfax High/Lanier Middle School Boundary Study - Staff Recommendation

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

On January 24, 2013, the School Board directed staff to undertake a boundary study aimed at providing enrollment relief for the current and projected overcrowding at Fairfax High School and its feeder, Lanier Middle School.  Fairfax High School is projected to be approximately 600 students over its capacity by the 2017-18 school year.  The genesis for this boundary study began in summer 2012 when the School Board acknowledged the June 13, 2012, letter from the City of Fairfax School Board asking the Fairfax County School Board to commit to a boundary study of Fairfax High School and its feeders so “that the gross over-enrollment projected in the CIP is avoided.”  It was understood that the overcrowding is due to the numbers of County students attending these schools and that the City of Fairfax would not be expected to fund additional capital improvements that would benefit County students.  It should be further noted that approximately 65% of the student population for both schools are Fairfax County residents and that no City of Fairfax student would be impacted with a boundary change as a result of the study.  The Board also approved a scope of study based on community input from meetings that were held in fall 2012.  The approved scope of study included: Fairfax High and Lanier Middle; Chantilly High and Rocky Run Middle; Falls Church High and Jackson Middle; Oakton High and Franklin Middle; Woodson High and Frost Middle; and Robinson Secondary School.

   

Unlike other boundary studies which seek to largely resolve enrollment imbalances, this study would seek to share some of the over-enrollment burden with other adjacent school pyramids.  There is insufficient capacity at surrounding schools to fully address the 600 seat capacity deficit projected for Fairfax High School.  The average capacity utilization among all the high schools in the scope of study is 106.5% for the 2017-18 school year and that average will not change as a result of this study. There is general concurrence that an enrollment reduction of approximately 300 students at Fairfax High and 150 students at Lanier Middle would provide partial relief to the severe enrollment pressures projected for 2017 and beyond and in so doing, provide a more equitable out year capacity utilization among the high schools upon full implementation.  It should be noted that, from the outset of this study, the Board also expressed interest in having an option for some families to move in fall 2013, before the official implementation in 2014. The intent of this option would be to provide an opportunity for some small enrollment relief to Fairfax and Lanier by allowing students to move to their newly assigned schools earlier than the official fall 2014 implementation. 

 

Three community engagement meetings were held in February at Oakton High, Lanier Middle, and Woodson High Schools.  Each meeting provided identical information proposing three boundary change options for consideration.

 

Option A - moved the fewest number of students

 

Option B - achieved the best capacity balance among the schools in the study

 

Option C - moved the most students out of Fairfax and created the largest

                boundary change ‘footprint’ 

 

Comments and input from each of these meetings may be found on the boundary study web page at http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/fairfaxlanier/index.shtml .

 

Discussion:

 

Several key issues emerged from the community engagement meetings which resonated significantly for staff: the desire for grandfathering students at their current schools beyond the “basic” phasing plan contained in School Board policy; concern over the creation of new split feeders; and the concern that no potential walkers should be moved to become bus riders. In addition, the Penderbrook/Fairfax Farms neighborhoods voiced a strong desire to move from the Fairfax/Lanier pyramid to the Oakton/Franklin pyramid.

 

Based on community input and the goals of the study, staff proposes a new combination of the boundary adjustments found in the options presented at the community meetings and recommends the boundary plan as described below and as provided in the attached Appendix A:

  • Move a portion of the Greenbriar West Elementary School attendance area east of Stringfellow Road from Lanier Middle to Rocky Run Middle School and from Fairfax High to Chantilly High School. This change eliminates the current split feeder assignment for Greenbriar West Elementary and reassigns approximately 40 high school students and 20 middle school students currently attending Fairfax and Lanier, respectively by the 2017-18 school year.
  • Move the Fairfax Station, Fairview Woods, and Fairfax Manor Subdivision portions of Oak View Elementary School’s attendance area from Frost Middle School and Woodson High School to Robinson Secondary School. This change eliminates an attendance area “island” at the middle and high school levels and sends these students to a much closer school. The proposed change reassigns approximately 100 students at the high school level and 40 students at the middle school level by the 2017-18 school year.  The middle school AAP assignment for these students would change from the AAP Center at Frost to the AAP Center at Lake Braddock Middle School, impacting approximately 20 students. This boundary change makes additional capacity available to move students from Fairfax and Lanier in to Frost and Woodson.
  • Move all of the Fairfax Villa Elementary attendance area (including areas north of Lee Highway) from Lanier Middle to Frost Middle School and from Fairfax High to Woodson High School.  The proposed change reassigns approximately 330 students at the high school level and 160 students at the middle school level by the 2017-18 school year.  The middle school AAP assignment for these students would change from the AAP Center at Rocky Run to the AAP Center at Frost Middle School, impacting approximately 20 students.

Reassignment of the Penderbrook/Fairfax Farms area is not recommended at this time.  To do so without removing other sections of the current Oakton attendance area simply transfers the significant overcrowding problem from Fairfax to Oakton, unduly burdening that school which is already projected to be at 109% of capacity by the 2017 school year.  Renovation of Oakton High, to include additional capacity, is scheduled to start in the 2017/2018 time frame.  Additional enrollment pressure should not be added to Oakton until extra capacity is provided. 

 

Staff believes this recommendation achieves the stated objectives of the study and is consistent with School Board boundary policies in that; no new split feeders are created, one split feeder is resolved and a large attendance area island is eliminated in favor of a boundary adjustment which sends students to a much closer middle and high school. 

 

The staff recommendation reassigns 261 students (208 from Fairfax High) in 2014-15 and 468 students (371 from Fairfax High) in 2017-18 at the high school level.  This proposal also reassigns 104 students (61 from Lanier Middle) in 2014-15 and 258 students (179 from Lanier Middle) in 2017-18 at the middle school level.

 

In order to address parental concerns expressed in the community dialog meetings and achieve adequate relief in a timely fashion, staff has proposed grandfathering both the rising 11th and 12th  grades at high schools and rising 8th grade students at middle schools beginning in fall 2014.  Staff further recommends an option which would allow rising 9th grade students to attend their newly assigned school beginning in the 2013-14 school year with transportation.  This option would provide some relief to Fairfax High beginning with the next school year and would benefit families who may not want to move their students at the 10th grade.       

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve adjustments to school attendance areas, in order to balance enrollment and utilization of school facilities and to contribute to the efficiency of the school division, and pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.6, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as shown in Appendix A to be effective for the 2014-2015 school year for the following schools:  Lanier, Frost, and Rocky Run Middle Schools; Fairfax, Woodson, Chantilly High Schools, and Robinson Secondary School.

Meeting Category:  May 9, 2013 - Regular Meeting No. 19

 

Subject:                  Proposed 2013 School Bond Referendum

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

On January 24, 2013, the School Board adopted the FY 2014-2018 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) that identified school facility needs anticipated during the coming five-year period.  Capital improvement programming provides the foundation for cost-effective investment and construction project management of facility needs identified throughout the planning period.  Voter authorization to issue bonds is needed in November 2013 to provide:

 

Construction funds for:

  • 2 new elementary schools
  • 1 elementary school addition
  • 9 elementary school renovations
  • 1 middle school renovation
  • 1 high school renovation

Planning funds for:

  • 1 high school addition
  • 8 elementary school renovations
  • 1 middle school renovation
  • 2 high school renovations

Site Acquisition funds for:

  • 1 new high school

Infrastructure Management funds for:

  • roof replacement
  • HVAC replacement
  • athletic infrastructure
  • asphalt paving
  • security enhancements
  • technology upgrades
  • Americans with Disabilities (ADA) requirements

Key Points:

 

The adopted FY 2014-18 CIP identified what are projected to be substantial enrollment increases over the 5-year planning period and beyond.  Specifically, these projected enrollment increases will require FCPS to accommodate more than 15,000 additional students by school year 2017-18, with additional growth expected through the 2021-22 school year when enrollment is projected to exceed 200,000 students. 

 

The 2013 bonds provide funds for construction, planning, and site acquisition for new facilities to help address the expected capacity deficits; in addition, renovation projects continue to include additional capacity in order to address the student membership growth in many schools. 

 

Facilities staff members have been working with the County staff to reconcile unused authority from multiple bond referendums over an extended period of time – an exercise which is performed periodically for all local governmental entities which utilize bond funds.  This reconciliation does not alter in anyway the current annual cash flow contribution from the County of $155 million; nor will it impact the timing of any projects currently listed in the CIP.  It reflects cumulative project savings over time allowing FCPS to request less bonding authority for the projects currently identified for funding. Out of the $359 million CIP listed in the bond referendum, the County currently already has bonding authority for $109 million.  Consequently, FCPS only needs to seek bonding authority for $250 million in new bond sales.

 

At the April 18, 2013, Board meeting, the Board approved a motion to postpone action on the bond referendum until the May 9 Board meeting.

 

Original Recommendation:

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2013 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $359,110,000.

 

Revised Recommendation:

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2013 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $250,000,000.

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  May 9, 2013 – Regular Meeting No. 19

 

Subject:                  Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments and School Closings

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

At the December 13, 2012, work session, the Facilities Planning Advisory Council (FPAC) recommended that FCPS Policy 8130.6 be amended in order to provide for a simplified and expedited process for boundary changes affecting between 5% and 15% of student populations at sending and receiving schools. The genesis for the recommendation was based on a desire to make small boundary adjustments to address boundary anomalies that may no longer be efficient or appropriate; balance enrollments; address changing conditions with respect to transportation efficiencies; and to improve pyramid alignments and small split feeders.  The current policy allows for an administrative change provided that no more than 5% of the enrollment of the sending and receiving schools is impacted.  Typically, an administrative change is used for new development and unoccupied housing.  In recent years, the administrative process has been used with more frequency for small boundary adjustments within established neighborhoods.  Under the administrative policy for enrollment change, the limitation is small (5%).  More extensive adjustments require a full boundary study which has evolved over the last several years to include a scope of study period of three to four months (or more), extensive communication efforts with web page development and monitoring, FAQs, multiple community engagement meetings, and the requisite School Board meetings for new business, public hearing(s), work session, and action.  Limited staff resources and time is an obstacle to more quickly addressing smaller single option boundary changes, many of which are sought by communities and School Board members to improve efficiency.  The School Board directed staff to prepare revisions to Policy 8130.6 for review and consideration of a boundary ‘lite’ or expedited process.  

 

Key Points:

 

The proposed revision adds a policy option for an expedited boundary process, effecting less than 15% of affected schools, as permitted by Code of Virginia Sections 22.1-79 (4) and (8).  The proposed policy revision allows the Superintendent to implement such changes, similar to an administrative adjustment.  However, unlike an administrative process, a vote of approval by the School Board would be required; and unlike a standard boundary study, a public hearing would not be required. 

 

Should the School Board adopt this policy revision, the staff regulations for implementing boundary adjustments would also be revised to parallel the three–tier policy options for boundary adjustments, retaining the requirements for meetings at sending and receiving schools, among others. Other minor revisions are incorporated for flow and clarity and to distinguish policy from process (implementation regulations).  Staff believes that the proposed policy revisions are in keeping with the applicable Code of Virginia sections and address the directive to provide for a policy to expedite and implement smaller boundary adjustments in order to achieve greater operation efficiencies.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt Policy 8130.7, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as detailed in the agenda item.

6. New Business

Staff Contact:         Phyllis C. Pajardo, assistant superintendent, Human Resources

 

Meeting Category:  May 9, 2013 - Regular Meeting No. 19

 

Subject:                 Employee Calendar - July 5 Holiday

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Background:

 

In 2001-2002, the School Board approved the reduction of two instructional school days due to the lack of inclement weather. Those two days were also granted for less than 12-month personnel. Recognizing that July 4, 2002, fell on a Thursday and that 12-month employees did not get the benefit of the two-day reduction, the Board approved Friday, July 5, 2002, as a designated holiday.

 

Key Points:

 

For the 2013-2014 school year, July 4, 2013, falls on a Thursday. To recognize that workload has increased for all employees while salaries have only slightly increased, declare Friday, July 5, 2013, a paid nonworkday for 12-month employees. This would also provide some energy efficiencies as all the buildings will be closed and air-conditioning minimized for a four-day weekend. There will be a very small cost associated with overtime for security and a few other critical employees.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve one additional paid nonworkday for 12-month personnel for the 2013-2014 school year.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
