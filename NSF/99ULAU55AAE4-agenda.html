African American History Month - February 2014

 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." This commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

 

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.  This year’s theme, Civil Rights in America, was chosen in recognition of the 50th anniversary of the 1964 Civil Rights Act.

National School Counseling Week - February 3-7, 2014

 

National School Counseling Week 2014, "School Counseling: Building Magical Futures,"  will be celebrated from Feb. 3-7, 2014, to focus public attention on the unique contribution of professional school counselors within U.S. school systems. National School Counseling Week, sponsored by the American School Counselor Association, highlights the tremendous impact school counselors can have in helping students achieve school success and plan for a career.  Every day, school counselors work with students, families, and staff to address the academic, personal, social, and career needs of all students.

 

Fairfax County Public Schools employs over 600 school counseling professionals, including school counselors, directors of student services, and career center specialists.  These professionals are committed to preparing students to become lifelong learners and productive members in a global society.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2014

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (programming , business management, information technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (sales, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

Approve the FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program Summary (FTS; presented as new business 12/19/13; public hearing held 1/8/14; work session held 1/13/14)

[Updated: 2/28/14 - Approved CIP Attachment]

 

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  January 23, 2014 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                  FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five year enrollment projections and capacity calculations, as well as identification of new construction, capacity enhancement, renovation, special program, site acquisition and infrastructure requirements.

 

The Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program Summary can be found in the Capital Program Recommendations chapter of the Proposed Capital Improvement Program booklet.

 

Discussion:

 

The school system continues to face a challenging situation in which student enrollment is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities and capacity enhancement projects and will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions, as well as boundary adjustments.

 

The CIP includes funding for five new elementary schools and a new high school.  The new elementary school needs are identified as follows:  one in the Eastern Fairfax area to relieve overcrowding in the Bailey’s Crossroads area; one in the Richmond Highway Corridor (Route 1 area) to relieve overcrowding in the Groveton/Hybla Valley area; one in the Northwest County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and Dulles Airport Access Road; one to relieve overcrowding in the Fairfax/Oakton area; and one on the army base to relieve overcrowding at Fort Belvoir Elementary School. 

 

It is also anticipated that beyond fiscal year 2019 an additional new high school will be needed to address the future growth in the western Fairfax County area. Funding for additions at South Lakes High School and two elementary schools to help relieve overcrowding in the Tysons/Vienna area (Westbriar and Marshall Road) are also identified. Capacity enhancements are proposed at both Herndon and Oakton High Schools as part of their renovations. Furthermore, the CIP includes construction funds for renovation at nine elementary and one middle school, plus a renovation at one high school, as well as planning funds for renovations at eight elementary, one middle, and two high schools. Many of the renovation projects will also include capacity enhancements.

 

Allocating funds for new schools will result in delays for some future renovation projects. Favorable construction pricing has helped mitigate these delays; however, they will occur if the school system does not receive additional capital funding authorization from the Board of Supervisors.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2015-2019 Capital Improvement Program Summary.

I move that the Board approve the following resolution regarding the renovation of Legacy High Schools in Fairfax County:

 

     WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) has five Legacy High Schools, built in the 1960s, which have not yet received complete renovations, including:

  • Langley High School (built 1965, partial renovation 1988)
  • West Springfield High School (built 1966, partial renovation 1989)
  • Herndon High School (built 1967, partial renovation 1991)
  • Oakton High School (built 1967, partial renovation 1991)
  • Falls Church High School (built as middle school 1960, repurposed as high school 1967, partial renovation 1988); and

     WHEREAS, the age and condition of the Legacy High Schools make it difficult to meet 21st Century curriculum standards at the high school level, such as those involving science and computer labs; and

 

     WHEREAS, providing adequate technology and science facilities in FCPS high schools has long-term ramifications for students’ learning and college readiness; and

 

     WHEREAS, research shows that the quality of school facilities is a factor in student and teacher attendance, teacher retention and recruitment, child and teacher health, and the quality of curriculum; and

 

     WHEREAS, high school renovations eventually impact all students in a high school pyramid, and

 

     WHEREAS, FCPS strives to provide equitable opportunities for all students;

 

     NOW THEREFORE BE IT RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board agrees that renovating the Legacy High Schools (Langley, West Springfield, Herndon, Oakton and Falls Church) is a high priority of the Capital Improvement Program and that FCPS shall work toward securing full funding for the renovations of all the Legacy High Schools by the 2017 bond referendum.

 

     NOW THEREFORE BE IT RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board directs staff to identify methods by which renovations could be completed sooner at the Legacy High Schools (Langley, West Springfield, Herndon, Oakton and Falls Church) and to present those options to the Board no later than July 2014, along with an assessment of how updating high school facilities impacts instruction.

Recommended Action
Appoint Rosemarie Annunziata, Lois Berlin, William Chapman, Edward Clymore, and John Robinson to serve as Hearing Officers for Teacher Dismissal Hearings pursuant to Section 22.1-311 of the Code of Virginia, effective immediately (HR; presented as new business 1/9/14)

Staff Contact:         Phyllis Pajardo, assistant superintendent, Human Resources

 

Meeting Category:  January 23, 2014 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                  Appointment of Hearing Officers for Teacher Dismissal Hearings

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Related To:  Legal Requirement

 

Key Points: 

 

During its 2013 session, the General Assembly amended and repealed several sections of the Code of Virginia regarding the procedures for the dismissal of teachers and appeal hearings requested by the teacher.  Among them, the procedure for a three member fact-finding panel has been repealed and Section 22.1-311 of the Code has been amended to provide that hearings are conducted by the school board or by an impartial hearing officer appointed by the school board.   Prior to the amendment to the Code of Virginia, the Board had determined that hearings would be conducted by a fact-finding panel.  Now, with the elimination of fact finding panels under Section 22.1-311, all such hearings may be conducted by a hearing officer. Rather than have the Board appoint a hearing officer each time one is needed for a hearing, it is instead recommended that the Board appoint a pool of hearing officers. An individual hearing officer can be drawn from this pool as needed for a teacher dismissal hearing.

 

The Code requires the hearing officer to possess some knowledge of and expertise in public education and education law and be capable of presiding over an administrative hearing. Staff is recommending the appointment of the following group of hearing officers: Hon. Rosemarie Annunziata (Ret.), Ms. Lois Berlin, Mr. William Chapman, Mr. Edward G. Clymore, and Mr. John Robinson, all of whom meet the requirements of the Code. 

 

Hon. Rosemarie Annunziata has 30 years of judicial and legal experience as an appellate judge, trial judge, and trial attorney.  She has experience adjudicating employment matters such as workers compensation disputes, discrimination and sexual harassment and retaliation claims, and termination disputes. Judge Annunziata has served as a Hearing Officer for Special Education Appeals for the Commonwealth of Virginia, Hearing Officer and Counsel, Fairfax County Civil Service Commission, and currently serves as Chair, Fairfax County Civil Service Commission.  Judge Annunziata has presided over several fact-finding hearings for Fairfax County Public Schools as the neutral chair of the three person panel.

 

Lois Berlin has over 30 years of experience as a teacher, principal, assistant superintendent, and superintendent in Virginia schools, including having served as Superintendent of Falls Church City Public Schools for over 7 years.

 

William Chapman has over 40 years of legal experience representing School Boards throughout Virginia, including Loudoun County, King George County and Tazewell County. He also has extensive experience in teacher and student discipline cases. He has litigated several freedom of speech and religion cases in state and federal court and has conducted training seminars for educators at VSBA and other association meetings.

 

Edward G. Clymore has over 45 years of experience as a teacher, principal, assistant superintendent, and superintendent in Virginia schools, including having served as Superintendent of Augusta County Public Schools for 17 years.

 

John Robinson has over 35 years of experience in human resources and public education. He is the past director of personnel services for Northern Virginia Community College and he served as the Executive Director of the Fairfax County Civil Service Commission for 22 years, which adjudicated employee grievances and appeals.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board appoint Rosemarie Annunziata, Lois Berlin, William Chapman, Edward Clymore, and John Robinson to serve as Hearing Officers for Teacher Dismissal Hearings pursuant to Section 22.1-311 of the Code of Virginia, effective immediately.

Meeting Category:  January 23, 2014 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                  Mount Vernon Woods Elementary School – Deed of Vacation and Release

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

A neighboring landowner in the vicinity of Mt. Vernon Woods Elementary School has requested that the School Board vacate and release certain outdated development restrictions applicable to the landowner’s real property.

 

Both the Board of Supervisors and the Fairfax County Park Authority, each of which also owns real property in this area, have already granted the landowner’s request.

 

The development restrictions applicable to the landowner’s real property date to a 1955 deed from Joseph and Wilma Baker, who at the time owned a 100 acre parcel of land in the Lee Magisterial District from which the School Board acquired its own 10 acre site which is now Mt. Vernon Woods Elementary School.  The 1955 development restrictions are as follows:

 

“That neither the parties of the second part nor the heirs, successors or assigns of the parties of the second part shall or will erect, or cause or suffer to be erected, or use or cause or suffer to be used on any portion of said premises any building except 2 dwelling houses for one family each which building shall cost to erect, at least sixteen thousand ($16,000.00) Dollars each, and which building shall be only one story high above the ground.”  The deed also went on to restrict the type of fencing and hedges allowed.

 

The 1955 development restrictions serve no present day purpose, and the School Board’s interests are not adversely affected by vacating these development restrictions. 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board authorize the Division Superintendent and the Assistant Superintendent, Department of Facilities and Transportation Services, either of whom may act without the other, to do all things necessary and appropriate on behalf of the School Board, and with the advice of legal counsel for the school division, to execute and to deliver a deed of vacation and release of the development restrictions of record applicable to certain real property, known as Fairfax County Tax Map # 101-2-01-0010C.

  • Parent Committee representatives and alternates participate in information sharing and decision-making activities at the school-system level through the FCPS EHS/FECEP/HS Policy Committee, also with support of FCPS EHS/FECEP/HS program personnel.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
