U. S. Constitution Day and Citizenship Day - September 17, 2019

 

Constitution Day and Citizenship Day are observed each year on September 17 to commemorate the signing of the Constitution on September 17, 1787, and “recognize all who, by coming of age or by naturalization, have become citizens.”  Recent legislation has renamed the day as Constitution Day and Citizenship Day and urges proper observance of the day. This year, the official observance day is Tuesday, September 17, and is the 232nd  anniversary of the nation’s founding document.

 

All schools in FCPS will observe this day with activities that recognize the adoption of the United States Constitution and those who have become citizens under the Constitution.  Schools will use a variety of curriculum resources to teach students about the importance of the Constitution and responsible citizenship.  Throughout the school year, high school students will have an opportunity to participate in service learning projects related to responsible citizenship, which can lead to a Civics Seal on their diploma.

Fifty-four Fairfax County Public Schools Earn 2019 Virginia Index of Performance Awards

 

Carson Middle School, Cooper Middle School, Longfellow Middle School, and Chesterbrook Elementary are among 5 schools statewide to earn the 2019 Governor’s Award for Educational Excellence, the highest recognition in the Virginia Index of Performance (VIP) awards for advanced learning and achievement. The VIP incentive program recognizes schools and divisions that exceed state and federal accountability standards and achieve excellence goals established by Virginia’s Governor and the Board of Education.

 

Recipients of the Award for Educational Excellence must meet all state and federal achievement benchmarks, achieve all applicable excellence goals for elementary reading, enrollment in Algebra I by the eighth grade, enrollment in college-level courses, high school graduation, attainment of advanced diplomas, attainment of career and industry certifications, and participation in the Virginia Preschool Initiative.

 

A total of 28 Fairfax County Schools were named recipients of the Board of Education Excellence Awards, and 22 schools earned the Board of Education’s Distinguished Achievement Awards.

 

“Congratulations to the students, teachers, support staff, and administrators at these Fairfax County Public Schools for earning the Commonwealth’s highest award for educational excellence,” said Superintendent Scott S. Brabrand. “These awards recognize the outstanding work being done in schools across the county in achieving our goals of providing an excellent and equitable education for every FCPS student.”

 

Schools earning the Board of Education Excellence Award include: Chantilly High, Churchill Road Elementary, Colvin Run Elementary, Floris Elementary, Forestville Elementary, Greenbriar West Elementary, Haycock Elementary, Keene Mill Elementary, Lake Braddock Secondary, Langley High, Madison High, Mantua Elementary, McLean High, Mosby Woods Elementary, Oak Hill Elementary, Oakton High, Poplar Tree Elementary, Robinson Secondary, Rocky Run Middle, Sangster Elementary, Spring Hill Elementary, Springfield Estates Elementary, Thomas Jefferson High For Science and Technology, West Springfield High, Westbriar Elementary, Willow Springs Elementary, Wolftrap Elementary, and Woodson High. These schools were honored for meeting all state and federal accountability benchmarks and for making significant progress toward goals for increased student achievement and expanded educational opportunities set by the Board of Education.

 

Schools earning the Board of Education Distinguished Achievement Award include: Louise Archer Elementary, Belvedere Elementary, Camelot Elementary, Canterbury Woods Elementary, Centreville High, Crossfield Elementary, Fairfax High, Flint Hill Elementary, Fox Mill Elementary, Frost Middle, Great Falls Elementary, Kilmer Middle, Lees Corner Elementary, Marshall High, Navy Elementary, Oakton Elementary, Franklin Sherman Elementary, Terra Centre Elementary, Thoreau Middle, Wakefield Forest Elementary, Waynewood Elementary, and West Springfield Elementary.

 

These schools were recognized for meeting all state and federal benchmarks and for making progress toward the goals of the governor and the Board of Education.

 

 

Nineteen Fairfax County Public Schools Earn 2019 Exemplar Performance School Awards

 

Forestville Elementary, Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology, and Wolftrap Elementary are among 52 schools statewide to earn the 2019 Board of Education Highest Achievement Award.

 

Recipients of the 2019 Board of Education Highest Achievement Award must earn a state accreditation rating of Accredited, and meet the following benchmarks:

  • 70-percent pass rate on state assessments in science and mathematics;
  • 75-percent pass rate on state assessment in English reading and writing;
  • No more than a five-percent gap (for schools with two student groups) or a 10-pecent gap (for schools with three or more student groups) in English reading and writing between the lowest-performing group and all other students in the school; and
  • No more than a five-percent gap (for schools with two student groups) or a 10-percent gap (for schools with three or more student groups) in mathematics between the lowest-performing group and all other students in the school.

 

Bailey’s Upper Elementary School for the Arts and Sciences, Bryant High, Centreville High, Edison High, Fairfax County Adult High, Frost Middle, Herndon High, Hutchison Elementary, Justice High, Kilmer Center, Kings Park Elementary, Lake Braddock Secondary, London Towne Elementary, Madison High, Mountain View High, and Stenwood Elementary are among the 183 Schools to earn the 2019 Board of Education Continuous Improvement Award.

 

To earn the Board of Education Continuous Improvement Award, a school must earn a state accreditation rating of Accredited or Accredited with Conditions and meet at least one of the following benchmarks for improved performance on accreditation-related school quality indicators:

  • A cumulative 10-point increase over three years in the combined rates for reading and mathematics and in the pass rate for science, with improvement each year on each indicator;
  • A cumulative 10-point increase over three years in the combined rates for reading and mathematics for two or more student groups, with improvement each year for each group on both indicators;
  • A cumulative 15-percent decrease in the chronic absenteeism rate over three years, with a decrease each year; or

For schools with a graduating class, a cumulative four-point increase in the Graduation and Completion Index over three years, with an increase each year, and a cumulative 15-percent decrease in the dropout rate, with a decrease each year.

 

“Being recognized with a Virginia Board of Education Highest Achievement Award is a testament to the hard work and dedication of each school’s staff and that staff’s focus on ensuring each child succeeds,” said Superintendent Scott S. Brabrand.

TJHSST 2019 First Tech Challenge World Champions

Recognition

 

On behalf of the Fairfax County School Board, it is my honor to recognize the outstanding achievements of the Robot Uprising Team based at Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology for receiving the Judge's Award at the 2019 FIRST Tech Challenge World Championship. Robot Uprising was the only American team to be awarded this honor, which recognizes a team whose unique efforts, performance or dynamics merit recognition outside of the existing award categories. Robot Uprising was awarded the Judge’s Award for their regional, state, national, and international outreach efforts on behalf of STEM Cross-Trainers. The Robot Uprising team has received multiple national awards, served as the robotics captain of the runner up state team, and is ranked 150th overall in the world in terms of robot performance.

 

During the 2019 season, the team dedicated over 500 hours of service to advocating for national STEM funding, demoing their robot at 30 STEM fairs and outreach events, and promoting opportunities for Title I students through Growing Up with Robots (GUR). Last year, team members Kathy and Charlie presented at the FCPS Title I Principal’s conference to expand their program to communities that might not otherwise have access to STEM programming, exemplifying their commitment to the pursuit of One Fairfax.

 

Beyond their accolades, Robot Uprising has advocated on a national level for STEM funding on Capitol Hill as part of the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) and the Perkins Bill, and on the international level, the team has mentored the only FTC team in Indonesia, working with them to develop their business and documentation practices.

 

Congratulations again to all the members of this team for their success and victories. We wish you all the best in competitions this year and in all of your future endeavors.

Childhood Cancer Awareness

Recognition

 

The American Childhood Cancer Organization (or ACCO) has designated September as Childhood Cancer Awareness Month to spotlight the types of cancers that largely affect children and to help raise funds for research and family support.

 

According to ACCO, 15,780 children under the age of 21 are diagnosed with cancer every year in the United States; approximately 1/4 of them will not survive the disease. A diagnosis turns the lives of the entire family upside down.

 

ACCO offers support, information and advocacy through their community of families, survivors, and friends affected by childhood and adolescent cancer. They are dedicated to improving the lives of children and families living with childhood cancer and engaged in this life-altering struggle. FCPS students being treated for childhood cancer participate in home-bound instruction while they are undergoing treatment or recuperating.

 

We are joined tonight by long-time teacher Jay Coakley. Retired from FCPS after 30 years of teaching physical education, Jay returned to FCPS as a part-time elementary physical education teacher at Woodburn Elementary. It was at Woodburn where Jay met a little girl named Ellie, who had been diagnosed with acute lymphoblastic leukemia in the spring of 2013. By the time she entered kindergarten that fall, Ellie had lost most of her hair and was wearing hats to school every day.

 

Jay noticed her affinity for “fun and cheery hats” and organized a hat drive to provide Ellie with a broader selection of hats, and Ellie’s Hats was born! Now, thanks to the hard work of the individuals who, like Jay, took an interest in Ellie and her hats, thousands of kids across the country who are battling cancer have received new hats and so much more. Ellie’s Hats does much more than send hats to children with cancer. They have helped to provide financial assistance to local families who have children with cancer.

 

We are delighted to have Ellie and her mother, Jennica, here with us tonight. Ellie is now in 6th grade and is into Marvel, Harry Potter and drama.

 

On behalf of the Board, I’d like to encourage everyone to visit the website for Ellie’s Hats at ellieshats.org and the American Childhood Cancer Organization at acco.org, to learn how you can help.

Hispanic Heritage Month 

Recognition

 

Each year, Americans observe National Hispanic Heritage Month by celebrating the histories, cultures and contributions of Americans whose ancestors came from Spain, Mexico, the Caribbean and Central and South America. This observation started in 1968 as Hispanic Heritage Week under President Lyndon Johnson. In 1988, President Ronald Reagan expanded the observation period to 30-days starting on September 15 and ending on October 15. It was enacted into law August 17, 1988.

 

National Hispanic Heritage Month is a wonderful opportunity for our schools and community to celebrate various cultures and traditions, and a tangible reinforcement of the School Board’s belief that “Our diversity is a strength that creates resilient, open, and innovative global citizens.” In FCPS, one in four of our students is of Hispanic Heritage, and boast ancestry from a variety of countries and cultures. These cultures in turn enrich our own school community. I encourage you to participate in the programs and events taking place during this time in your schools and community.

 

FCPS values its diversity and acknowledges that all people contribute to the well-being of the community. FCPS is fortunate to have many groups whose members work with our Latinx and immigrant students empowering them to be the next generation of leaders and professionals. These include, but are not limited to, our parent liaisons and school leaders, the Hispanic Educators Association, Edu-Futuro, and Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee.

 

I am very pleased to see the various groups, including our Latinx Lideres who are here with us today to celebrate and to show their pride in our community. 

 

 

Todos los años Estados Unidos celebra el Mes Nacional de la Herencia Hispana festejando la historia, cultura y contribuciones de los estadounidenses cuyos antepasados vinieron de España, México, el Caribe y Centro América y Sur América. Esta celebración a la hispanidad empezó en 1968 con el nombre Semana de la Herencia Hispana durante el gobierno del presidente Lyndon Johnson. En 1988, el presidente Ronald Reagan alargó el período de celebración a 30 días, del 15 de septiembre al 15 de octubre. El 17 de agosto de 1988 se convirtió en ley.

 

El Mes Nacional de la Herencia Hispana es una excelente oportunidad para que nuestras escuelas y la comunidad celebren varias culturas y tradiciones, y es un refuerzo tangible de la creencia de la Junta Escolar de que «Nuestra diversidad es una fortaleza que forma ciudadanos del mundo resilientes, abiertos e innovadores». En FCPS, uno de cada cuatro alumnos es de origen hispano y se siente orgulloso de sus raíces y su cultura. Las diferentes culturas de nuestros alumnos a su vez enriquecen nuestra comunidad escolar. Los animo a que participen en los programas y los eventos que se llevarán a cabo en este período de celebración en sus escuelas y en la comunidad.

 

FCPS valora su diversidad y reconoce que todas las personas contribuyen al bienestar de la comunidad. FCPS tiene la suerte de contar con numerosos grupos cuyos miembros trabajan con nuestros alumnos Latinx y nuestros alumnos inmigrantes empoderándolos para que sean la próxima generación de líderes y profesionales.  Estos grupos incluyen, pero no se limitan a, nuestros parent liaisons y líderes escolares, la Asociación de Educadores Hispanos y el Comité de Vigilancia del Desempeño de Alumnos Minoritarios.

 

Me complace ver a varios grupos, incluyendo a nuestros Líderes Latinx quienes se encuentran hoy aquí con nosotros para celebrar y mostrar su orgullo en nuestra comunidad.

Related to: Standards of Accreditation (8 VAC 20-131-10)

 

Summary/Background:

 

With revisions to the Standards of Accreditation (SOA) adopted in fall 2017, new school quality indicators were introduced to measure school accreditation outcomes. The alternative accreditation plans previously approved by the Virginia Board of Education for five special purpose schools in FCPS must be revised and resubmitted by September 2019 for consideration by the Virginia Board. The five affected schools are: Bryant High School, Fairfax County Adult High School, Key Center School, Kilmer Center School, and Mountain View High School. Upon approval by the Virginia Board, the alternative accreditation plans will grant waivers to these five schools, guaranteeing full “Accredited” status for three years, 2019-20 through 2021-22.

 

The SOAs establish alternative accreditation plans as an option for certain types of schools designated to serve special populations, including special education schools and alternative schools. The purpose of an alternative accreditation plan is to explain how the special purpose and population served by the school results in an incomplete depiction of the school’s quality when standard calculation methods are applied. Each plan outlines modified calculation methods that will be used when determining the school’s performance level toward the school quality indicators, so as to more appropriately reflect school outcomes.

 

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the alternative accreditation plans for submission to the Virginia Board of Education, as detailed in the agenda item.
