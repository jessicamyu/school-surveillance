National School Lunch Week, October 14-18, 2019


National School Lunch Week is officially scheduled for October 14-18 by an act of Congress. Using the theme "School Lunch:  What's on your playlist", spotlights the wide variety of flavors, dishes, delivery options and tastes that can be customized in today’s school lunch.

 

National School Lunch Week promotes excitement about the benefits of students consuming wholesome, healthy school lunches of fresh fruits and veggies, whole grains and milk.  The 2019 event is designed to help students and school nutrition professionals connect and share with parents, school officials, the media and the public what each loves most about school lunches.  The National School Lunch program is dedicated to the health and well-being of our nation’s children, and has been joined through the years by many other excellent child nutrition programs. There is a continued need for nutrition education and awareness of the value of school nutrition programs and the School Board encourages all residents to become aware and concerned about their children's, and their own, nutrition habits in hopes of achieving a more healthful citizenry for today and the future.

400 Years of African American History in Virginia

 

This past summer, the state of Virginia held a ceremony in recognition of the arrival of African Americans on the shores of Virginia, some 400 years ago.   While this is, and has been a momentous date, it is also important to note that according to the Library of Congress, Africans initially came to this New World as Adventurers who joined into the Age of the Exploration and crisscrossed the globe.  In the early 17th century, a year before English pilgrims arrived at Plymouth, Massachusetts, a group of Africans arrived in the Jamestown Colony as indentured servants.  It was after this period, that on August 25th, 1619, that the first enslaved Africans arrived on our shores.  They had been stolen by pirates and ended up on an English privateer ship that arrived in desperate need of rations.  They landed at Point Comfort Virginia seeking to trade their human cargo for provisions, an event that led to a nearly 300 year history of slavery in this nation.  When they arrived, slavery did not exist in the English Colonies.

 

Africans came to our shores from West Africa.  At the dawn of the era of transatlantic slavery, Africa was a vast and diverse land, the home of many ancient cultures.  West Africa was a land marked by a rich and varied culture that had long been the site of trading posts with the Europeans and was a major center for scholarship, that included the work of astronomers, mathematicians, and theologians.  Africans who arrived in Virginia brought the skills of their homeland including herding, blacksmithing, and artistry along with their own culture and beliefs.  Africans came ashore at Point Comfort which is present day Fort Monroe in Hampton, Virginia.  President Barack Obama declared Fort Monroe a national monument in 2011.

 

Ironically, it was this same Point Comfort that is now known as Fort Monroe that served a pivotal role during the civil war.  The status of enslaved African Americans after the Confederate states engaged in the Civil war became an issue in early 1861.  Major General Butler, commander of the Fort, learned that 3 slaves made their way to Point Comfort, presented themselves to the Union Army and refused to return to slaveholders who supported the Confederacy.  The three African Americans, Frank Baker, James Townsend, and Sheppard Mallory, had been leased by their masters to assist the Confederate Army.  These men escaped at night and sought asylum at Fort Monroe. When the Confederate Major requested the men’s return, the Union Major refused.  He based that decision on his belief that the Confederate states had succeeded from the United States and the laws that would have mandated return of the slaves, was null and void.  The African Americans began working for the Union Army and eventually received pay for their services to the Union Army and the Union Navy.  This marked the practice and development and authorization of African American military units where thousands of former slaves and free African Americans began to enlist in the United States Colored Troops.  The African Americans ultimately comprised nearly 10 percent of all the troops in the Union Army.  The camps surrounding these forts is also the place where Africans were encouraged and supported to seek education and to learn to read and write.  Now that a Federal Commission has been established to recognize 400 years of African-American History, we encourage students, teachers and the community to recognize the significance of that moment in 1619 and celebrate the resilience and determination of African Americans who defied the odds and have contributed so much to the building of this nation.

3.06 LGBT History Month Recognition

The Fairfax County School Board along with the National PTA, the Fairfax County Council of PTAs, and the Fairfax Education Association recognize October as LGBT (Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual and Transgender) History Month

 

According to the National PTA, "negative attitudes toward lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender students puts them at an increased risk for violence and bullying. Negative attitudes also impact a student's ability to learn at school."

 

LGBT History Month is recognized in October nationwide and in FCPS to acknowledge and celebrate that LGBT students, staff, and families are an integral component of our nation, and of Fairfax County Public Schools.

 

This special month was first recognized in 1994; October was chosen to commemorate National Coming Out Day on October 14, and the First March on Washington for Gay and Lesbian Rights on October 14, 1979.

 

One of the first Gay-Straight Alliances in Virginia was founded at Hayfield High School in 2000, and now students are involved in clubs at many of the high schools in the county, welcoming not only LGBT students but their friends and those interested in creating safe schools.

 

More than a dozen Fairfax County schools have active Gender and Sexuality Alliances or Gay Straight Alliances through which students are able to network, socialize and educate their communities about inclusion of the LGBTQ community in our schools. Students do this through forums, posters, announcements and activities such as ally week. The existence of GSAs is an asset to our schools. Educational research indicates that schools which have GSAs have better mental health outcomes for all students. The equity liaisons in our schools also help to make our schools more welcoming for all students and staff.

 

In addition to student clubs, Fairfax County Public Schools boasts an organization for LGBTQ employees, parents of LGBTQ students, and LGBTQ family members. FCPS Pride holds monthly coffee houses and an annual picnic for the community and supports activities such as holiday celebrations and the LGBTQ prom.

 

LGBT History Month in October is an opportunity for Fairfax County Public Schools staff and students to celebrate, and to learn more about, these important constituents in our schools and neighborhoods.

National School Bus Safety Week, October 21-25, 2019

 

The week of October 21-25 has been designated as National School Bus Safety Week. Based on the theme “My School Bus, The Safest Form of Student Transportation!”​​​​​, the week focuses on student safety when waiting for and boarding a school bus. School bus safety is an issue for all citizens of Fairfax County, who need to be aware of laws requiring them to stop and wait behind a school bus when it is loading and unloading student riders.

The major responsibility for school bus safety in Fairfax County rests firmly in the hands of our school bus drivers and attendants, who daily transport more than 144,000 students in over 1,625 school buses on more than 6,800 separate routes traveling over 16.8 million operational miles each year.

 

Motorists are encouraged to practice patience and drive gently around school buses in our neighborhoods and school communities.

Problem Statement:

Fairfax County Public Schools is both directly and indirectly affected by policy and budget decisions made by local, state, and federal officials. As such, it is critical that FCPS provide input to policymakers as they debate various policy options, sharing the possible programmatic and financial impacts of legislative and policy decisions on Fairfax students, teachers, staff and the broader community.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

The School Board annually adopts a legislative program and legislative priorities to inform local, state and federal public officials and others of its positions on legislative policy and budget issues of potential importance to Fairfax County Public School students, teachers, staff, and community. The program contains the School Board’s positions on state and federal issues of major and current significance.

 

The Fairfax County School Board 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program will include additions, deletions and (where applicable) formatting changes from the Board’s 2019 legislative positions. The Board will discuss these staff recommended changes and others at a Legislation work session scheduled for October 14, 2019, as well as the Board’s state and federal priorities. Full draft text as well as a complete table of proposed changes to the draft 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program will be included as part of the Board’s Work Session agenda item. Final Action on this agenda item is scheduled for October 24, 2019.

Summary/Background:

 

The Human Resources Department recommends that the School Board award a contract to George Mason University for the provision of the Elementary Professional Development School (PDS) Internship Program.  

 

The internship program provides George Mason University Elementary PDS students an opportunity to gain practical and clinical teaching experiences through semester or yearlong internships, to include substitute assignments.  Over the past 5 years, FCPS has hosted an average of 50 elementary PDS students annually.

 

The internship program benefits FCPS by deeply engaging local, highly qualified teacher candidates to the school division resulting in highly qualified teacher hires. Other benefits to the program include built in subbing hours that allow for year-long participants to sub for FCPS for up to 45 days helping to combat FCPS’ subbing shortage.

 

The internship program also promotes a more robust student teaching experience resulting in higher quality teaching skills and outcomes. The vast majority of the George Mason University Elementary PDS graduates are hired in FCPS, often times in their PDS site locations, which sometimes includes Title I schools.

 

The period of this contract is 5 years.  The total estimated payment to George Mason University for the 2019-2020 school year is approximately $170,000; Policy 5011 requires that the School Board approve “any contract or contract amendment awarded between the School Board and other governmental entity that exceeds $250,000.”  Policy 5011.3 (III)(A)(1).

That the School Board (i) award the contract to the George Mason University for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program, beginning with 2019-2020 school year, and (ii) authorize the Director of the Office of Procurement Services to execute, deliver and administer the contract on behalf of the School Board.

Meeting
Oct 10, 2019 - Regular Meeting
Category
6. Consent Agenda - 10 p.m. Our adopted rules of Parliamentary Procedure, Robert's Rules, provide for a consent agenda listing several items for approval of the Board by a single motion.
Type
Action

                                                                             Updated 10/9/19                       

 

 

Subject: Possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in public schools (Code of Virginia § 18.2-251.1:1)

 

Staff Contact: Teresa Johnson, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services

 

Other Staff Present: Lea Skurpski, DSS director; Lorraine Trouton, FCPS manager, School Health Services

 

Meeting Category: October 10, 2019 – Work Session

 

School Board Action Required: Discussion

 

Ignite Link: Caring Culture, Student Health and Wellness

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

Effective July 1, 2019, § 18.2-251.1:1. Possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in public schools was authorized by Virginia law requiring that no school nurse employed by a local school board, person employed by a local health department who is assigned to the public school pursuant to an agreement between the local health department and the school board, or other person employed by or contracted with a local school board to deliver health-related services shall be prosecuted under § 18.2-248, 18.2-248.1, 18.2-250, 18.2-250.1, or 18.2-255 for the possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil for storing, dispensing, or administering cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil, in accordance with a policy adopted by the local school board, to a student who has been issued a valid written certification for the use of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in accordance with subsection B of § 54.1-3408.3.

 

In order to address this issue, FCPS has established a policy to address the possession, storage, and administration of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil to students at school, as authorized by Virginia law.

 

To further address procedural and documentation requirements, Regulation 2102, First Aid, Emergency Treatment and Administration of Medication for Students has been revised, in collaboration with the Fairfax County Health Department, to allow for the storage of THC-A and CBD oil in accordance with the student’s individualized health plan and in compliance with documentation requirements outlined in the Code of Virginia.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the policy to address possession, administration, and distribution of cannabidiol oil and THC-A oil, as detailed in the agenda item.

Subject: 2020 FCSB State and Federal Legislative Program

 

Staff Contact: Michael Molloy, Director of Government Relations

 

Meeting Category: October 10, 2019 - Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4, Resource Stewardship

 

 

Problem Statement:

Fairfax County Public Schools is both directly and indirectly affected by policy and budget decisions made by local, state, and federal officials. As such, it is critical that FCPS provide input to policymakers as they debate various policy options, sharing the possible programmatic and financial impacts of legislative and policy decisions on Fairfax students, teachers, staff and the broader community.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

The School Board annually adopts a legislative program and legislative priorities to inform local, state and federal public officials and others of its positions on legislative policy and budget issues of potential importance to Fairfax County Public School students, teachers, staff, and community. The program contains the School Board’s positions on state and federal issues of major and current significance.

 

The Fairfax County School Board 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program will include additions, deletions and (where applicable) formatting changes from the Board’s 2019 legislative positions. These changes and updates are intended to adjust position statements to reflect actions taken over the past year by state and federal governments, to reflect the Board’s current approaches to policy debates and issues, and to clarify, simplify and streamline existing position language where appropriate.

 

Proposed staff recommended changes to the FCSB 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program will be presented to the Board. The Board will discuss these staff recommended changes and others at a Legislation work session scheduled for October 14, 2019, as well as the Board’s state and federal priorities. Full draft text as well as a complete table of proposed changes to the draft 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program will be included as part of the Board’s Work Session agenda item. Final Action on this agenda item is scheduled for October 24, 2019.

 

Recommendation:

That the Board adopt the 2020 Fairfax County School Board State and Federal Legislative Program

These areas of focus have specific strategic actions to move the Division toward its goals. Each strategic action has targeted metrics to show the Division’s current baseline data, an interim target to achieve by school year 2020, and an aspirational statement that describes what success would look like for students, families, employees, and the community.

It represents one of the four goals (one for each goal area) that will be presented this fall.

 

All goal reports provide information about actions FCPS undertook during the year to support achievement of its aspirations, outcome data on School Board approved metrics, ROI analyses couched within a Strategic Decision-Making Framework and Future Considerations related to the results from the reporting year. The Goal 4 Resource Stewardship report addresses performance on indicators of Efficient Budgeting and Allocation, specifically, those related to the following four aspirations:

  • FCPS will maintain a cost per pupil ± $1,000 of the average cost per pupil of the Washington Area Boards of Education (WABE) average;
  • All spending will be allocated using the Strategic Decision-Making Framework;
  • All schools will have adequate classroom space for students, eliminating the need for trailers;
  • FCPS will maintain schools that all students, families, and staff consider safe.

Other Staff Present: Frances Ivey, deputy superintendent

Meeting Category: October 10, 2019 – Regular Meeting

 

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4: Resource Stewardship

 

 

Summary/Background:

 

Staff recommends that the School Board continue to lease 47,000 square feet of warehouse space, located in Springfield near the Capital Beltway and known as the Instructional Programs Support Center (IPSC). This warehouse primarily serves the Department of Instructional Services and, to a lesser extent, the Department of Special Services, Department of Information Technology, Department of Facilities and Transportation Services, and the Department of Financial Services. Specific program use is as shown on the attachment.

 

School system warehouse needs are projected to remain constant. Neither FCPS nor Fairfax County facilities are available to meet this need. This warehouse facility has been under lease by FCPS since 1999. The IPSC warehouse is strategically located near the School Board’s Forte Support Center warehouse facility which provides efficient and effective warehouse service delivery to schools and administrative centers.

 

The material terms of the current lease are as follows:

 

         Lease Item

Current Lease
  • Premises Size

47,000 Square Feet
  • Lease Term

5-Year
  • Renewal Options

(1) 5-Year
  • Lease Rate (RSF)

$12.62
  • Annual Lease Cost

$593,305
  • Annual Escalation

3.0%

Staff and legal counsel will brief the School Board in closed meeting about the progress of negotiations and lease details, as authorized by the Virginia Freedom of Information Act.

 

 

Recommendation:

That the School Board authorize the Division Superintendent, Chief Operating Officer or the Assistant Superintendent for Facilities and Transportation Services (any one of whom may act without the others), in consultation with legal counsel for the school system, to negotiate, execute and administer on behalf of the School Board, a lease agreement for the Instructional Programs Support Center for warehouse space located at 6732 Industrial Drive, Springfield, Virginia 22151, including all related documentation, within the parameters discussed by the School Board in closed meeting.

Subject: Award of Contract – Running Track Replacement at Westfield High School

 

 

Staff Contact: Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

 

Meeting Category: October 10, 2019 – Regular Meeting

 

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4

 

 

Summary/Background:

 

The running track at Westfield High School needs to be replaced and requires extensive subsurface work. Funding for this project will be provided from the Athletic Infrastructure Replacement Upgrade Fund.

 

The Office of Facilities Management (OFM) proposes to utilize FieldTurf USA, Inc., the contractor which was selected and awarded per the competitively procured, AEPA 016H Athletic Surfaces – Tracks and Courts, Contract #4400006593, for this project. FieldTurf has performed successfully on other track replacement projects for FCPS, including the 2018 Edison High School and South County School projects, which were completed on time and within budget.

 

Use of the referenced Athletic Surfaces contract represents a cooperative procurement pursuant to section 2.2-4304(B)(2) of the Virginia Public Procurement Act, which expressly permits such for track surfaces and associated work, and is sponsored through the Association of Educational Purchasing Agencies.

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board award the contract for the running track replacement at Westfield High School to FieldTurf USA, Inc., in the amount of $788,905, and authorize the Division Superintendent, or the Assistant Superintendent of Facilities and Transportation Services, to execute and administer the contract on behalf of the School Board.

Subject: Award of Contract – Title IX Softball Field Modification Ossian Hall Park (Annandale High School)

 

 

Staff Contact: Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and

Transportation Services

 

 

Meeting Category: October 10, 2019 – Regular Meeting

 

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4

 

 

Summary Background:

 

Fairfax County Public Schools and Fairfax County Government have jointly partnered to support the modification of the softball field at Ossian Hall Park (Annandale High School). The modification of this field ensures the compliance with Title IX. The modification includes installation of a new press box/concession stand and two new dugouts. Funding for the softball modification project was approved by Fairfax County School Board on July 25, 2019, as part of the FY2019 Year End Budget Review and by the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors on September 24, 2019, as part of the FY2019 Carryover Review. Specifications have been prepared, and the project has been advertised. Bids will be received on October 16, 2019. The bid tabulation and recommendation for award will be furnished prior to the November 7, 2019, School Board meeting.

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board award a contract for the Title IX Softball Field Modification Project at Ossian Hall Park (Annandale High School) to the lowest responsive and responsible bidder, and authorize the Division Superintendent, or the Assistant Superintendent of Facilities and Transportation Services, to execute, deliver, and administer the contracts on behalf of the School Board.
