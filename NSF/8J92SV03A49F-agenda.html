Teacher Appreciation Week - May 7-11, 2012

 

Every day, close to 15,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potentials for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

 

 

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2012

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

Since 1992, the President of the United States has proclaimed May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

 

 

Children's Book Week - May 7-13, 2012

 

Children’s Book Week, a national celebration of reading, storytelling, and book-related events, has been observed since 1919.   Established to promote higher standards in children’s books, the week grew to involve librarians, publishers, and booksellers.   Nationwide, author and illustrator events take place each year and the Children’s Choice Book Awards, the only national child-chosen book awards program, are announced during the week.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2012

 

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month every year in May. This annual event provides opportunities to raise awareness about communication disorders and to promote services that can improve the quality of life for those who experience problems with speaking and listening. An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families. 

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to 10,275 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, base school itinerant services, and early childhood programs.  FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

School Nutrition Employee Week, May 7-11, 2012

 

Caring. Trained. Thoughtful. Professional. Dedicated.  These are just a few words that describe today's school nutrition professional.

 

School nutrition employees in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate daily their professional commitment to providing students with nutritious menu choices that reflect current research and meet the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. School nutrition employees must balance many roles and follow numerous federal, state and local regulations to ensure safe and healthy meals are available in schools. They are trained sanitation and food safety experts and must manage financially self-sufficient programs. School nutrition professionals also provide nutrition education to students, as well as healthy catering services to their communities. They use their creativity to make the cafeteria a fun and welcoming place all year long and perform their jobs each day because they care passionately about the children they serve.

 

FCPS school nutrition employees are committed to imparting food and nutrition knowledge and skills to students, using the school cafeteria as the nutrition laboratory and are influential in preparing students to learn.

RESOLUTION

SUPPORTING THE UNIFIED PREVENTION COALITION OF FAIRFAX COUNTY

AND ITS “PARENTS WHO HOST, LOSE THE MOST” CAMPAIGN

 

 

     WHEREAS, 65 percent of underage youth say they get alcohol from family and friends, even though it is illegal in Virginia for a person under 21 years of age to consume or possess alcohol, and it is illegal for an adult to provide alcohol to a person under 21 years of age; and

 

     WHEREAS, alcohol is the most commonly used illegal drug by Fairfax County students; and underage alcohol use is associated with traffic crashes and fatalities, other drug use, violence and gang involvement, rape, depression and suicide, and educational failure; and

 

     WHEREAS, the 2010-2011 Fairfax County Youth Survey reported that 36.5 percent of high school seniors and 20 percent of tenth graders had consumed alcohol in the last 30 days, and that 20 percent of seniors had engaged in binge drinking in the last two weeks; and

 

     WHEREAS, the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County, an independent non-profit organization of over 50 collaborative partners, including school officials, county government agencies, concerned residents, and community groups, has been instrumental in addressing underage drinking and is now in the sixth year of a campaign known as “Parents Who Host, Lose the Most: Don’t be a party to teenage drinking,” to educate parents and to increase awareness of and compliance with Virginia underage drinking laws; and

 

     WHEREAS, prevention efforts, including parents talking with their teens and modeling appropriate behavior, are critical, particularly during prom and graduation season;

 

     NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board supports the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County and recognizes the month of May 2012 as “Parents Who Host, Lose The Most” month in Fairfax County.

4.01 Proposed Attendance Area Adjustments - Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary Schools

Apr 26, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 18

Recommended Action
Approve adjustments to Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary School attendance areas in order to contribute to the efficiency of the school division, pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.6, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as shown in Appendix A to be effective for the 2012-13 school year (FTS; presented as new business 3/29/12; public hearing held 4/11/12, work session held 4/16/12)

Staff Contact:         Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  April 26, 2012 – Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                 Proposed Attendance Area Adjustment

                             Freedom Hill/Lemon Road Boundary Study

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

On February 9, 2012, the Fairfax County School Board directed Facilities Planning Services to undertake an elementary school boundary study for Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary Schools.  The purpose of the boundary study is to relieve current and projected overcrowding at Freedom Hill Elementary and to take advantage of existing capacity surplus at Lemon Road Elementary.  The School Board also authorized construction of an 8-room addition at Lemon Road to provide additional capacity by the 2013 school year. Further, the School Board authorized a second broader study of other adjacent schools in the area to be undertaken in 2013 or 2014 to reassign other areas to Lemon Road in order to fully utilize the classroom addition.  The Board indicated that minimizing impact on demographics, specifically English Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) and the Free and Reduced Meals (FRM) programs should be given careful consideration.

 

Key Points:

 

The study area is a triangular portion of the Freedom Hill attendance area that is situated south of Rt. 7, east of I-495, and north of I-66.  This section of the Freedom Hill attendance area is closest to Lemon Road.  This study area contains approximately 325 elementary students attending at Freedom Hill.  Currently, Freedom Hill has a capacity utilization of 116%, which is projected to increase to 136% by 2016.  Lemon Road has a current capacity utilization of 72%, which is projected to decline to 59% over that same time period.  To accommodate continuing enrollment pressure, Freedom Hill relies on eight classroom trailers to accommodate current enrollment growth while Lemon Road relies on student transfers from out of boundary to bolster enrollment.

 

Discussion:

 

Staff proposed two potential options to move a portion of this triangular area to Lemon Road Elementary with implementation in fall 2012 to take advantage of the current 100 student capacity surplus.

 

Option A proposed the reassignment of a portion of Idylwood Apartments, located along the northwest side of Idylwood Road and southwest of Route 7, from Freedom Hill to Lemon Road Elementary.  This option would move approximately 81 students beginning in the fall 2012 school year; that number of students is projected to grow to approximately 120 students by the 2016-17 school year.  Option B proposed the reassignment of Marshall Heights and Dominion townhomes, a portion of Idylwood and Tysons View Apartments, located on the northwest side of Idylwood Road and southwest of Route 7, from Freedom Hill to Lemon Road Elementary.  This option would move approximately 96 students for the fall 2012 school year and approximately 147 students by the 2016-17 school year.  Although Option A would move slightly fewer students for this fall and in the out years, it would have a slightly increased impact on the demographics for both schools.  Option B moves slightly more students with somewhat less impact on demographics for both schools.  

 

Three community engagement meetings were held with approximately 160 persons attending in total.  The third meeting was directed to those ESOL families requiring enhanced translation services.  Background information on the community dialogue meetings, including comments from table groups, individual and online feedback forms may be found at:  http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/freedomlemon/index.shtml

 

Generally, staff found that Option B garnered the most favorable comments in that it moved more students from Freedom Hill and resulted in minimal changes to the demographic percentages for both schools.  Several comments recommended the inclusion of other areas from Freedom Hill in an effort to keep communities together.  In reviewing these suggested changes to the options, staff concluded that they would result in too many students being moved into Lemon Road in the initial year as the numbers would exceed the capacity of that school.  The areas suggested for inclusion will be considered for the 2013/2014 study when the building addition will increase Lemon Road’s capacity.

 

Staff concurs that Option B provides the most reasonable option to provide relief to Freedom Hill and to utilize the current available capacity at Lemon Road with limited impact on the demographics.  This option provides flexibility to include other areas within the triangular study area from Freedom Hill as well as consider other adjacent schools, (Shrevewood, Westgate) for reassignment to Lemon Road in the future after the 8-classroom addition is complete.

 

It should be noted that any Advanced Academic Program (AAP) center students within the area moved to Lemon Road Elementary would be reassigned to the AAP center at Haycock Elementary, consistent with all other Lemon Road AAP students.  This change impacts a small number of students and results in greater transportation efficiency.  However, all rising 6th grade students impacted by this change would be grandfathered with transportation at their current AAP center assignment at Louise Archer Elementary.  Students in the impacted area who are receiving Level IV (school based) AAP service do not have the option to remain at Freedom Hill.  Although Lemon Road Elementary does not currently have a Level IV AAP program, that may be considered in the future. 

 

Families in the impacted area from Freedom Hill who currently receive School Age Child Care (SACC) services will continue to receive these services at Lemon Road. 

 

There will not be any changes to middle and high school assignments including the middle school AAP center assignment.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve adjustments to Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary School attendance areas in order to contribute to the efficiency of the school division, pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.6, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as shown in Appendix A to be effective for the 2012-13 school year.

Name the new middle school to be opened in September 2012 at the South County site “South County Middle School” (Cluster V; presented as new business 2/23/12)

[Posted 4/25/12]

Staff Contact:            Frances W. Ivey, assistant superintendent, Cluster V

 

Other Staff Present:  Judy Matlock, director, Cluster V 

 

Meeting Category:     April 26, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                    Naming of School

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

Community input for the naming of the new middle school at the South County site was garnered in a variety of ways including a community meeting held on February 13, 2012; an initial survey link sent to South County Secondary students’ families and staff on February 27, 2012; and a subsequent survey link sent to Home Owners Associations for distribution to residents located within the middle school boundary area, local media and South County Secondary School pyramid student families on April 18, 2012.  Hundreds of emails, phone calls and letters were also received by the Board and individual school board members.  These efforts were made to give all the residents who live within the new middle school boundaries the greatest opportunity for input on the names being considered and selected.

 

 

The following names have been considered and recommended from the community:

 

Doris Stevens Middle School -Doris Stevens was a teacher, author, and women’s right-to-vote leader who, along with many other suffragists, was imprisoned at the Occoquan Workhouse. Their imprisonment and the historic “night of terror” in 1917 marked a widely recognized turning point in the suffrage movement. Stevens’ book Jailed for Freedom provided a firsthand account of that turning point and is considered an important historical reference.

Harold J. Wilson Middle School -This individual liked to write poetry. He helped start a church ball league that later became the Lorton Little League. He was involved in the Boy Scouts and the Sons of the American Legion. He started a garden project at the Lorton Prison that involved prisoners growing produce and distributing it to homeless shelters in Washington, D.C. He delivered excess milk from the prison dairy to the Lorton Community Action Center for several years.

South County Middle School - This name maintains the connection with the current high school. For several years, the families in the community have referred to the middle school by this name.

Nike Middle School - Project Nike, an American anti-aircraft missile system, is named after the goddess Nike. This missile system historical site is located near the grounds of the new middle school.

Dr. Theodor Seuss Geisel Middle School - This author provided many great stories to our national readers. He also drew political cartoons during World War II.

George A. Malcolm Middle School - Deputy Sheriff George Malcolm served the Lorton Community for several years. He also served as the head of the Lorton Valley School and as Deputy Treasurer of the Mount Vernon and Lee Districts. He was killed in the line of duty and is interred in the Pohick Church Cemetery.

 

The community meeting resulted in “Harold J. Wilson” receiving the most support, followed by “South County”, with “Nike” and “Dr. Theodor Seuss Geisel” tying for third place.  Due to the first on-line survey’s limited engagement of the middle school community, a broad on-line survey of all students, parents and community members residing within the middle school boundary was conducted and concluded this week.  “South County” received a significant majority of all votes (60%), followed by “Doris Stevens” (17%), “Nike” (8.4%), “Harold J. Wilson” (7.8%), “Dr. Theodor Seuss Geisel” (4%) and “George A. Malcolm” (3%) Middle School.

             

Results of community input by vote tally:

  • South County Middle School
  • Doris Stevens Middle School
  • Nike Middle School
  • Harold J. Wilson Middle School
  • Dr. Theodor Seuss Geisel Middle School
  • George A Malcolm Middle School

 

Motion by Dan Storck, Mount Vernon District Representative:

 

I move that the School Board name the new middle school to be opened in September 2012 at the South County site “South County Middle School.”

Staff Contact:        Kim P. Dockery, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services

 

Meeting Category: April 26, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                 Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2012-2013

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Related To:   Legal Requirement  

 

Key Points: 

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2012-2013, as required by VDOE Superintendent’s memo #324-11, dated December 16, 2011.  This report will be used by the Virginia Department of Education in preparing the state plan for special education.  As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-proscribed abbreviated format on a date no later than May 14, 2012.  

 

New this year is an inclusion of an ‘excess cost’ calculation, used to determine that IDEA funds are used to support only those costs in excess of the average cost of a general education student, as well as a comparison of FY 2011 actual and FY 2012 budgeted special education costs as a test of FCPS’ eligibility to receive federal funds under the IDEA Maintenance of Effort rules.

 

The 2012-2013 Section 611, Part B (grades K-12) application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $32,557,475. This amount is equal to the final 2011-2012 award as revised by Superintendent’s memo #052-12 dated February 24, 2012. Estimated expenditures include cost associated with maintaining a mandatory 15% set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS).  Section 611, Part B, award will fund a total of 459.5 FTE positions.

 

The 2012-2013 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $789,151. This amount is equal to 2011-2012 award.  These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for preschool-age students.  Section 619 funds seven FTE positions.

 

The amounts above reflect no change from the 2011-2012 award.  The 2012-2013 application reflects funding of the same programs funded in the 2011-2012 application.  Small adjustments in both sections 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, and the state restricted indirect cost rate.  Section 611 also includes costs for instructional materials for Responsive Instruction, as part of the CEIS set-aside.  None of the above represent new or additional operating resources.

 

The Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) reviewed the plan and funding application at its March 14, 2012 meeting.

 

Options:  

  1. Approve the plan and application as detailed in the agenda item.
  2. Amend or reject the plan and application and provide direction to staff for further action.

Recommendation:  

 

That the School Board approve the 2012-2013 Annual Special Education plan, Section 611, Part B funding application and Section 619, Part B Preschool Grant Application, as detailed in the agenda item.

Subject:                  Strategic Governance Manual Revisions

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Key Points:

 

The Public Engagement Committee (PEC) discussed School Board special recognitions at the October 20, November 10, and  December 5, 2011, meetings, and then presented the recommended language to the Governance Committee on March 12, 2012, for further discussion.  The recommendations regarding special recognitions were then presented to the full Board on March 12, and there was consensus to include the information in the Strategic Governance Manual as Appendix G.

 

The PEC recommendations on guidelines for School Board members electronic outreach were discussed at several PEC meetings held January through March 2012.  The Board received legal advice regarding potential restrictions, and then the revised guidelines were presented to the full Board on April 16, 2012; there was consensus to include the guidelines information in the Strategic Governance Manual as Appendix H.

 

The Governance Committee discussed revisions to the language regarding citizen participation (Section 5.C., Citizen Participation, under Regular Meetings:  (1)  revise the language to reflect that the speakers’ list opens to three business days prior to the meeting (instead of the Monday preceding the meeting) to accommodate any Monday holidays; (2)  add “resolutions” to the list of topics eligible for speakers to sign up (in addition to new business and action items).  When the recommended changes were presented to the Board on March 12, there was consensus to add to the Strategic Governance Manual.

 

Recommendation

 

That the School Board approve updates and revisions to the Strategic Governance Manual as follows:  add Appendix G, School Board special recognitions; add Appendix H, Guidelines for School Board member electronic outreach; and revise Section 5, Meetings, as detailed in the agenda item.
