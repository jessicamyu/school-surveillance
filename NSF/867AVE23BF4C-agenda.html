Staff Contact: Kevin L. North, Assistant Superintendent, Human Resources

SUBJECT: Working Condition Survey Executive Briefing—Eric Hirsch

TYPE: Information

BACKGROUND:
      For decades, researchers have sought to identify the factors that make a difference in teachers’ effectiveness. Some have focused on personal traits; others have looked to teacher education and experience. More recent studies have shown clearly that the workplace can enable or constrain good teaching. Not only is the workplace important in mediating the effectiveness of teachers within their classrooms, the character of the workplace is enormously important in determining who enters teaching and who stays.

      To better understand FCPS working conditions, FCPS, in collaboration with the NEA contracted with the New Teacher Center (NTC) of the University of California, Santa Cruz to help us develop and administer a Working Conditions Survey to all school-based, licensed educators in the division. The survey was conducted last spring and initial results were presented to administrators in a workshop conducted by NTC in April 2008. An advisory committee worked with NTC to advise and tailor the survey to the needs of the division. This committee was comprised of teachers from each school level, employee organization representatives, a parent organization, and a student.

KEY POINTS:
      1. The survey results are intended to be used as an impetus for conversations around the relationship between teacher working conditions, teacher retention, and student learning. School administrators were asked to begin working with the survey results for their school by working with teacher leaders to identify several things that are working well, as well as several areas for school improvement.

      2. As part of a process of continuous improvement, working with survey data at the school level and addressing perceptions about working conditions will be an ongoing process. Providing support to schools around strategies for interpreting the survey results, how to have constructive conversation among faculty about the survey, and how to bring about real school improvement are areas FCPS is addressing as a part of the process.

      3. The survey results provide an opportunity for principals to share their successes and promising practices that other administrators may find beneficial.

SUBJECT: Comprehensive Planning for Schools

TYPE: Discussion

BACKGROUND:

The School Board directed Facilities Planning staff to establish and regularly update a Facilities Comprehensive Plan, organized by regional areas, which reflects School Board policy and guides decision making to ensure sound instructional practices; a reasonable balance of capacity utilization; and wise, efficient, and environmentally-responsible use of resources. Implicit in this planning process is greater public engagement in planning for school facilities and CIP resource allocation recommendations which are, at present, solely staff generated.

To that end, as an element of the CIP, staff has proposed a concept to initiate a comprehensive planning initiative based on four regions that mirror the Fairfax County Comprehensive Plan, including a timeline for the implementation of four Board appointed citizen planning groups, a study period and reporting recommendations to the School Board.

KEY POINTS:

In light of the existing and growing capacity deficits due to recent and projected high enrollment, staff has recommended holding further work on renovations in abeyance in order to focus on identifying and prioritizing needed capacity enhancements, e.g., building additions, modular additions, boundary and/or program changes.

The suggested planning geography is based on longstanding County regional planning areas and smaller planning districts. While these geographies do not align to high school pyramids and school boundaries, the underlying census block groups generally align very well within in both school boundaries and County Planning areas and can provide a rich data base to inform both planning and projections for schools.

OPTIONS:
      As an alternative to this process at this time, staff could develop a list of capacity enhancement projects at schools for which it is clear that boundary changes or program moves are clearly not viable options. These projects could be incorporated into next year’s CIP. This would leave a list of overcrowded schools for which staff believes there might be solutions through boundary or program changes or capacity enhancements predicated on future new major residential developments. This would allow the School Board to 1) move forward with the creation of a citizen planning group for one (or more) of the four planning areas which are deemed to be the most urgent and 2) initiate the planning process as recommended by staff. Lessons learned from this pilot study area(s) could be used to refine the process and launch the planning groups and studies for the remaining regions over whatever time period is deemed appropriate by the School Board.

Staff hopes to be able to provide a preliminary list of schools in these categories to the School Board at the work session.

RECOMMENDATION:

Concur with initial comprehensive planning concept for plan geography and appointed citizen planning groups

SUBJECT Suspension of Policy 4680 - Salary Redlining Procedure

Type: Discussion

BACKGROUND:

Policy 4680 authorizes redlining of salaries for up to two years for those employees whose salaries have been reduced as the result of a change in position, if that change was made for the benefit of the school system, rather than a voluntary choice of the employee. The policy provides that redlining may be modified by the Board if deemed necessary for budgetary reasons.

KEY POINTS:

The redlining policy plays a valuable role in minimizing morale problems and easing the transition for employees to lower level jobs by insuring that their salary remains at a higher level. However, this policy was envisioned to ease the transition to lower level positions during normal times. The current budget situation places us in extraordinary times and if we are to realize planned savings from reorganizations and budget reductions, the policy needs to be suspended.

Failure to suspend the redlining policy will mean that very little, if any, money is saved in reorganization and in budget-related reductions that involve people moving to lower level positions or shorter contract lengths. As a result, in order to meet the budgetary targets, it will be necessary to conduct additional reductions in force, in which more employees lose their FCPS employment entirely. In order to maximize the number of employees in employment, while spreading the financial burden of budget cuts equitably, staff recommends the Board suspend the redlining policy effective January 1, 2009, until further notice.

The recommended suspension of redlining would be prospective only, and would not impact the salaries of those currently in redline status---unless those employees were subsequently subject to reorganization or RIF. The suspension also would not preclude the Superintendent or Assistant Superintendent for Human Resources to extend salary levels for short periods in order to settle legal disputes.

OPTIONS:

The School Board could maintain the redlining policy in its current form and make compensating budget cuts in programs, or it could implement budget cuts by laying off larger numbers of staff.

RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board suspend Policy 4680, Salary Redlining Procedure, as described in the agenda item, for budgetary reasons effective January 1, 2009; further direct the Superintendent to suspend the redlining provisions of any implementing regulations, effective January 1, 2009; the suspension of the policy and implementing regulations shall remain in effect until further School Board action.

    55.02 Discussion - Change to the Annual Organizational Meeting Process
    Meeting
    Dec 8, 2008 - Work Session No. 51-55
    Category
    55. Governance - 4:30 p.m.
    Type
    Information

    The Board will discuss the possibility of changing the annual organizational meeting process from January to July of each year. Currently the School Board’s organizational meeting is scheduled for the first meeting in January; the election of the chairman and the vice chairman is conducted at this meeting, along with the confirmation of various appointments (clerks, deputy clerks, agents, deputy agents), future meeting dates, and signature authorities. The Code of Virginia specifies that the organizational meeting may be scheduled in either January or July, and the School Board is considering changing their schedule to July. From 1928 to 1995, the organizational meeting was held in July. Since the first elected School Board took office in January 1996, the organizational meeting has been held in January. This schedule is included in the Board’s Strategic Governance Manual.
