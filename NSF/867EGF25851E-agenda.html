African American History Month - February 2008

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." This commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.

Since 1926, The Association for the Study of Afro-American Life and History (ASALH) has established the national theme for the month-long celebration of African American History Month. In honor of its founder, the Association has devoted the 2008 Annual Black History Theme to both the labors of Woodson and the origins of multiculturalism.

National Career and Technical Education Month


Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from registered nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (office administration and technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (merchandising, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle school, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

Resolution
Naming
the Football Stadium at
West Potomac High School
“The Jeffrey C. Dietze Stadium”


Whereas, Jeffrey C. Dietze has been the only director of student activities at West Potomac High School during its 23-year existence, ever loyal to the concept of West Potomac High School in forming a new community school and shepherding it to success, per aspera ad astra; and

Whereas, he has displayed constant devotion to students’ advancement in whatever interests or specialties they may have chosen; and

Whereas, he has made enormous sacrifices of his time and talent to West Potomac High School and the West Potomac community; and

Whereas, he has devoted himself to student activities and athletics, not just at West Potomac High School and in Fairfax County Public Schools, but in the commonwealth of Virginia and in the nation, as a steady mentor to youth and to new student activities directors; and

Whereas, every student who ever participated in a West Potomac High School activity, club, fine arts program, or sports team did so because of
Jeff Dietze’s meticulous planning;

Now, therefore, be it resolved that the Fairfax County Public School Board names the stadium at West Potomac High School “The Jeffrey C. Dietze Stadium” in honor of his valued service to the students of West Potomac High School and to the community.
