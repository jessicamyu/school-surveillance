International Baccalaureate Primary Years Program (PYP)

PYP FACTS
International Baccalaureate (IB) Primary Years Program (PYP) serves all children by providing an educational framework based upon current research about how children learn. PYP focuses on the development of the whole child, inside and outside the classroom. In a PYP school, existing curriculum is organized into five elements: knowledge, concepts, skills, attitudes, and action. Interdisciplinary inquiry units are built at each grade level around six main themes: who we are; where we are in place and time; how we express ourselves; how the world works; how we organize ourselves; and sharing the planet. The purpose of these integrated units is to help students deepen their understanding of core concepts by making connections across disciplines and in a variety of learning experiences. The Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) Program of Studies content and learning objectives would serve as instructional goals and essential learning elements in each inquiry unit and concept based lesson.

Instructional materials and lesson preparation facilitate grade level communications, high expectations of all students, and vertical articulation through the development of the inquiry units. Lesson development and implementation require teachers to work in teams to build and implement inquiry units, reflect on student responses, and revise the lessons.

Students in PYP schools are required to study a second language in addition to the language of instruction of the school. Exposure to the language, rather than fluency, is expected at the elementary level. Student accountability is maintained and progress monitored through traditional assessments and tests. In addition, PYP requires that teachers maintain individual portfolios of student achievement as an important mechanism for documenting progress.

Service to the community is a key ingredient in the PYP school. Beginning in kindergarten, and continuing through each grade level, students develop and carry out age appropriate service activities.

Implementation of PYP requires several years of extensive teacher training and professional development. Visiting teams from IBPYP review the units of inquiry created by the teachers and look for evidence of participation by all staff members and students in the school. IBPYP is a rigorous program for both adults and students. Schools must be prepared to make a multi-year commitment and secure the support of the school staff members, the community members, the district, and the School Board before a school is accepted into the IBPYP program.

FCPS HISTORY WITH PYP PROGRAM
Exploration of the IBPYP in FCPS began in 2002 when School Board members and the assistant superintendent for instruction requested information on this program. Interest was also generated from some elementary schools that fed into Middle Years Programs (MYP) and high school IB programs.

In the spring of 2003, the Office of Elementary Instruction was asked to support schools that were interested in learning more about, and possibly implementing PYP. The schools included: Terraset, Hunters Woods, Woodley Hills, Ft. Belvoir, Ravensworth, Parklawn, and Sleepy Hollow. The Office of Elementary Instruction invited interested principals, and selected elementary and early childhood staff members to attend a PYP orientation session in June 2003.

At the annual Leadership Conference in August 2003, a meeting was held for principals and clusters to determine the level of interest at each identified school, and discuss next steps. At that meeting some schools indicated that they were not ready to proceed with PYP and others expressed continuing interest. It was agreed that the Office of Elementary Instruction would request funding in the FY2005 budget to support training for the interested schools. This request was not funded. In February 2004, the elementary office hired an IBPYP trainer to conduct a two-day introductory PYP training for school teams and instructional services staff. PYP training materials were purchased for all attendees. During the summer of 2004, an elementary summer curriculum project was created to help teachers develop inquiry units at the PYP schools. The training was provided by a PYP trainer.

At a School Board work session in July 2004, Terraset Elementary presented a proposal to become an IBPYP focus school. In preparation for this proposal, Terraset received support from the parent community and school staff members. No action was taken at the meeting regarding Terraset’s focus school request. School Board committee members requested that the PYP issue be revisited in the future. In August 2004, the staff at Terraset participated in a four-day PYP training to begin the development of inquiry units.

Six schools continue to express interest in exploring PYP. Teachers from each school attended PYP introductory training over the past year. Terraset is the only school that has completed advanced training and has begun the development of Units of Inquiry.
