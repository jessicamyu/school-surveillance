African American History Month - February 2015

 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." The commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

 

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.  This year’s theme—A Century of Black Life, History, and Culture—focuses on the efforts made by African Americans to institute change and to share their knowledge and talents while ushering in a new era of equality, opportunity, and racial democracy.

National School Counseling Week - February 2-6, 2015

 

National School Counseling Week 2015, "Celebrate School Counseling," will be observed from February 2-6, 2015, to focus public attention on the unique contribution of professional school counselors within U.S. school systems. National School Counseling Week, sponsored by the American School Counselor Association, highlights the tremendous impact school counselors can have in helping students achieve school success and plan for a career.  Every day, school counselors work with students, families, and staff to address the academic, personal, social, and career needs of all students.

 

Fairfax County Public Schools employs over 600 school counseling professionals, including school counselors, directors of student services, and career center specialists.  These professionals are committed to preparing students to become lifelong learners and productive members in a global society.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2015

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (programming , business management, information technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (sales, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

[Updated 1/27/15 - Adopted CIP attached]

 

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  January 22, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                 FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five-year enrollment projections and capacity calculations, as well as identification of new construction, capacity enhancement, renovation, special program facilities, and site acquisition.

 

The Superintendent’s Updated Proposed FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program Summary can be found in the Capital Program Recommendations chapter of the Proposed Capital Improvement Program booklet.

 

The Updated Proposed CIP document includes the following language in the Summary Highlights, on page 4: Recommended boundary adjustment options and program changes are included in the CIP for future consideration only. Any option chosen for potential implementation will be discussed and decided through a transparent process that engages the community, in accordance with School Board Policy and Regulations. This includes adjustments needed for Advanced Academic Program centers at existing facilities and newly identified site locations.

 

This language can also be found below each map located within the Regional Summaries section of the document.

 

Additionally, also added to the Summary Highlights, on page 4, is language clarifying the purpose of the CIP and describing how it is used as a planning document for future conversations.

 

Discussion:

 

The school system continues to face a challenging situation in which student enrollment is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities, provide capacity enhancement projects, and it will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions, and boundary adjustments to accommodate growing enrollment needs.

 

The CIP proposes four new elementary schools. The new elementary school needs are identified as follows: one in the Richmond Highway Corridor (Route 1 area) to relieve overcrowding in the Groveton/Hybla Valley area; one in the Northwest County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and Dulles Airport Access Road; one to relieve overcrowding in the Fairfax/Oakton area; and one on the army base to relieve overcrowding at Fort Belvoir Elementary School which is jointly funded with the Department of Defense. 

 

Funding for additions at South Lakes High School and Westbriar are also identified. Capacity enhancements are proposed at Langley, West Springfield, Herndon and Oakton High Schools as part of their renovations. Furthermore, the CIP, from 2013 bond referendum, includes construction funds for renovation at nine elementary and one middle school, plus a renovation at one high school, as well as planning funds for renovations at eight elementary, one middle, and two high schools. Many of the renovation projects will also include capacity enhancements.

 

Project costs have also been updated in this document to reflect those currently being experienced. The current and anticipated enrollment increases continue to present a major challenge as the school system struggles to provide sufficient capacity in our schools. Providing the additional new schools and capacity enhancements required to accommodate enrollment growth will cause delays in the schedule of many future renovation projects. We anticipate the next bond referendum will be in the fall of 2015. In addition, beginning in FY 2016, the Board of Supervisors approved an additional $13.1 million to offset future infrastructure replacement requirement. 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program Summary, as updated.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
