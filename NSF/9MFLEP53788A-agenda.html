Teacher Appreciation Week - May 4-8, 2015

 

Every day, over 15,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potentials for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

 

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2015

 

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

Since 1992, the President of the United States has proclaimed May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

 

School Nurse Day - May 6, 2015

School Nurse Day was established in 1972 to foster a better understanding of the role of school nurses in the educational setting.  This annual event recognizes the contributions that school nurses make every day to improve the health and success of our nation’s children.  This year’s theme, “The School Nurse:  Caring for Others, Caring for Ourselves,” reflects the significant roles that school nurses have in health care in their school communities, as well as in modeling health and resiliency.

School nursing is a specialized practice of professional nursing that advances the health and well-being of students and is an integral part of a quality school health program. School nurses promote health, safety, prevention, and wellness; intervene with actual and potential health problems; and provide case management services.  They are an invaluable bridge connecting health care providers, public health, and families.

The school system works closely with the Fairfax County Health Department to provide quality services to the children of Fairfax County Public Schools and recognized May 6 as School Nurse Day in Fairfax County.

RESOLUTION

SUPPORTING THE UNIFIED PREVENTION COALITION OF FAIRFAX COUNTY

AND ITS “PARENTS WHO HOST, LOSE THE MOST” CAMPAIGN

 

 

 

     WHEREAS, underage youth say they can easily get alcohol from family and friends, even though it is illegal in Virginia for a person under 21 years of age to consume or possess alcohol, and it is illegal for an adult to provide alcohol to a person under 21 years of age; and
 

     WHEREAS, alcohol is the most commonly used illegal drug by Fairfax County students; and underage alcohol use is associated with other drug use, traffic crashes and fatalities, rape, depression, suicide, educational failure, violence and gang involvement; and
 

     WHEREAS, the 2013-2014 Fairfax County Youth Survey reported that 36.2 percent of high school seniors and 17.5 percent of tenth graders had consumed alcohol in the last 30 days, and that 18.9 percent of seniors had engaged in binge drinking in the last two weeks; and
 

     WHEREAS, the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County, an independent non-profit organization of over 60 collaborative partners, including school officials, county government agencies, concerned residents, students, and community groups, has been instrumental in addressing underage drinking and binge drinking, and is now in the ninth year of a campaign known as “Parents Who Host, Lose the Most: Don’t be a party to teenage drinking,” to educate parents and to increase awareness of and compliance with Virginia underage drinking laws; and

 

     WHEREAS, prevention efforts, including parents talking with their teens and modeling appropriate behavior, are critical, particularly during prom and graduation season;
 

     NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board supports the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County and recognizes the month of May 2015 as “Parents Who Host, Lose The Most” month in Fairfax County.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2015

 

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month every year in May. The theme this year is “Early Intervention Counts,” a campaign to make people aware of the warning signs of speech, language, and hearing problems and the positive effect that early intervention can have to reverse and even prevent them. This annual event provides opportunities to promote services that can improve the quality of life for those who experience problems with speaking and listening. An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families. 

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to more than 10,000 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, via base school itinerant services, and in early childhood programs.  FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

School Nutrition Employee Week, May 4-8, 2015

 

Caring. Trained. Thoughtful. Professional. Dedicated.  These are just a few words that describe today's school nutrition professional.

 

School nutrition employees in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate daily their professional commitment to providing students with nutritious menu choices that reflect current research and meet the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. School nutrition employees must balance many roles and follow numerous federal, state and local regulations to ensure safe and healthy meals are available in schools. They are trained sanitation and food safety experts and must manage financially self-sufficient programs. School nutrition professionals also provide nutrition education to students, as well as healthy catering services to their communities. They use their creativity to make the cafeteria a fun and welcoming place all year long and perform their jobs each day because they care passionately about the children they serve.

 

FCPS school nutrition employees are committed to imparting food and nutrition knowledge and skills to students, using the school cafeteria as the nutrition laboratory, and are influential in preparing students to learn.

Contact:                   Pam Goddard, Executive Assistant and Clerk of the Board

 

Meeting Category:  April 23, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                  Guidelines for the Election of School Board Chair and Vice Chair

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Related To:             Strategic Governance

 

Key Points:

 

At the October 9, 2014, forum work session, the Board discussed the topic of developing a proposal that would improve the process by which the School Board elects its chair and vice chair.  There was consensus to refer to the Governance Committee a review of the current process; the committee would make recommendations to the Board regarding alternative options.

 

At the November 24, 2014, Governance Committee meeting, committee members reached no consensus on recommendations and agreed to seek more guidance from the full Board. After further discussion at the January 12, 2015, Governance work session, the Board asked the Governance Committee to create a suggested timeline for running for chair and vice chair.

 

At the February 2, 2015, Governance Committee meeting, the members agreed to recommend guidelines on a timeline, including a one-week notice to all Board members regarding candidacy before the first Board meeting in July.  The Governance Committee chairman drafted the guidelines and the committee agreed to the recommendation at the March 9 Governance Committee meeting. 

 

At the March 16, 2015, Governance work session, the committee presented the recommended guidelines for the election of the chair and vice chair to the full Board.  There was Board consensus to support the recommended guidelines and to schedule the recommendation for Board action. 

 

The following language, if approved, would be added to the Strategic Governance Manual (as a new section after B.5., page 35 in the current Manual):

 

Election of School Board Chair and Vice Chair

 

All School Board members will conduct campaigns for leadership positions, or support for a candidate, with integrity and respect for all School Board members.  A recommended timeline for candidates for chair or vice chair includes:

  1. Start consulting with other School Board members in early June to assess support.
  2. Notify the other 11 School Board members of interest in being a candidate in a timely manner, but no later than a week before the vote at the first regular business meeting held in July.  This may be done by email, phone call or individual meeting.
  3. If deciding within a week of the vote to run for chair or vice chair, or to accept nomination to one of these positions, notify the other 11 School Board members within 24 hours of this decision (preferably by group email).

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve guidelines for the election of School Board chair and vice chair, as detailed in the agenda item, and update the Strategic Governance Manual accordingly.

Key Points:

 

At the October 9, 2014, forum work session, the Board discussed the topic of developing a proposal that would improve the process by which the School Board elects its chair and vice chair.  There was consensus to refer to the Governance Committee a review of the current process and make recommendations to the Board regarding alternative options.

 

At the November 24, 2014, Governance Committee meeting, committee members reached no consensus on recommendations and agreed to seek more guidance from the full Board. II.         School Board Members

  1. The chair and the vice chair of the School Board shall be elected by the School Board members during the organizational meeting, which is the first regular business meeting held in July, and shall serve a one-year term (until their successors are elected at the next year's organizational meeting.) Code of VA § 22.1-72 and 22.1-76(A)]

Approve the 2015-2016 Annual Special Education plan, Section 611, Part B grant funding application and Section 619, Part B Preschool grant funding application, as detailed in the agenda item (SS; presented as new business 4/9/15)

Staff Contact:             Jane H. Lipp, Assistant Superintendent, Special Services

 

Other Staff Present:  Lea Woolsey, Director, Office of Operations and Strategic Planning 

 

Meeting Category:     April 23, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                     Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2015-2016

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Related To:                 Legal Requirement

 

Key Points: 

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report for the 2015-2016 school year, as required by VDOE Superintendent’s memo #317-14, dated December 5, 2014.  This report will be used by the Virginia Department of Education in preparing the state plan for special education.  As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-prescribed abbreviated format on a date, no later than May 15, 2015.

 

The 2015-2016 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $33,523,856.  This amount is equal to the final 2014–2015 award as stated in Superintendent’s memo #026-15, dated February 6, 2015.  Estimated expenditures include cost associated with the voluntary 15% set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS), and a mandatory private school set-aside of 0.5%.  Section 611, Part B, award will fund a total of 415.6 FTE positions.

 

The 2015-2016 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $730,708.  This amount is equal to the final 2014-2015 award.  These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for school-age students.  Estimated expenditures include costs associated with a mandatory private school set-aside of 0.8%.  Section 619 funds six FTE positions.

Apr 23, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 18
Category
6. New Business
Type

[Posted 4/22/15]

 

Staff Contact:          Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:   April 23, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                    Proposed 2015 School Bond Referendum

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Background:

 

On January 22, 2015, the School Board adopted the FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) that identified school facility needs anticipated during the referenced five-year period.  Capital improvement programming provides the foundation for cost-effective investment and construction project management of facility needs identified throughout the planning period.  Voter authorization in November 2015 will be necessary in order to issue school bonds to provide:

 

Construction funds for:

  • 6 elementary school renovations
  • 2 high school renovations
  • 1 high school addition
  • 3 modular building relocations

Planning funds for:

  • 1 new elementary school
  • 3 elementary school renovations
  • 2 middle school renovations

Key Points:

 

The adopted FY 2016-2020 CIP identified what are projected to be substantial enrollment increases over the 5-year planning period and beyond.  Specifically, these projected enrollment increases will require FCPS to accommodate more than 12,000 additional students by school year 2019 -2020, with additional growth expected through the 2024-25 school year when enrollment is projected to exceed 199,000 students. 

 

The 2015 bonds provide funds for construction, and planning for new facilities to help address the expected capacity deficits; in addition, renovation projects continue to include additional capacity in order to address the student membership growth in many schools.

 

Facilities staff members have been working with the County staff to reconcile unused authority from multiple bond referendums over an extended period of time – an exercise which is performed periodically for all local governmental entities which utilize bond funds.  This reconciliation does not alter in anyway the current annual cash flow contribution from the County of $155 million; nor will it impact the timing of any projects currently listed in the CIP.  It reflects cumulative project savings over time allowing FCPS to request less bonding authority for the projects currently identified for funding. Out of the $352.5 million CIP listed in the bond referendum, the County currently already has bonding authority for $42.5 million.  Consequently, FCPS only needs to seek bonding authority for $310 million in new bond sales.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and to take all necessary and appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2015 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $310,000,000, as detailed in the agenda item.
