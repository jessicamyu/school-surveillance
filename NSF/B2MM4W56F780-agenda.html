African American History Month - February 2019

 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." The commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

 

This year's theme, "Black Migrations" emphasizes the movement of people of African descent to new destinations and subsequently to new social realities. Beginning in the 20th century, African American migration patterns included relocation from southern farms to southern cities; from the South to the Northeast, Midwest, and West; from the Caribbean to US cities; and the emigration of noted African Americans to Africa and to European cities, such as Paris and London, after the end of World War I and World War II.

 

Such migrations resulted in a more diverse and stratified interracial and intra-racial urban population amid a changing social milieu, the emergence of both black industrial workers and black entrepreneurs; the growing number and variety of urban churches and new religions; new music forms like ragtime, blues, and jazz; the blossoming of visual and literary arts, as in New York, Washington, D.C., Chicago, and Paris in the 1910s and 1920s.

 

African-American millenials have reversed the historic trend in recent years with an uptick in black populations in Atlanta, Houston and other southern communities, bringing the North-South migration full circle.

National School Counseling Week 2019, "School Counselors: Providing Lessons for Life," will be celebrated from February 4-8, to focus public attention on the unique contribution of school counselors within U.S. school systems. National School Counseling Week, sponsored by ASCA, highlights the tremendous impact school counselors can have in helping students achieve school success and plan for a career. National School Counseling Week is always celebrated the first full week in February.

 

Fairfax County Public Schools employs close to 700 school counseling professionals, including school counselors, directors of student services, and career center specialists. These professionals are committed to preparing students to become lifelong learners and productive members in a global society with a focus on social emotional wellness, academic success, and college and career readiness. Thank you to all of our school counseling professionals for the tremendous work they do!

 

As part of National School Counseling Week, it is an honor to congratulate two current and one recently retired school counseling professionals for recent recognitions at the state and regional level.

  • Judy Edwards, retired career center specialist from Lake Braddock Secondary School, was named one of the Potomac and Chesapeake Association for College Admission Counseling’s 2018 Counselors of the Year. Ms. Edwards advocated for her students, helped to coordinate a district-wide college fair, and was active in seeking out local and district-wide scholarship opportunities for students.
  • Erin Hurley, school counselor at Cherry Run Elementary School, was named the 2018 Elementary School Counselor of the Year by the Virginia School Counselor Association. Ms. Hurley has been cited by students and colleagues for her dedication to teaching students coping skills, nurturing them emotionally, and reminding them that there is support for the way they are feeling.
  • Dr. Terri Mason, school counselor at Hunt Valley Elementary School, was named the Virginia Counselor of the Year by the Virginia Counselors Association. Students and staff at Hunt Valley ES praised Dr. Mason for being thoughtful and friendly, expressed hope in knowing they always have someone to talk to, and admire her ability to always put the whole child at the forefront of conversations.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2019

Career and Technical Education or CTE month is a public awareness campaign held every February to celebrate CTE and the achievements and accomplishments of CTE programs across the country. This year's theme, "Celebrate Today, Own Tomorrow!" raises awareness of the innovation and excellence that exists in our CTE programs as well as the crucial role that CTE plays in readying our students for careers and our nation for economic success.

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (programming, accounting, cyber security); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (sales, fashion and entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, robotics and engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education. Thank you to all of our CTE teachers and staff who help students realize Portrait of a Graduate outcomes and ensure students are college and career ready!

Ignite Link: Goal 4 – Resource Stewardship

 

Outcome:

 

Fairfax County Public Schools continues to face a challenging situation whereby student membership (in certain locations) is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities, provide capacity enhancement projects and it will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions, and boundary adjustments to accommodate growing enrollment needs.

 

New School Construction: The Capital Improvement Program (CIP) proposes three new elementary schools for the next five-year horizon through FY 2024: 1) North West County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and the Dulles Airport access road (funded); 2) Fairfax/Oakton area (partially funded); and 3) Silver Line Metro Innovation Center Station area (unfunded). To meet needs beyond the five year horizon the following are unfunded proposals: 1) a new western high school in the vicinity of the Herndon area Silver Line Metro to alleviate capacity concerns at multiple high schools; and, 2) five new and/or repurposed schools.

 

Capacity Enhancements: The CIP proposes additions at West Potomac High School, Justice High School, and Madison High School, which are all partially funded. The CIP proposes the relocation of three modular additions (2 funded, 1 unfunded).

 

Renovations: Funding for renovations at five named high schools are proposed in the CIP. Additionally, funds for the renovation of five named middle schools and one unnamed middle school are proposed. Lastly, funds for renovations at 30 named elementary schools and three unnamed elementary schools are included. Many of these renovation projects will include capacity enhancements.

 

Site Acquisition: Funds have been provided as a placeholder to acquire a school site in the future.

 

The FY 2020–24 CIP also recommends possible solutions for future consideration to resolve capacity deficits. These options include interior modifications, temporary classrooms, program location suggestions, and boundary adjustments. Any boundary adjustment or program change would be discussed and decided through a transparent process that engages the community in accordance with School Board Policies and Regulations.

 

Included in the CIP is an environmental sustainability report, which includes a summary of the renewable energy report that the board requested at its May 28 forum meeting.

 

Summary/Background:

 

The FY 2020-2024 CIP includes five-year student membership projections and capacity calculations, as well as the Capital Construction Cash Flow which identifies plans for new construction, capacity enhancements, renovations, and site acquisition to accommodate future capacity needs.

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the FY 2020-2024 Capital Improvement Program.

Subject: Contract Award for the Provision of Graduate course work for Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) Teachers

 

 

Staff Contact:  Francisco Duràn, chief equity officer

                         Leigh Burden, assistant superintendent, Financial Services

 

 

Other Staff Present: Michelle R. Pratt, director, Procurement Services

                                 Kathleen Walts, executive director, Professional Learning and Family Engagement

 

 

Meeting Category: January 24, 2019 - Regular Meeting

 

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

 

Summary/Background (Key Points): 

 

The Chief Equity Officer recommends that the School Board award a contract to George Mason University ("GMU") for the provision of graduate course work to eligible FCPS instructional personnel.  GMU will offer courses for FCPS teachers who aspire to obtain a master's degree under the master's Cohort Tuition Sharing Program, the Title I Master's Degree Tuition Sharing Program and the Special Education contract courses program.  Each program is referred to as the "Program".  Courses are to be taken in GMU's College of Education and Human Development.

 

The Master's Cohort Tuition Sharing Program was established to provide FCPS teachers on the highest steps of the BA lanes of the 2017 FCPS salary scale with an opportunity for an expedited path to a master's degree and allow them to move to a higher lane on the current scale.  The Program pays 50 percent of the cost for teachers to further their education.  Qualified candidates are those who are in the identified pay lanes (BA, BA+15, BA+30 - steps 15 and higher), and are accepted into a master's degree program at a regionally accredited university.  Participants are required to commit to a minimum of three additional years with FCPS in return for the benefit.

 

The Tuition Reimbursement program is to encourage FCPS teachers to seek opportunities to achieve a master's degree.  The Tuition Reimbursement program is available to teachers and other employees, but priority is given to the teachers on the bachelor's salary lanes in steps 14 and higher.  Additional priority goes to Instructional Assistants who are in the degree programs that will lead to licensure with the State of Virginia and potentially fill a position in a critical shortage area.  Students are invoiced for, and obligated to pay, the full tuition from a regionally accredited college or university such as GMU and them apply for reimbursement through Professional Learning and Family Engagement.  Reimbursement is not guaranteed due to funding limitations.  This agreement also allows employees on BA Lanes 15 and higher to receive an additional discount (10%) on top of the Virginia Education Discount (15%) offered to all educators by GMU.

 

Finally, GMU will provide contract courses for eligible FCPS employees that can be used as qualification for a provisional license in Special Education with the State of Virginia.  The goal of this program is to fill urgent vacancies in special education classrooms across Fairfax County Public Schools.  FCPS is invoiced for an obligated to pay 100 percent of the "contract course" tuition rate.  The program allows up to 150 FCPS staff per year to meet the eligibility requirement for provisional licensure and fill an urgent need in special education classrooms in FCPS.  Participants are required to commit to an FCPS Special Education positions for a three (3) year period and join a Special Education cohort (at their own expense) to obtain full licensure within three years.

 

Estimated contract expenditures by FCPS over the three (3) year term is a maximum of $1.675 million, or a range between $200,000 and $520,000 per academic year based upon the level of participation in the cohorts.

 

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve and award a contract to George Mason University for the provision of graduate course work to eligible FCPS instructional employees effective May 1, 2018 through June 30, 2021 and authorize the Division Superintendent or Deputy Superintendent to execute and administer such contract on behalf of the School Board.
