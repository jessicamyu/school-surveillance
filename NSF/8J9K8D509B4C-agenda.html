Category
4. WS No. 19 Facilities - 3:45 p.m.
Type
Discussion

[Updated 9/6/11 - additional attachment]

Staff Contact:                Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:       September 12, 2011 – Work Session No. 19

 

Subject:                          Use of Video Surveillance Cameras—Cafeteria/Interior Cameras (CCTV)

 

School Board Action Required:     Discussion

 

Background:

 

The Department of Facilities and Transportation Services, in collaboration with the high school principals’ association, supports expansion of the use of video surveillance cameras to monitor cafeterias and building interiors.  We make note that the use of interior cameras is widely accepted and used in public school systems across the country to enhance security and safety programs. In Virginia, interior camera systems are employed in a number of divisions, to include Virginia Beach City Schools, Prince William, Loudoun, Stafford, Fauquier, and Culpepper.  In the immediate metropolitan area, DC, Montgomery, Prince Georges, and Frederick all utilize interior camera systems to enhance security in their schools.  The use of any video camera is limited to public areas where there is no reasonable expectation of privacy.

 

There are two primary reasons for this advocacy.  First, we believe that these cameras serve as an effective deterrent that will reduce both the number and severity of incidents within our school buildings.  Second, the cameras will enable administrators to more precisely identify the participants in future incidents both in a more timely and a less disruptive manner. 

 

Video surveillance can be a strong deterrent.  The office of safety and security (OSS) conducted an analysis of external vandalism for four years (SY’s 2003-2008) and compared the exterior vandalism repair costs of those nine high schools that had external video surveillance systems with those that did not.  The analysis revealed that the nine schools that did have the video systems accounted for more than 68% of the total reductions of external vandalism for all high schools. 

 

Video surveillance is in place throughout the community and already exists on FCPS school buses.  The office of transportation reports that video surveillance has been an invaluable tool for post incident review for disciplinary and administrative purposes. 

 

Video surveillance may help deter incidents of flash mob behavior.  The recent experiences with seemingly spontaneous group actions are known to have been coordinated through social media networks and communications.  Group behaviors create such chaos and confusion, it is often difficult, if not impossible, to identify accurately  the actual offenders.  As has been the case on FCPS school buses, having video images of incidents is an incredibly important tool in investigating incidents. 

 

Existing external video surveillance has been instrumental in identifying offenders and illuminating the context of incidents on numerous occasions.  As schools began obtaining external video systems, OSS requested that these schools notify OSS of instances of its usefulness.  While not required of schools, OSS received 392 notifications since 2005.  In the last two school years, 168 incidents were reported (43% of the total for all years), which reflects the fact that as more schools obtained the systems, more incidents were captured on video.  It is also important to note that on more than one occasion, video images have provided evidence that have proven an incident did not occur as reported.

 

Video cameras are considered a force multiplier for staff by allowing the monitoring of multiple areas by a single individual and from remote locations. They can assist in situations involving liability claims and they provide a level of security after hours, when school staff is typically absent from the building.  Additionally, the high school principals association has provided comments regarding their enthusiastic and unanimous support for the use of internal video cameras in cafeterias (see attached).

 

There are three options for the implementation of internal video surveillance systems: (1) cameras can be authorized for only cafeterias; (2) they can be approved for cafeterias and areas that have high volumes of circulation and gathering; or (3) they can be authorized for the entire interior.  The disadvantage of limiting the number or specific areas is that by doing so, it can result in the movement of unwanted behaviors to those areas that do not have video surveillance, as these areas become well known to students.  Such realities may result in a renewed request for expansion of existing systems, which is a more expensive proposition than providing a complete installation at one time.

 

The cost to implement cafeteria only systems is estimated to be approximately $8,000 per site.  For a school wide interior deployment, including cafeterias, the cost is approximately $120,000.  The actual costs will vary, depending upon the size and design of a specific school.  The high school principals association has indicated that the costs could be absorbed with existing funding sources.

 

The challenges of utilizing cameras systems include: cost, both initial and recurring; they require staff with technical knowledge to leverage existing technology to minimize financial expenditures; they require training for school staff to operate; video equipment can be vandalized; and video surveillance is considered intrusive by some members of the community.   

 

Key Points:

 

The use of video surveillance systems is widely accepted as a reasonable tool to assist schools in maintaining a safe and secure environment.  The high school principal’s association supports the use of video surveillance for cafeteria monitoring and have provided extensive comments detailing their position. 

 

While there is an absence of scientific studies to investigate the effectiveness, local analysis suggests that there is a clear benefit.  There are significant cost considerations that include initial implementation and continued maintenance and life cycle replacement.  Training and specialized staff are required for the use of video surveillance systems. 

 

Fairfax County Public Schools currently deploys 372 exterior cameras at 30 schools and cameras are used on virtually all FCPS school buses.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the use of security cameras in school cafeterias and other interior locations within schools.
