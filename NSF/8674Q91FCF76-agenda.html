Laura Ashley Piper Scholarship - History

Laura Ashley Piper served as student representative to the Fairfax County School Board from 1986 to 1987. This outstanding student, talented athlete, and exceptional leader graduated from Robinson Secondary School and the Air Force Academy and was commissioned a Second Lieutenant in the Air Force. She was killed in action over northern Iraq in April 1994. To honor Laura Ashley Piper’s strong commitment to student participation and to recognize her high standards of achievement and involvement and her willingness to continually accept responsibility throughout her lifetime, the School Board of Fairfax County has established the annual $1,000 Laura Ashley Piper Scholarship.

Each year, every high school may nominate one senior student who exemplifies the “attributes of personal excellence and commitment to student government” of Lt. Piper. A committee from the Office of School Counseling Services, Department of Instructional Services, considers the nominees. There is an engraved plaque in the School Board Office with each winner’s name.

SCHOOL BOARD CHARACTER AWARD

K.E. Scarry

Oakton High School



The School Board Character Award, first presented in June 2002, was established by the Student Advisory Council and 2000-2001 Student Representative to the School Board Chris Giovarelli to be presented to a junior or senior who demonstrates a continuous record of high morals, strong integrity, and good character, and who is recognized as a role model for other Fairfax County Public Schools students in his or her day-to-day behavior. 


K.E. Scarry, a senior at Oakton High School, has demonstrated the qualities and leadership envisioned by the SAC and Mr. Giovarelli when they established this award. 


As an 8th grader, K formed “Sunshine for Soldiers” to support troops overseas, in training camps, and in hospitals, sending care packages and cards, letters, and e-mails so that servicemen and women knew how much they are appreciated.  Her organization collected over 13,000 items to support over 1,600 troops.


Beginning with her freshman year, K became involved in Best Buddies, taking on increasingly active leadership roles, and currently serving as its president.  Best Buddies is dedicated to enhancing the lives of those with intellectual and developmental disabilities by creating one-on-one friendships with nondisabled students, focusing on abilities, not disabilities.  Under K’s leadership, Oakton has become a more inclusive and welcoming environment for students of all abilities.


Additionally, K is an outstanding student, athlete, and community leader, coaching soccer and baseball Special Olympics teams and traveling with her church group on numerous mission trips.  Her positive attitude and enthusiasm make her one whom other students willingly follow. 


K is a true leader, walking the walk in serving others and making her school and local communities better places.  As one nominator wrote, "Not only is she a dedicated and creative volunteer but her kindness, concern, and respect for others have greatly enhanced our program and our community.  Recently, one of our program participants told me his mother said that the world is a better place because of this young woman."

Citizens who want to address either a new business or an action item, as listed on the School Board regular meeting agenda, may do so online starting at 6 a.m. on the Monday preceding the meeting or by calling the School Board office at 571-423-1075 during normal business hours. Subject: Award of Contract for Synthetic Turf Field at Herndon High School

School Board Action Required: Decision

Related To: School Board Policy 5011.2, Authority to Contract

Key Points:

    • Herndon High School and its community desire to have synthetic turf installed on its main stadium and practice fields. There are two cooperative purchasing contracts for synthetic turf available to the School Board and pricing was requested from:
        • · General Sports Venue/Astroturf (U.S. Communities contract; Astroturf manufacturer)
        • · Atlas Tracks, Inc. (Association of Educational Purchasing Agencies contract; FieldTurf Tarkett manufacturer)
      • Atlas Tracks, Inc. provided the most favorable pricing for this project.

Funding for the project will be provided as follows:


$400,000 from FCPA (turf bond program).


$150,000 from BOS via CRS (turf mini-grant program).


$784,615 from the “local contributing parties”: Herndon Optimists $50,000; Herndon High School Boosters $200,000; and Herndon Youth Soccer $150,000, with the balance of $384,615 financed by the local contributing parties through Virginia Heritage Bank.



All parties mentioned above will be signatories to the agreement with the School Board.

6.02 Amendment 16 to the ERFC Benefit Plan - Recommendation to approve Amendment 16 to the ERFC Benefit Plan (ERFC and ERFC 2001) for the purpose of simplifying administration and clarifying the application of certain administrative rules without changing benefits (HR; action scheduled 6/24/10)
Meeting
Jun 10, 2010 - Regular Meeting No. 21
Category
6. New Business - 8:40 p.m.
Type
Information

Staff Contact: Jeanne M. Carr, ERFC Executive Director/CIO

Meeting Category: June 10, 2010 – Regular Meeting No. 21

Subject: Amendment 16 to the Educational Employees of Fairfax County Defined Benefit Plan (ERFC and ERFC 2001)

School Board Action Required: Information

Related To: Legal Requirement
Key Points:
    The ERFC Board of Trustees is recommending several amendments to the ERFC Defined Benefit Plan (ERFCand ERFC 2001) for the purpose of simplifying administration and clarifying the application of certain administrative rules. None of the proposed changes is expected to affect the benefits payable under the Plan.

A summary of the proposed amendments is provided below.
      A. Simplifying the administration of benefits for certain members who have credit for service before July 1, 1988
      The benefit structure in ERFC was substantially amended in 1988 because of changes in the benefits provided by VRS. One benefit provision – an alternative calculation - was adopted at that time to preserve the value of certain benefits that members had expected under the pre-1988 plan. Now, more than 20 years later, the formula under the amended plan consistently produces a higher amount of benefits. In retirement calculations performed for retiring members over the past two and a half years, the alternative calculation has not produced a higher benefit for even one retiree. Analysis of the formula, moreover, demonstrates that it is extremely unlikely that this alternative calculation will produce a higher benefit for any future retirees. In connection with the recent automation of benefit calculations, the Trustees recommend amending section 5.02(b)(3) of the ERFC Plan document to streamline this administrative function by removing the alternative calculation.
    B. Clarifying rules for purchase of service credit in ERFC
      Members of ERFC (but not ERFC 2001) are permitted to purchase service credit for work that is not otherwise credited in the Plan. Under rules adopted by the Trustees, these purchases are more costly as a member approaches retirement; a purchase made less than five years before actual retirement is subject to a substantial monetary penalty both to encourage earlier purchases and to give the Plan a longer period to invest the proceeds of a purchase before the Plan begins to pay out benefits to the member who purchased the credit. When a member makes such a purchase with the intent of waiting five years before retiring but later changes his or her plans and retires sooner, the amount of the credit is recalculated so that the penalty is appropriately charged to the member. The Trustees recommend amending section 11.01(b) of the ERFC Plan document to clarify that the Plan will not refund the purchase price of the service credit to a member whose purchase is subject to such a recalculation.
    C. Clarifying the rules for leaving accumulated contributions on deposit
      Members who leave FCPS positions that are covered by ERFC or ERFC 2001 have the option of leaving their member contributions on deposit, and thus preserving their rights under the Plan in the event they return to covered employment at a later date. If the member was not vested in the Plan and ten years passes without the member returning to covered employment or requesting a refund of the member contributions, the Plan provides that the contributions shall become the property of the Plan, subject to refund (but without any additional interest) if the member or the member’s estate later requests the refund. The Trustees recommend amending the ten-year rule, in Section 12.02 of the ERFC Plan and in Section 7.02 of ERFC 2001, in order to clarify that it operates only if there is a single continuous ten-year absence from covered employment.

      D. Clarifying the rules for redeposit of member contributions after returning to covered employment
      In ERFC 2001, a member who leaves covered employment and withdraws his or her member contributions has the right to redeposit those contributions and reinstate all prior credit in the Plan upon returning to covered employment within five years. (This rule does not apply in ERFC, because that is a closed Plan; a member returning to covered employment after withdrawing contributions from ERFC is not permitted to re-enter ERFC.) The Trustees recommend amending Section 7.04 of ERFC 2001 to clarify that the member must make this redeposit on his or her first return to covered employment after withdrawing the member contributions.

      E. Clarifying the time limits for taking legal action against ERFC or ERFC 2001
      Both ERFC and ERFC 2001 contain two-year time limits for the commencement of any legal action against the Plan. These limits were adopted to protect the Plan, the Trustees, and the School Board from claimants seeking additional benefits many years after benefit payments had begun, and to provide a definite time period where the Virginia Code is unclear about the applicable time limits. The Trustees recommend amending Section 16.17 of the ERFC Plan and Section 11.15 of ERFC 2001 to clarify that in cases where the Plan has made erroneous benefit payments, the Trustees can file a suit (if necessary) to recover that money within two years after they determine that such an erroneous payment occurred.


Recommendation:
      That the School Board approve Amendment 16 to the ERFC Benefit Plan (ERFC and ERFC 2001) for the purpose of simplifying administration and clarifying the application of certain administrative rules without changing benefits.

Staff Contact: Dean Tistadt, Chief Operating Officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

Meeting Category: June 10, 2010 – Regular Meeting No. 21

Subject: Southwestern Regional Planning Study

School Board Action Required: Information

Key Points:

The Southwestern Regional Planning Committee which began meeting on October 2009 has completed Phases I and II of their assigned work. Staff commends and compliments all the members of the ad hoc planning group, particularly the leadership, including the co-chairs and sub-committee chairs, for their commitment to the work, the resulting analysis and creative solutions. Staff is especially grateful for the collegial spirit with which the committee engaged staff and for their patience as staff worked to support this first ad hoc planning process. The ideas bought forward by the committee provided new planning perspectives to staff in consideration of the focus issues: overcrowding in the region and renovation challenges with Clifton ES.

The updated staff report reaffirms the topographic and access challenges associated with renovating Clifton. One of the major, but not the only, challenge is the absence of public water and the water quality issues of the existing wells.

Unique characteristics of the Clifton Elementary School site will result in higher than normal renovation costs. Staff estimates that a full scope renovation of Clifton ES will cost about $35,287 per student which is significantly higher than similar renovations of other FCPS schools which cost about $22,805 per student. This cost factor is magnified due to the fact that the most recent projections indicate that enrollment is declining at Clifton Elementary School with an enrollment of 298 students projected for School Year 2015-16.

Should the School Board decide to close Clifton Elementary School, the building can be kept by the School Board in case of future unanticipated need. In the meantime, the School Board could consider leasing the building at a very minimal cost to the Town of Clifton to address that community’s understandable concerns about having a place to meet and gather. Other adaptive reuse options for the building and site could also be considered.

The FCPS educational specifications were developed to ensure a rich and appropriate educational environment at each of our schools. Staff does not believe that we should do a reduced scope renovation at Clifton Elementary School as a means to reduce cost unless the School Board is prepared to make similar reductions at all schools for future renovations. If existing excellent student achievement is a criterion by which to make a decision to reduce scope, we have a number of upcoming renovations for consideration.

FCPS has a history of closing schools when enrollments become too low or when site and building challenges do not allow renovations to bring a building up to current educational specifications or when the costs to do so become prohibitive. Wilton Woods, Pine Ridge, Masonville, Walnut Hill and Virginia Hills were all schools that were closed by action of the School Board. More recently, the School Board decided to move Graham Road from its current location to the Devonshire site due to constraints and costs associated with the existing site.

Options:

Staff believes that the only viable option to closing Clifton Elementary School is to renovate that school and construct additions at several schools within the study area. Staff does not support keeping Clifton open and building a new school as this would be costly for both the capital program and for the operating fund.

The staff and the ad hoc committee reports offer different options to address overcrowding in the area elementary schools and to address the potential renovation of Clifton ES. The sub-committee reports outlining other solutions with pros and cons of the options is attached along with the participation results of the community outreach meetings.

Recommendations:

Staff recommends that the School Board accept the Southwestern Regional Planning Committee Phase II report and the updated staff report.

Staff is recommending that based upon the information contained in these two reports that the School Board (1) build a new elementary school on the Liberty Middle School site; (2) build one or more additions on elementary schools in that region; (3) conduct a boundary study based upon the new school, the addition(s), and potential program moves; (4) close Clifton Elementary School upon completion of the new elementary school.

Should the School Board decide to renovate Clifton Elementary School, staff recommends that it be a complete renovation based upon the FCPS educational specifications. Furthermore, staff would recommend that additions be built on 2 or more elementary schools in the region to provide capacity to provide relief to overcrowding and conduct a boundary study based upon these additions and potential program moves.
