National Red Ribbon Week, October 23-31, 2019

 

National Red Ribbon Week was established in memory of Enrique Camarena, a U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration agent who was killed in the line of duty while investigating a major drug cartel in Mexico.  To honor his battle against illegal drugs, friends and neighbors began to wear red badges of satin and some parents began to form coalitions.  National Red Ribbon Week grew from these grassroots efforts to form a national movement against the use of illegal drugs.  Its annual campaign encourages students to take a visible stand against substance abuse and make a personal commitment to follow a drug-free lifestyle.  The 2019 Red Ribbon Theme is "Send a Message, Stay Drug Free" which is a call to action to speak out in support of healthy choices.  The theme is also a reminder that by staying drug free, you are sending a message to yourself and others about how much you value yourself, your community and your future.

 

 

Native American Heritage Month - November 2019

 

Native American Heritage Month started in 1915 as American Indian Day by the Boy Scouts of America.  Over the years, individual states recognized Native American days, but it wasn’t until 1990 that President George H.W. Bush designated November as National American Indian Heritage Month.  Since 1994, a presidential proclamation has proclaimed the month to be Native American Heritage Month in honor of the significant contributions made by the first Americans to the establishment and growth of the U.S.

National Career Development Month - November 2019

 

November 2019 is National Career Development Month. Career center specialists, school counselors, career experience specialists, employment and transition representatives, and career and transition teachers play unique roles in career development for students. With a focus on readiness for postsecondary education and careers, these educators are employed to help students explore and develop their abilities, interests, strengths, and talents as they relate to further education and entry into the workforce.

 

These educators collaborate with parents to enhance academic and career planning for students. Together with all teachers in PreK-12, these educators help students investigate skills, qualities, and academic opportunities that relate to career fields, encourage lifelong learning, and promote future readiness. Students explore careers and other postsecondary options through classroom instruction, the academic and career plan, academic advising, work-based learning, and related activities. Through career awareness activities, exploration, and development, students learn the skills and training necessary to be successful in future careers and achieve gainful employment and personal fulfillment. Career development activities are essential for building portrait of a graduate attributes in all of our students, and we thank all of our educators who are leading the way with career development.

Fairfax County Public Schools 2019 Excellence in Equity Awards

 

 

The Excellence in Equity Award is a new FCPS recognition of schools that have made considerable gains in improved school performance across all student subgroups and significant reductions in achievement gaps among student subgroups.  The awards are based on school performance in the 2018-19 school year under revised accreditation standards that were approved by the state Board of Education in 2017.  Under the revised accreditation system, schools are evaluated on school quality indicators grouped in three categories: academic achievement, achievement gaps, and student engagement and outcomes.

 

Schools earning the Excellence in Equity Award for being a top ten school to achieve the highest average increase across all reporting groups in English for the 2018-19 school year are: Aldrin Elementary, Armstrong Elementary, Crestwood Elementary, Fort Hunt Elementary, Garfield Elementary, Greenbriar West, Hollin Meadows, Justice High, Oak Hill Elementary, and Rose Hill Elementary.

 

The top ten schools earning the Excellence in Equity Award for the highest average increase across all reporting groups in Mathematics for the 2018-19 school year are: Armstrong Elementary, Fairhill Elementary, Forestdale Elementary, Fort Belvoir Primary Elementary, Greenbriar West Elementary, Herndon Middle, Herndon High, Irving Middle, Mount Eagle Elementary, and Woodson High.

 

Schools earning the Excellence in Equity Award for being a top ten school to achieve the greatest reduction of the English achievement gap for the 2018-19 school year are: Armstrong Elementary, Baileys Upper Elementary School for the Arts & Sciences, Bryant Alternative High, Bucknell Elementary, Crestwood Elementary, Flint Hill Elementary, Garfield Elementary, Greenbriar West Elementary, Lake Anne Elementary, and Oak Hill Elementary.

 

The top ten schools earning the Excellence in Equity Award for achieving the greatest reduction of the mathematics achievement gap for the 2018-19 school year are: Centreville Elementary, Edison High, Fairhill Elementary, Greenbriar West Elementary, Hunters Woods Elementary, Marshall High, Mount Eagle Elementary, Orange Hunt Elementary, Willow Springs Elementary, and Wolftrap Elementary.

 

Additionally, 107 schools will be recognized for achieving all Gap Groups meeting level 1 requirements in both English and mathematics for the 2018-19 school year. This recognition will occur at the Region principal meetings in November 2019.

  • All staff will view student behavior through a culturally responsive lens
  • All students will report healthy social-emotional skills and lifestyle behaviors on the Fairfax County Youth Survey
  • All schools will have low rates of students with frequent absences
  • No students will have disruptive behavior referrals
  • All employees will have the opportunity to take advantage of wellness resources

Subject: 2020 FCSB State and Federal Legislative Program

 

Staff Contact: Michael Molloy, Director of Government Relations

 

Meeting Category: October 24, 2019

 

School Board Action Required: Final Action

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4, Resource Stewardship

 

 

Problem Statement:

Fairfax County Public Schools is both directly and indirectly affected by policy and budget decisions made by local, state, and federal officials. As such, it is critical that FCPS provide input to policymakers as they debate various policy options, sharing the possible programmatic and financial impacts of legislative and policy decisions on Fairfax students, teachers, staff and the broader community.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

The School Board annually adopts a legislative program and legislative priorities to inform local, state and federal public officials and others of its positions on legislative policy and budget issues of potential importance to Fairfax County Public School students, teachers, staff, and community. The program contains the School Board’s positions on state and federal issues of major and current significance.

 

The Fairfax County School Board 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program includes additions, deletions and (where applicable) formatting changes from the Board’s 2019 legislative positions. These changes and updates are intended to reflect actions taken over the past year by state and federal government, to reflect the Board’s current approaches to policy debates and issues, and to clarify, simplify and streamline existing position language where appropriate.

 

Proposed staff recommended changes to the FCSB 2020 State and Federal Legislative Program were introduced in a presentation and discussion at the Board’s Regular Meeting on October 10, 2019. Those and other changes were further discussed at the Board’s October 14, 2019 legislative work session.

 

The following changes have been made to the document based on information presented and discussed at the October 10 Regular Meeting October 14 Work Session:

 

  • Completed final formatting of document by removing draft version strikethroughs and underlines; including cover page, table of contents, etc.; and updating letter and number position identifiers.
  • Included placeholder pages for State (p.4) and Federal (p.22) Board Priority Positions, content to be determined by the Board
  • Reworded end of second paragraph of 2020 Joint Legislative Priority Position to include additional state funding effort data to reflect Board feedback. Parallel language changes will be made by the County (p.5)
  • Added language to F.3 Early Childhood Education supporting a change in the income eligibility threshold for Virginia Preschool Initiative (VPI) program funding (language parallels similar position in the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors legislative program) (p.8)
  • Moved new position regarding advocacy for moving away from chronic absenteeism as a school quality measure under the Standards of Accreditation and the state’s federal ESSA plan from FED. to T.18 Standards of Accreditation (p.14)
  • Added language to Z.9 Student Safety supporting allowing school boards to identify a designee to undertake required annual school safety plan reviews (response to Board Follow Up request from 09/12/2019 closed session) (p.19)
  • Added language to AB.2 Teachers and Instructional Personnel requesting Board discretion over appropriate weighting of student academic progress in teacher evaluation systems (responding to current state level discussions of potential changes to teacher evaluation guidelines) (p.20)

 

 

Beyond these incorporated changes, additional Board member amendments as well as final language for the Board’s State and Federal Priorities are anticipated for the Board’s consideration when this agenda item is scheduled for Final Action on October 24, 2019.

Each strategic action has targeted metrics to show the Division’s current baseline data, an interim target to achieve by school year 2020, and an aspirational statement that describes what success would look like for students, families, employees, and the community.

 

This report describes actions and outcomes from SY 2018-19, related to Goal 2 of the Strategic Plan, Caring Culture, which details FCPS’ commitment to championing a welcoming environment and healthy life choices for students and staff. It represents one of the four goals (one for each goal area) that will be presented this fall.

 

All goal reports provide information about actions FCPS undertook during the year to support achievement of its aspirations, outcome data on School Board approved metrics, ROI analyses couched within a Strategic Decision-Making Framework and Future Considerations related to the results from the reporting year. The Goal 2 Caring Culture report addresses performance on indicators of welcoming environments and health life choices, specifically, those related to the following four aspirations:

  • All students, families, and staff will feel respected and included at school
  • All staff will view student behavior through a culturally responsive lens
  • All students will report healthy social-emotional skills and lifestyle behaviors on the Fairfax County Youth Survey
  • All schools will have low rates of students with frequent absences
  • No students will have disruptive behavior referrals
  • All employees will have the opportunity to take advantage of wellness resources

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the Strategic Plan Goal 2: Caring Culture report as detailed in the agenda item.

Summary/Background:

 

The Instructional Services Department recommends that the School Board award a contract to the University of Wisconsin for the provision of English language proficiency (ELP) assessments.  These assessments are mandated by the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) for use by all public schools in Virginia.

 

The total estimated payment to the University of Wisconsin for this assessment for the 2019-2020 school year is expected to be $1.1M; the payment amount is based upon the total number of assessments submitted for scoring in a given school year.  Policy 5011 requires that the School Board approve “any contract or contract amendment awarded between the School Board and other governmental entity that exceeds $250,000.”  Policy 5011.3 (III)(A)(1).

 

These assessments also comply with federal legislation [Section 3113(b)(2) and Section 1111 (b) (7) of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 2015] and related guidance from the VDOE, which requires that all English learners (ELs):

 

1)  be assessed in English language proficiency upon entry into a Virginia public school; and

2)  be assessed annually in English language proficiency.  

 

The assessment suite from the University of Wisconsin, developed by the World-Class Instructional Design and Assessment (WIDA) Consortium, measures an English learner’s proficiency in English reading, writing, speaking, and listening skills for the dual purposes of establishing the student’s eligibility for English for Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) services and monitoring the student’s progress in acquiring English proficiency.

 

Assessments within the WIDA suite are:

  • WIDA Assessing Comprehension and Communication in English State to State for English Language Learners® 2.0 (WIDA ACCESS for ELLs 2.0), used for annual assessment of most ELs
  • WIDA Alternate ACCESS for ELLs, used for annual assessment of certain ELs with significant cognitive abilities
  • WIDA-ACCESS Placement Test (W-APT), used for entering kindergarten ELs
  • WIDA Screener, used for ELs entering or re-entering FCPS from outside Virginia public schools
  • WIDA Measure of Developing English Language (MODEL), used for ELs entering in second semester of kindergarten or first semester of grade 1

 

The Virginia Board of Education adopted the WIDA ACCESS for ELLs 2.0 in 2007 and the WIDA Alternate ACCESS for ELLs in 2013 as the state-approved ELP annual assessments for EL students. 

 

In FCPS, these assessments are used to:

 

  • Determine students’ ELP level, eligibility for ESOL services, and eligibility for testing accommodations and exemptions;
  • Calculate EL progress as part of the combined rate for English Achievement and English Achievement Gap school quality indicators within the Standards of Accreditation (SOA);
  • Help teachers and families understand students’ current level of English language proficiency and progress toward proficiency along the developmental continuum;
  • Provide teachers with information they can use to plan for and support student learning;
  • Produce data necessary to calculate annual ESOL staffing allocations for each school; and
  • Generate information required for annual application for federal Title III, Part A funding.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board (i) award the contract to the University of Wisconsin for English language proficiency assessments as required by the Virginia Department of Education, for the 2019-2020 school year, and (ii) authorize the Director of the Office of Procurement Services to execute, deliver and administer the contract on behalf of the School Board.
