Autism Awareness Month - April 2012

 

Autism is a complex developmental disability that affects an individual's social interaction and communication. It is known as a spectrum disorder, because it affects each individual in different ways and to varying degrees.


Autism Awareness Month, celebrated every year in April, provides an opportunity for families, friends, and local communities to raise public awareness about autism. It is sponsored by the Autism Society of America (ASA). Individuals and communities can get involved by participating in activities sponsored by their local ASA chapter or by promoting services related to autism.

 

 

Mathematics Awareness Month - April 2012

Mathematics Awareness Month is held each year in April to increase public understanding of and appreciation for mathematics. Sponsored by the Joint Policy Board for Mathematics, the goal is to increase the visibility of mathematics as a field of study and to communicate the power and intrigue in mathematics to a larger audience.  The 2012 theme is “Mathematics, Statistics, and the Data Deluge,” which focuses on the massive amounts of data that surround us in modern society.

National Library Week/School Library Month - April 2012

 

National Library Week is a national observance sponsored by the American Library Association and libraries across the country each April.  It is a time to celebrate the contributions of our nation's libraries and librarians and to promote library use and support. All types of libraries - school, public, academic and special - participate.  This year, National Library Week will be observed April 8-14 with the theme, "You Belong @ your library. ”

 

School Library Month is the American Association of School Librarians' celebration of school librarians and their programs.  Every April school librarians are encouraged to create activities to help their school and local community celebrate the essential role that strong school library programs play in a student's educational career.

 

The School Board welcomes this opportunity to commend Fairfax County Public Schools’ librarians and to express its gratitude for the energy, knowledge, and vision that they tirelessly and unselfishly provide to the school system.

Virginia Index of Performance Awards

 

Sixty-six Fairfax County public schools have earned a 2012 Virginia Index of Performance award for advanced learning and achievement from Governor Bob McDonnell and the Virginia Board of Education. The awards recognize schools and divisions that exceed minimum state and federal accountability standards and meet or exceed a number of performance indicators.

 

Twenty-two Fairfax County public schools received the 2012 Governor’s Award for Educational Excellence, a recognition of schools that have met all state and federal achievement benchmarks for at least two consecutive years and achieve excellence goals for elementary reading, enrollment in Algebra I by the eighth grade, high school graduation, enrollment in college level programs and attainment of advanced diplomas, increased attainment of career and industry certifications, and participation in the Virginia Preschool Initiative.

 

Thirty Fairfax County public schools received the 2012 Board of Education Excellence Award, a recognition of schools and divisions that have met all state and federal achievement benchmarks for at least two consecutive years and have made significant progress toward goals for increased student achievement and expanded educational opportunities.

 

Thirteen Fairfax County public schools received the 2012 Board of Education Competence to Excellence Award, a recognition of schools that have met all state and federal benchmarks for at least two consecutive years and are making progress toward designated goals.

 

Belvedere Elementary School is one of two schools in the state that was recognized with a 2012 Board of Education Rising Star Award. This award recognizes schools that meet all state and federal accountability standards for two consecutive years and have improved their VIP scores significantly from the previous year.

Staff Contact:             Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:     March 29, 2012 – Regular Meeting No. 16

 

Subject:                     Proposed Attendance Area Adjustment

                                  Freedom Hill/Lemon Road Boundary Study

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Background:

 

On February 9, 2012, the Fairfax County School Board directed Facilities Planning Services to undertake an elementary school boundary study for Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary Schools.  The purpose of the boundary study is to relieve current and projected overcrowding at Freedom Hill Elementary and to take advantage of existing capacity surplus at Lemon Road Elementary.  The School Board also authorized construction of an 8-room addition at Lemon Road to provide additional capacity by the 2013 school year. Further, the School Board authorized a second broader study of other adjacent schools in the area to be undertaken in 2013 or 2014 to reassign other areas to Lemon Road in order to fully utilize the classroom addition.  The Board indicated that minimizing impact on demographics, specifically English Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) and the Free and Reduced Meals (FRM) programs should be given careful consideration.

 

Key Points:

 

The study area is a triangular portion of the Freedom Hill attendance area that is situated south of Rt. 7, east of I-495, and north of I-66.  This section of the Freedom Hill attendance area is closest to Lemon Road.  This study area contains approximately 325 elementary students attending at Freedom Hill.  Currently, Freedom Hill has a capacity utilization of 116%, which is projected to increase to 136% by 2016.  Lemon Road has a current capacity utilization of 72%, which is projected to decline to 59% over that same time period.  To accommodate continuing enrollment pressure, Freedom Hill relies on eight classroom trailers to accommodate current enrollment growth while Lemon Road relies on student transfers from out of boundary to bolster enrollment.

 

Discussion:

 

Staff proposed two potential options to move a portion of this triangular area to Lemon Road Elementary with implementation in fall 2012 to take advantage of the current 100 student capacity surplus.

 

Option A proposed the reassignment of a portion of Idylwood Apartments, located along the northwest side of Idylwood Road and southwest of Route 7, from Freedom Hill to Lemon Road Elementary.  This option would move approximately 81 students beginning in the fall 2012 school year; that number of students is projected to grow to approximately 120 students by the 2016-17 school year.  Option B proposed the reassignment of Marshall Heights and Dominion townhomes, a portion of Idylwood and Tysons View Apartments, located on the northwest side of Idylwood Road and southwest of Route 7, from Freedom Hill to Lemon Road Elementary.  This option would move approximately 96 students for the fall 2012 school year and approximately 147 students by the 2016-17 school year.  Although Option A would move slightly fewer students for this fall and in the out years, it would have a slightly increased impact on the demographics for both schools.  Option B moves slightly more students with somewhat less impact on demographics for both schools.  

 

Three community engagement meetings were held with approximately 160 persons attending in total.  The third meeting was directed to those ESOL families requiring enhanced translation services.  Background information on the community dialogue meetings, including comments from table groups, individual and online feedback forms may be found at:  http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/freedomlemon/index.shtml

 

Generally, staff found that Option B garnered the most favorable comments in that it moved more students from Freedom Hill and resulted in minimal changes to the demographic percentages for both schools.  Several comments recommended the inclusion of other areas from Freedom Hill in an effort to keep communities together.  In reviewing these suggested changes to the options, staff concluded that they would result in too many students being moved into Lemon Road in the initial year as the numbers would exceed the capacity of that school.  The areas suggested for inclusion will be considered for the 2013/2014 study when the building addition will increase Lemon Road’s capacity.

 

Staff concurs that Option B provides the most reasonable option to provide relief to Freedom Hill and to utilize the current available capacity at Lemon Road with limited impact on the demographics.  This option provides flexibility to include other areas within the triangular study area from Freedom Hill as well as consider other adjacent schools, (Shrevewood, Westgate) for reassignment to Lemon Road in the future after the 8-classroom addition is complete.

 

It should be noted that any Advanced Academic Program (AAP) center students within the area moved to Lemon Road Elementary would be reassigned to the AAP center at Haycock Elementary, consistent with all other Lemon Road AAP students.  This change impacts a small number of students and results in greater transportation efficiency.  However, all rising 6th grade students impacted by this change would be grandfathered with transportation at their current AAP center assignment at Louise Archer Elementary.  Students in the impacted area who are receiving Level IV (school based) AAP service do not have the option to remain at Freedom Hill.  Although Lemon Road Elementary does not currently have a Level IV AAP program, that may be considered in the future. 

 

Families in the impacted area from Freedom Hill who currently receive School Age Child Care (SACC) services will continue to receive these services at Lemon Road. 

 

There will not be any changes to middle and high school assignments including the middle school AAP center assignment.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve adjustments to Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary School attendance areas in order to contribute to the efficiency of the school division, pursuant to Virginia Code Section 22.1-79(4), and Policy 8130.6, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as shown in Appendix A to be effective for the 2012-13 school year.

Subject:  Science Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee Members

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Related To:  Student Achievement Goal 1. Academics

 

Key Points:

 

This committee will review and recommend instructional materials for use in K-12 science courses.  These materials will be considered basal materials. 

 

The Constitution of Virginia gives the Virginia Board of Education the authority to adopt instructional materials for use in the schools.  Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) Regulation 3004.3 Adopted Basal Instructional Materials incorporates state adoption guidelines and requirements and specifies procedures for establishing committees to review textbooks and other basal instructional materials and make recommendations to the Fairfax County School Board for local adoption.  Each FCPS  review committee is composed as follows:

  • Teachers 50%
  • School-based administrators 25%
  • Community Members 25%

 

Central office staff members serve as nonvoting, ex-officio members to guide and assist the work of   the committee.  All recommended materials will be reviewed for content accuracy by a qualified content expert prior to public review.

 

Each School Board member shall appoint one community member to the Science Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee.   School based administrators and teachers will be nominated by Cluster Assistant Superintendents.  Participating staff members will be from the Instructional Services Department, Department of Special Services, and schools. 

 

The School Board will approve members to review and recommend basal instructional materials to be used in Fairfax County Public Schools.

That the School Board appoint committee members to the Science Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee, as detailed in the agenda item.

RESOLUTION ON THE GOAL OF THE FAIRFAX COUNTY SCHOOL BOARD

TO START HIGH SCHOOLS AFTER 8 A.M. AND TO DIRECT THE SUPERINTENDENT TO IDENTIFY AND REPORT ON SCHOOL DIVISIONS WITH LATER STARTING HIGH SCHOOLS

 

     WHEREAS, the National Institutes of Health (NIH), the Centers for Disease Control (CDC), and the National Sleep Foundation (NSF) all conclude that adolescents on average need about nine hours of sleep per night for optimal performance, health, and brain development; and

 

     WHEREAS, most teens experience delayed sleep onset during puberty, and the hormones that help regulate sleep make it difficult for most teens to fall asleep before 11 p.m. or to be alert before 8 a.m.; and

 

     WHEREAS,  Fairfax County Public School (FCPS) buses begin picking students up at 5:45 AM and drop students off at high schools between 6:45 and 7:00 AM, at a time when teen bodies typically demand sleep; and

 

     WHEREAS, the 2010 Fairfax County Youth Survey found that two-thirds of FCPS 8th, 10th, and 12th grade students report sleeping seven hours or less on an average school night, resulting in a large nightly sleep deficit; and the survey further found that nearly one-third of teen students showed signs of depression; and

 

     WHEREAS, sleep plays an important role in learning and memory; and sleep debt affects teens’ ability to think, perform, and react appropriately; and

 

     WHEREAS, there is ample evidence that later high school start times result in adolescent students getting more sleep on school nights; and,

 

     WHEREAS, research shows there are many benefits from later high school start times, including  decreases in teen depression, car crashes, behavior problems, discipline referrals, absenteeism, tardiness, and dropout rates ; and,

 

     WHEREAS, these benefits serve the Board's Student Achievement Goals that students will “achieve their full academic potential in the core disciplines” (Goal 1.1) and “make healthy and safe life choices” (Goal 2.8), as well as Operational Expectation 7 that “The Superintendent shall establish and maintain a learning environment that is safe, healthful, nondiscriminatory, respectful, and conducive to effective learning";

 

     THEREFORE BE IT RESOLVED, that, because of the benefits to student health, well-being, and performance, it is a goal of the Fairfax County School Board to achieve high school start times after 8 a.m.; and

 

     BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board direct the Superintendent to identify and provide a list of practices in school divisions that start high schools after 8 a.m. -- including successful approaches in school districts that have changed from before to after 8 a.m. and in school districts that have traditionally had later morning start times (including, but not limited to: Arlington County and Loudoun County, VA; Minneapolis, MN; Wilton, CT; and Brevard, FL) -- to determine and report to the School Board by June 2012 on how such school districts:

  • Scheduled elementary, middle and high schools
  • Arranged transportation for elementary, middle and high school students to and from school
  • Scheduled sports and after-school activities for high school students around their start and end times
  • Used successful change management techniques to involve and prepare parents, teachers, students and other stakeholders and the school division for a different schedule, in cases where a shift to later start times has occurred.

Mar 29, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 16
Category
6. New Business
Type
Information

Staff Contact:          Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category: March 29, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 16

 

Subject:                   Accelerated Renovation of High, Middle and Elementary School Group Bathrooms

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Related To:           School Board Policy 5011.2 Authority to Contract

 

Key Points:

 

The Department of Facilities and Transportation (FTS) recommends the accelerated renovation of group bathrooms at schools currently in the renovation queue whose renovations are not scheduled to begin within the next five years.

 

There are currently 44 schools in the Renovation Queue which will not begin their renovations within the next five years.  The total estimated to renovate these group bathrooms is approximately $8.35M. The scope of the bathroom facility renovations will include the replacement of all fixtures, partitions, bathroom hardware, and finishes within the space.  In addition, each group bathroom will be brought into alignment with all prevailing codes to include ADA requirements.

 

Funding for these projects will be provided from the construction reserve.  Staff further recommends that the projects be prioritized starting with the three “legacy” high schools, then middle schools, and then elementary schools (to include Centreville High School). The initial projects to be performed will be the three legacy high schools in their current queue order: Herndon, Oakton, and Falls Church; followed by the middle schools: Rocky Run, Hughes, Cooper, Frost, and Twain; lastly, the elementary school and Centreville High School bathrooms will be renovated in the order of their queue position.

 

This work can begin in the near future with the intent of completing the three legacy high and five middle schools (a total of approximately 92 bathrooms) by the end of the 2012-2013 school year. It is anticipated that the entire initiative will be completed throughout an approximate six year period to minimize its impact upon the current renovation schedule.

 

Staff believes that the completion of this work will provide a substantive improvement to these schools for students, staff, and community users while reducing the overall maintenance and utility costs at each building.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the accelerated renovation of high, middle and elementary school group bathrooms; (2) delegate authority to the Division Superintendent or the Chief Operating Officer to allocate funding for the bathroom renovation projects from the construction reserve; and (3) authorize the Division Superintendent or Chief Operating Officer (either of whom may act without the other) to solicit bids, negotiate, execute and administer on behalf of the School Board the design and construction contracts necessary to renovate the group bathroom facilities.
