Walt Whitman Middle School

60th Anniversary

Recognition

 

 

Walt Whitman Middle School proudly celebrates 60 years of educating and nurturing the children of Mount Vernon. It opened on September 5,1961 with an enrollment of 1,066 students at the site on Old Mount Vernon Road that is now Mount Vernon High School.

 

In 1973, it was decided the original location would be better suited for Mount Vernon High School, so Whitman traded places with the high school, moving to the Richmond Highway location that is now known as Original Mount Vernon High School. With the closing of Fort Hunt High School and the merger of Stephen Foster and Bryant Intermediate Schools in 1985, Whitman was moved again to its present location on Sherwood Hall Lane.

 

When it opened, Whitman was one of the first original eleven intermediate schools in Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS). Intermediate schools were created to ease the transition from elementary school to high school by providing students with a specialized program of study geared to the specific needs of their age group. In the 1990’s, intermediate schools were renamed as middle schools to better reflect a focus on nurturing student growth academically, socially, and personally.

 

Whitman Middle School’s mission calls for collective responsibility and collaboration with peers, parents, and community members to create a positive, respectful culture that sets high expectations, achievable goals, and a pathway to success for all students. Whitman Middle School’s dedicated teachers inspire students to discover and pursue opportunities as they become innovative and productive global citizens. The staff and administration of Whitman share the beliefs that all students can learn, and that diversity is a strength in creating a resilient, open-minded community of learners.

 

The school has implemented many innovative programs, practices, and strategies to address the school's changing demographics and students with special needs. Whitman is an International Baccalaureate Middle Years Program and supports a thriving AVID program. In 2018, Whitman became FCPS’ first Community School. The program’s goal is to leverage community resources, combining them with an integrated focus on academics, health and social services, youth and community development, and community engagement to lead to improved student learning, stronger families, and an overall healthier community.

HIGH SCHOOL VOTER REGISTRATION AND EDUCATION

RESOLUTION

 

WHEREAS, voting is the most important civic responsibility of United States citizens and is the cornerstone of our democracy; and

 

WHEREAS, a Virginia law requires every public high school to provide opportunities for eligible students to register to vote during the school day, and National Voter Registration Day is September 28; and

 

WHEREAS, the Ethical and Global Citizen attribute of the FCPS Portrait of a Graduate is an expectation for all students that includes understanding and valuing the rights, privileges, and responsibilities associated with the United States, and schools are committed to developing students who will exercise those rights and responsibilities as citizens; and

 

WHEREAS, civic education contributes to the development of engaged citizens and increases the likelihood of future participation in voting, providing information to students about voter registration, voting procedures (when and where to vote), and how to become informed about election issues prepares students to exercise their voting rights; and

 

WHEREAS, young people who are registered to vote participate in numbers close to the participation rates of older voters, voting is habit forming. When young people exercise their voting rights, they are likely to become lifelong voters, exercising their duties and responsibilities as citizens; and

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board recognizes the efforts of the League of Women Voters of the Fairfax Area, the Fairfax County Office of Elections, Fairfax County Council of PTAs, and the FCPS social studies team and teachers who provide voter registration opportunities and nonpartisan information about voting to eligible students; and

 

THEREFORE, BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board will support and recognize schools that make significant contributions to student voter registration and voter education. This recognition is sponsored by the League of Women Voters of the Fairfax Area and the Fairfax County Council of PTAs.

Subject: Contract Award for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program

 

Staff Contact: Leigh Burden, assistant superintendent, Department of Financial Services

                       Sean McDonald, interim assistant superintendent, Department of Human Resources

 

Other Staff Present: Michelle R. Pratt, director, Office of Procurement Services, Dean Brooks, recruitment administrator, Department of Human Resources

 

School Board Action Required: New Business

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

For over 15 years, the Elementary Education program in the College of Education and Human Development (CEHD) at George Mason University (GMU) has partnered with elementary schools in Fairfax County Public Schools using a Professional Development School (PDS) framework. The partnership engages 23 elementary schools in three pathways to partnership—collaborative inquiry, clinical practices, and partner schools—each of which supports field hours and internship students in the elementary teacher preparation program. This mutually beneficial partnership simultaneously supports teacher candidates’ growth, in-service classroom teachers’ and university-based faculty’s professional development, PK-6 student learning, and ongoing inquiry.

 

The partnership between GMU’s Elementary Education program and FCPS’s PDS sites supports teacher candidates (student interns) and pre-internship/field hours students from CEHD in the following ways 1) a full school year internship (referred to as ‘yearlong’ teacher candidates), 2) a one semester internship (referred to as “semester long” teacher candidates) and/or 3) as a field hours teacher candidates in either fall or spring.

 

Semester long and yearlong teacher candidates work under the direction of trained mentor teachers who are known as mentor teachers/advanced mentor teachers. Yearlong teacher candidates receive a $450 monthly stipend in return for acting as substitute teachers for a limited number of days (minimum 35 days and up to 45 days) during their placement. Substitute teaching will include limited time coverage (less than half day), half day and full day subbing, and providing additional support in classrooms outside of the internship placement.

 

Estimated contract expenditures by FCPS over the five (5) year term is between $139,450 and $217,260 per academic year based upon the number of teacher candidates participating in the PDS Internship Program.

 

Recommendation:

That the School Board approve and award a contract to George Mason University for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program effective July 1, 2021 through June 30, 2026, and authorize the Division Superintendent or Deputy Superintendent to execute and administer such contract on behalf of the School Board.

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

In accordance with past practice, the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors adopted an updated version of the Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution on July 13, 2021 (the “2021 Purchasing Resolution”) to reflect legislative changes made during the preceding session of the Virginia General Assembly. Historically, the Fairfax County School Board also has annually adopted the newly-revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, following its adoption by the Board of Supervisors.

 

Relevant changes to the 2021 Purchasing Resolution are summarized below, together with recommended conditions (or exceptions) to the School Board’s adoption of the 2021 Purchasing Resolution.  Consistent with past practice, the recommended conditions consist of (i) a restatement of the so-called “Construction Exception” to correct an inaccurate description of the Superintendent’s procurement authority; and (ii) a reservation of the School Board’s right to conduct procurements for goods, services, and insurance in accordance with school division policies and regulations and Virginia law whenever such action is in the best interests of the school division.

 

Relevant changes and recommended conditions are as follows:

 

County’s Administrative Changes Relative to FCPS:

 

  1. Added definition of Prevailing Wages (Page 10).
  2. Added non-discrimination language to align with One Fairfax Policy (Page 15).
  3. No change, revised language to provide clarity regarding the requirement to notify the Board of Supervisors or School Board of the award of consulting services contracts in excess of $200,000 (Page 18).
  4. Amended language to increase the dollar threshold at which public notice of Emergency Purchases must be posted (Page 23).
  5. Added language regarding the responsibility criteria of bidders from Article 2, Section 2, Paragraph A.1. to align with the existing list of responsibility criteria that the Purchasing Agent may consider (Page 32).
  6. Revised language regarding limitation of liability to be consistent with Virginia law (Page 42).

 

FCPS Conditions to the Purchasing Resolution:

 

As was done in connection with the School Board’s annual adoption of the 2009 through 2020 Purchasing Resolutions, school system staff recommends that the School Board adopt the 2021 Purchasing Resolution.

Subject: School Board Authorization to Refinance Fairfax County Economic Development Authority Revenue Bonds originally issued for School Facilities

 

 

Staff Contact: Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

 

Meeting Category: September 23, 2021 – Regular Meeting

 

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

Fairfax County's Department of Management and Budget has advised school system staff that the Board of Supervisors plans to sell Economic Development Authority (EDA) Revenue Refunding Bonds in late October 2021 in order to refund a prior bond issue to achieve interest rate savings.  The Board of Supervisors is scheduled to authorize the issuance and sale at its meeting on October 5, 2021.

 

The planned October 2021 bond sale is a result of a monthly review of outstanding debt performed by Fairfax County's Financial Advisor to determine which prior bond issues are candidates for refunding.  Given the low interest rate environment, this is an opportunity to provide debt service savings related to two issuances of bonds for Fairfax County Public Schools.   In 2003, the County sold bonds for the Laurel Hill Facilities Project, which financed the South County High School (then South County Secondary School) and were last refinanced in 2012.  In 2005, the County sold bonds for the School Administration Building and were last refinanced in 2014. 

 

Further, it was determined that County General Fund debt service savings can be achieved by again refinancing the remaining outstanding debt on these bonds. 

 

The attached document has been provided by bond counsel.

 

 

Recommendation

 

The School Board adopt the resolution requesting that the Board of Supervisors issue and sell school refunding bonds on behalf of the School Board and for the benefit of the Fairfax County Public Schools.

Key Points:

 

This document meets the requirements of the Virginia Department of Education, Standard 6 (§22.1-253.13:6) in the Standards of Quality 2011.  Standard 6 requires each local school board to adopt biennially a Divisionwide Comprehensive Plan.

 

The purpose of this plan is to provide a platform for communicating major divisionwide initiatives and operational plans.  Information provided in this plan has been previously presented to the School Board in a variety of reports. The plan consists of School Board student achievement measures along with operational plans for the system.

 

The plan will be presented for public comment on October 7, 2021.  The Board is scheduled to approve the plan at its October 21, 2021 meeting.
