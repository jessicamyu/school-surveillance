RESOLUTION

NAMING THE SCIENE HALLWAY AT STONE MIDDLE SCHOOL

"THE SUE NIDZGORSKI SCIENCE WING"

 

WHEREAS, Sue Nidzgorski passionately and faithfully served Stone Middle School students, staff, parents and community members for 23 years (1993-2016) as a Science teacher, bringing the school and community together through a common love of science and experiments; and

 

WHEREAS, she was committed and dedicated to improving the entire Science department and Stone school community, mentored and coached countless students, and made a lasting impact through her remarkable appreciation for Science, as well as her profound love for teaching and building strong relationships with all; and

 

WHEREAS, Ms. Nidzgorski was a leader who constantly found ways to build, sustain and enhance the Stone community through the exploration of new ideas, incorporation of field trips, guest speakers, and assemblies to bring Science into everyday learning; and

 

WHEREAS, she inspired students and colleagues to be the best version of themselves by making each day count, ensuring her classroom environment was one of excitement and positive energy, in which her example lives on in the countless lives she touched over the years; and

 

WHEREAS, Ms. Nidzgorski’s incredible sense of humor and positive outlook on life was infectious to those who had the benefit of being in her presence and where each person was able to learn and grow from her strong example, as she was an amazing mentor and role model to all.

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, that the Science Hallway at Stone Middle School be named "The Sue Nidzgorski Science Wing" in honor of this incredible educator, leader, colleague and friend.

Resolution Recognizing the 20th Anniversary of September 11

 

 

WHEREAS, Saturday, September 11, 2021, marks the 20-year anniversary of the terrorist attacks on the World Trade Center towers in New York City, the Pentagon in Arlington, Virginia, and United Airlines Flight 93 that went down in Somerset County, Pennsylvania. Since this tragic day the United States has been defined by its resolve, its values, and the resilience with which it has overcome this tragedy; and

 

WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools continues to embrace democratic values and fundamental liberties, instead of fear and oppression; and

 

WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools and the entire Nation honors the memory of those who lost their lives in the 9/11 attacks. A special moment of silence will be observed in every Fairfax County Public School on Friday, September 10; and

 

WHEREAS, in remembrance, September 11th has been nationally declared Patriot Day. A day for the Nation to remember and honor those whose lives were lost and by volunteering in our community in honor of the first responders and those who defend our Nation; and

 

WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools encourages all educators to share the history and lessons learned from this tragic day with their students and to correct misconceptions and stereotypes about all cultures and religions in a sensitive manner; and

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, that the Fairfax County School Board encourages students and families to honor the memory of those who died by observing September 11, 2021, with an act of service, whether that act is volunteering at a local school, mentoring neighborhood students, visiting a nursing or veteran’s home, volunteering at a children’s hospital, donating blood, or some other act of giving.

Hispanic Heritage Month Recognition

 

Each year, Americans observe National Hispanic Heritage Month by celebrating the histories, cultures and contributions of Americans whose ancestors came from Spain, Mexico, the Caribbean and Central and South America. This observation started in 1968 as Hispanic Heritage Week under President Lyndon Johnson. In 1988, President Ronald Reagan expanded the observation period to 30-days starting on September 15 and ending on October 15. It was enacted into law August 17, 1988.

 

National Hispanic Heritage Month is a wonderful opportunity for our schools and community to celebrate various cultures and traditions, and a tangible reinforcement of the School Board’s belief that “Our diversity is a strength that creates resilient, open, and innovative global citizens.” In FCPS, one in four of our students is of Hispanic Heritage, and boast ancestry from a variety of countries and cultures. These cultures in turn enrich our own school community. I encourage you to participate in the programs and events taking place during this time in your schools and community.

 

FCPS values its diversity and acknowledges that all people contribute to the well-being of the community. FCPS is fortunate to have many groups whose members work with our Latinx students empowering them to be the next generation of leaders and professionals. These include, but are not limited to, our parent liaisons and school leaders, the Hispanic Educators Association, Edu-Futuro, and Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee.

The School Board will not hear statements involving issues that have been scheduled for public hearings (such as Capital Improvement Program, budget, and boundaries), or personal attacks on any person. Complaints regarding individual students or school-based employees should not be raised at public meetings. Subject: Challenged Materials Interdepartmental Review Committee


School Board Action Required: Information

 

Related To: Legal Requirement

 

Key Points:

 

The current version of Regulation 3009, Challenged Library and Instructional Materials establishes an Interdepartmental Review Committee to review challenges of library and instructional materials by residents. Six School Board members (on a rotating basis) will each select an individual to serve a one-year term on the Interdepartmental Review Committee. For 2021-2022, the Board members making appointments are Megan McLaughlin (Braddock), Elaine Tholen (Dranesville), Melanie K. Meren (Hunter Mill), Karen Corbett Sanders (Mount Vernon), Karl Frisch (Providence), and Stella Pekarsky (Sully). Last year the School Board district representatives from Lee, Mason, Springfield, and three At-Large representatives were selected to appoint individuals.

 

Two appointees will participate at each of the three levels of challenged materials: elementary, middle, and high school. The level will be determined by a random selection. If the deliberations involve multiple levels, a randomly selected School Board appointee from each involved level will participate.

 

The student representative to the School Board appoints an individual to serve on the Interdepartmental Review Committee for challenges that involve high school materials.

 

Other committee members are appointed to serve one-year terms as set forth in the current version of Regulation 3009.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board appoint individuals to serve a one-year term on the Challenged Materials Interdepartmental Review Committee, as detailed in the agenda item.

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

In accordance with past practice, the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors adopted an updated version of the Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution on July 13, 2021 (the “2021 Purchasing Resolution”) to reflect legislative changes made during the preceding session of the Virginia General Assembly. Historically, the Fairfax County School Board also has annually adopted the newly-revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, following its adoption by the Board of Supervisors.

 

Relevant changes to the 2021 Purchasing Resolution are summarized below, together with recommended conditions (or exceptions) to the School Board’s adoption of the 2021 Purchasing Resolution.  Consistent with past practice, the recommended conditions consist of (i) a restatement of the so-called “Construction Exception” to correct an inaccurate description of the Superintendent’s procurement authority; and (ii) a reservation of the School Board’s right to conduct procurements for goods, services, and insurance in accordance with school division policies and regulations and Virginia law whenever such action is in the best interests of the school division.

 

Relevant changes and recommended conditions are as follows:

 

County’s Administrative Changes Relative to FCPS:

 

  1. Added definition of Prevailing Wages (Page 10).
  2. Added non-discrimination language to align with One Fairfax Policy (Page 15).
  3. No change, revised language to provide clarity regarding the requirement to notify the Board of Supervisors or School Board of the award of consulting services contracts in excess of $200,000 (Page 18).
  4. Amended language to increase the dollar threshold at which public notice of Emergency Purchases must be posted (Page 23).
  5. Added language regarding the responsibility criteria of bidders from Article 2, Section 2, Paragraph A.1. to align with the existing list of responsibility criteria that the Purchasing Agent may consider (Page 32).
  6. Revised language regarding limitation of liability to be consistent with Virginia law (Page 42).

 

FCPS Conditions to the Purchasing Resolution:

 

As was done in connection with the School Board’s annual adoption of the 2009 through 2020 Purchasing Resolutions, school system staff recommends that the School Board adopt the 2021 Purchasing Resolution.

Recommendation:

That the School Board adopt the newly-revised 2021 Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, previously adopted by the Board of Supervisors, subject to the conditions detailed in the agenda item.

Subject: Contract Award for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program

 

Staff Contact: Leigh Burden, assistant superintendent, Department of Financial Services

                       Sean McDonald, interim assistant superintendent, Department of Human Resources

 

Other Staff Present: Michelle R. Pratt, director, Office of Procurement Services, Dean Brooks, recruitment administrator, Department of Human Resources

 

School Board Action Required: New Business

 

Ignite Link: Goal 4 – Resource Stewardship

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

For over 15 years, the Elementary Education program in the College of Education and Human Development (CEHD) at George Mason University (GMU) has partnered with elementary schools in Fairfax County Public Schools using a Professional Development School (PDS) framework. The partnership engages 23 elementary schools in three pathways to partnership—collaborative inquiry, clinical practices, and partner schools—each of which supports field hours and internship students in the elementary teacher preparation program. This mutually beneficial partnership simultaneously supports teacher candidates’ growth, in-service classroom teachers’ and university-based faculty’s professional development, PK-6 student learning, and ongoing inquiry.

 

The partnership between GMU’s Elementary Education program and FCPS’s PDS sites supports teacher candidates (student interns) and pre-internship/field hours students from CEHD in the following ways 1) a full school year internship (referred to as ‘yearlong’ teacher candidates), 2) a one semester internship (referred to as “semester long” teacher candidates) and/or 3) as a field hours teacher candidates in either fall or spring.

 

Semester long and yearlong teacher candidates work under the direction of trained mentor teachers who are known as mentor teachers/advanced mentor teachers. Yearlong teacher candidates receive a $450 monthly stipend in return for acting as substitute teachers for a limited number of days (minimum 35 days and up to 45 days) during their placement. Substitute teaching will include limited time coverage (less than half day), half day and full day subbing, and providing additional support in classrooms outside of the internship placement.

 

Estimated contract expenditures by FCPS over the five (5) year term is between $139,450 and $217,260 per academic year based upon the number of teacher candidates participating in the PDS Internship Program.

 

Recommendation:

That the School Board approve and award a contract to George Mason University for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program effective July 1, 2021 through June 30, 2026, and authorize the Division Superintendent or Deputy Superintendent to execute and administer such contract on behalf of the School Board.
