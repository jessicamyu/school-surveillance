Purchasing Month - March 2008
          Public procurement and fixed asset management professionals support and enhance the delivery of government services by promoting ethical purchasing practices. Public purchasing involves managing goods, establishing sources and shipping through inventory and warehousing, disposing of surplus items, and determining which product or service to buy to meet a specific need at the lowest possible price within the required deadline without sacrificing quality or service; and

          Public purchasing and materials management professionals in Fairfax County Public Schools, through professional development and certification from accredited programs, are dedicated to pursuing efficiency in school system operations and to providing the highest value for taxpayers’ dollars.
Read Across America Day - March 3, 2008

National Education Association's (NEA) Read Across America is an annual reading motivation and awareness program that calls for every child in every community to celebrate reading on or around Dr. Seuss's birthday.

In cities and towns across the nation, teachers, teenagers, librarians, politicians, actors, athletes, parents, grandparents, and others develop NEA's Read Across America activities to bring reading excitement to children of all ages. Governors, mayors, and other elected officials recognize the role reading plays in their communities with proclamations and floor statements. Athletes and actors issue reading challenges to young readers. And teachers and principals seem to be more than happy to dye their hair green or be duct-taped to a wall if it boosts their students' reading.

Music in Our Schools Month - March 2008

          March has been officially designed by the National Association for Music Education for the observance of Music In Our Schools Month, the time of year when music education becomes the focus of schools across the nation.

          The purpose of Music In Our Schools Month is to raise awareness of the importance of music education for all children - and to remind citizens that school is where all children should have access to music. It also is an opportunity for music teachers to bring their music programs to the attention of the school and the community and to display the benefits school music brings to students of all ages.

Youth Art Month - March 2008
          Art education contributes powerful educational benefits to all elementary and secondary students. Art education develops students' creative problem-solving and critical thinking abilities; teaches sensitivity to beauty, order, and other expressive qualities; gives students a deeper understanding of multicultural values and beliefs; and brings to life what students learn in other subjects.

          The Fairfax County School Board believes that support should be given to art teachers as they attempt to strengthen art education in schools and communities, and all citizens are urged to take an interest in and give full support to quality school art programs for children and youth.

FAIRFAX COUNTY SCHOOL BOARD RESOLUTION

Whereas, the Standards of Quality set the minimum requirements for Virginia to meet its constitutionally defined obligation to maintain “an educational program of high quality” for the children of Virginia; and

Whereas, the Constitution of Virginia, the Commentaries on the Constitution, and various opinions of the state’s attorney general have held that the state has an obligation to fund education at levels that retain a realistic relationship between state funding and the actual local costs of providing that education; and

Whereas, the state’s current educational funding formulas are “rebenchmarked” every two years to recognize that the costs to school divisions of offering programs that meet the Standards of Quality change over time due to market forces specific to each locality; and

Whereas, the state’s current funding methodologies already use various statistical mechanisms to significantly understate the true cost of education to localities, which in turn reduces the state’s funding obligations for education; and

Whereas, the Virginia Board of Education has regularly recommended changes to the Standards of Quality that would help bring state standards and funding into closer alignment with actual school division best practices, many of which remain unfunded by the state; and

Whereas, Virginia’s Joint Legislative Audit and Review Commission has identified a number of areas in which the state has consistently underfunded the Standards of Quality, most recently in its 2002 Review of Elementary and Secondary School Funding, many of which remain unfunded by the state, and

Whereas, to make up for these deficits in state spending, virtually every locality in Virginia spends well above and beyond the minimums set by the Standards of Quality to provide a high quality education entirely at local taxpayer expense; and

Whereas, Virginia school divisions are held highly accountable by both the state and federal governments for the academic achievement of every child and are subject to sanction if they fail to meet those academic achievement goals; and

Whereas, a critical component of meeting these achievement goals is the ability to recruit and retain highly qualified school personnel, an ability that is directly affected by prevailing costs and by available resources;

Now, therefore, be it resolved that the Fairfax County School Board strongly opposes changes to Virginia’s education funding methodologies that further distance the relationship between what it actually costs a locality to provide an education and how the state funds education;

And, be it further resolved that the Fairfax County School Board strongly opposes changes to Virginia’s education funding methodologies that shift more of the state’s cost burden of education to local governments and to local taxpayers.

SUBJECT: Proposed 2008-2009 Program/Attendance Area Adjustment

TYPE: Action

BACKGROUND:

          In July 2007, the School Board authorized and directed staff to undertake two boundary studies: 1) Springfield Estates/Forestdale Elementary Schools and, 2) the West County High School study which includes Chantilly, Herndon, Oakton, South Lakes, and Westfield High Schools. Also included in the study is the Madison High School attendance area island north of the Dulles Access Road and its feeder schools which impact Hughes and Thoreau Middle Schools and Sunrise Valley and Wolftrap Elementary Schools.

KEY POINTS:

          The Springfield Estates/Forestdale Elementary School Study implements the return of an attendance island (Springfield Crossing Apartments) that had been administratively assigned to Springfield Estates, to its base school Forestdale Elementary.

          The West County Boundary Study addresses programmatic issues related to low and high enrollment schools by adjusting enrollments between South Lakes High School, which is under-enrolled, and Chantilly and Westfield High Schools which have high enrollment.
RECOMMENDATION:

          1. That the School Board approve a boundary adjustment for Springfield Estates Elementary School and Forestdale Elementary School as shown in Appendix A.

          2. That the School Board approve a boundary adjustment for South Lakes, Oakton, Chantilly, Madison, and Westfield High Schools as shown in Appendix B.

          3. That the School Board approve a boundary adjustment for Thoreau and Hughes Middle Schools and Wolftrap and Sunrise Valley Elementary Schools as shown in Appendix B.

BOUNDARY ADJUSTMENT RECOMMENDATIONS

In October 2002, the School Board adopted revised Policy 8130.5 governing the adjustment of special program center locations and school/program attendance areas. The policy requires that, under most circumstances, conditions necessitating program location or attendance area adjustments be evaluated jointly with the affected communities. Solutions emerging from the “Community Boundary Town Meeting” process are to be recommended to the School Board for public hearing and action. The recommendations contained in this agenda item reflect input from a series of community town meetings conducted to review adjustments to attendance areas of existing schools. Meetings were announced via press release, mail outs, “backpack” and “Keep in Touch” notices to parents, and web-site postings. Citizen comment from each meeting was captured in summaries posted to the FCPS web page after each meeting. Scenario details (maps and impact tables) were similarly posted.

The following section summarizes the reviews undertaken with affected communities and resulting boundary change recommendations. Detailed outlines of these recommendations describing the geographic areas and numbers of projected students are presented in Appendices A and B.

GENERAL EDUCATION SCHOOL ATTENDANCE AREA REALIGNMENTS

Appendix A Boundary adjustments between Springfield Estates and Forestdale Elementary Schools
Realign attendance areas to relieve overcrowding at Springfield Estates Elementary School. The study area included the Springfield Estates and Forestdale Elementary School attendance areas.

Appendix B Boundary adjustments between Chantilly, Madison, Westfield, Oakton and South Lakes High Schools

Realign attendance areas to relieve overcrowding and program issues at Chantilly, Madison, Oakton, Westfield and South Lakes High Schools. The study area included Chantilly, Herndon, Oakton, Westfield and South Lakes High Schools and a portion of the Madison High School attendance area.

Appendix B Boundary Adjustments between Hughes/Thoreau Middle Schools and Sunrise Valley/Wolftrap Elementary Schools
Realign attendance areas to relieve overcrowding at Thoreau, reduce time and distance traveled and correspond with recommended adjustments at the high school level.
