African American History Month - February 2016

 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." The commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

 

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.  This year’s theme—A Century of Black Life, History, and Culture—focuses on the efforts made by African Americans to institute change and to share their knowledge and talents while ushering in a new era of equality, opportunity, and racial democracy.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2016

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (programming , business management, information technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (sales, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

RESOLUTION

ON

HUMAN TRAFFICKING AWARENESS MONTH

 

 

          WHEREAS, human trafficking, a heinous crime, is thought by many Americans to be confined to other parts of the globe but is instead the fastest-growing criminal enterprise in the world and is occurring so regularly here in Northern Virginia that our area is now one of the top hubs of teen trafficking in the United States; and

 

          WHEREAS, human traffickers prey on middle and high school teens but have also been known to hone their skills in targeting and exploiting psychologically and emotionally vulnerable men, women, and children as young as 9 years old at shopping malls, metro stations, bus stops, and via Facebook and other forms of social media; and

 

          WHEREAS, trafficking victims are from all ethnic and socio-economic backgrounds and often unaware that they are being carefully manipulated and coerced by their new found “friends” until they realize all too late that they have become trapped and are either threatened with or have become actual victims of violence; and

 

          WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools and the Just Ask Prevention Foundation have instituted a teen sex trafficking prevention curriculum in grades six through ten and twelve and developed effective awareness campaigns that have become models for other jurisdictions; and

 

          WHEREAS, student groups at Fairfax County high schools, educators, parents, medical professionals, law enforcement officials, civic organizations, and the business community understand the importance of bringing awareness about teen sex trafficking to young people and wish to mobilize students and others in the community in order to create an environment where this crime is not tolerated and its victims are not overlooked;

 

          NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board joins the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors in recognizing the month of January 2016 as Human Trafficking Awareness Month in Fairfax County and supports the efforts of all those who are seeking to eliminate human trafficking and its impact on our community.

[Updated attachments added 1/20/16]

 

Staff Contact:              Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services                                   

 

Meeting Category:       January 21, 2016 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                       FY 2017-2021 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2017-2021 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five-year student enrollment projections and capacity calculations, as well as the Capital Construction Cash Flow which identifies plans for new construction, capacity enhancements, renovations, and site acquisition to accommodate future capacity needs.

 

Discussion:

 

Fairfax County Public Schools continues to face a challenging situation whereby student enrollment in certain locations is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities, provide capacity enhancement projects, and it will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions, and boundary adjustments to accommodate growing enrollment needs.

 

New School Construction: The CIP proposes four new elementary schools: one on the army base to relieve overcrowding at Fort Belvoir Elementary School which is jointly funded with the Department of Defense; one in the Richmond Highway Corridor (Route 1 area) to relieve overcrowding in the Groveton/Hybla Valley area; one in the North West County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and the Dulles Airport access road; and one to relieve overcrowding in the Fairfax/Oakton area.  It proposes funds for a gymnasium addition at Bailey’s Upper ES. It also proposes construction of a new high school in the Western end of the Silver Line metro vicinity to alleviate capacity concerns at multiple high schools.   

 

Capacity Enhancements: Funding for additions at Westbriar Elementary School and South Lakes High School are identified, as well as for potential additions at two high schools (one in the eastern part of the county) and a middle school. Funds are also proposed for the relocation of four modular additions.

 

Renovations: Funding for renovations at 6 named high schools and one unnamed high school are proposed in the CIP.  Additionally, funds for renovations at 16 named elementary schools and 12 unnamed elementary schools are included. Lastly, funds for the renovation of five named middle schools and one unnamed middle school are proposed. Many of these renovation projects will also include capacity enhancements.

 

Site Acquisition: Funds have been proposed for a site acquisition in the future.

 

Like last year, the FY 2017–21 CIP also recommends possible solutions for future consideration to resolve capacity deficits. These options include interior modifications, temporary classrooms, program location suggestions, and boundary adjustments. Any boundary adjustment or program change would be discussed and decided through a transparent process that engagements the community, in accordance with School Board Policy and Regulations.

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2017-2021 Capital Improvement Program.

In July 2015, the School Board approved Ignite, the strategic plan for Fairfax County Public Schools, which represents the cooperative work of the School Board and Leadership Team, with significant community input, to create a long term plan for continuous improvement. At the core of this plan is the FCPS Portrait of a Graduate. The Ignite Plan contains four strategic goals: Student Success, Caring Culture, Premier Workforce, and Resource Stewardship. All of the goals contain overarching strategies, desired outcomes, actions, and monitoring metrics. This school year, 2015-2016, marks the first year of implementation of the Strategic Plan. The purpose of this report is to provide an update on the current work that supports Goal 1, Student Success.

 

The following are the last two of four overarching strategies and the desired outcomes for Goal 1 Student Success.  The first two strategies were presented on December 17, 2015

 

Overarching Strategy 3:

Provide quality Early Childhood experiences aligned with Portrait of a Graduate outcomes that are designed to prepare students to successfully enter Kindergarten

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • All qualifying future FCPS students will have access to high quality early education programs
  • All agencies will coordinate their efforts to provide similar early childhood experiences
     

Overarching Strategy 4:

Provide students with relevant opportunities to explore options and prepare for College and Career

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • A portfolio of school options will provide multiple pathways to a high school diploma

Students will be fluent in two or more languages

                                                                                         FAIRFAX COUNTY SCHOOL BOARD RESOLUTION ON

                                                                              OPPOSING A CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENT TO DIVEST LOCAL

                                                                    SCHOOL BOARDS OF THE SOLE AUTHORITY TO APPROVE CHARTER SCHOOLS

 

 

      WHEREAS, in its 2016 session, the General Assembly will be considering a resolution seeking a constitutional amendment to divest local school boards of the sole authority to approve charter schools, vesting authority with a gubernatorial appointed board; and

 

      WHEREAS, local school boards, which are elected or appointed by their community and entrusted with the authority to supervise schools, are best equipped to assess the needs of their students and the locality; and

 

      WHEREAS, local school boards are responsible for the outcomes of all schools within the school system, including charter schools; and

 

      WHEREAS, research has shown that charter school outcomes are mixed, with the majority doing no better than the traditional public schools that feed them; and

 

      WHEREAS, state-authorized charter schools would result in a massive unfunded mandate to local school divisions, costing millions of dollars to local taxpayers;

 

      NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, the Fairfax County School Board hereby opposes the resolution seeking a constitutional amendment to divest local school boards from the sole authority to approve charter schools.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
