Children's Book Week - May 4 -10, 2015

 

Children’s Book Week, a national celebration of reading, storytelling, and book-related events, observes its 96th anniversary in 2015 and is the longest-running national literacy initiative in the country.  Established to promote higher standards in children’s books, the week grew to involve librarians, publishers, and booksellers and is administered by Every Child a Reader, a 501(c)(3) literacy organization dedicated to instilling a lifelong love of reading in children. Nationwide, author and illustrator events take place each year and the Children’s Choice Book Awards, the only national child-chosen book awards program, are announced during the week.

Recognition of National Adult and Continuing Education Week - May 18-22, 2015

The Adult and Community Education (ACE) program of Fairfax County Public Schools began with an action by the School Board in 1955 and, in the 60 years of the program’s existence, thousands of adults have learned essential skills and completed their high school educations.

Through the years, adults have learned new languages, acquired job skills, transitioned to new careers, and grown as workers, citizens, and parents. The Fairfax County community has looked to ACE to help develop our workforce, provide solutions to community problems through education, and create a culture of lifelong learning for residents of all ages.

Adult and Community Education has made major contributions to the school system as a laboratory of innovation for the piloting of new programs and services in support of PreK-12 education through family literacy, summer programs, extended day programs, enrichment courses, and driver education.

Through creative programming and targeted advocacy for the adult learner, as well as providing enrichment opportunities for youth beyond the school day, ACE has helped to create a dynamic learning community for all generations in Fairfax County.

RESOLUTION HONORING THE 50TH ANNIVERSARY OF

 ROBERT FROST MIDDLE SCHOOL

 

 

     WHEREAS, in 2015, Robert Frost Middle School will proudly celebrate the 50th anniversary of its founding and its reputation for providing students with an excellent academic foundation so that they may embark on their futures fully prepared with the critical thinking skills, global and environmental awareness, ethics, sense of citizenship, and compassion to solve problems and make difficult decisions in an ever-changing world; and

 

     WHEREAS, over the years, Frost Middle School has been a model of academic excellence and has attained many academic accomplishments, including the Virginia Governor’s Award for Excellence in 2010, 2011, 2012, and 2014, as well as the Virginia Board of Education Distinguished Achievement Award in 2013, the Golden Wellness Award in 2013, the Healthier U.S. School Challenge Bronze Award, the Virginia Music Educators’ Association’s Blue Ribbon from 2009 through 2013, and the Recognized ASCA Model Program designation in 2015 from the American School Counselor Association; and

 

     WHEREAS, Frost Middle School works collaboratively with the Parent-Teacher Association and a committed local community to provide a safe learning environment, strong communication to its families, and opportunities for service learning and extra-curricular activities to enhance students’ educational experience; and 

 

     WHEREAS, Frost Middle School stresses the acquisition of skills that will lead to success and offers a multitude of programs serving a wide range of students’ abilities and needs, an advanced academic center, honors courses, and a wide variety of world language courses; and

 

     WHEREAS, thousands of students have passed through Frost Middle School’s doors and benefitted from a comprehensive education and a smooth transition to high school as a result of the efforts of dedicated teachers and staff, in a nurturing and positive environment, with the support of school leaders and involved parents;

 

     NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board honors Frost Middle School on the occasion of its 50th Anniversary and commends its history of providing educational excellence and enrichment opportunities to the students and community.

Regulation 2601 Student Rights and Responsibilities, has been reviewed and revised.

 

A. In V. Summer School Attendance, changed sentence from “A school principal may impose a short-term suspension and recommend a long-term suspension and/or expulsion in any situation involving prohibited conduct” to read “A school principal may impose a short-term suspension and/or make a referral to the Division Superintendent in any situation involving prohibited conduct.” In paragraph four, changed “A student who is expelled for a violation that occurred during a summer school session may also be expelled from all FCPS programs during the regular school term” to read “A student who is expelled, reassigned, or suspended for a violation that occurred during a summer school session may also be expelled, reassigned, or suspended during the regular school term.”

G. In chapter I, footnote 4, removed “registration” and added “the use appropriate use.”

 

H. In chapter II.2.b.(2), removed “or harassment based on a person's race, color, religion, national origin, sex, disability, personal or physical attributes, or matters pertaining to sexuality, including sexual orientation, gender identity, or gender expression.”

 

I. In chapter II.A.2.b.(3), added “Discriminatory harassment (which is harassment based on a person's race, color, religion, national origin, gender, disability, personal or physical attributes, or matters pertaining to sexuality, including sexual orientation, gender identity, or gender expression).”

 

J. In chapter II.A.2.b.(4), added “Sexual harassment (which includes unwelcome sexual advances, regardless of sexual orientation; requests for sexual favors; and other inappropriate verbal, electronic, or physical conduct of a sexual nature that creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive environment).”

 

K. ”In chapter II.A.2.b.(2). (3), and (4) footnote 5 change to footnote 5, 6, and 7, and changed from “The principal is responsible for investigating and documenting all reported incidents of bullying and harassment. In addition, the principal shall notify the Office of Equity and Employees Relations of any reports of such, where the misconduct is based upon one or more of the personal characteristics listed above” to read “The principal is responsible for investigating and documenting all reported incidents of bullying, discriminating harassment, and sexual harassment. All allegations and supporting information should be recorded in the Bullying and Harassment Management System (BHMS). See Regulation 4952 for procedural requirements.”

 

L. In chapter II.A.5.b.(1)(a), changed “of malicious intent” to read “that the student intended to use such weapon to threaten, intimidate, or harm another.”

 

M. In chapter II.A.5.c., added “to threaten, intimidate, or harm another.”

 

N. In chapter II.C.3.c.(3), removed “hand delivered” and added “by U.S. mail, and, if possible, by e-mail or facsimile.”.

Adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and to take all necessary and appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2015 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $310,000,000, as detailed in the agenda item (FTS; presented as new business 4/23/15)

Staff Contact:          Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:   May 7, 2015 - Regular Meeting No. 19

 

Subject:                    Proposed 2015 School Bond Referendum

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

On January 22, 2015, the School Board adopted the FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) that identified school facility needs anticipated during the referenced five-year period.  Capital improvement programming provides the foundation for cost-effective investment and construction project management of facility needs identified throughout the planning period.  Voter authorization in November 2015 will be necessary in order to issue school bonds to provide:

 

Construction funds for:

  • 6 elementary school renovations
  • 2 high school renovations
  • 1 high school addition
  • 3 modular building relocations

Planning funds for:

  • 1 new elementary school
  • 3 elementary school renovations
  • 2 middle school renovations

Key Points:

 

The adopted FY 2016-2020 CIP identified what are projected to be substantial enrollment increases over the 5-year planning period and beyond.  Specifically, these projected enrollment increases will require FCPS to accommodate more than 12,000 additional students by school year 2019 -2020, with additional growth expected through the 2024-25 school year when enrollment is projected to exceed 199,000 students. 

 

The 2015 bonds provide funds for construction, and planning for new facilities to help address the expected capacity deficits; in addition, renovation projects continue to include additional capacity in order to address the student membership growth in many schools.

 

Facilities staff members have been working with the County staff to reconcile unused authority from multiple bond referendums over an extended period of time – an exercise which is performed periodically for all local governmental entities which utilize bond funds.  This reconciliation does not alter in anyway the current annual cash flow contribution from the County of $155 million; nor will it impact the timing of any projects currently listed in the CIP.  It reflects cumulative project savings over time allowing FCPS to request less bonding authority for the projects currently identified for funding. Out of the $352.5 million CIP listed in the bond referendum, the County currently already has bonding authority for $42.5 million.  Consequently, FCPS only needs to seek bonding authority for $310 million in new bond sales.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and to take all necessary and appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2015 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $310,000,000, as detailed in the agenda item.

Key Points: 

  

On February 5, 2015, the School Board adopted the FY 2016 Advertised Budget. The advertised budget was presented to and discussed by the School Board and County Board of Supervisors at a joint budget work session on February 27, 2015.

 

On April 28, 2015, the County Board of Supervisors adopted their FY 2016 budget that included an increase in the transfer to the School Operating Fund of 3.2 percent above the FY 2015 Approved Budget. The increase of $56.7 million is $14.0 million less than requested in FCPS’ advertised budget. The Virginia General Assembly adopted its 2014-2016 budget, as amended on April 15, 2015, which provides an increase of $3.9 million in revenue and a decrease in expenditures of $6.0 million as compared to the estimate included in FCPS’ FY 2016 Advertised Budget.

 

Revenue:

In total, revenue in the School Operating Fund reflects a net reduction of $10.1 million as compared to the FY 2016 Advertised Budget.  Funding from the county is $14.0 million less than requested, and this reduction is offset by a projected increase in state revenue of $3.9 million.

 

Expenditures:

In total, expenditures in the School Operating Fund reflect a net reduction of $10.1 million as compared to the FY 2016 Advertised Budget. This includes $6.0 million in savings due to a decrease in the employer contribution rate for the Virginia Retirement System (VRS) from 14.50 percent to the rate of 14.06 percent for FY 2016 and a reduction placeholder of $7.6 million. These reductions are offset by an increase of $3.5 million for the cost of enrollment growth and changes in student demographics and $0.1 million for School Board Member salary increases. In order to balance the budget, the School Board will need to identify reductions of $7.6 million prior to the budget being adopted on May 21, 2015. To meet the required level of expenditure reductions, the Superintendent has recommended a reduction totaling $7.6 million, which is detailed on page three of the agenda item.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
