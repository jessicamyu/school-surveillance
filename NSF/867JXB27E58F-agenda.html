    Native American Heritage Month - November 2006

    The strength of our Nation comes from its people. As the early inhabitants of this great land, the native peoples of North America played a unique role in the shaping of our Nation's history and culture. During this month when we celebrate Thanksgiving, we especially celebrate their heritage and the contributions of American Indian and Alaska Native peoples to this Nation.

    Since our Nation's birth, pluralism and diversity have been hallmarks of the American experience and success. In 1782, the Founding Fathers chose as our national motto "E Pluribus Unum," which means "out of many, one." Today, America's unity, derived from a mix of many diverse cultures and people, grandly embodies the vision expressed by our Founders. American Indian and Alaska Native cultures have made remarkable contributions to our national identity. Their unique spiritual, artistic, and literary contributions, together with their vibrant customs and celebrations, enliven and enrich our land.

    National School Psychologist Month - November 2006

    All children and youth can learn with the proper supports, and have the inalienable right to an education that meets their needs Children’s mental health is directly linked to their learning and development and sound psychological principles are integral to instruction and learning; social, and emotional development; prevention and early intervention.

    Schools provide an optimal context to provide mental health services because of the nature of the learning environment, and the fact that most children are accessible and familiar there and school-based mental health services are a critical part of a continuum of mental health services for children and youth; and

    School psychologists are specially trained to deliver a continuum of mental health services and academic supports within the school setting and school psychologists facilitate collaboration and help parents and educators identify and reduce risk factors. They create effective, caring schools, access needed community resources, and implement research-driven prevention and intervention strategies.

    Lewis Rauch began his employment with Fairfax County Public Schools in 1995 as a project manager in the Office of Design and Construction Services and became the director of the Office of Design and Construction Services in 2002. During the past 11 years, Lewis was involved in the renovation of 18 schools, oversaw the construction and opening of 9 schools, and in the successful first Public-Private partnership that resulted in the early construction of the South County Secondary School. 

    Lewis also was instrumental in the successful build-out and occupancy of the central administrative building in 2005 and 2006 that will benefit the school system and its customers for many years to come. Under Lewis' leadership the school system was able to build a completely new elementary school in less than 12 months after Dogwood Elementary School burned to the ground in November, 2000, and to conduct repairs quickly and efficiently to Floris Elementary School after its roof collapsed thus allowing students to return to the school in only two weeks. 

    The Fairfax County School Board expresses its sincere appreciation for the great work done by Lewis Rauch during his time with Fairfax County Public Schools and wishes him well in his new position in Loudoun County as their capital construction program manager.

    3.01 Spotlight on Learning - Literary Circles at Franklin Middle School
    Meeting
    Oct 19, 2006 - Regular Meeting No. 6
    Category
    3. Presentations to School Board
    Type
    Information



    Adult Presenters:Terri Rubin, Middle School Language Arts Specialist
    School:Franklin Middle School
    Topic:Literature Circles: Book Clubs for Middle School
    Administrators:Sharon Eisenberg, Principal, Franklin MS
    Maureen McLoughlin, Asst. Principal, Franklin MS
    Jodi Brown, teacher, Franklin MS
    Description of Presentation:Academic Goal 1 states that all students will obtain, understand, analyze, communicate, and apply knowledge and skills to achieve success in school and in life. Tonight’s Spotlight on Learning showcases one example of how our middle school English classrooms are helping students meet that goal. Literature circles are an integral part of reading instruction in our middle schools. In these small groups, similar to adult book clubs, students work together to analyze and understand a text. Together students explore and communicate their individual understanding of, and questions about, the text. As a result, each student’s comprehension of that text is deepened and extended. This is an opportunity for students to apply the reading comprehension skills they have been taught.

    Elementary students also enjoy their own versions of literature circles with shorter, easier text, and high school students also participate in small group book discussions.
    As you will see, literature circles encourage both student engagement and active reading. Literature circles are one step for our middle school students along the path to becoming lifelong readers.

            That the School Board approve the award of contract for providing center-based model HMO benefits for Fairfax County Public Schools employees to Kaiser Permanente, effective January 1, 2007, as recommended by the Office of Benefit Services.
