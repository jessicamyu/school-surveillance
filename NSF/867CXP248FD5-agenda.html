Staff Contact: Pat Murphy, assistant superintendent, Accountability

SUBJECT: Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee Annual Report

TYPE: Discussion

RELATED TO: Student Achievement Goals - Linkage and Outreach In the Community

BACKGROUND:
      The MSAOC responds to the School Board’s charge of for “determining how to best establish a partnership between minority families and schools so that parents become informed advocates for their children.”

KEY POINTS:
      The Committee has carried out the charge by holding three meetings with parents at different locations to garner their ideas and comments. As a result of these meetings the Committee has prepared a series of tip sheets or suggestions for parents on how they can be more effective advocates for their children and partner with the schools.

RECOMMENDATION:
    1. To increase the effectiveness of efforts already underway—such as promoting the Advocacy Handbook for Parents or maximizing the impact of the new “Building the Future” initiative—MSAOC has identified a number of specific actions that the School Board can take to advance our goals:
    2. Ensure that FCPS student goals are widely discussed and disseminated in the community in clear and easy format and can be accessed from the FCPS website in multiple languages.

    3. Support the recruitment and training of Parent Liaisons who can communicate with the minority parent community and make the position full-time.
    4. Promote the Advocacy Handbook for Parents (and translate the Handbook into additional languages, including Chinese and Arabic).
    5. Encourage schools to offer meetings at a variety of times for parents (including weekends), and use as many communications means as possible to reach parents (e.g., “Keep in Touch,” e-mail, FCPS 24/7, biweekly e-mail reports, automated phone calls, phone trees, Parent Liaison networks).
    6. Support the development of free educational seminars and workshops for parents in multiple languages to help them become advocates for their children.
    7. Support the expansion of diversity training for school counselors, teachers, administrators, and others in the school system.
    8. Continue to form partnerships with community centers, faith-based organizations, language-specific groups, and others that already interact with hard-to-reach families. Participate in parent meetings or events such as those sponsored by MSAOC, CPMSAC and Nadir Por Vida.

    9. Use visual, easy-to-understand formats for printed communication with parents in multiple languages.
    10. Support an open climate at schools so that parents feel more welcome and empowered to take ownership of their children’s education. Help school administrators and teachers explain to parents why the School Board is important to their children.

SUBJECT: Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) Annual Report 2008

TYPE: Discussion

RELATED TO: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)

BACKGROUND:

Regulations Governing Special Education Programs for Children with Disabilities in Virginia require that a local advisory committee for special education, appointed by each local school board, advise the school board through the division superintendent of unmet needs in the education of children with disabilities. The regulations state that the composition of the committee shall include parents of children with disabilities and persons with disabilities.

KEY POINTS:

The ACSD made the following recommendations:
      • Establish a permanent FCPS Reading Committee charged to meet every three years to review the efficacy of reading programs in use and examine any new trends in current reading research in order to make recommendations and revisions to FCPS reading programming. This committee would be comprised of FCPS reading specialists, special and general education teachers, private sector reading experts, parents and a representative from the School Board. A report of the committee findings should be made available to the School Board.
      • Assess and choose reading programs based on their usability across the diverse student populations in FCPS and on standard criteria approved by the Superintendent and the School Board.
      • Evaluate all children in special education for an accurate yearly reading level with a standardized norm-referenced measure, as opposed to the widely used DRA2 which is an informal measure, (that is standardized to give grade level equivalents). The results should be collected county wide and reported to the school board annually.
      • Allocate more uninterrupted time on basic reading instruction at the elementary level. If a student at the secondary level is in need of explicit, targeted, and intensive reading instruction, the corresponding courses should be mandatory and count toward graduation.
      • Allocate the majority of the FCPS reading training budget to programs that can be implemented at both the elementary and secondary levels rather than on programs that end at the elementary level.
      • Create a formalized process that requires intervention with a standardized reading program when a student is more than a year and a half behind in grade level.
      • Add “IEP team review of the Record of Reading Process” to the IEP Meeting Agenda to ensure the system-wide implementation of the form.
      • FCPS implement a formal accountability mechanism to ensure that the following administrative groups are effectively using their training with regards to IDEA, NCLB and other State and Federal Regulations:

        Principals and their designees;
        PRSs and ISRTs;
        The Office of Monitoring and Compliance

      • FCPS increase the availability of the following:

        1. Smaller school, pyramid and cluster-based training so to better fulfill the specific training needs of these groups;
        2. IEP training focused on improving the effectiveness of IEPs, to include meaningful goal writing, data collection, and baseline data collection for both new and veteran teachers;
        3. Low-incidence disability training for both new and veteran teachers.

      • To support the increasing need for well trained substitute paraprofessionals throughout the school year, FCPS increase the paraprofessional substitute salary to be commensurate with the substitute teacher salary.
      • FCPS mandate that My PLT be a part of each school’s School Improvement Plan to further ensure accountability across the board with easy tracking and data collection regarding how much and what types of training each FCPS employee receives.
      • After the initial pilot, FCPS develop a set of guidelines to ensure that schools go through the self-assessment process consistently throughout the district. The guidelines should specify the following:

        1. Need for broad parent involvement in the process, including as many parents of children with disabilities as practicable;
        2. Inclusion of students with disabilities in the process as appropriate, and fully at the high school level;
        3. Review of individual responses to the Self-Assessment so that group analysis of the responses can determine where perspectives may be radically different or further information about school programs is needed;
        4. Development of a team process for analyzing the survey findings, resulting in an "as is" written synopsis of the school's current inclusion practices;
        5. Process for holding discussions that respect the viewpoints and priorities of all involved, in order to create an action plan to move the school from its current "as is" state to a more inclusive community. The action plan must include specific actions, persons responsible for the actions, timelines for completion, and criteria by which the actions’ effectiveness can be evaluated;
        6. Requirement that an action plan be incorporated into the school plan; and
        7. Need for wide dissemination of the school plan and opportunities for public discussion.
        8. Review of each principal’s plan for disseminating the survey, including who receives the assessment, what the expected response rates are, and how the principal intends to use the plan to improve the school.

SUBJECT: Gifted and Talented Advisory Committee Annual Report

TYPE: Discussion

RELATED TO: Student Achievement Goal 1

BACKGROUND:

The status report of the Gifted and Talented Advisory Committee was written in response to the charge of the Board “provide recommendations on how the school system can support and meet the needs of gifted students in achieving the School Board’s three student achievement goals with additional consideration given to how to implement the goals in a way that addresses the unique needs of gifted students.” GTAC asked for and received a great deal of information on a variety of topics related to Academic Goal I as it pertains to gifted learners. Through a series of meetings and conversations, assessment became a recurring theme and the committee decided to continue to learn about this topic and provide a “status” report to the school board instead of a formal report with recommendations. This report also meets the state requirement that staff provide an update on progress made toward the goals of the Local Plan for the Gifted. The update of the Local Plan shared with GTAC is attached.

KEY POINTS:
        • The committee is interested in learning more about assessment as it applies to advanced academics.
        • Time for further study and evaluation is needed.
        • Based on the information that was reviewed this year, GTAC suggests that the charge for FY’09 read: “GTAC will provide recommendations on how the school system can support and meet the needs of gifted students in achieving the School Board’s three student achievement goals with an emphasis on assessment.”
RECOMMENDATION: Acceptance of the Annual Report
