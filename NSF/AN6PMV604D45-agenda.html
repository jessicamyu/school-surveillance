Veterans Day - November 11, 2017

 

 Veterans' Day (formerly Armistice Day), is the anniversary of the Armistice which was signed in the Forest of Compiegne by the Allies and the Germans in 1918, ending World War I, after four years of conflict.

At 5 a.m., on Monday, November 11, 1918, the Germans signed the Armistice, an order was issued for all firing to cease; so the hostilities of the First World War ended. This day began with the laying down of arms, blowing of whistles, impromptu parades, and closing of places of business. All over the globe there were many demonstrations; no doubt the world had never before witnessed such rejoicing.

 

 Veterans' Day is observed annually in the United States in honor of all those, living and dead, who served with the U. S. armed forces.

 

 The School Board directs school personnel to provide students with instructional activities that honor veterans and recognize the role of the military in American democracy.  Many schools have special events, to which they invite veterans and members of the school community on active duty military.

  

 

 

American Education Week - November 12-18, 2017

 

 American Education Week was established in 1921 by the National Education Association (NEA) to raise public awareness of the importance of public education in America.  This year’s theme, Great Public Schools:  A Basic Right and Our Responsibility, reminds all Americans of the importance of public education and offers the opportunity to celebrate public education and honor those who ensure that every child receives a quality education. As part of the observance, community leaders are invited to serve as educators for a day in order to get a glimpse at a day in the life of a school employee.  American Education Week features a special observance on each day of the week, including parents, education support professionals, and substitute educators. 

 

 

 

  

Geography Awareness Week - November 2 - 18, 2017

 

  Established by a joint resolution of the U.S. Congress in 1987, Geography Awareness week is observed in November and promotes what geography is, why it is important and the relevance of a geographic education in preparing citizens to understand and debate pressing social and environmental issues and problems.  2017 marks the 30th anniversary and will be celebrated from November 12 through the 18th.   

 

This year’s theme is “The Geography of Civil Rights Movements” which mainstreams the discussion around human rights within the study of geography.

School psychologists are uniquely qualified members of school teams serving students, staff and families.  School psychologists receive specialized advanced graduate preparation that includes coursework and practical experiences relevant to both psychology and education.  They apply expertise in mental health, learning, and behavior, to change lives by taking actions to help children and youth succeed academically, socially, behaviorally, and emotionally.  In alignment with Ignite, school psychologists help individuals realize their own abilities, develop the skills they need to cope with the normal stresses of life, to work productively and fruitfully, and be valuable contributors in the community. 

 

School psychologists collaborate with school teams in the development of universal prevention practices that foster resiliency, increase educator understanding of social/emotional development, reduce barriers to learning, and increase student protective factors.  They support effective behavior management leading to improved school climate, respond to crises, and teach and implement trauma-informed practices.   

School psychologists utilize expertise in data collection and analysis to identify concerns and to monitor interventions, which may include the formal evaluation process.  In their work with students, school psychologists help students develop the social-emotional and life skills necessary to realize personal achievement and growth, and to build resilience and a sense of belonging.  Through delivery of direct services, using evidenced-based practices, school psychologists provide individual and group counseling to address challenges and concerns.  School psychologists consult with staff and parents to further their understanding of complex student needs and to help them develop the necessary skills to appropriately respond to student needs. 

 

School psychologists support families by sharing information about student skill development and wellness.  They develop interventions that may be used at home and they connect families with available community resources.

 

All children and youth can face problems from time to time related to learning, social relationships, making difficult decisions, or managing emotions such as feeling depressed, anxious, worried, or isolated. School psychologists help students, families, educators, and members of the community understand and resolve both long-term, chronic problems and short-term issues that students may face. They are a highly skilled and ready resource in the effort to ensure that all FCPS children and youth thrive in school, at home, and in life.

The School Board annually adopts a legislative program and legislative priorities to inform local, state and federal public officials and others of its positions on legislative policy issues of importance to Fairfax County Public Schools. The program contains the School Board’s positions on state and federal issues of major and current significance.

 

The 2018 Fairfax County School Board State and Federal Legislative Program will include additions, deletions and (where applicable) formatting changes from the Board’s 2017 legislative positions. Meeting Category:  November 9, 2017 – Regular Meeting

 

Subject:  One Fairfax Policy

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Key Points:

 

In July 2016, the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors and the Fairfax County School Board jointly adopted the One Fairfax Resolution that directed the development of policies and strategic actions to advance opportunity and achieve racial and social equity in Fairfax County.

 

In June 2017, a draft One Fairfax Policy was completed and presented at a Joint Boards work session on June 13, 2017.  The Successful Children and Youth Policy Team (SCYPT) Executive Committee reviewed the draft policy and individual Board members, paired by district, were briefed on the policy July-September 2017. 

 

The proposed One Fairfax Policy defines expectations for consideration of racial and social equity, and in particular, meaningful community involvement when planning, developing, and implementing policies, practices, and initiatives.  It provides a framework to advance equity in alignment with the stated visions and priorities of Fairfax County.  The policy is intended to inform all other policies and applies to all publicly delivered services in Fairfax County Government and Fairfax County Public Schools.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the One Fairfax Policy, as detailed in the agenda item.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
