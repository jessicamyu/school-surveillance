School-Based Gifted and Talented (GT) Centers

Why Offer School-Based GT Centers?
FCPS offers a continuum of gifted services for students beginning in kindergarten. The highest level of service is a GT center program. Currently Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) has 23 GT centers at the elementary level. Eligible students are bussed to GT center locations from surrounding schools.

The next level is the school-based GT center program. This program provide an important option for gifted learners who need the challenge of a GT center curriculum but do not want to leave their base school. The school-based center also provides another avenue of access for gifted services to students who may need to practice and strengthen their basic skills but have the capacity to think, reason, and problem-solve on very high levels.

During the 2003-04 school year, GT center eligible students at Beech Tree Elementary School were given the option of remaining at Beech Tree Elementary School to receive the differentiated curriculum in a school-based GT center program. Eight GT center-identified students and twelve school-based identified students made up the first class. This third and fourth grade multiage class was taught by a teacher trained in working with gifted learners. The success of this first school-based GT center led to requests from five other schools to implement a similar model for the 2004-05 school year. These schools are: Annandale Terrace, Braddock, Cameron, Columbia, and Wolf Trap elementary schools.

It is important to note that the intent of the school-based GT center is to expand opportunities for more students to participate, not to eliminate the GT center program. This model supports the goal of Target 6, which is to increase the number of Black, Hispanic, and economically disadvantaged students in gifted programs

How is the GT Center Curriculum Different from General Education?
In a GT center program, changes are made to the curriculum in order to provide an appropriate level of challenge for highly gifted learners with a strong emphasis on critical and creative thinking, and problem-solving. The mathematics curriculum is accelerated by one grade level and reading material and resources that are used in other subject areas are also advanced levels. Students have ongoing opportunities for reflection and self-assessment that develop an understanding of the characteristics, demands, and responsibilities of advanced intellectual development. The school-based GT centers maintain the same high level of challenge as the regular GT centers.

Benefits to students, community, and FCPS

Ø The faculty collaboration that occurs during grade-level planning meetings, school planning meetings, and staff development offerings results in general education teachers learning how to use powerful instructional methods common to the GT centers. Some of these methods include:

- Socratic seminars and student debates.
- Independent inquiry and research models.
- Questioning techniques that foster higher level thinking.
- Strategies that develop critical and creative thinking skills (e.g. Scamper, Synectics, Paul’s Model of Reasoning, etc.).
- Curriculum design that begins with formulating the enduring understandings children need to know (e.g. Understanding By Design, concept based instruction).

Ø In providing differentiation for advanced learners in the base school, teachers ensure that students who have specific academic strengths are challenged through flexible instructional groupings that include the GT center classes. For example, members of the child study team may decide that a general education student should attend a GT center class for daily instruction in a particular subject area such as math or language arts.

Ø GT center students are involved in special curriculum-related activities and/or events. Collaboration between general education and GT center teachers frequently results in all students at a particular grade level taking part in the events. Some examples of these special learning activities/events include:

- Pioneer Days or Renaissance Fair
- Dramatic performances and simulations
- Mathematics competitions
- History Day
- Science Exhibitions (including inventions and experiments)
    These special curricular activities may have originated in the GT centers, but they are now very much a part of the grade-level program at many GT center schools.
    Ø Fewer students will require transportation to another school.

    Possible challenges to implementation

    Ø Some parents may object to multiage classrooms and/or smaller GT centers.
    Ø GT center teachers will have a smaller cadre of colleagues who are trained in gifted education for planning and collaboration.
    Ø Capacity of school facility to support growth of model.

    Costs

    Ø Ongoing professional development for new GT center teachers (approximately $1,000 per teacher).
    Ø Purchase of text books and instructional materials to support school-based GT centers (approximately $5,000 per classroom).
