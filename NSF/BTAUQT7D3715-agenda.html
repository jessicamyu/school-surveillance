Subject: Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee Annual Report

 

Staff Contact: Sloan J. Presidio, assistant superintendent, Instructional Services Department (ISD)

 

Other Staff Present:

Wilfred Grant, co-chair, Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee (MSAOC)

Edward Osborne, co-chair, MSAOC

Shirley Norman Taylor, esq. member, MSAOC

Robert Shaw, past chair and member, MSAOC

 

Meeting Category: September 29, 2020-Work Session

 

School Board Action Required: Discussion

 

Related To: Policy 1710, Citizen Advisory Committees to the School Board

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

This is the 28th anniversary of the Minority Student Achievement Oversight Committee (MSAOC), issuing its first report to the School Board in 1994. Today, MSAOC presents its Twenty-Sixth (26th) Annual Report to the School Board as required and in response to the 2019-20 School Board charge.

 

We are gravely concerned that minority student achievement and equity are at the margins of the core mission of FCPS.

 

The Committee had hoped that the Chief Equity Officer’s position would have been filled by the reopening of school but instead delayed because the job was advertised before asking stakeholders what the duties should be—thereby causing a reposting and further delay. Without staff, much of the CEO responsibilities have gone undone.

 

Since we have been collecting SOL test data, the achievement gap is at a standstill and most recently widen. FCPS has consistently ignored the impact of schools with high concentrations of low-income Black, Hispanic and English Learners thus widening the achievement gap. The Committee has asked for an independent review of the division’s attempts to close the achievement gap but have not received a response.

 

More than a decade there has been disparities in admitting qualified Black and Hispanic students to TJHSST; today nearly 80 percent of the class of 2024 is composed of a single racial group; and the vast majority of middle schools, particularly those without Level IV Centers, have few to none of their 8th graders admitted; calls for sweeping changes in the selection process. Too many parents use a side-door process of paying for admission test preparation, thus denying students who cannot afford it a fair chance or don’t use test preparation as an unfair advantage.

 

The Committee believes that subjective decision-making in student discipline and teacher hiring have resulted in racial/ethnic disparities. Any coherent equity training must address this bias in decision making head-on.

 

The Committee looks forward to the final consultants’ report concerning the Advanced Academic Program, particularly whether private testing and parent appeal process further exacerbates inequities.

Finally, the pandemic no doubt will widen the achievement gap because for low-income students, particularly racial/ethnic minorities, positive teacher relationship is critical for advancing minority student achievement.

 

Recommendation:

The committee will address the School Board Charge and present recommendations as documented in the attachments.
