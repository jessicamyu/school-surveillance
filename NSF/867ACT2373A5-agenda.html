African American History Month - February 2009

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." This commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.

SUBJECT: Review of Grading Regulations

TYPE: Action

RELATED TO: Student Achievement Goal 1

BACKGROUND:

Some parents have raised concerns about whether FCPS methods of computing grades and applying weights for advanced courses are adversely affecting FCPS applicants for college admissions, honors program placements, and merit-based scholarship awards. FCPS staff has collaborated with a group representing those parents to conduct a joint investigation into the issue. The resulting report summarizes facts surrounding colleges’ and universities’ consideration of student applications. Its intent is to provide a basis for the School Board’s review of current grading procedures and governing regulations.


KEY POINTS:

1. GPAs computed on 10-point scales are higher than those of FCPS graduates, especially when greater weights are applied to advanced courses.

2. Changing scales could increase GPAs for more students across a range of GPAs.

3. Changing the weights will increase GPAs for students who complete more honors and Advanced Placement/International Baccalaureate (AP/IB) courses.

4. Grades in core courses, rigor of curriculum, number of advanced courses, SAT/ACT scores, and weighted GPA are identified as the most important factors in college admissions.

5. Standardized test scores and weighted GPAs are the two most important considerations in merit-based scholarship awards and honors program placements.

6. The weights most commonly seen by admissions officers are 0.5 points for honors and 1.0 points for AP/IB courses.

OPTIONS:

Option 1: No action
Option 2: Change weights only (0.5 for honors and 1.0 for AP/IB, and dual enrollment)
Option 3: Change scale only (10-point)
Option 4: Change both
    The Superintendent initially recommended that the School Board approve Option 2, change weights only. At the January 12, 2009, work session, the Board discussed the various proposed options with the Superintendent. The conclusion of the work session discussion resulted in three proposed changes to the grading scale.
RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board approve increasing the grade point weight from 0.5 to 1.0 for all Advanced Placement (AP) and International Baccalaureate (IB) courses, with the weights to be retroactive for all AP/IB courses on students’ transcripts effective January 2009.

That the School Board approve increasing the grade point weight to 1.0 for dual enrollment college courses and to 0.5 for all courses deemed to be of honors caliber, and if practicable, the increased grade point weight shall be effective for such courses on students' transcripts for the 2009-2010 school year.

That the School Board direct the Superintendent to recommend a grading scale that accurately measures and communicates the level of student achievement, aligns more closely with grading scales used by other school districts, and reflects the high academic standards of Fairfax County Public Schools; this recommendation shall be presented to the Board in time to take effect for the beginning of the 2009-2010 school year.

BOARD AMENDMENTS:
    • I move to amend the main motion by striking the words “a grading scale” in the third clause, and replacing it with the following language “…variants on a basic ten-point grading scale with pluses and minuses”; the clause would therefore read “That the SB direct the Superintendent to recommend variants on a basic ten-point grading scale with pluses and minuses that accurately measures and communicates the level of student achievement, aligns more closely …” (Strauss)
    • I move to amend the main motion by adding the words “…by the end of March 2009” to the third clause; the clause would therefore read “this recommendation shall be presented to the Board by the end of March 2009 in time to take effect for the beginning of the 2009-2010 school year.” (Strauss)
    • I move to amend the main motion by striking the words “… with the weights to be retroactive for all AP/IB courses on students’ transcripts effective January 2009” in the first clause, and replacing it with the following language “…and if practicable, the increased grade point weight shall be effective for such courses on students’ transcripts effective for the 2009-2010 school year”; the first clause would therefore read “That the School Board approve increasing the grade point weight from 0.5 to 1.0 for all Advanced Placement (AP) and International Baccalaureate (IB) courses, and if practicable, the increased grade point weight shall be effective for such courses on students’ transcripts for the 2009-2010 school year.” (Raney)

SUBJECT: FY 2010-2014 Capital Improvement Program

TYPE: Action

KEY POINTS:

Enrollment projections, capacity estimates and other background information supporting the recommendation are contained in the Cluster Data compact disc attached to the FY 2010-2014 Capital Improvement Program booklet and in the attachment to this agenda item. Details are provided in the narrative, Capital Improvement Program, and its supporting materials.

The Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2010-2014 Capital Improvement Program Summary can be found in the attachment to the Capital Improvement Program booklet.

RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board approve the FY 2010-2014 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2010 -2014 Capital Improvement Program Summary.

BOARD AMENDMENT:

I move to amend the CIP to: (1) construct a building addition to South County Secondary School, instead of a new middle school, to accommodate the projected capacity deficits; (2) pay for the addition with funds to be approved on the 2009 Bond Referendum; (3) adjust the CIP cash flow to accommodate the completion of the addition for the 2011-2012 school year; and (4) apply the cost savings to advance the planning and construction of other renovations and needed capacity enhancement projects in the order provided in the CIP. (Gibson)

Rationale
EVERY DOLLAR SPENT ON ONE CAPITAL PROJECT
IS A DOLLAR WE CANNOT SPEND ON ANOTHER CAPITAL PROJECT
    1. South County Secondary School (SCSS) is projected to be over capacity by only 17 students at the high school level and 208 students at the middle school level in the “out years,” 2012-2013. It is not fiscally prudent to build a new middle school to accommodate 208 students at any time. This is especially true when we have so many other unmet, and unfunded, capital needs.
    2. While Lake Braddock Secondary School has capacity to accommodate additional students from SCSS, that capacity is at the high school level, not the middle school level. Thus, a boundary change is not a feasible way to accommodate enrollment growth at SCSS.
    3. The School Board committed $2 million of bond funds to address “BRAC-related” growth in the southern part of Fairfax County. Yet all of the BRAC-related growth is projected to occur outside the SCSS attendance area. Using the $2 million BRAC-growth money to build a new south county middle school (SCMS) will prevent the Board from using that money for the purpose it was intended.
    4. Of all the middle schools in southern Fairfax County, Whitman Middle School – with a projected capacity deficit of 300 students by 2012-2013 – is the school most urgently in need of additional capacity. Building a new SCMS will reduce the capital resources available to address capacity needs at Whitman.
    5. Because of expected growth in student enrollment, the CIP postpones the renovation of many schools in need of renovation (including what have been referred to as the 5 “legacy” high schools, Langley, West Springfield, Herndon, Oakton, and Falls Church). Spending $52 million to build a new middle school – when an addition that can accommodate the projected enrollment at SCSS can be built for $12 million – will delay the renovation of all schools not already funded for renovation. In particular, building a middle school will delay the renovation of Sunrise Valley, Garfield, Terra Centre, and Westgate Elementary Schools, and Thoreau Middle School by 2 years, and the renovation of Langley and West Springfield High Schools by 1 year.
    6. At the January 12, 2009 work session, staff raised the possibility that the Board of Supervisors may have to reduce the annual cash flow to FCPS for capital projects, to fit within the County’s limits on bonded indebtedness. If that happens, building a new middle school by 2012 will further delay these renovation projects, along with others.
    7. To build the middle school in accordance with the Board’s action of 2008, the school system will have to obtain short-term “bridge funding,” to pay for construction until bond proceeds can be raised. Interest on any bridge funding will have to come from the School Board’s bond program, and will further reduce funding available for other capital projects.
    8. When the Board voted to build the new SCMS last summer, Board members knew that we might have to revisit the decision at the time we adopt the CIP. Since then the situation has changed, dramatically and for the worse. What may have been politically important last summer would, in my view, be fiscally and politically irresponsible now. When we and the Board of Supervisors are struggling to provide basic services at a time of declining revenue in the midst of a severe recession, we cannot afford to spend $52 million on a new 1,250-student middle school, to accommodate 208 students.
    9. Building an addition at SCSS allows us to relieve overcrowding at SCSS sooner than by building a new school. At the same time, it frees up $40 million in capital resources that we can use to address capacity and renovation needs at other schools.

SUBJECT: School Board Organizational Meeting Schedule

TYPE: Action

BACKGROUND:

On November 17, 2008, the Governance Committee discussed the possibility of changing the timing of the Board's organizational meeting from January of each year to July of each year. From 1927 to 1995, the Board's organizational meeting was held every year in July. In January 1996, the first elected School Board members took office and held the organizational meeting at the first meeting in January, and since that time the annual organizational meeting has been held in January.

Section 22.1-72 of the Code of Virginia states, in pertinent part: "Each school board shall hold its annual organizational meeting for the purpose of establishing its regular meeting schedule for the ensuing year as follows ... (iii) in January or July, if the school board serves a county constituting a school division and its members are elected in whole or in part."

Changing the organizational meeting from January to July would be consistent with the fiscal year, and school and state calendars, and School Board committee and liaison appointments would be effective for this same cycle. In the year following a School Board election (which is held every four years), an additional organizational meeting vote would be required in January.

The Board discussed the issue at the December 8, 2008, work session, and there was consensus to have further discussion. At the December 18, 2008, forum, there was majority support for the change and for further discussion once the issue had been scheduled for new business and action.


RECOMMENDATION:

That the School Board change the annual organizational meeting from January of each year to July of each year, effective July 9, 2009, and as authorized by Section 22.1-72(iii) of the Code of Virginia.

SUBJECT: Alternative Accreditation Plan Requests for Key and Kilmer Centers

TYPE: Action

RELATED TO: Student Achievement: Goal 1

BACKGROUND:
      Kilmer Center was accredited with warning in mathematics and science by the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) for 2008-2009. Key Center met accreditation for 2008-2009; however, the center may not make accreditation in 2009-2010. Because both centers are special purpose schools, serving students with severe disabilities for whom the standard measures of achievement that are used for school accreditation are inappropriate, staff at the VDOE’s Office of School improvement recommended that Kilmer and Key Centers submit alternative accreditation plans to the Virginia Board of Education for approval. This recommendation is based on the Regulations Establishing Standards for Accrediting Public Schools in Virginia, Section 8 VAC 20-131.280.C. The regulation states:
        Subject to the provisions of 8 VAC 20-131-330, the governing school board of special purpose schools such as those provided for in §22.1-26 of the Code, Governor’s schools, special education schools, alternative schools, or career and technical schools that serve as the student’s school of principal enrollment may seek approval of an alternative accreditation plan from the Board of Education. Special purpose schools with alternative accreditation plans shall be evaluated on standards appropriate to the programs offered in the school and approved by the Board prior to August 1 of the school year for which approval is requested. Any student graduating from a special purpose school with a Standard, Advanced Studies, or Modified Standard Diploma must meet the requirements prescribed in 8 VAC 20-131-50.

KEY POINTS:
      Kilmer and Key Centers are submitting separate applications for an alternative accreditation plan. Both applications describe the:
          · mission and purpose of the center
          · student population served by the center
          · instructional program and support services offered by the center
          · strategies and instruments used by the center to evaluate student progress toward established goals and objectives.

      In addition to the descriptive information, both applications provide detailed explanations of why Standard 8 VAC-20-131-280—Expectations for School Accountability—is not appropriate for the center.

      Kilmer and Key Centers are submitting similar alternative accreditation plans. Because of NCLB requirements, students will participate in the Virginia Alternate Assessment Program (VAAP). VAAP results, along with other measures, will be used to determine the accreditation rating, as follows.
          · If the VAAP results meet the accreditation rating, then the alternative accreditation plan will not be used.
          · Scores for each content area will be assigned a certain number of points based on the VAAP “cut score.” Because of the small pool of students at each grade level, the scores at all grade levels will be combined. To achieve accreditation, the scores must average at least 70 points. In addition, scores for students who are not in attendance for 75% of the total number of instructional days during which evidence for the VAAP is being collected will be excluded from the accreditation rating calculation. Additional points will be added based on appropriate student progress measurement criteria specific to each center.
              o For Kilmer Center, three points will be added for each student who transitions to a less restrictive educational setting. In addition, three points will be added for the successful achievement of each of the achievement indicators described in the Kilmer Center School Improvement Plan for a total of six possible additional points.
              o For Key Center, three points will be added for the successful achievement of each of the achievement indicators from the Key Center School Improvement Plan for a total of nine possible additional points.

OPTIONS:
      1. Approve the requests for approval of alternative accreditation plans for Kilmer and Key Centers to be submitted to the Virginia Board of Education.

      2. Continue with the current requirements from the Virginia Department of Education and have Kilmer and Key Centers be accredited with warning for 2009-2010.

RECOMMENDATION:
      That the School Board approve the Alternative Accreditation Plan requests for Key and Kilmer Centers, as detailed in the agenda item.

    6.03 School Board Policy - Recommendation to adopt Policy 1375.3, Charity Drives, as reviewed and discussed at the Policy work session (S; work session held 1/12/09; action scheduled 2/5/09)
    Meeting
    Jan 22, 2009 - Regular Meeting No. 12
    Category
    6. New Business - 11:35 p.m.
    Type
    Information
    Staff Contact: Anne Murphy, Division Counsel

    Meeting Type: January 22, 2009 - Regular Meeting No.12

    SUBJECT: School Board Policy

    TYPE: New Business

    RELATED TO: Goal 3

    BACKGROUND:

    At the October 23, 2008 forum, Ms. Wilson recommended the amendment of Policy 1375 to facilitate the approval of student-initiated charity fundraising, including international charities. Other Board members requested information about the charity fund-raising practices of area local jurisdictions. They also requested guidelines that staff could use to assess charities prior to approving them for fundraising drives.

    KEY POINTS:
      • The attached revisions to Policy 1375 clarify that student initiated fund-raising relating to Goal 3 will be permitted. They also authorize fundraising for international as well as local organizations.
      • The on-line policies of Montgomery, Arlington, Loudon, Alexandria and Prince William schools had no or very little information on this topic, e.g. Arlington and Alexandria permit charity drives with the approval of the principal, but provide no other guidance. A nation-wide email survey of NSBA in-house lawyers also produced limited responses. A few jurisdictions that permitted charity fund-raising had had problems with insuring compliance with accounting rules, actual fraud, or aggressive recruiting of students and staff by non profits.
      • Consistent with the Board’s request, staff investigated potential guidelines for schools’ use in assessing the bona fides of potential recipient charities. Examples of such guidelines included section 501(c)(3) status under the Internal Revenue Code, accreditation under the Better Business Bureau’s charity accountability guidelines, inclusion on the “approved” list of a designated charity umbrella organization such as United Way or Combined Federal Campaign, and/or exclusion from the U.S. Department of State list of terrorist organizations.
      • Leadership Team members, however, and particularly principals, recommended against requiring all recipient charities to comply with such guidelines, for several reasons. First, some potential recipients of school fund-raising efforts (such as needy families, area disaster victims, or smaller nontraditional charities) are unlikely to qualify under such guidelines. Moreover, students are increasingly taking the lead to identify and develop their own relief projects and the proposed recipients may be nontraditional entities who will not qualify under federal and related guidelines. Maintaining Policy 1375’s existing approval system will allow principals to determine whether 501 (c)(3) and/or accredited charity status is important to a given fund drive, while also providing them the flexibility to approve or disapprove fund-raising efforts for non-traditional recipients.

    OPTIONS:
      1. Leave policy as is
      2. Approve amended policy (see attachment)
      3. Amend policy to provide more detailed guidance and/or to reflect other School Board priorities

    RECOMMENDATION:

    That the School Board approve Policy 1375.3, Charity Drives, as reviewed and discussed at the Policy work session.

    Attachmentment: Policy 1375.3, Charity Drives

    6.07 Appointments to the Joint Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee - Recommendation to appoint School Board members to the newly-established joint Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee, which will begin meeting by the end of January 2009 and will report back to a joint meeting of the Boards on opportunities and next steps for any immediate and long-term actions to better align governmental responsibilities and resources (SB; action scheduled 2/5/09)
    Meeting
    Jan 22, 2009 - Regular Meeting No. 12
    Category
    6. New Business - 11:35 p.m.
    Type
    Information


    Staff Contact: Dan Storck, Chairman and Mount Vernon District

    Meeting Type: January 22, 2009 – Regular Meeting No. 12

    SUBJECT: Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee

    TYPE: New Business

    BACKGROUND:

    The Chairman of the School Board and the Vice Chairman of the Board of Supervisors have recently had discussions about the establishment of a joint County/School Board committee that would pursue cost savings and/or improved County services by streamlining, combining, or consolidating separate County/School functions. As a result of the grim budget situation, there is an increasing need and demand for public services with far fewer funds available to provide them, and the situation is likely to worsen. There has not been any joint Board-level review of combining or streamlining functions in more than ten years, and both Boards have a strong commitment to shared values. A 4 to 6 member joint Board of Supervisors /School Board committee would be appointed to investigate and recommend to both Boards any immediate and long-term actions to better align governmental responsibilities and resources.

    The charge of this joint committee would include: 1) Review existing County and School operations and identify immediate and longer-term combinations or improved alignments of resources, responsibilities or programs; 2) Review County and School equipment and facilities and identify immediate and longer-term options for streamlining, consolidations or integration; and 3) Be bold in identifying policy options where changes have the potential for improved services or availability or decreased costs.

    Potential opportunities for change would include SACC/middle school after-school program, school health and safety (including public health nurses, clinic aides, SRO, and crossing guards), community schools, and transportation

    RECOMMENDATION:

    That the School Board appoint School Board members to the newly-established joint Board of Supervisors/School Board Smart Services Committee, which will begin meeting by the end of January 2009 and will report back to a joint meeting of the Boards on opportunities and next steps for any immediate and long-term actions to better align governmental responsibilities and resources.
