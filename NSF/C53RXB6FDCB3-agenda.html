Month of the Military Child Recognition

 

 

April is the Month of the Military Child.  Here in Fairfax County, we are extremely fortunate to have over 13,500 students who are part of a military family as well as part of our Fairfax County Public Schools family.  Since 2018, when the Virginia Department of Education, the Virginia Council on the Interstate Compact on Educational Opportunity for Military Children and Fairfax County began certifying purple star schools, we have certified 31 elementary schools with this designation.  

 

Our military connected students bring a strong sense of community and a wealth of life experiences to our schools.  Our military families often move every 3 years, that means that in the course of a student’s K-12 education they can move as many as 5 times.  In addition to the physical moves, military connected students often deal with their parents being deployed multiple times and worry about potential deployments when we see areas of unrest around the world.

 

Celebrating the Month of the Military Child provides a powerful opportunity to raise awareness and build support to help these children meet the unique challenges they face!  It is an opportunity to recognize and thank military children for their heroism, character, courage, sacrifices, and continued resilience.

 

We invite you to join us on Wednesday, April 20 for “Purple Up Day” as we encourage everyone to wear purple to celebrate our military connected students.

Social Worker of the Year Recognition

 

On behalf of the Fairfax County School Board, I am honored to recognize – Mrs. Claudia McDowell -- who was recently named 2022 National School Social Worker of the Year. Mrs. McDowell is a social worker at Lynbrook Elementary School in the Lee District.

 

Mrs. McDowell  was named social worker of the year by the School Social Work Association of America (SSWAA) for her commitment to the profession and for positively impacting the lives of students, families, and staff of the community she serves. On April 8, Mrs. McDowell received her award at SSWAA National Conference in Chicago.

 

Mrs. McDowell began working at Lynbrook Elementary School 10 years ago. She has been instrumental in working with the school’s attendance program, helping to counter chronic absenteeism by developing support systems to increase all student’s sense of belonging, confidence, and engagement in the school. She has also started a mindfulness program in Kindergarten classes where students get to see her, get to know her, and shows that she is truly there to support the students, and their families.

 

Lynbrook Elementary Principal Kathleen Sain recently shared that she is “always there to help in a moment of need, she has had many times where on her way home from work or in the early evening she has dropped off supplemental food gift cards to families that have an immediate food or formula need.”

 

Mrs. McDowell has gone above and beyond – not only in the school and classrooms – but during difficult times too. There was a fire in Fairfax County that impacted some families who attend Lynbrook. Mrs. McDowell coordinated with County officials and the Red Cross to provide clothing and food. She also helped to arrange temporary transportation so students could get to school. She launched a crisis team and worked with teachers so they could help the students who were living through this traumatic event. This is what led her colleagues to nominate her for this year’s award.

 

Mrs. McDowell, you truly are an inspiration to all of us and I hope many future school social workers. On behalf of the Fairfax County School Board, I thank you for your service to our FCPS students and the Lynbrook community.

Key Points:

Based on the Virginia Department of Education’s (VDOE) review of the Local School Wellness Policy, changes have been made to the following sections: Section IV. I. 2., Section IV. J. 1., Section IV. J. 2., Section IV. J. 4., Section IV. J. 5., Section IV. J. 6., and Section IV. K. 6.

 

  • Section IV. I. 2. has been revised to address current nutrition requirements established under the Healthy, Hunger-free Kids Act of 2010.
  • Section IV. J. 1. has been added to address Farm to School experiential activities and nutrition education.  Farm to School experiential activities (i.e., gardening and cooking demonstrations) and nutrition education will be encouraged with core subjects and electives throughout the school year as a part of existing curricula. 
  • Section IV. J. 2. has been added to address food and beverages sold outside of the school meal programs shall meet the standards established in USDA's Nutrition Standards for All Foods Sold in Schools (Smart Snacks).
  • Section IV. J. 4. has been added to address the restriction of food and beverage marketing to only those foods and beverages that meet the nutrition standards set forth by USDA's Nutrition Standards for All Foods Sold in Schools (Smart Snacks).
  • Section IV. J. 5. has been added to address thirty (30) school-sponsored fundraisers annually each school year, per site during the school day to be exempt from Smart Snacks Standards.  Exempt fundraisers are tracked and monitored by the school administration.  Records are kept on file at the school’s main office.
  • Section IV. J. 6. has been added to address any fundraiser that sells food or beverages, whether the items meet the USDA-FNS Smart Snacks rule or are exempt, may not be conducted during school meal service times. This includes from 6:00 a.m. to the end of the breakfast period and from the beginning of the first lunch period to the end of the last lunch period. The income from any food or beverage sold to students during these times shall accrue to the non-profit nutrition program account.
