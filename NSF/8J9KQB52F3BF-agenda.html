Budget, Financial Management, and Food and Nutrition Services Operational Expectations

A. Budget: The Superintendent shall develop and maintain a multi-year financial plan that is related directly to the Board’s student achievement goals and operational expectations, and that avoids long-term fiscal jeopardy to the district.  The Superintendent will develop a budget that:

  1. Is in a summary format that is understandable, transparent and easily accessible by the community, and presented in a manner that demonstrates the relationship between the budget and the priorities within the student achievement goals and operational expectations.
  2. Accurately projects revenues and expenditures.
  3. Shows the amount spent in each budget category for the previous three fiscal years, the amount budgeted for the current fiscal year, and the amount budgeted for the next fiscal year.
  4. Explains budget-planning assumptions, identifying significant trends and changes.
  5. Accurately reflects anticipated changes in employee compensation, including inflationary adjustments, step increases, and benefits.
  6. Provides the Board with a fiscal forecast of needs five (5) years into the future.

B. Financial Management: The Superintendent will manage financial affairs in a fiscally accountable, transparent manner consistent with School Board budget and goals, maximizing effectiveness and conserving resources. The Superintendent will:

  1. Ensure that resources are expended in accordance with the budget and School Board expectations.
  2. Receive, process, and disburse funds under controls consistent with generally accepted accounting principles, including:
    a. Settling all debts in a timely manner;
    b. Maintaining complete and accurate financial records;
    c. Conducting an annual audit of all school division funds and accounts by an independent auditing firm;
    d. Conducting a regular fiscal and performance audit of business functions;
    e. Providing public access to audit results.
  3. Base purchases upon prudent judgment and sound financial practice, including comparative pricing as appropriate, while taking into account long-term quality and cost.
  4. Maintain reasonable insurance coverage on school division property and against liability losses to Board members, staff members, and the school division itself.

C. Food and Nutrition Services:  The Superintendent will operate a self-supporting child nutrition program that meets or exceeds Federal guidelines and that promotes healthy choices and wellness.

Contact:               Tessie Wilson, Audit Committee Chairman and Braddock District Representative

 

Meeting Category: November 14, 2011 - Work Session No. 40

 

Subject:                Recommendations for External Management Audit Reviews

 

School Board Action Required: Discussion

 

Key Points: 

 

At the October 17, 2011, work session, the Board discussed the following areas for possible management audit review:

  • Internal Audit - function
  • Communications - function
  • Technology Facilities/ transportation – organization
  • Instructional Services – structure
  • Staff development
  • Employee benefits
  • Preventive maintenance
  • Span of control
  • Delivery of education services

The Board requested that the Audit Committee make recommendations for consideration at the November 14 work session.  The Audit Committee is recommending the following two areas for external management audit reviews:

 

Cell Phone/Blackberry Review

 

FCPS utilizes over 2,500 telecommunication wireless devices to support timely communication within FCPS and with the community 24/7.  The current annual expense is $1.4 million.  It would be helpful to review the contracts and billing associated with Blackberries, Cell Phones, and Pagers to ensure that FCPS is receiving the best possible pricing for the devices and services from the multiple vendors.  In addition, it would be helpful to review the processes associated with service and support of these devices.

 

Facilities Maintenance Review

 

The Office of Facilities Management (OFM) is severely understaffed as has been reported to the School Board for the past several years.  The lack of adequate staffing results in delays in responding to non-emergency work orders and for an almost total absence of preventative maintenance being performed on FCPS buildings and mechanical infrastructure.

 

The American Productivity and Quality Center (APQC) have proposed a Process and Performance Management (PPM) professional development opportunity for OFM.  The outcome of this engagement is to better define measure, improve, and scale-up successful services and to define and create structures for processes within OFM that need to be improved.  This will ensure that OFM business processes are efficient and effective and prioritized within the resource constraints that currently exist and most likely will continue to exist in the future.

 

Recommendation

 

That the School Board authorize staff to issue a Request for Proposal.

5.02 Use of Video Surveillance Cameras - Cafeteria/Interior Cameras (CCTV)
Meeting
Nov 14, 2011 - All-day Work Sessions Nos. 38-42
Category
5. WS No. 42 Facilities - 3:30 p.m.
Type
Discussion

Staff Contact:        Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  November 14, 2011 – Work Session No. 42

 

Subject:                 Use of Video Surveillance Cameras—Cafeteria/Interior Cameras (CCTV)

 

School Board Action Required:  Discussion

 

Background:

 

Staff has proposed to the School Board that the board amend its policy to allow the installation of surveillance cameras in high schools in cafeterias and other high traffic areas.  The amended policy would not require the installation of such cameras but would allow principals, with the support of their individual communities, to install them only in those areas of the school that are consistent with the revised policy.

 

At the September 12, 2011, work session, the School Board requested additional information on three issues. 

 

  • Provide feedback from community outreach efforts to ascertain public sentiment regarding the use of interior video surveillance systems in high schools.

High school principals were directed to share with their community the proposal to implement interior video surveillance in cafeterias and hot spot locations within their school and to obtain data on community reaction.  Principals met with their local PTSA’s in September and October and were asked to make a judgment about whether their communities supported or opposed interior cameras.  The principals reported that 80% of the PTSA’s either supported (68%, n =17) or took no position (12%, n =3).  Eight percent (n = 2) were opposed, and 12% (n =3) were split.  Additionally, staff and principals also met with the County Council of PTSA’s where the response was split. 

 

Comments from community members were captured from several schools and are included in the Board information. Those supporting interior cameras spoke of increased student and property safety; deterrent enhancement; as well as the objective nature of cameras. The concerns of those opposed to interior cameras included cost; no proven deterrent; privacy and trust; and the need for more adult supervisors or volunteers in our schools.

 

Schools also presented information on the proposal to faculty, staff and students; however, no data were collected. 

 

  • Identify the methodology and data points for evaluating the effectiveness and gained efficiencies from the use of interior video surveillance systems.

In the immediate Washington DC metro area, interior video surveillance is commonplace.  Indeed, across the United States, school systems use video surveillance and consider them to be invaluable.  As best we can determine, no studies have been conducted on the efficacy of these cameras.

 

Staff believes that the cameras are an effective deterrent and an investigative tool.  Our specific experience with external cameras in FCPS strongly suggests that cameras do reduce both the number of and degree of incidents.  In the study conducted by the office of safety and security, high schools with exterior surveillance cameras averaged 3.5 times the rate of vandalism cost reduction as did the schools without cameras over four years.  These high schools also averaged over twice the rate of vandalism incident reduction as did schools without cameras.  Additionally, since 2005, 392 incidents of various types that utilized video surveillance for review have been reported to the office of safety and security, including 168 incidents in the last 2 school years.

 

FCPS also has cameras on school buses.  While no data have been kept with respect to the efficacy of these cameras, transportation services and school principals use the recordings often to determine what actually happened and who did exactly what.  Cameras on school buses are strongly believed to be a very useful and important tool in deterring inappropriate behavior and in investigating incidents.

 

We are recommending that schools that decide to install interior cameras will report the following data annually and that these data will become part of the Operational Expectations for the department of facilities and transportation.

 

  • Number of incidents in specific areas of the schools in which cameras have been installed.
  • Number of successful investigations of incidents using video recordings.
  • Results of the Working Conditions Survey that will now include a question specifically about cameras for those schools with cameras.
  • Results of the Fairfax Youth Survey regarding feeling safe at school.
     
  • Identify funding sources for implementation and ongoing costs.

No additional funding will be required should the School Board amend the policy to allow interior cameras.  Non-instructional, school based and available proffer funds would be utilized to pay for the surveillance systems.  Non-instructional school based funds are part of the local school activity accounts that are managed by the schools with guidance from financial services.  These funds are not budgeted as part of the appropriated funds and hence, no allocations are provided to them.  The typical revenue sources for these funds are from retaining 15% of the receipts from community use, parking, and other fees collected by the schools.  Other sources include the school’s share of the vending machine profits as well as the school’s portion of the monopole receipts.  Proffer funding is available for 8 high schools.  There may be some limited funding available from the department of facilities and transportation services for those schools who desire a surveillance system, but do not have sufficient funding.

 

Current analysis of the cost of installing cameras in every high school’s cafeteria and hot spots is that the total maximum cost would be approximately $885,000.  The community survey suggests some high schools will not install cameras which will reduce this total cost.  Available proffers for eight of the high schools can fund about $122,500 of these costs.

 

New surveillance equipment comes with a 3 year parts and labor warranty from the vendor.  Thereafter, preventive maintenance and repairs are provided through an existing contract for these services, which is funded through monopole receipts.

 

Key Points:

 

The amended policy would not require the installation of such cameras but would allow principals, with the support of their individual communities, to install them only in those areas of the school that are consistent with the revised policy.

 

High School principals met with their local PTSA’s, who were largely supportive of the use of interior video surveillance cameras.

 

Interior video systems are used in most school systems across the country and throughout the Washington Metropolitan area.

 

FCPS analysis of external vandalism reductions and its use on school buses strongly suggests that the use of video surveillance is an effective tool.

 

Schools that decide to install interior cameras will report data that will become part of the Operational Expectations for the department of facilities and transportation.

 

No additional funding will be required should the School Board amend the policy to allow interior cameras.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the use of security cameras in high school cafeterias and hot spot locations identified by the principal and that the installation of such cameras will not be mandatory but rather will be made on a school by school basis with the support of individual school communities.
