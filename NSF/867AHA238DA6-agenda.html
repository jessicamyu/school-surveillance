SUBJECT: Review of Grading Regulations

TYPE: Discussion

RELATED TO: Student Achievement Goal 1

BACKGROUND:

Some parents have raised concerns about whether FCPS methods of computing grades and applying weights for advanced courses are adversely affecting FCPS applicants for college admissions, honors program placements, and merit-based scholarship awards. FCPS staff has collaborated with a group representing those parents to conduct a joint investigation into the issue. The resulting report summarizes facts surrounding colleges’ and universities’ consideration of student applications. Its intent is to provide a basis for the School Board’s review of current grading procedures and governing regulations.


KEY POINTS:

1. GPAs computed on 10-point scales are higher than those of FCPS graduates, especially when greater weights are applied to advanced courses.

2. Changing scales could increase GPAs for more students across a range of GPAs.

3. Changing the weights will increase GPAs for students who complete more honors and Advanced Placement/International Baccalaureate (AP/IB) courses.

4. Grades in core courses, rigor of curriculum, number of advanced courses, SAT/ACT scores, and weighted GPA are identified as the most important factors in college admissions.

5. Standardized test scores and weighted GPAs are the two most important considerations in merit-based scholarship awards and honors program placements.

6. The weights most commonly seen by admissions officers are 0.5 points for honors and 1.0 points for AP/IB courses.

OPTIONS:

Option 1: No action
Option 2: Change weights only (0.5 for honors and 1.0 for AP/IB, and dual enrollment)
Option 3: Change scale only (10-point)
Option 4: Change both

RECOMMENDATION:
    The Superintendent is recommending that Option 2 be implemented. Option 2 enables FCPS to maintain high academic standards while recognizing and rewarding students who choose to take the most rigorous courses. Option 3 lowers performance expectations and grading standards, and would not substantively address decisions on merit scholarship or honors at the time of admission. Option 1 does not address the merit scholarship or honors at admissions issue, but does maintain high academic standards and performance expectations.

    The Superintendent recommends implementing increased weighting for AP/IB courses retroactively on all transcripts issued at the end of the first semester of this school year. Increased weights for honors classes would begin with courses taken prospectively with the 2009-10 school year. It is possible to implement both changes for the 2009-10 school year, but a delay would eliminate the possibility of benefiting some current seniors seeking merit scholarships or, honors at admissions.

Meetings were announced via press release, mail outs, “backpack” and “Keep in Touch” notices to parents, and web-site postings. Citizen comment from each meeting was captured in summaries posted to the FCPS web page after each meeting. Scenario details (maps and impact tables) were similarly posted.

    Staff Contact: Anne Murphy, Division Counsel

    SUBJECT: School Board Policy 1375

    TYPE: Discussion

    RELATED TO: Goal 3

    BACKGROUND:

    At the October 23, 2008 forum, Ms. Wilson recommended the amendment of Policy 1375 to facilitate the approval of student-initiated charity fundraising, including international charities. Other Board members requested information about the charity fund-raising practices of area local jurisdictions. They also requested guidelines that staff could use to assess charities prior to approving them for fundraising drives.

    KEY POINTS:
      • The attached revisions to Policy 1375 clarify that student initiated fund-raising relating to Goal 3 will be permitted. They also authorize fundraising for international as well as local organizations.
      • The on-line policies of Montgomery, Arlington, Loudon, Alexandria and Prince William schools had no or very little information on this topic, e.g. Arlington and Alexandria permit charity drives with the approval of the principal, but provide no other guidance. A nation-wide email survey of NSBA in-house lawyers also produced limited responses. A few jurisdictions that permitted charity fund-raising had had problems with insuring compliance with accounting rules, actual fraud, or aggressive recruiting of students and staff by non profits.
      • Consistent with the Board’s request, staff investigated potential guidelines for schools’ use in assessing the bona fides of potential recipient charities. Examples of such guidelines included section 501(c)(3) status under the Internal Revenue Code, accreditation under the Better Business Bureau’s charity accountability guidelines, inclusion on the “approved” list of a designated charity umbrella organization such as United Way or Combined Federal Campaign, and/or exclusion from the U.S. Department of State list of terrorist organizations.
      • Leadership Team members, however, and particularly principals, recommended against requiring all recipient charities to comply with such guidelines, for several reasons. First, some potential recipients of school fund-raising efforts (such as needy families, area disaster victims, or smaller nontraditional charities) are unlikely to qualify under such guidelines. Moreover, students are increasingly taking the lead to identify and develop their own relief projects and the proposed recipients may be nontraditional entities who will not qualify under federal and related guidelines. Maintaining Policy 1375’s existing approval system will allow principals to determine whether 501 (c)(3) and/or accredited charity status is important to a given fund drive, while also providing them the flexibility to approve or disapprove fund-raising efforts for non-traditional recipients.

    OPTIONS:
      1. Leave policy as is
      2. Approve amended policy (see attachment)
      3. Amend policy to provide more detailed guidance and/or to reflect other School Board priorities

    RECOMMENDATION:

    Staff recommends Option 2, to clarify that student-initiated fundraising is permissible, including solicitations for organizations outside the immediate Fairfax community.
