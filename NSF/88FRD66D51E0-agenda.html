Staff Contact:            Susan Quinn, chief financial officer, Department of Financial Services    

 

Meeting Category:    April 4, 2011 – Work Session No. 98

 

Subject:                      Budget, Financial Management, and Food and Nutrition Services Operational Expectations Monitoring Report         

 

School Board Action Required:      Decision                                             

 

Key Points:

 

Currently, the annual Operational Expectations Monitoring Report for Budget, Financial Management, and Food and Nutrition Services is scheduled for presentation in June 2011.  Staff is recommending that the annual report date be changed to November. 

 

Since the majority of the indicators measure activities over a fiscal year, a June reporting period includes information from the prior year.  This provides the School Board with dated information for activities completed nearly 12 months prior and limits Financial Services’ ability to implement any suggestions in the next monitoring report.  A November reporting date would include information from the fiscal year that was just completed, providing the School Board with timely information.    

 

FCPS is currently in the process of implementing FOCUS, and required implementation activities are rapidly accelerating.  In addition to coordinating the close of the fiscal year, Financial Services will be intensely engaged with implementation activities up to and beyond the July go-live. 

 

Transitioning this year, and delaying Financial Services’ monitoring report from June to November would support a successful FOCUS implementation and year-end close and would provide the greatest value from the monitoring report.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve a new annual reporting date of November for the Budget, Financial Management, and Food and Nutrition Services Operational Expectations Monitoring Report, as detailed in the agenda item.

6.02 Scope of Study: Elementary School Boundary Solutions - Lacey Site & Middle/High School Boundary Solutions - Annandale High School Overcrowding

Apr 4, 2011 - All Day Work Sessions Nos. 95-100
Category
6. WS No. 99 Facilities - 3 p.m.

Staff Contact:             Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:     April 4, 2011 – Work Session No. 99

 

Subject:                       Scope of Study:

                                      Elementary School Boundary Solutions - Lacey Site

                                      Middle/High School Boundary Solutions - Annandale High School Overcrowding   

 

School Board Action Required:   Discussion

 

Background:

 

On April 29, 2010, the School Board authorized the formation of an ad hoc committee for the Annandale Regional Planning Study.  The mission of the ad hoc study group was to examine overcrowding at the middle and high school levels, particularly Annandale High School, and review attendance area options for a new elementary school at the Lacey site in the Annandale area of the county.  Since the issues are two-fold, two separate study committees were formed 1) to focus on the high and middle schools and; 2) to focus on the elementary schools.

 

On January 10, 2011, both the elementary and middle/high school study groups presented their reports to the School Board.  The reports contain boundary solution options for both a new elementary school and for addressing overcrowding at Annandale High School.  Background information on the formation of these ad hoc committees, their reports and presentations to the School Board, and supporting staff materials may be found at http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/annandalestudy/index.htm.  The middle/high school planning group also included a series of non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.

 

On March 14, the School Board discussed the advantages and challenges associated with several non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The Board ultimately directed staff to return with a recommendation for a preferred non-boundary solution and a recommendation for a scope of boundary study for the new elementary school at the Lacey site and for a middle/high school scope to address the overcrowding at Annandale High School.  Staff was directed to incorporate the boundary and non-boundary approaches into a parallel process since both could offer advantages and challenges from many perspectives.  Additional information for a non-boundary solution to address overcrowding at Annandale High School is addressed in a separate agenda item, which includes a more fully developed matrix of the considerations previously discussed at the March 14, 2011, work session.  

 

The objectives of this regional study are twofold: 1) establish a boundary for a new elementary school on the Lacey site with the goal of improving enrollment balance in the region and; 2) reduce overcrowding at Annandale High School with the goal of improving enrollment balance in the region.  Boundary options for consideration will be developed pursuant to School Board Policy 8120 and in consideration of the issues expressed in the ad hoc committee reports.  The discussion below provides a recommended scope of study for boundary solutions, as directed. 

 

Discussion: Scope of Study – New elementary school at the Lacey site

 

The work of the Annandale Regional Planning Study ad hoc committee (elementary group) generally validated the scope of study and issues as initially identified by staff.  The elementary group is to be commended for taking the study to the next step with the provision of multiple scenarios for a new elementary school boundary.  Their work included the multiple variables associated with options for a K-5 and K-6 model, various feeder alignments and the resulting complications and constraints which arise with school capacities, road networks, appropriate bus and walking access, among other considerations.  Staff generally concurs with the pros and cons developed for these multiple scenarios, which are sufficiently developed to serve as a basis for several elementary school boundary options.  One of the boundary concepts developed by the ad hoc included a portion of Pine Spring Elementary, a school which was not included in the initial scope of study.  However, staff concurs with the committee’s assessment that Pine Spring should be included in the scope of the study.  In addition, staff recommends inclusion of North Springfield and Bren Mar Park elementaries since continued enrollment pressures at Bren Mar Park and projected capacity surplus at North Springfield should be reviewed as part of the overall goal of balancing enrollment in the region.  Therefore, staff recommends retaining the schools identified in the initial scope for the ad hoc study and the addition of Pine Spring, Bren Mar Park, and North Springfield Elementary Schools, as noted above, for a total of 11 elementary schools.

 

Scope of Study – New elementary school at Lacey site: Annandale Terrace, Beech Tree, Belvedere, Braddock, Bren Mar Park, Columbia, North Springfield, Pine Spring, Sleepy Hollow, Westlawn, and Woodburn Elementary Schools.

 

Note:  The additional capacity provided by the new elementary school will not fully address the elementary school capacity deficit in the region.  Additional recommendations for program and/or facilities modifications may also be included as part of the study.  Staff also proposes to retain the flexibility to consider boundary options for a K-5 and K-6 grade school model.  Middle and high school feeder alignments for the new elementary school boundary options will be coordinated with, and incorporated into, boundary change options to address overcrowding at the middle and high school level.  It should be further noted that implementation of the boundary changes is proposed to occur in fall 2012 to coincide with the opening of the elementary school at the Lacey site. 

 

Discussion: Scope of Study – Relieve overcrowding at Annandale High School

 

The work of the Annandale Regional Planning Study ad hoc committee (middle/high school group) also validated the scope of study and issues relating to overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The committee’s report included several boundary change options, which would rely on the utilization of surplus capacity to balance middle and high school enrollments in the region.  The committee is to be commended for the creative non-boundary approach to solving an overcrowding issue as well as the practical approach to the pros and cons associated with several options for boundary changes.  The following comments and conclusions presented in their report are particularly noteworthy:

 

  • That the K-5/K-6 configuration should be eliminated and all schools should move to a consistent elementary/middle school model in order to maximize flexibility to move students as enrollment patterns change;  
  • That efforts should be focused on uniting communities and eliminating split feeders at the elementary and middle school level;
  • That the Board consider options other than the traditional ‘boundary change’ that has been used in the past to relieve overcrowding;
  • That if a boundary solution is pursued, Twain and Key Middle Schools be included in the scope of study;
  • That transportation be considered carefully, especially when bussing across major interstate roads, which transect much of the study region;
  • That if a boundary solution is pursued, the Board consider implementing the boundary changes with rising 9th graders, thereby allowing all students who begin at Annandale High School the opportunity to finish.

 

The schools currently included in the regional study are the following: Annandale, Edison, Falls Church, Lee, Stuart, West Springfield, and Woodson High Schools; Lake Braddock Secondary School; Frost, Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, and Poe Middle Schools.  These schools are generally contiguous to Annandale High School and most have some capacity to contribute to overcrowding relief at Annandale High School.  Staff concurs with the ad hoc’s comment to include Twain and Key Middle Schools since these feeders to Edison and Lee High Schools would provide additional flexibility for boundary options. Including these two middle schools will allow for greater focus on the concern for split feeders in the region and both have some capacity to receive students.  It should be noted that any elementary school shifts away from Holmes or Poe to align feeders would necessitate adding a 6th grade to those schools.  In the absence of any specific directive on elementary school model, staff proposes to consider both K-5 and K-6 elementary model for the new elementary school and to review changes to existing schools, which allows flexibility to consider multiple feeder alignment options and take advantage of existing and future school capacities.  Staff also concurs with the concern for the major roadways and associated traffic, which may present significant obstacles to achieving transportation efficiencies with boundary changes.  None of the boundary scenarios reviewed by the ad hoc committee involved West Springfield High School, which does not contribute any capacity towards a solution.  In light of the forgoing discussion, staff would retain the schools initially identified in the scope of study except for West Springfield High School, and consider including Key and Twain Middle Schools for a total of six high schools, seven middle schools, and one secondary school, Lake Braddock, which includes both middle and high school grades. 

 

Scope of Study – Middle and High School level: Annandale, Edison, Falls Church, Lee, Stuart, and Woodson High Schools; Lake Braddock Secondary School; Frost, Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, Key, Poe, and Twain Middle Schools.

 

Note:  The middle and high school options proposed with the study will be coordinated with and include feeder options for the proposed new elementary school boundary options.  Staff will also coordinate closely with Transportation early in the process to obtain information related to bus times and distances associated with all proposed boundary options, including the grandfathering provisions as suggested by the ad hoc committee.  Additional recommendations for program and/or facilities modifications may also be included as part of the study.  It should be further noted that implementation for the proposed boundary study, if approved by the School Board, would begin in SY 2012. 

 

The attached maps depict the scope of study for the elementary school boundary study and for the middle/high school studies.  An update to the six-year projections is currently underway.  Staff believes the study should be based on the most recent data.  Information on school capacity and the SY 2015-16 building utilizations will be provided upon completion of projections and in advance of the scheduled April 28, 2011, School Board action on the scope of these studies. 

 

Options:

 

Modify the scope as outlined above.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board accept the scope of study for the inter-related elementary school study and middle and high school study in the Annandale region, as detailed in the agenda item.

 

That the School Board direct a parallel process to review both boundary and non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.

6.03 Annandale Regional Planning Study - Non-Boundary Solutions
Meeting
Apr 4, 2011 - All Day Work Sessions Nos. 95-100
Category
6. WS No. 99 Facilities - 3 p.m.
Type
Discussion

Staff Contact:              Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:     April 4, 2011 – Work Session No. 99

 

Subject:                        Annandale Regional Planning Study – Non-Boundary Solutions

 

School Board Action Required:   Discussion

 

Background:

 

On March 14, 2011, the School Board received a staff report and matrix which outlined the advantages and challenges associated with several non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The March work session continued the discussion of the Annandale Regional Planning Study Committee which presented their report to the Board in January 2011. Background information on the formation of this ad hoc study committee, their reports and presentations to the School Board and supporting staff materials may be found at http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/annandalestudy/index.htm.

 

The Board directed staff to return with a more detailed summary analysis of the non-boundary solutions and a recommended scope of two boundary studies; one for the new elementary school at the Lacey site and one to address the overcrowding at Annandale High School. The recommendations for the scope of the two studies are addressed in a separate agenda item.  Staff was further directed to develop a process which allows for a parallel track to consider and review the boundary and non-boundary approaches since both could offer advantages and challenges from many perspectives.  

 

Discussion: Non-Boundary Solutions

 

The non-boundary options presented by the Annandale Regional Planning Study Committee reflect a value on preserving the rich diversity and strong sense of belonging present in the community.  In addition, these options would maintain students within a single instructional cohort and reduce the impact of reassignment to multiple school locations generally associated with a boundary study.  While the non-boundary solutions offer some clear advantages, the challenges must be considered and balanced realistically within the context of competing needs for limited capital funding and the instructional implications of unique grade configurations or school models.  Pursuant to the request for continued review of the non-boundary solutions, staff offers a summary analysis of the multiple options based on the matrix and considerations for evaluation provided for the March 14, 2011, work session.  After discussion at this work session, the Board directed that staff discontinue consideration of options 1-C and 3 as neither option was considered viable.  The Board asked that staff continue to consider options 1-A, 1-B, and 2 and bring back to the Board staff’s recommendation for which of those options is most viable and which option should be further evaluated along with a boundary solution.

 

Option 1A –Reconfigure pyramid structure: Holmes to 6-7 and Poe to 8-9

Unlike the other non-boundary solutions, Option 1A proposes to retain the K-5 elementary school model and avoids the cost and capacity implications associated with inserting a 6th grade into the overcrowded elementaries that feed into the Stuart and Annandale pyramids.  Under this proposal, only two grade levels are maintained at Holmes and Poe and three grade levels, 10-12, would be maintained at Annandale High School.  Many of the same challenges previously identified with other non-boundary options would also arise but Option 1A places more moderate demands with respect to cost and time for implementation relative to other non-boundary options. 

 

  • Costs– Building modification to convert space at Holmes to accommodate the expanded 6th and 7th grades is estimated at approximately $400,000 with a resulting facility capacity of approximately 1200 students.  Holmes has adequate existing space for physical education, music and art; surplus technical education space could be subdivided for other uses. For Poe, building modification to relocate and convert classrooms to science labs and convert special instructional spaces to accommodate the 9th grade is estimated at approximately $2,500,000 with a resulting facility capacity of approximately 1200 students. These estimates assume a team model for grades 6 and 7 at Holmes and a departmental model for grades 8 and 9 at Poe.
  • Transportation– An estimated 12 additional busses would be needed with changes to bell schedules at Poe to allow 8th and 9th graders access to afterschool activities at the high school level.  Additional busses would be needed to shuttle students among these schools for access to some higher level classes. Many walkers would become bus riders and there could be impacts for Academy classes. 
  • Staffing - Significant human resource challenges would be created with respect to licensure, for K-6 vs. middle school education vs. secondary education endorsements.  However, it is noted that all human resource challenges are resolvable with some additional funding for staffing when non-traditional grade levels are merged.

 

There could be negative instructional implications resulting from increased transitions between multiple school buildings. Students would be required to move from 5th grade elementary school to Holmes for grades 6 and 7, to Poe for grades 8 and 9 and then to Annandale for grades 10 through 12.  Wakefield Forest students would make that transition at 7th and 8th grades, moving between two buildings in two years.  It should be further noted that this option would also create new unique school models with Glasgow remaining the only 6-8 middle school, Poe and Holmes becoming the only 7-9 junior high school and Annandale becoming the only 10-12 senior high school, further constraining future flexibility for boundary adjustments.

 

 From an advantage perspective, this option could address overcrowding at Annandale High School with more moderate facilities costs and would keep student cohorts together from grades 6 through 12 without adjusting boundaries, making Option 1A the most viable of the non-boundary solutions offered.  Should the Board desire to continue to explore a non-boundary solution to overcrowding at Annandale High School, staff believes that Option 1A offers the greatest potential and would merit further analysis and review concurrently with a traditional boundary study.  Updates to the matrix previously provided will be forthcoming, to include the latest projection data once it is available.

 

Option 1B – Reconfigure pyramid structure, Holmes and Poe to a 7-9 middle school

This option is a variation of other options in that some 6th grades are added to elementary schools to create space at Holmes and Poe, which in turn become grades 7-9 and Annandale High School becomes grades 10-12. This option would immediately create space in Annandale high school.  Similar to Option 1A, many of the same challenges to implementation would arise plus some new challenges associated with the return of 9th grade to a middle or junior high school model.  This option also creates the same unique school configuration issues within the district as noted in Option 1A above which further constrains the Board to make future boundary adjustments to balance enrollments among these schools.

 

  • Cost – Preliminary estimates to return 6th grade back to the base elementary school in order to create space at Holmes and Poe is conservatively estimated at over $12 million dollars.  Additional costs to accommodate necessary modifications (science labs and other required instructional spaces) at Poe and Holmes are conservatively estimated at $4.5 to $5 million for each school. Similar equipments cost would be incurred as described in Option 2 with respect to music, theatre, uniforms, graphic arts and photography labs. 
  • Timing – In addition to the timing needed to create capacity at the elementary level to implement the grade changes proposed, additional time for planning and construction will be required to prepare space at the middle schools to accommodate the 9th grade. This option would not allow for any enrollment relief for Annandale in a time for the targeted 2012-13 school year.
  • Capacity Imbalances – In addition to the capacity issues at the elementary level discussed above, it is estimated that Poe would be at 100% of capacity and Holmes at 75% of capacity with this option.
  • Staffing– Significant human resource challenges would be created with respect to licensure, for K-6 vs. middle school education vs. secondary education endorsements.
  • Transportation - Providing transportation for 9th graders in order to access high school after school curricular activities would be required as well as mid-day busses to shuttle students for access to higher level classes.  Bell conflicts have been identified and there could be impacts on Academy classes. Additional busses would likely be required at the middle and high school level and some walkers would become bus riders.             

 

Based on the cost and timing factors and the fact that creating these unique grade model configurations has not proven to be efficient or to have enhanced instructional benefits inherent to the structure, staff concludes that this option is not viable and that further study of this option is not warranted from an instructional or facilities perspective.

 

Option 2 – Create a small secondary school at Holmes Middle School

This option is instructionally viable as evidenced by the other highly successful secondary schools in the district.   The proposal to create a 7-12 school with a focus on world languages and/or other magnet type academic focus (AP/IB/Arts and Humanities) is laudable.  However, multiple challenges were identified with this option:

 

  • Timing– this option will require several years to plan and construct – Annandale High School requires immediate enrollment relief beginning no later than the 2012 school year.
  • Costs– Design and Construction has provided preliminary conservative estimates for the physical modifications needed to accommodate the growth and the requirements for a secondary school - $22,000,000.  This includes a minimum 90,000 square foot building addition for the construction of a wing for fine arts, science, physical education, and other electives as well as expansions to the administrative suite, cafeteria and serving area, and media center.  Exterior improvements to accommodate additional required parking, storm water management, and bus access could cost approximately $2,000,000.  Additional costs have been identified from instructional services to purchase and/or develop spaces and resources for computer graphics and photography labs, theatre (auditorium and black box) guitar and jazz ensemble inventories, uniform costs (concert and marching band, orchestra and chorus) and additional science and lab equipment.  Preliminary estimates far exceed $1,000,000 and could ranger significantly higher since the construction costs associated with building a black box theatre and expanding and equipping the science labs are presently unknown.  
  • Elementary Capacity– This option also assumes 6th grade returns to the elementary school buildings at a time when the majority of K-5 elementary schools are at or over capacity.  Building additional capacity into the elementary schools where it could be accommodated would add significant costs to the proposal as well as exacerbate the timing concerns for implementation. Moreover, boundary changes at all school levels would likely be required. While the opening of a new elementary school at Lacey will provide some additional capacity, the impact of this additional capacity will not be known until the elementary boundary study concludes.
  • Staffing– Significant human resource challenges would be created with respect to licensure (K-6 vs. Middle School Education) and to recruitment of teachers in world languages which is a highly critical field.

 

There are some instructional advantages with this concept as provided on the matrix, most notably, the Title I resources and funding for 6th grade teachers and students which are not otherwise available at the middle school level.  However, staff concludes that further study of this option is not advisable at this time.  The creation of a secondary magnet school under this option could be considered as part of a long term facilities planning initiative but the implementation of any of the other non-boundary solutions offered would likely foreclose on this potential opportunity. 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board direct staff to further explore with the community Non-boundary Option 1A as part of a regional study to alleviate overcrowding at Annandale High School, to be undertaken concurrently with a traditional boundary study.
