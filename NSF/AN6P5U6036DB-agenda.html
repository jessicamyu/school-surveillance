Teacher Appreciation Week - May 7-11, 2018

 

Teachers are real life superheroes.  They educate, innovate, encourage, and support, touching the lives of millions of children.  Every day, over 15,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potential for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

 

 

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2018

 

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

Since 1992, the President of the United States has proclaimed May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

 

 

School Nurse Day - May 9, 2018

School Nurse Day was established in 1972 to foster a better understanding of the role of school nurses in the educational setting.  This annual event recognizes the contributions that school nurses make every day to improve the health and success of our nation’s children.  This year’s theme, “School Nurses:  Better Health. Better Learning,” reflects the significant roles that school nurses have in health care in their school communities, as well as in modeling health and resiliency.

School nursing is a specialized practice of professional nursing that advances the health and well-being of students and is an integral part of a quality school health program. School nurses promote health, safety, prevention, and wellness; intervene with actual and potential health problems; and provide case management services.  They are an invaluable bridge connecting health care providers, public health, and families.

The school system works closely with the Fairfax County Health Department to provide quality services to the children of Fairfax County Public Schools and recognized May 9 as School Nurse Day in Fairfax County.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2018

 

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month every year in May. The theme this year is “Communication for All” This annual event provides opportunities to promote services that can improve the quality of life for those who experience problems with speaking and listening. An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families. 

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to more than 10,000 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, via base school itinerant services, and in early childhood programs. FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

School Nutrition Employee Week, May 7-11, 2018

 

Caring. Trained. Thoughtful. Professional. Dedicated.  These are just a few words that describe today's school nutrition professional.

 

School nutrition employees in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate daily their professional commitment to providing students with nutritious menu choices that reflect current research and meet the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. School nutrition employees must balance many roles and follow numerous federal, state and local regulations to ensure safe and healthy meals are available in schools. They are trained sanitation and food safety experts and must manage financially self-sufficient programs. School nutrition professionals also provide nutrition education to students, as well as healthy catering services to their communities. They use their creativity to make the cafeteria a fun and welcoming place all year long and perform their jobs each day because they care passionately about the children they serve.

 

FCPS school nutrition employees are committed to imparting food and nutrition knowledge and skills to students, using the school cafeteria as the nutrition laboratory, and are influential in preparing students to learn.

Subject: Strategic Plan Goal 1: Student Success

 

Staff Contact: Sloan Presidio, assistant superintendent, Instructional Services Department

 

Other Staff Present: Francisco Durán, chief academic and equity officer

 

Meeting Category: April 26, 2018 – Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Ignite Link: Strategic Plan Goal 1: Student Success

 

Summary/Background:

As outlined in the Strategic Plan Goal 1: Student Success, FCPS is committed to reaching, challenging, and preparing every student for success in school and life. This report highlights successes as well as important challenges facing the school system, including continued gaps in opportunity, access, and achievement among student sub-groups.

The report format includes separate chapters for each of the four strategies under Goal 1. Each chapter includes a strategy introduction, the related desired outcomes, student achievement data, and analysis to highlight the associated successes and challenges. The report’s appendix includes additional details on the division’s student achievement results.

 

Overarching Strategy 1: Enhance instructional practices to ensure that all students receive an education in a dynamic environment designed to foster life-long learning and support them in achieving their full potential.

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • The curriculum will reflect POG outcomes in all content areas, where students are appropriately challenged

  • Achievement gaps will be eliminated

  • Teachers, students, and parents will have access to contemporary and effective technology resources

  • All students will demonstrate digital citizenship skills

  • Centralized Support will be available for schools and school staff based on student achievement needs

     

Highlights

  • Gains in reading and mathematics SOL pass rates across all subgroups over multi-year timeframe

  • Increase in number of English Learners earning high school credits

  • Increase in available student, teacher, and parent resources to support Digital Citizenship

  • Increase in number of FCPS schools that are fully accredited

     

    Challenges

  • Closing gaps in opportunity, access, and achievement among student sub-groups

  • Providing equitable access to technology

     

    Overarching Strategy 2: All students will achieve their full potential through the use of assessment and data systems for decision making that support student attainment of the outcomes defined by the Portrait of a Graduate

     

    Desired Outcomes:

     

  • A fully-balanced assessment system will reflect POG outcomes in all content areas

  • Data tools to monitor progress

  • Grading will be an accurate reflection of learning

Highlights

  • Increased use of performance-based assessment for measuring content knowledge and Portrait of a Graduate skills

  • Expanded pilot of Capstone Experiences

  • Increased availability of data tools to teachers and school-based administrators

Challenges

  • Need for state policy flexibility for use of performance-based assessment to meet state accountability requirements

  • Supporting teacher data literacy skills to enhance data-based decision-making practices

 

Overarching Strategy 3: Provide quality Early Childhood experiences aligned with Portrait of a Graduate outcomes that are designed to prepare students to successfully enter Kindergarten.

 

Desired Outcomes:

 

  • All qualifying future FCPS students will have access to high quality early education programs

  • All agencies will coordinate their efforts to provide similar early childhood experiences

Highlights

  • Students that participate in PreK experiences out-perform students that do not have a PreK experience

  • Increased PreK opportunities for preschoolers and infants and toddlers in FCPS and County

  • Increased access to professional learning for early childhood community educators

  • Development of the Fairfax County’s Equitable School Readiness Strategic Plan

Challenges

  • Limited number of PreK opportunities for qualifying students

  • Neediest communities have higher numbers of students entering without a PreK experience

 

Overarching Strategy 4: Provide students with relevant opportunities to explore options and prepare for College and Career

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • A portfolio of school options will provide multiple pathways to a high school diploma

  • Students will be fluent in two or more languages

Highlights

  • Expansion of high school pathways that include coursework leading to in demand career preparation

  • Increased dual enrollment offerings

  • Increased “resume ready” credentials

  • Increased work-based learning experiences

Challenges

  • Division accountability systems for college and career indicators

  • Expansion of work-based learning opportunities

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the Strategic Plan Goal 1: Student Success report as detailed in the agenda item.

Subject: Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2018-2019

 

Staff Contact: Teresa Johnson, assistant superintendent, Special Services

 

Other Staff Present: Lea Skurpski, director, DSS

                                 Jackie Prentice, finance coordinator, DSS

 

Meeting Category: April 26, 2018 - Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Ignite Link: Goal 1: Student Success

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report for the 2018-2019 school year, as required by the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) Superintendent's memo #341-17 dated December 15, 2017. This report will be used by VDOE in preparing the state plan for special education. As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-prescribed abbreviated format on a date, no later than May 11, 2018.

The 2018-2019 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $34,895,733. This amount is equal to the final 2017–2018 award as stated in Superintendent's memo #302-17, dated October 20, 2017. Estimated expenditures include costs associated with the voluntary 15 percent set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS); a mandatory proportionate set-aside, which ensures that FCPS spends a proportionate share of funds on special education services for parentally-placed private school children with disabilities; materials/supplies; and state restricted indirect costs of 1.3%. The IDEA Section 611, Part B, award will fund a total of 404.4 FTE positions.

The 2018-2019 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $774,964. This amount is equal to the final 2017-2018 award as stated in Superintendent's memo #214-17, dated July 21, 2017. These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for school-age students. Estimated expenditures include costs associated with state restricted indirect costs of 1.3%. IDEA Section 619 (Preschool) funds 6.0 FTE positions.

The revenue amounts above reflect no change from the 2017-2018 awards. 2018-2019 application reflects estimated costs for the current programs funded in the 2017-2018 application. Small adjustments in both sections 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, materials/supplies, and the state restricted indirect cost rate.

The 2018-2019 Annual Plan was presented to the Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) at the March 14, 2018, meeting.

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the 2018-2019 Annual Special Education plan, Section 611, Part B grant funding application and Section 619, Part B preschool grant funding application, as detailed in the agenda item.

7.03 R2601.31P Student Rights and Responsibilities [DSS; WS 5/14/18; Action 6/14/18; adopt Regulation 2601.32P, Student Rights and Responsibilities, as detailed in the agenda item. ]

 Subject:  Regulation 2601, Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R) approval

 

Staff Contact:  Teresa Johnson, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services 

 

Other Staff Present:  Mary Ann Panarelli, director, Office of Intervention and Prevention Services, DSS

                                  Stefan Mascoll, coordinator, Student Safety and Wellness Office, DSS

 

Meeting Category:  April 26, 2018 - Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Ignite Link:  Goal 2: Caring Culture

 

Problem Statement:

 

Regulation 2601, Student Rights and Responsibilities, needs to be updated to reflect legislative changes, to clarify items, and to address concerns raised by principals and the community.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

A.  Chapter I.A.1., removed sentence defining Justifiable Absence.

 

B.  Chapter I.A.2., added definition of Chronic Absence.

 

C.  Chapter I.A.3., changed definition of Unexcused Absences.

 

D.  Chapter I.A.4., updated consequences of Five or More Unexcused Days of Absences.

 

E.  Chapter I.D., added “Social Media” parental guidance.

 

F.  Chapter I.J.1., removed “director, Office of Equity and Employee Relations”, and replaced with “Family and Student Ombudsman.”

 

G.  Chapter I.K.2., updated to refer discrimination issues to the Family and Student Ombudsman.

 

H.  Chapter II.A., added footnote stating “Students who are in kindergarten through third grade ordinarily are not subject to suspensions from school of more than three days. Virginia law provides for exceptions where such students engage in dangerous misconduct. (See chapter II.C.3 o this regulation).”

 

I.  Chapter II.A.2.a.12., Added "(12)Possession or use of a smoking or vaping device (e.g. a vape, a juul, or a like device). To the extent the foregoing contains alcohol, nicotine, or an illegal drug, specific consequences are listed below in this regulation." to forms of Disruptive or Inappropriate Behavior.

 

J.  Chapter II.A.3.a., clarified the term Smoking Device.

 

K.  Chapter II.C.3., added section regarding Suspension of Students in Grades K-3.

 

L.  Chapter II.C.4.d., added parenthetical "without a referral to the Division Superintendent or recommendation for reassignment.”

 

M.  Chapter II.C.4.d., II.C.7.b., added footnote stating “Any appeal of short-term suspension in conjunction with a referral to the Division Superintendent or recommendation for reassignment shall be directed to the Division Superintendent.”

 

N.  Definition of SR&R Terms Used Throughout Regulation, added definition of “Aggravating Circumstances”.

 

O.  Definition of SR&R Terms Used Throughout Regulation, updated definition of “Long-Term Suspension”.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt Regulation 2601.32P, Student Rights and Responsibilities, as detailed in the agenda item.
