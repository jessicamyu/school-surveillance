National Volunteer Week - April 10-16, 2011

 

Established in 1974, National Volunteer Week has grown exponentially in scope each year since, drawing the support and endorsement of all subsequent U.S. presidents, governors, mayors and other respected elected officials.

 

National Volunteer Week is about inspiring, recognizing, and encouraging people to seek out imaginative ways to engage in their communities. It’s about demonstrating to the nation that by working together, in unison, we have the fortitude to meet our challenges and accomplish our goals. National Volunteer Week is about taking action, encouraging individuals and their respective communities to be at the center of social change—discovering and actively demonstrating their collective power to foster positive transformation.

 

During the 2009-2010 school year,163 schools, centers, and alternative high schools reported that 42,366 volunteers donated 535,274 hours of volunteer service to FCPS schools.  If our volunteers had been paid at the entry-level instructional assistant salary of $13.95 per hour, the bill for 2009-2010 alone would have totaled almost $7.5 million dollars. The educational value of the work performed by our volunteers is, of course, incalculable.

RESOLUTION
NAMING THE LIBRARY AT
WILLIAM HALLEY ELEMENTARY SCHOOL
“THE JANET K. FUNK LIBRARY”

 

WHEREAS, Janet K. Funk has provided outstanding leadership and service to the William Halley Elementary School community by serving as its principal since it opened in September 1995 until her retirement in June 2010; and

 

WHEREAS, Mrs. Funk performed with distinction and merit throughout her 30 years with Fairfax County Public Schools, earning recognition as the 1998-1999 Principal of the Year and serving as president of Phi Delta Kappa (Northern Virginia Chapter) from 1993 to 1994 and as president of the Fairfax Association of Elementary School Principals from 2000 to 2001; and

 

WHEREAS, her strengths as a leader and a life-long learner have led to creating and supporting a welcoming school culture that nurtures learning in children and adults, using collaboration and research-based best practices as its foundation; and

 

WHEREAS, Mrs. Funk has consistently encouraged others to research and develop their own talents and strengths, and her leadership at Halley and in Fairfax County Public Schools has made a profound difference in the lives of many staff members, children, and their families; and

 

WHEREAS, she is known for her tireless work to create and support a professional learning community focused on student achievement; and she has left an impressive legacy of excellence in academics and in character development;

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board names the library at William Halley Elementary School “The Janet K. Funk Library” in recognition of the innumerable hours Mrs. Funk has contributed to Halley’s students, parents, staff, and surrounding community, and for her leadership, integrity, hope, love of learning, and love of children—traits for which she has come to be known.

Staff Contact:             Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:     April 14, 2011 – Regular Meeting No. 17

 

Subject:                       Scope of Study:

                                        Elementary School Boundary Solutions - Lacey Site

                                        Middle/High School Boundary Solutions - Annandale High School Overcrowding   

 

School Board Action Required:   Information

 

Background:

 

On April 29, 2010, the School Board authorized the formation of an ad hoc committee for the Annandale Regional Planning Study.  The mission of the ad hoc study group was to examine overcrowding at the middle and high school levels, particularly Annandale High School, and review attendance area options for a new elementary school at the Lacey site in the Annandale area of the county.  Since the issues are two-fold, two separate study committees were formed 1) to focus on the high and middle schools and; 2) to focus on the elementary schools.

 

On January 10, 2011, both the elementary and middle/high school study groups presented their reports to the School Board.  The reports contain boundary solution options for both a new elementary school and for addressing overcrowding at Annandale High School.  Background information on the formation of these ad hoc committees, their reports and presentations to the School Board, and supporting staff materials may be found at http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/annandalestudy/index.htm.  The middle/high school planning group also included a series of non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.

 

On March 14, 2011, at its work session, the School Board discussed the advantages and challenges associated with several non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The Board ultimately directed staff to return with a recommendation for a preferred non-boundary solution and a recommendation for the scope of a boundary study for the new elementary school at the Lacey site and for a middle/high school scope to address the overcrowding at Annandale High School. 

 

At the work session on April 4, 2011, the School Board discussed and generally reaffirmed the objectives of this regional study to 1) establish a boundary for a new elementary school on the Lacey site with the goal of improving enrollment balance in the region and; 2) reduce overcrowding at Annandale High School with the goal of improving enrollment balances in the region.  Boundary options for consideration will be developed pursuant to School Board Policy 8130 and in consideration of the issues expressed in the ad hoc committee reports.   Further, the School Board discussed the recommended scope of study and unanimously concurred with staff’s recommendation to retain the elementary schools initially identified in the scope of study with the addition of Pine Spring, Bren Mar Park, and North Springfield Elementary Schools.  The Board further concurred with the middle/high school scope of study, with the addition of Key and Twain Middle Schools and exclusion of West Springfield High School.

 

Discussion: Scope of Study – New elementary school at the Lacey site

 

The work of the Annandale Regional Planning Study ad hoc committee (elementary group) generally validated the scope of study and issues as initially identified by staff.  The elementary group is to be commended for taking the study to the next step with the provision of multiple scenarios for a new elementary school boundary.  Their work included the multiple variables associated with options for a K-5 and K-6 model, various feeder alignments and the resulting complications and constraints which arise with school capacities, road networks, appropriate bus and walking access, among other considerations.  Staff generally concurs with the pros and cons developed for these multiple scenarios, which are sufficiently developed to serve as a basis for several elementary school boundary options.  One of the boundary concepts developed by the ad hoc committee included a portion of Pine Spring Elementary, a school which was not included in the initial scope of study.  However, staff concurs with the committee’s assessment that Pine Spring should be included in the scope of the study.  In addition, staff recommends inclusion of North Springfield and Bren Mar Park Elementaries since continued enrollment pressures at Bren Mar Park and projected capacity surplus at North Springfield should be reviewed as part of the overall goal of balancing enrollment in the region.  Therefore, staff recommends retaining the schools identified in the initial scope for the ad hoc study and the addition of Pine Spring, Bren Mar Park, and North Springfield Elementary Schools, as noted above, for a total of 11 elementary schools.

 

Scope of Study – New elementary school at Lacey site: Annandale Terrace, Beech Tree, Belvedere, Braddock, Bren Mar Park, Columbia, North Springfield, Pine Spring, Sleepy Hollow, Westlawn, and Woodburn Elementary Schools.

 

Note:  The additional capacity provided by the new elementary school will not fully address the elementary school capacity deficit in the region.  Additional recommendations for program and/or facilities modifications may also be included as part of the study.  Staff also proposes to retain the flexibility to consider boundary options for a K-5 and K-6 grade school model.  Middle and high school feeder alignments for the new elementary school boundary options will be coordinated with, and incorporated into, boundary change options to address overcrowding at the middle and high school level.  It should be further noted that implementation of the boundary changes is proposed to occur in fall 2012 to coincide with the opening of the elementary school at the Lacey site. 

 

Discussion: Scope of Study – Relieve overcrowding at Annandale High School

 

The work of the Annandale Regional Planning Study ad hoc committee (middle/high school group) also validated the scope of study and issues relating to overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The committee’s report included several boundary change options, which would rely on the utilization of surplus capacity to balance middle and high school enrollments in the region.  The committee is to be commended for the creative non-boundary approach to solving an overcrowding issue as well as the practical approach to the pros and cons associated with several options for boundary changes.  The following comments and conclusions presented in the committee’s report are particularly noteworthy:

 

·         That the K-5/K-6 configuration should be eliminated and all schools should move to a consistent elementary/middle school model in order to maximize flexibility to move students as

            enrollment patterns change;  

·         That efforts should be focused on uniting communities and eliminating split feeders at the elementary and middle school level;

·         That the Board consider options other than the traditional ‘boundary change’ that has been used in the past to relieve overcrowding;

·         That if a boundary solution is pursued, Twain and Key Middle Schools be included in the scope of study;

·         That transportation be considered carefully, especially when bussing across major interstate roads, which transect much of the study region;

·         That if a boundary solution is pursued, the Board consider implementing the boundary changes with rising 9th graders, thereby allowing all students who begin at Annandale High

            School the opportunity to finish.

 

The schools currently included in the regional study are the following: Annandale, Edison, Falls Church, Lee, Stuart, West Springfield, and Woodson High Schools; Lake Braddock Secondary School; Frost, Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, and Poe Middle Schools.  These schools are generally contiguous to Annandale High School and most have some capacity to contribute to overcrowding relief at Annandale High School.  Staff concurs with the ad hoc committee’s comment to include Twain and Key Middle Schools since these feeders to Edison and Lee High Schools would provide additional flexibility for boundary options. Including these two middle schools will allow for greater focus on the concern for split feeders in the region and both have some capacity to receive students.  It should be noted that any elementary school shifts away from Holmes or Poe to align feeders would necessitate adding a 6th grade to those schools.  In the absence of any specific directive on elementary school model, staff proposes to consider both K-5 and K-6 elementary model for the new elementary school and to review changes to existing schools, which allows flexibility to consider multiple feeder alignment options and take advantage of existing and future school capacities.  Staff also acknowledges the concern for the major roadways and associated traffic, which may present significant obstacles to achieving transportation efficiencies with boundary changes.  None of the boundary scenarios reviewed by the ad hoc committee involved West Springfield High School, which does not contribute any capacity towards a solution.  In light of the forgoing discussion, staff would retain the schools initially identified in the scope of study except for West Springfield High School, and consider including Key and Twain Middle Schools for a total of six high schools, seven middle schools, and one secondary school, Lake Braddock, which includes both middle and high school grades. 

 

Scope of Study – Middle and High School level: Annandale, Edison, Falls Church, Lee, Stuart, and Woodson High Schools; Lake Braddock Secondary School; Frost, Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, Key, Poe, and Twain Middle Schools.

 

Note:  The middle and high school options proposed with the study will be coordinated with and include feeder options for the proposed new elementary school boundary options.  Staff will also coordinate closely with Transportation early in the process to obtain information related to bus times and distances associated with all proposed boundary options, including the grandfathering provisions as suggested by the ad hoc committee.  Additional recommendations for program and/or facilities modifications may also be included as part of the study.  It should be further noted that implementation for the proposed boundary study, if approved by the School Board, would begin in SY 2012. 

 

The attached maps depict the scope of study for the elementary school boundary study and for the middle/high school studies.  An update to the six-year projections is currently underway.  Staff believes the study should be based on the most recent data.  Information on school capacity and the SY 2015-16 building utilizations will be provided upon completion of projections and in advance of the scheduled April 28, 2011, School Board action on the scope of these studies. 

 

Options: Modify the scope of study outlined above

 

           

RECOMMENDATIONS:

 

1.    That the School Board adopt the following criteria for the boundary studies for the new elementary school at the Lacey site and to relieve overcrowding at Annandale High School to the extent feasible and practicable: help balance and accommodate current and projected enrollments; to have geographic proximity; to provide socio-economic and ESOL balance; to adhere to core capacity; to respect man-made and natural barriers; to minimize split neighborhoods and split feeder assignments; to maximize program stability; and to minimize additional capital/operating costs; to consider not only these criteria, but also any other factors that will contribute to the improved operational efficiency and instructional effectiveness of the school system, pursuant to Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments and School Closings.

2.    That the School Board direct the Superintendent to conduct a boundary study to include: Annandale Terrace, Beech Tree, Belvedere, Braddock, Bren Mar Park, Columbia, North Springfield, Pine Spring, Sleepy Hollow, Westlawn, and Woodburn Elementary Schools in order to establish an attendance area for a new elementary school at the Lacey site, pursuant to Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments and School Closings.

 

3.    That the School Board accept the scope and objectives of the middle and high school study in the Annandale region and further direct the Superintendent to conduct a study to include:  Annandale, Edison, Falls Church, Lee, Stuart and Woodson High Schools; Lake Braddock Secondary School; Frost, Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, Key, Poe, and Twain Middle Schools, pursuant to Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments and School Closings.

6.02 Annandale Regional Planning Study - Non-Boundary Solutions - Recommendation to direct staff to further explore with the community Non-boundary Option 1A as part of a regional study to alleviate overcrowding at Annandale High School, to be undertaken concurrently with a traditional boundary study (FTS; work session held 3/14/11; action scheduled 4/28/11)

[Posted 4/8/11]

 

Staff Contact:              Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:      April 14, 2011 – Regular Meeting No. 17

 

Subject:                        Annandale Regional Planning Study – Non-Boundary Solutions

 

School Board Action Required:   Information

 

Background:

 

On March 14, 2011, the School Board received a staff report and matrix which outlined the advantages and challenges associated with several non-boundary solutions to address overcrowding at Annandale High School.  The March work session continued the discussion of the Annandale Regional Planning Study Committee, which had presented its report to the Board in January 2011. Background information on the formation of this ad hoc study committee, its reports and presentations to the School Board and supporting staff materials may be found at http://www.fcps.edu/fts/planning/annandalestudy/index.htm.

 

The Board directed staff to return with a more detailed summary analysis of the non-boundary solutions and a recommended scope of two boundary studies: one for the new elementary school at the Lacey site and one to address the overcrowding at Annandale High School. The recommendations for the scope of the two studies are addressed in a separate agenda item.  Staff was further directed to develop a process which allows for a parallel track to consider and review the boundary and non-boundary approaches since both could offer advantages and challenges from many perspectives.

 

At the April 4, 2011, work session, staff summarized the analysis of the non-boundary solutions as detailed below. The Board generally concurred with staff analysis and directed further study of Option 1A.

 

Discussion: Non-Boundary Solutions

 

The non-boundary options presented by the Annandale Regional Planning Study Committee reflect a value on preserving the rich diversity and strong sense of belonging present in the community.  In addition, these options would maintain students within a single instructional cohort and reduce the impact of reassignment to multiple school locations generally associated with a boundary study.  While the non-boundary solutions offer some clear advantages, the challenges must be considered and balanced realistically within the context of competing needs for limited capital funding and the instructional implications of unique grade configurations or school models.  Pursuant to the request for continued review of the non-boundary solutions, staff offers a summary analysis of the multiple options based on the matrix and considerations for evaluation provided for the March 14, 2011, work session.  After discussion at this work session, the Board directed that staff discontinue consideration of options 1-C and 3 as neither option was considered viable.  The Board asked that staff continue to consider options 1-A, 1-B, and 2 and bring back to the Board staff’s recommendation for which of those options is most viable and which option should be further evaluated along with a boundary solution.

 

Option 1A –Reconfigure pyramid structure: Holmes to 6-7 and Poe to 8-9

Unlike the other non-boundary solutions, Option 1A proposes to retain the K-5 elementary school model and avoids the cost and capacity implications associated with inserting a 6th grade into the overcrowded elementaries that feed into the Stuart and Annandale pyramids.  Under this proposal, only two grade levels are maintained at Holmes and Poe and three grade levels, 10-12, would be maintained at Annandale High School.  Many of the same challenges previously identified with other non-boundary options would also arise but Option 1A places more moderate demands with respect to cost and time for implementation relative to other non-boundary options. 

 

  • Costs– Building modification to convert space at Holmes to accommodate the expanded 6th and 7th grades is estimated at approximately $400,000 with a resulting facility capacity of approximately 1200 students.  Holmes has adequate existing space for physical education, music and art; surplus technical education space could be subdivided for other uses. For Poe, building modification to relocate and convert classrooms to science labs and convert special instructional spaces to accommodate the 9th grade is estimated at approximately $2,500,000 with a resulting facility capacity of approximately 1200 students. These estimates assume a team model for grades 6 and 7 at Holmes and a departmental model for grades 8 and 9 at Poe.
  • Transportation– An estimated 12 additional busses would be needed with changes to bell schedules at Poe to allow 8th and 9th graders access to afterschool activities at the high school level.  Additional busses would be needed to shuttle students among these schools for access to some higher level classes. Many walkers would become bus riders and there could be impacts for Academy classes. 
  • Staffing - Significant human resource challenges would be created with respect to licensure, for K-6 vs. middle school education vs. secondary education endorsements.  However, it is noted that all human resource challenges are resolvable with some additional funding for staffing when non-traditional grade levels are merged.

 

There could be negative instructional implications resulting from increased transitions between multiple school buildings. Students would be required to move from 5th grade elementary school to Holmes for grades 6 and 7, to Poe for grades 8 and 9 and then to Annandale for grades 10 through 12.  Wakefield Forest students would make that transition at 7th and 8th grades, moving between two buildings in two years.  It should be further noted that this option would also create new unique school models with Glasgow remaining the only 6-8 middle school, Poe and Holmes becoming the only 7-9 junior high school and Annandale becoming the only 10-12 senior high school, further constraining future flexibility for boundary adjustments.

 

 From an advantage perspective, this option could address overcrowding at Annandale High School with more moderate facilities costs and would keep student cohorts together from grades 6 through 12 without adjusting boundaries, making Option 1A the most viable of the non-boundary solutions offered.  Should the Board desire to continue to explore a non-boundary solution to overcrowding at Annandale High School, staff believes that Option 1A offers the greatest potential and would merit further analysis and review concurrently with a traditional boundary study.  Updates to the matrix previously provided will be forthcoming, to include the latest projection data once it is available.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board direct staff to further explore with the community Non-boundary Option 1A as part of a regional study to alleviate overcrowding at Annandale High School, to be undertaken concurrently with a traditional boundary study.

6.04 Proposed 2011 School Bond Referendum - Recommendation to adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 8, 2011, general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $252,750,000 (FTS; action scheduled 4/28/11)

Category
6. New Business

[REVISED - 4/11/11]

 

Staff Contact:               Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:      April 14, 2011 - Regular Meeting No. 17

 

Subject:                         Proposed 2011 School Bond Referendum

 

School Board Action Required:     Information

 

Background:

 

On January 20, 2011, the School Board adopted the FY 2012-2016 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) that identified school facility needs anticipated during the coming five-year period. Capital improvement programming provides the foundation for cost-effective investment and construction project management of facility needs identified throughout the planning period. Voter authorization to issue bonds is needed in November 2011 to provide construction funds for renovation for one high school, one middle school and six elementary schools, planning funds for renovations for two high schools, one middle school and 14 elementary schools, and funding for modular relocations.  Capital funding continues to be required for essential infrastructure, such as roof and HVAC replacements, athletic infrastructure, asphalt paving, security enhancements, and upgrades to facilities to meet Americans with Disabilities (ADA) requirements.

 

Key Points:

 

The adopted FY 2012-16 CIP identified substantial costs savings which have been achieved over the past several years as the result of favorable market conditions.  The application of approximately $29,000,000 in cost savings to other CIP projects has allowed renovation planning and construction for 11 projects to begin sooner, ultimately reducing the necessary funding request for the 2011 Bond.  It should be further noted that renovations continue to include additional capacity to address the student membership which continues to grow in many schools.  Given that the Fairfax County Executive has recommended that the allocation of $155 million per year for FCPS capital expenditures become permanent, and given the substantial costs savings that have been occurring (but will not continue indefinitely) a school bond in the amount of $252,750,000 is requested as outlined in Attachment B. 

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 8, 2011, general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $252,750,000.
