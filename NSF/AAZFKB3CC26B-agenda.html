African American History Month - February 2017

 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." The commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

 

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.  This year’s theme—The crisis in Black Education—focuses on the crucial role of education in the history of African Americans.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2017

Career and Technical Education or CTE month is a public awareness campaign held every February to celebrate CTE and the achievements and accomplishments of CTE programs across the country.  This year's theme, "Celebrate Today, Own Tomorrow!" raises awareness of the innovation and excellence that exists in our CTE programs as well as the crucial role that CTE plays in readying our students for careers and our nation for economic success. 

 

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (programming , business management, information technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (sales, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

National School Counseling Week - February 6-10, 2017

 

National School Counseling Week 2017, "School Counseling: Helping Students Realize their potential" will be celebrated from February 6-10, to focus public attention on the unique contribution of school counselors within U.S. school systems.  National School Counseling Week, sponsored by ASCA, highlights the tremendous impact school counselors can have in helping students achieve school success and plan for a career.  National School Counseling Week is always celebrated the first full week in February.

 

Fairfax County Public Schools employs over 600 school counseling professionals, including school counselors, directors of student services, and career center specialists.  These professionals are committed to preparing students to become lifelong learners and productive members in a global society.

Approve the FY 2018-2022 Capital Improvement Program.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                      Revised 1/24/17

 

Staff Contact:  Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

       Other Staff Present: Susan Quinn, chief operating officer

                                         Kevin Sneed, special projects administrator, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category: January 26, 2017 – Regular Meeting

 

Subject: FY 2018-2022 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required:   Information

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2018-2022 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five-year student enrollment projections and capacity calculations, as well as the Capital Construction Cash Flow which identifies plans for new construction, capacity enhancements, renovations, and site acquisition to accommodate future capacity needs.

 

Discussion:

 

Fairfax County Public Schools continues to face a challenging situation whereby student enrollment in certain locations is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities, provide capacity enhancement projects and it will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions and boundary adjustments to accommodate growing enrollment needs.

 

New School Construction: The CIP proposes three new elementary schools: one in the Richmond Highway Corridor (Route 1 area); one in the North West County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and the Dulles Airport access road; and one to relieve overcrowding in the Fairfax/Oakton area. It also proposes construction of a new high school in the Western end of the Silver Line metro vicinity to alleviate capacity concerns at multiple high schools.   

 

Capacity Enhancements: Funding for additions at South Lakes High School, West Potomac High School, Stuart High School, and Madison High School are proposed. Funds are also proposed for the relocation of four modular additions.

 

Renovations: Funding for renovations at five named high schools and one unnamed high school are proposed in the CIP.  Additionally, funds for renovations at 21 named elementary schools and 11 unnamed elementary schools are included. Lastly, funds for the renovation of four named middle schools and one unnamed middle school are proposed. Many of these renovation projects will include capacity enhancements.

 

Site Acquisition: Funds have been proposed for a site acquisition in the future.

 

Like the past two years, the FY 2018–22 CIP also recommends possible solutions for future consideration to resolve capacity deficits. These options include interior modifications, temporary classrooms, program location suggestions, and boundary adjustments. Any boundary adjustment or program change would be discussed and decided through a transparent process that engages the community in accordance with School Board Policy and Regulations.

 

Summary of Revision:   

 

Priority Recommended Boundary Adjustments (page 41). The Thoreau MS potential scoping will be in Spring 2017. The potential boundary study will be in Fall 2017. The Bucknell ES potential scoping will be in Fall 2017. The potential boundary study will be in Spring 2018. Both continue to be anticipated for an effective date of SY 2018-19.  This revision has been made, as per direction received at the January 23, 2017 School Board Work Session.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2018-2022 Capital Improvement Program.

Key Points:

 

On December 21, 2016, proposals were received for RFP #17-011, for Relocation Services (Disassembly, Assembly, Transportation and Demolition) for Portable Classroom Trailers.  The Selection Advisory Committee (SAC) is evaluating the proposals in accordance with the criteria set forth in School Board Policy 8240.6.  To handle the volume of disassembling, transporting, assembling and demolition of trailers, it is in the best interest of FCPS to engage multiple contractors as opposed to awarding a contract to one offerer.  Awarding a contract to multiple contractors provides FCPS the opportunity to take advantage of the lowest price, creates flexibility and classroom readiness by the start of the next school year.  Funding for these projects will be provided from either operating or bond funds.  The SAC will recommend a list of qualified contractors for notice of award prior to the February 9, 2017, School Board meeting.

6.07 ERFC 2001 Plan Modifications [COO; action 2/9/17; direct the ERFC Board of Trustees to initiate the process to implement the ERFC Plan modifications as detailed in the agenda item]
Meeting
Jan 26, 2017 - Regular Meeting
Category
6. New Business - 9:30 p.m.
Type

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        

 

                       

Staff Contact:  Susan Quinn, chief operating officer

 

Other Staff Present:  Jeanne Carr, executive director and chief investment officer, ERFC

                                     Chace Ramey, assistant superintendent, Department of Human Resources

                                     Kristen Michael, assistant superintendent, Department of Financial Services

 

Meeting Category:  January 26, 2017 – Regular Meeting

 

Subject:  ERFC 2001 Plan Modifications                 

 

School Board Action Required:  New Business   

 

Related To:  Legal Requirement

 

Key Points:

 

FCPS hired Aon Hewitt, a provider of retirement consulting and actuarial services, to conduct a study which benchmarked FCPS’ retirement benefit levels to surrounding school divisions, Fairfax County and the federal government.  The study also included the adequacy of income replacement at retirement provided by the retirement programs that FCPS offers. 

 

On May 12, 2016, ERFC presented the history of the FCPS retirement programs along with the key provisions of the existing plans.  Aon Hewitt then provided a briefing on the results of its study, illustrating retirement benefit levels for FCPS employees at various age and service combinations with comparisons to the peer group.  Aon Hewitt also presented ERFC options for possible future consideration.  The options presented included:  lowering the interest crediting rate on ERFC member accounts from 5 percent to 4 percent, instituting a minimum retirement age of 55, increasing the averaging period for the final average salary from 3 years to 5 years and changing the cost-of-living adjustment to equal 100 percent of the Consumer Price Index with a cap of 4 percent. 

 

On July 21, 2016, the School Board considered the benefit impact of the options for possible consideration.  At work sessions in September, November and December, 2016, the School Board reviewed the employer cost savings associated with those options for new hires, new hires and non-vested employees and expanding the options to all members except those currently eligible for full service retirement.  On January, 19, 2017, the School Board considered the employer cost savings associated with those same options with increasing the minimum retirement age from age 55 to age 60 for new hires and non-vested employees. The School Board arrived at consensus to move forward with the proposed ERFC changes for new hires and non-vested members with a minimum age requirement of 55.    

 

Recommendation:  That the School Board direct the ERFC Board of Trustees to initiate the process to implement the following ERFC Plan modifications, effective July 1, 2017:

 

All ERFC members:

      1. Lowering the interest crediting rate on ERFC member accounts from 5 percent to 4 percent

 

ERFC 2001 members hired on or after July 1, 2017, and non-vested members as of June 30, 2017:

      2. Instituting a minimum retirement age of 55

      3. Increasing the period for calculating the final average salary from 3 years to 5 years

      4. Changing the cost-of-living adjustment to equal 100 percent of the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U) with a cap of 4.0 percent; and present the ERFC 2001 amended plan documents to the School Board for approval, by June 2017.

                                  Jen Glaser, director, Counseling and College and Career Readiness, ISD

                                  Kathy Oliver, director, Student Testing, CAO

                          

                                       

Meeting Category:  January 26, 2017 – Regular Meeting

 

Subject: Strategic Plan Goal 1, Student Success, Strategies 3 and 4

 

School Board Action Required: New Business      

 

Related To: Strategic Plan—Goal 1

 

Key Points:    

 

In July 2015, the School Board approved Ignite, the strategic plan for Fairfax County Public Schools, which represents the cooperative work of the School Board and Leadership Team, with significant community input, to create a long term plan for continuous improvement. At the core of this plan is the FCPS Portrait of a Graduate. The Ignite Plan contains four strategic goals: Student Success, Caring Culture, Premier Workforce, and Resource Stewardship. All of the goals contain overarching strategies, desired outcomes, actions, and monitoring metrics. This school year, 2015-2016, marks the first year of implementation of the Strategic Plan. The purpose of this report is to provide an update on the current work that supports Goal 1, Student Success.

 

The following are the last two of the four overarching strategies and the desired outcomes for Goal 1 Student Success.  The first two strategies were presented on December 15, 2016

 

Overarching Strategy 3:

Provide quality Early Childhood experiences aligned with Portrait of a Graduate outcomes that are designed to prepare students to successfully enter Kindergarten.

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • All qualifying future FCPS students will have access to high quality early education programs
  • All agencies will coordinate their efforts to provide similar early childhood experiences
     

Overarching Strategy 4:

Provide students with relevant opportunities to explore options and prepare for College and Career.

 

Desired Outcomes:

  • A portfolio of school options will provide multiple pathways to a high school diploma

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the Strategic Plan - Goal 1 Student Success Strategies 3 and 4 as detailed in the agenda item.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
