Agenda Item: Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) Annual Report 2005


Staff Contact: Alice Farling, Assistant Superintendent, Department of Special Services


Background:
Regulations Governing Special Education Programs for Children with Disabilities in Virginia require that a local advisory committee for special education, appointed by each local school board, advise the school board through the division superintendent of unmet needs in the education of children with disabilities. The regulations state that the composition of the committee shall include parents of children with disabilities and persons with disabilities.


Key Issues: The ACSD made the following recommendations:

1. The School Board includes in its budget the funds to implement a schoolwide Positive Behavioral Support Program in all schools by the year 2010.
2. Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) increase joint training of parents and educators to encourage greater collaboration and a common understanding of issues.
3. FCPS create and implement uniform divisionwide procedures governing Functional Behavioral Assessments (FBAs) and Positive Behavior Intervention Plans (PBIPs).
4. FCPS develop a review process for schools with high rates of suspensions in an effort to reduce those rates.
5. FCPS implement procedures for identifying general education students who are suspended and may need special education services.
6. Create a task force to hold a community discussion about how best to serve students in crisis, to include issues related to locked seclusion rooms and physical restraints.
7. FCPS increase training of secondary special and general education staff on differentiated learning and instruction, supports that benefit all students, and collaborative teaching models.
8. FCPS and the School Board recognize those secondary schools that most effectively teach students with low-incidence disabilities in inclusive settings.



    School Board Discussion/Decision:

    For information and discussion.

    Staff Recommendation:

    Overall, staff members support the recommendations of the ACSD and will continue to collaborate with the committee in implementing the recommendations wherever feasible. Staff shares the vision of schoolwide approaches to positive behavioral supports across the school system. Staff has been addressing the area of student behavior through the Target 9 committee and through training provided for school teams in schoolwide approaches to positive behavioral support; 41 schools will have completed training by September 2005 with funding provided by the Department of Special Services (DSS), via a grant, and the Target 9 Committee (future funding sources will need to be determined). There are many other approaches schools use to proactively address student behavior. For example, six schools have been trained in The Responsive Classroom and many schools have schoolwide character education programs.

    DSS staff members have reviewed and provided input into proposed guidelines developed by the Virginia Department of Education regarding the seclusion and restraint of students with physically aggressive behavior; these guidelines have also been reviewed by the State Special Education Advisory Committee. As suggested in the proposed state guidelines, DSS staff members have already drafted a policy for School Board approval to clarify the responsibilities of schools in addressing the needs of students with physically aggressive behavior. In addition, a new regulation has been drafted to clarify procedures schools must follow in managing aggressive behavior. This regulation states that physical intervention and/or seclusion shall be used only as a last resort when other interventions have been exhausted; the regulation also communicates the requirements for staff training and the requirements for documenting and reporting incidents where physical restraint and/or seclusion are used. The decision to convene a task force is recommended for deferral pending adoption of the policy, implementation of the new regulation, and a review of updated information by county personnel regarding their efforts to address the mental health needs of students in crisis.

    Agenda Item: Focus School Policy

    Staff Contact: Ann Monday

    Background
    Focus Schools offer unique schoolwide instructional opportunities to help individual schools explore instructional initiatives that would benefit their students and community. The Focus initiative could be a concentration of a particular discipline, additional instructional strategies, resources, and staffing. Focus Schools provide a special instructional initiative for their students living within the school’s specified boundaries.

    Focus Schools are unique in that they build on existing strengths in the school community and cultivate them in innovative and engaging ways resulting in strong community and parental support, involvement and satisfaction.

    Currently schools seeking a Focus initiative contact the Instructional Services Department (ISD) to discuss the proposal and are provided an application. Schools complete the application and submit the application to ISD. Staff presents to the Board, on a yearly basis, a Focus School report that outlines the current funding status of the Focus schools and presents proposals for consideration.

    In addition to the Focus School concept, Fairfax County Public Schools established two other types of programs: magnet schools and county-initiated instructional initiatives. Magnet and foreign language immersion schools are schools that receive additional resources to enhance and enrich the Program of Studies for students, usually chosen through a lottery system, from both in and outside of their immediate attendance areas. Instructional initiatives, such as Project Excel, Success by Eight, and Focus 2007 schools, are designed by the county and placed in select schools to improve student achievement through alternative learning models, increased time for learning and targeted staff development.

    On July 12, 2004, staff presented a Focus School report that included proposals for Terraset to become a Focus School and Kilmer to receive funding to support its Focus program. However, the School Board took no action on the proposals.

    At the March 10, 2005 School Board meeting, board members requested ISD to establish guidelines for approval of Focus Schools.

    On June 13, 2005, staff presented a draft Focus School policy to the board. The School Board requested ISD to provide an overview and history of the current Focus Schools.

    Key Issues
    · The average cost of a Focus School initiative is approximately $170,000.
    · Does FCPS support the Focus School concept?
    · If Focus Policy is adopted, the School Board may consider identifying a funding source to support implementation of Focus initiatives.

    School Board Discussion/Policy Decision
    At the June 13, 2005 School Board work session, board members requested staff to update the draft Focus School policy and provide an overview and history of the current Focus Schools.

    At the March 10, 2005 School Board meeting, board members requested staff to establish guidelines for adoption of Focus Schools based on the following criteria:
    · Consensus that parent involvement and buy-in of the Focus Schools concept was necessary.
    · Equity and balance throughout the County should be a factor; strategic approach would be needed to provide that balance.
    · Modified calendar school process should remain separate.
    · In communities where parents might not be as involved, staff might bring program recommendation forward.
    · Policy must include an evaluation process of Focus Schools.
    · Staff is to develop draft policy for Board review.

    Staff Recommendation
    · Adopt a Focus School Policy and identify funding source for Focus initiatives.

    Options
    Resources:
    · Option 1: Schools may request additional resources for up to five years. Such resources would be for planning, training and other costs associated for initial implementation of the Focus School program. Ongoing staffing expenses should be incorporated within the normal staffing model.
      · Option 2: Schools may request additional resources including planning, training and other costs associated for initial implementation of the Focus School program as well as ongoing staffing expenses.
        · Option 3: All additional expenses must be borne by the school via grants and/or normal resource allocations to that school.
        Evaluation/Reaffirmation
        · Option 1: At a minimum, each school must re-affirm it’s “Focus” once every five years. Such a re-affirmation must include support from the parents and staff. The school must also be meeting minimum academic performance standards set by the school board.

        · Option 2: A school shall keep its “Focus” until such time that the community and staff seek to have it dropped or changed.

        · Option 3: Once approved, the school “Focus” shall adhere to the periodic QPAS evaluation cycle.

        Agenda Item:Attendance area boundary process


        Staff Contact: Dean A.Tistadt


        Background
        At the conclusion of the South County Secondary School boundary process, some members of the School Board indicated an opinion that improvements could be made to the process itself that would enhance and improve future boundary processes.

        Key Issues
        While staff has not heard from most School Board members, we do believe that improvements can be made to boundary communication. Brad Center previously recommended the following points be reviewed:

        · Improve public communication of boundary meetings and identifying possible areas to be affected. Specifically, our South County communication did not indicate clearly that we would consider during the process the potential of moving students to schools other than South County.

        · Revisit the Board policy on boundaries regarding the list of criteria used when determining boundaries. There is no method for the Board or general public to determine which, if any, criteria are more important.

        · Possible improvements to the boundary town meeting format/process. Possibly open use our survey tool as we do for the Superintendent search.

        · Determine a set of objectives for each boundary study and clearly articulate them up front. Doing so will enable us determine if public, staff, or Board proposals are within the previously stated study objectives, and avoid moving in directions other than those originally stated.

        · Include numbers of children attending private schools when determining boundary proposals.

        · Include transportation implications (cost, traffic volumes) of boundary proposals if possible.

        School Board Discussion/Decision
        Staff will ensure that we improve future communications. If other changes are desired in the process, we need guidance from the School Board

        Staff Recommendation
        Communications will be improved.
