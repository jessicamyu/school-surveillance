Bullying Prevention Month- October

 

In an effort to promote awareness of school bullying, the Virginia School Board Association Board of Directors has designated the month of October as VSBA Bullying Prevention Month. Childhood bullying is a significant problem nationwide. It can cause school absenteeism, mental and physical stress, poor school performance, poor self-esteem, and in some cases, school violence. Statistics show that 160,000 children in the United States miss school each day as a result of being bullied. School board members, superintendents, teachers, and parents play a critical role in creating a climate where bullying is not tolerated. It has been proven when adults and children stand together, bullying ends.

 

 

Fire Prevention Week - October 6-12, 2019

 

 

Fire Prevention Week 2019 focuses on the fact that in a fire, seconds count. The official theme, "Not every hero wears a cape.  Plan and practice your escape!" This year's theme recognizes the everyday people who motivate their households to develop and practice a home fire escape plan; these seemingly basic behaviors can have life-saving impact.  The campaign works to celebrate people of all ages who learn about home fire escape planning and practice, bring that information home, and spur their families to action.  The theme also focuses on what a home escape plan entails and the value of practicing it.  A home escape plan includes working smoke alarms on every level of the home, in every bedroom, and near al sleeping areas.  It also includes two ways out of every room, usually a door and a window, with a clear path to an outside meeting place that's a safe distance from the home.  Home escape plans should be practiced twice a year by all members of the household.

 

 

 

Mental Health Awareness Week - October 6-12, 2019

The National Alliance on Mental Illness or NAMI and participants across the country are raising awareness of mental illness during Mental Illness Awareness Week which is October 6-12. Each year NAMI works to fight stigma, provide support, educate the public and advocate for equal care. NAMI believes that mental illness issues are important to address year round, but highlighting these issues during Mental Health Awareness Week provides a time for people to come together and display the passion and strength of those working to improve the lives of the tens of millions of Americans affected by mental illness. This year’s theme is Cure Stigma. There's a virus spreading across America, harming the 1 in 5 Americans affected by mental health conditions. It shames them into silence. It prevents them from seeking help. The virus we are talking about is stigma against people with mental health conditions. But there's good news, stigma is 100% curable. Compassion, empathy and understanding are the antidote. Your voice can spread the cure.

NATIONAL DYSLEXIA AWARENESS MONTH
 

WHEREAS, dyslexia is a language-based learning disability that causes difficulties with specific language skills such as reading, writing, spelling, and word pronunciation; and

WHEREAS, the onset and severity of dyslexia varies for each individual and impacts up to 15-20% of the population as a whole; and

WHEREAS, literacy is a critical skill needed for educational success and individuals with dyslexia benefit from effective teaching approaches that are multisensory, systematic, and explicit; and

WHEREAS, Governor Ralph Northam has recognized October 2019 as DYSLEXIA AWARENESS MONTH in Virginia to acknowledge educators specializing in effective teaching strategies for individuals with dyslexia; and to celebrate the many achievements of adolescents, students, and adults with dyslexia; and

WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools has instituted dyslexia awareness training for all elementary and secondary instructional staff and administrators; and

WHEREAS, Fairfax County Public Schools has launched the FCPS Online Dyslexia Handbook to provide information and resources to FCPS schools and families alike as they support students with dyslexia; and

WHEREAS, parents, educators, and students will benefit from increased awareness about the nature of dyslexia and the combination of strengths and difficulties that individuals with dyslexia can have;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED the Fairfax County School Board recognizes October 2019 as DYSLEXIA AWARENESS MONTH and encourages all residents to learn more about dyslexia and to offer support to parents, educators and individuals affected by dyslexia.

Summary/Background:

 

The Human Resources Department recommends that the School Board award a contract to George Mason University for the provision of the Elementary Professional Development School (PDS) Internship Program.  

 

The internship program provides George Mason University Elementary PDS students an opportunity to gain practical and clinical teaching experiences through semester or yearlong internships, to include substitute assignments.  Over the past 5 years, FCPS has hosted an average of 50 elementary PDS students annually.

 

The internship program benefits FCPS by deeply engaging local, highly qualified teacher candidates to the school division resulting in highly qualified teacher hires. Other benefits to the program include built in subbing hours that allow for year-long participants to sub for FCPS for up to 45 days helping to combat FCPS’ subbing shortage.

 

The internship program also promotes a more robust student teaching experience resulting in higher quality teaching skills and outcomes. The vast majority of the George Mason University Elementary PDS graduates are hired in FCPS, often times in their PDS site locations, which sometimes includes Title I schools.

 

The period of this contract is 5 years.  The total estimated payment to George Mason University for the 2019-2020 school year is approximately $170,000; Policy 5011 requires that the School Board approve “any contract or contract amendment awarded between the School Board and other governmental entity that exceeds $250,000.”  Policy 5011.3 (III)(A)(1).

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board (i) award the contract to the George Mason University for the Elementary Professional Development School Internship Program, beginning with 2019-2020 school year, and (ii) authorize the Director of the Office of Procurement Services to execute, deliver and administer the contract on behalf of the School Board.

Meeting Category: Regular Meeting, September 26, 2019

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Ignite Link: Goal 1: Student Success

 

Related to: Standards of Accreditation (8 VAC 20-131-10)

 

Summary/Background:

 

With revisions to the Standards of Accreditation (SOA) adopted in fall 2017, new school quality indicators were introduced to measure school accreditation outcomes related to achievement gaps (English and mathematics), chronic absenteeism, dropout rate, and college-career-civic readiness.

 

The alternative accreditation plans previously approved by the Virginia Board of Education for five special purpose schools in FCPS do not address these new indicators and, therefore, must be revised and resubmitted by September 2019 for consideration by the Virginia Board. The five affected schools are: Bryant High School, Fairfax County Adult High School, Key Center School, Kilmer Center School, and Mountain View High School. Upon approval by the Virginia Board, the alternative accreditation plans will grant waivers to these five schools, guaranteeing full “Accredited” status for three years, 2019-20 through 2021-22.

 

The SOAs establish alternative accreditation plans as an option for certain types of schools designated to serve special populations, including special education schools and alternative schools. The purpose of an alternative accreditation plan is to explain how the special purpose and population served by the school results in an incomplete depiction of the school’s quality when standard calculation methods are applied. Each plan outlines modified calculation methods that will be used when determining the school’s performance level toward the school quality indicators, so as to more appropriately reflect school outcomes.

 

 

Recommendation: That the School Board approve the alternative accreditation plans for submission to the Virginia Board of Education, as detailed in the agenda item.

Recommended Action

Subject: Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution

 

Staff Contacts:  Marty Smith, chief operating officer

                          Leigh Burden, assistant superintendent Financial Services

                                         

Other Staff Present:  Michelle R. Pratt, director, Procurement Services

                                               

Meeting Category:  September 26, 2019 – Regular Meeting

 

Ignite Link:  Resource Stewardship

 

Summary/Background:

 

The Fairfax County Board of Supervisors adopted on July 16, 2019, the updated version of the Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution (“Purchasing Resolution”), following legislative changes resulting from the 2019 session of the General Assembly. Historically, the Fairfax County School Board also has annually adopted the newly revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, following its adoption by the Board of Supervisors.

 

County’s Administrative Changes Relative to FCPS:

 

  1. Moved Cooperative Procurement text from Article 1, General Provisions to Article 2, Procurement Policies at pages 5 and 20.
  1. Change stated method of advertising procurement opportunities to match current practices.  This change is within existing authority at pages 13 and 14.
  1. Changed stated method of publishing notice of sole source and emergency procurements to match current practices.  This change is within existing authority at pages 21 and 23.
  1. Clarified that insurance or electric utility services can be procured through an association following a determination in advance that the action is fiscally advantageous at page 23.
  1. Corrected authority to enter into contracts with workshops or employment services organizations without competition at page 24.
  1. Established exemption for Fairfax County Public Schools to purchase multidivision online courses and virtual school programs at page 25.
  1. Created new Article 3, which accomplishes several things.  First, it consolidates mandatory contract terms currently in the Purchasing Resolution into a single Article.  Second, it adds other required or prohibited terms that had not previously been included in the Purchasing Resolution.  Including the required and prohibited terms in a single Article in the Purchasing Resolution will provide better guidance to County staff and vendors regarding acceptable contract terms.

 

New

      1. Authority to bind County
      2. Established no supplier shall establish a limitation of liability in formation of all County contracts
      3. Established authority for the Purchasing Agent to require compliance with nonvisual access standards.
      4. Venue for any claim under a contract
      5. Choice of law will be governed by laws of the Commonwealth of Virginia
      6. Order of precedence for contract / ordering documents
      7. Indemnification by the County prohibited
      8. Contracts Subject to Appropriation by Board of Supervisors
      9. Binding Arbitration or Mediation
      10. Limitation of Rights and Waiver of Remedies
      11. Confidentiality provisions prohibited
      12. Unilateral Modification prohibited

 

Moved

  1. Non-discrimination against a bidder or offeror
  2. Immigration Reform and Control Act Compliance
  3. Increased minimum contract value from $10,000 to $100,000 for right of County to audit contractors accounting and financial records.
  4. Authorization to Transact Business in the Commonwealth
  5. Drug free workplace for the contractor’s employees

 

  1. Established prohibition of use vendor rebates for personal use by County employees at page 62.
  1. Established that a gift of services pursuant to the Virginia State Government Volunteers Act is not a procurement such that the County is not offering consideration and the person acts of free will and without any financial gain at page 67.
  1. Code of Virginia citations and other cross-references have been updated throughout the document where necessary. 

 

FCPS Conditions to the Purchasing Resolution:

 

As was done in connection with the School Board’s adoption of the 2009, 2010, 2011, 2012, 2013, 2014, 2015, 2016, 2017 and 2018 Purchasing Resolutions, school system staff recommends that the School Board adopt the 2019 Purchasing Resolution, subject to the following two conditions:

 

  • The “FCPS Construction Exception,” which is set forth in Article 1, Section 3(A)(2.) and Article 3, Section 1(2.) of the Purchasing Resolution and which is by definition an exception to the Purchasing Resolution, shall be revised to read as follows:

 

“The Fairfax County School Board shall be responsible for construction, related architectural and engineering services, related consulting services, maintenance, repair, and related services in connection with building, furnishing, equipping, renovating, maintaining, and operating the buildings and property of the school division in accordance with §22.1-79 of the Code of Virginia. The Fairfax County School Board’s procurement, award, and execution of contracts under this provision shall be in accordance with the policies and regulations of the school division and with the Code of Virginia, including the VPPA.”

 

Note: The following text is presently contained in the above-referenced Articles of the Purchasing Resolution adopted by the BOS, but this text would be excluded from the School Board’s adoption because it inaccurately describes the Superintendent’s authority: “The school division’s Superintendent or his designee shall have the same authority as the County Purchasing Agent to execute and administer contracts. Execution of contracts under this section shall be conducted under the rules and regulations established by the Fairfax County School Board in accordance with the mandatory sections in the Code of Virginia.”]

 

  • The Fairfax County School Board reserves the right to procure and award contracts for goods, services, and insurance pursuant to its own policies and regulations and pursuant to the Code of Virginia, including the VPPA, where such is in the best interest of the school division.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopts the newly revised 2019 Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution, previously adopted by the Board of Supervisors, subject to the conditions detailed in the agenda item.

Key Points:

 

The current version of Regulation 3009, Challenged Library and Instructional Materials establishes an Interdepartmental Review Committee to review challenges of library and instructional materials by residents. Six School Board members (on a rotating basis) will each select an individual to serve a one-year term on the Interdepartmental Review Committee. For 2019-2020, the Board members making appointments are Megan McLaughlin (Braddock), Jane Strauss (Dranesville), Pat Hynes (Hunter Mill), Karen Corbett Sanders (Mount Vernon), Dalia Palchik (Providence), and Thomas Wilson (Sully). Last year the School Board district representatives from Lee, Mason, Springfield, and the three At-Large representatives were selected to appoint individuals.

 

Two appointees will participate at each of the three levels of challenged materials: elementary, middle, and high school. The level will be determined by a random selection. If the deliberations involve multiple levels, a randomly selected School Board appointee from each involved level will participate.

 

The student representative to the School Board appoints an individual to serve on the Interdepartmental Review Committee for challenges that involve high school materials.

 

Other committee members are appointed to serve one-year terms as set forth in the current version of Regulation 3009.

 

Recommendation:

Subject: Possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in public schools (Code of Virginia § 18.2-251.1:1)

 

Staff Contact: Teresa Johnson, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services

 

Other Staff Present: Lea Skurpski, DSS director; Lorraine Trouton, FCPS manager, School Health Services

 

Meeting Category: September 26, 2019 - Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required: Information

 

Ignite Link: Caring Culture, Student Health and Wellness

 

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

Effective July 1, 2019, § 18.2-251.1:1. Possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in public schools was authorized by Virginia law requiring that no school nurse employed by a local school board, person employed by a local health department who is assigned to the public school pursuant to an agreement between the local health department and the school board, or other person employed by or contracted with a local school board to deliver health-related services shall be prosecuted under § 18.2-248, 18.2-248.1, 18.2-250, 18.2-250.1, or 18.2-255 for the possession or distribution of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil for storing, dispensing, or administering cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil, in accordance with a policy adopted by the local school board, to a student who has been issued a valid written certification for the use of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil in accordance with subsection B of § 54.1-3408.3.

 

In order to address this issue, FCPS has established a policy to address the possession, storage, and administration of cannabidiol oil or THC-A oil to students at school, as authorized by Virginia law.

 

To further address procedural and documentation requirements, Regulation 2102, First Aid, Emergency Treatment and Administration of Medication for Students has been revised, in collaboration with the Fairfax County Health Department, to allow for the storage of THC-A and CBD oil in accordance with the student’s individualized health plan and in compliance with documentation requirements outlined in the Code of Virginia.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the policy to address possession, administration, and distribution of cannabidiol oil and THC-A oil, as detailed in the agenda item.
