Teacher Appreciation Week May 3-7, 2010

Every day, more than 14,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potentials for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

This year, the School Board asked parents, students, and administrators to tell us some of the activities that they have planned for Teacher Appreciation Week. The Board learned that teachers will be served food at events including breakfasts, lunches, and ice cream socials. Some schools will hang signs and banners of appreciation, and one school will literally roll out a red carpet for teachers to enter the building from the parking lot. PTA volunteers and administrators will cover classes and recess for teachers for short periods of time and signs of appreciation will include books, instructional supplies, flowers and notes, and performances by students.

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2010

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

The President of the United States proclaims May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2010

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month each May to raise public awareness of communication disorders that affect an estimated 42 million people. One in 10 families is affected by a communication disorder.

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to 10,178 students and deaf and hard of hearing services to 441students. Fairfax County Public Schools annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of the deaf and hard of hearing.

Recognition of Child Nutrition Employee Week - May 3-7, 2010

Caring. Trained. Thoughtful. Professional. Dedicated. These are just a few words that describe today's child nutrition professional.

Child nutrition employees in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate daily their professional commitment to providing students with nutritious menu choices that reflect current research and meet the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. School nutrition employees must balance many roles and follow numerous federal, state and local regulations to ensure safe and healthy meals are available in schools. They are trained sanitation and food safety experts and must manage financially self-sufficient programs. School nutrition professionals also provide nutrition education to students, as well as healthy catering services to their communities. They use their creativity to make the cafeteria a fun and welcoming place all year long and perform their jobs each day because they care passionately about the children they serve.

FCPS child nutrition employees are committed to imparting food and nutrition knowledge and skills to students, using the school cafeteria as the nutrition laboratory and are influential in preparing students to learn.

Three Fairfax County public schools have been recognized as Title I Distinguished Schools. Parklawn Elementary School was one of only two schools in Virginia to be recognized under the National Title I Distinguished Schools program. Parklawn was selected by a peer review panel to represent Virginia in the category of “Closing the Achievement Gap Among Student Groups.” Parklawn Elementary School has met adequate yearly progress (AYP) participation and achievement requirements since 2003, and there has been a significant increase in achievement rates in nearly all subgroups in both reading and math during the last five years.

Belvedere Elementary School and Pine Spring Elementary School were named Title I Distinguished Schools by the Virginia Board of Education for maintaining full state accreditation under the Virginia Standards of Learning (SOL) program for two consecutive years. Title I Distinguished Schools are also cited for meeting federal benchmarks in reading and mathematics and for having average test scores in both subjects at the 60th percentile or higher. Belvedere and Parklawn are among 102 schools honored statewide for raising the academic achievement of economically disadvantaged students. Belvedere also received the distinction in 2007, 2008, and 2009, and Pine Spring was cited in 2007 and 2009.

RESOLUTION SUPPORTING COUNTYWIDE INITIATIVE
TO REDUCE UNDERAGE DRINKING


WHEREAS, 65 percent of underage youth say they get alcohol from family and friends, even though it is illegal in Virginia for a person under 21 years of age to consume or possess alcohol, and it is illegal for an adult to provide alcohol to a person under 21 years of age; and

WHEREAS, alcohol is the illegal drug most commonly used by Fairfax County students, and underage alcohol use is associated with traffic crashes and fatalities, other drug use, violence and gang involvement, rape, depression and suicide, and educational failure; and

WHEREAS, the 2008 Fairfax County Youth Survey reports that 43 percent of high school seniors and almost 27 percent of tenth grade students consumed alcohol in the last 30 days and that the prevalence of high-risk drinking by high school students increases with age; and

WHEREAS, the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County Public Schools and its school-community coalitions have brought together concerned residents, school officials, county government agencies, and many community groups to address the underage drinking issue and, in spring 2007, launched an ongoing educational and public awareness campaign known as “Parents Who Host, Lose the Most: Don’t be a party to teenage drinking” to educate parents and to increase awareness of and compliance with Virginia laws prohibiting underage drinking; and

WHEREAS, prevention efforts, including parents talking to their teens and modeling appropriate behavior, are critical, particularly during prom and graduation season;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board wholeheartedly supports the Unified Prevention Coalition’s Countywide Initiative to Reduce Underage Drinking and recognizes the month of May 2010 as Parents Who Host, Lose the Most Month in Fairfax County Public Schools.

Revised

Staff Contact: Dean A. Tistadt, Chief Operating Officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

Other Staff Present: Lee Ann Pender, director, Administrative Services

Meeting Category: April 22, 2010 - Regular Meeting No. 18

Subject: School Board Policy

School Board Action Required: Information

Background:

School system staff has been working with Fairfax County’s Department of Planning and Zoning and the County’s Planning Commission to coordinate use of School Board-owned sites for telecommunication uses. Thus far, uses have primarily been at high schools, with a few non-high school exceptions where light poles existed previously. On January 21, 2010, FCPS staff met with the Fairfax County Planning Commission’s Policy and Procedures Committee to review a potential change in School Board policy that would allow inconspicuous poles, such as tree poles, at FCPS locations to include elementary and middle school sites. In addition, staff met with the School Board at the April 5, 2010, work session to advise the School Board of the potential benefits that could result from expansion of the FCPS monopole program. At that time the School Board directed staff to forward its recommendations for information, discussion, and action by the School Board.
Key Points:

1. Public sites, such as School Board-owned property, remain the preferred locations for new telecommunication facilities.

2. Ideally, telecommunication facilities should be constructed in areas to blend with an existing pattern of tall structures, and should be inconspicuous so as to resemble other man-made structures, such as light poles, and natural features, such as trees.

3. The FCPS monopole program generates revenues that support the instructional program, communications safety initiatives, as well as local school programs/activities.

4. FCPS staff will continue to coordinate with Fairfax County’s Department of Planning and Zoning and the County’s Planning Commission to expand the FCPS monopole program, including the introduction and implementation of telecommunication facilities at elementary and middle school sites, in order to:
                  a. Increase revenues to the school system
                  b. Increase services to the community
                  c. Enhance the instructional program by taking a critical step towards providing Internet services to all students
Recommendation:

That the School Board adopt Policy 8335.2, Wireless Telecommunications Facilities, as reviewed and discussed at the Policy work session.
