Agenda Item: Exploratory Special Education Parent Survey

Staff Contact: Alice M. Farling, Assistant Superintendent, Dept. of Special Services

Background: The results of an Exploratory Special Education Parent Survey initiated by the Department of Special Services are being shared with the School board for information; the survey report was prepared by an independent consultant. The purpose of the survey was to gather information from parents to assist the department, cluster offices, and schools in the continuous improvement of special education services. The survey was developed with input from a number of stakeholders including principals and the Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities.

Parents were able to complete surveys via the Internet or they could choose to complete a paper copy. School-based staff provided information to parents about the on-line and paper surveys following Individualized Education Program (IEP) meetings or following the special education eligibility process. The surveys were available to parents from December 1, 2004, to December 1, 2005, in several languages (English, Spanish, Arabic, Korean, Farsi, Urdu, and Vietnamese).

Key Issues: While more than 2,300 parents responded to the survey, this number represents a small percentage of parents with children who were being evaluated for special education services or parents who had children receiving special education services. For this reason, results can be interpreted as those of the respondents only. Of the parents who responded to the survey, the large majority (often more than 90 percent) were satisfied with the eligibility process, the IEP meeting, and special education services. Parent awareness and use of several special education resources varied with the specific resources.

School Board Discussion/Decision: For information and discussion.

Staff Recommendation: Share the results of the FCPS survey and lessons learned with Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) staff and collaborate with VDOE in developing a new instrument, as required by the US Department of Education, which will allow parents to report on whether schools facilitated parent involvement as a means of improving services and results for children with disabilities.

Provide technical assistance, in collaboration with Department of Accountabilty, in the development of school-based data collection strategies and instruments that allow



schools to collect data from parents of special education students when deemed appropriate; collaborate with Cluster Offices to integrate any patterns of parent concerns into individual school improvement plans.

Review implementation of central and school-based practices designed to facilitate parent awareness of special education resources and expand efforts where needed.

Agenda Item: Online IEP System Update

Staff Contact: Alice Farling


Background: Special education teachers prepare over 42,000 Individualized Education Programs (IEPs) and addendums annually for more than 23,685 special education students. Once the IEPs or addendums are approved, multiple copies are made and forwarded to the appropriate offices for review and data entry into the special education database. Once the Online IEP system is implemented, IEPs will be maintained, reviewed and approved electronically. Only two hard copies of the IEP would be required for signature, one will be retained at the school and the other is provided to the parent. Teachers with the appropriate security will be able to readily access student classroom and testing accommodations electronically.

Key Issues: Provide information on the current status of the Online IEP project

School Board Discussion/Decision: Support the ongoing effort to automate the IEP process.


Staff Recommendation: Continue to support the implementation of the Online IEP

Agenda Item: Report on Supply and Demand at Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology – Responses to Blue Ribbon Commission Recommendations

Staff Contact: Pat Murphy

Background:

The Office of Program Evaluation conducted an evaluation of the admissions process at Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology (TJHSST) from 2001 – 2003. The evaluation resulted in three separate reports: Report One, which focused on an analysis of the admissions test, Report Two, which focused on the academic preparation and performance of applicants, and Report Three, which focused on the high school program choices made by highest-performing students. In late 2003, the School Board passed a resolution establishing a Blue Ribbon Commission (BRC), an external and objective group of experts on admissions issues from across the country, to study admissions at TJHSST. As a result of this committee’s recommendations, the admissions process has been revised, beginning with the 2004-05 applicant class, and further evaluation of admissions at TJHSST has been initiated in three areas: the supply side, the demand side, and understanding long-term effects.

The purpose of this document is to address the questions raised by the BRC. Responses to the questions are summarized under the headings of the supply side, the demand side, and understanding the long-term outcomes. Where data are not currently available (i.e., for questions 9-13), plans for data collection and analyses are outlined.

Key Issues:

The study so far seems to suggest the following preliminary conclusions:
· The new admissions process after one year is associated with a slightly higher degree of diversity in the semifinalist pool and accepted class.
· The new admissions process has not resulted in fewer or less qualified applicants.
· The TJHSST admissions test is technically sound which means that it meets the expectations in the field for reliability.
· Although the majority of the math test items (68%) on the admissions test were POS math 7 items, variation in performance is associated with level of mathematics taken and the ability to apply concepts in the most complex ways.
· From a student survey of talented Black and Hispanic students who might have applied to TJHSST, “not wanting to leave their friends” (81%) was the main reason for not applying.
· From a staff survey, “conditions and practices for curriculum, instruction, and expectations” should be maintained. There is less agreement about “school openness” (e.g., addressing diversity issues based on input from staff, students, and parents).
    School Board Discussion/Decision:

    · For discussion only.

    Staff Recommendation:

    The following additional analyses have been identified as relevant to the major areas of concern expressed by the BRC:
    · Analyses of the number, ethnicity, and division affiliation of students who were accepted at TJHSST following the 2004-05 changes in the admissions process, but chose not to attend TJHSST.
    · Analyses of changes in the number of students receiving free and reduced-priced meals in the semifinalist pools and accepted class following the 2004-05 changes in the admissions process.
    · Analyses of the attrition rate of the 2008-09 class (first graduating class after the change) compared with the attrition rates of the three previous classes.
    · Analyses of TJHSST staff and student perceptions of the school conditions and practices over time.
