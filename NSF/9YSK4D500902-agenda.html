Teacher Appreciation Week - May 2-6, 2016

Teachers are real life superheroes.  They educate, innovate, encourage, and support, touching the lives of millions of children.  Every day, over 15,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potential for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

 

 

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2016

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

Since 1992, the President of the United States has proclaimed May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

 

 

School Nurse Day - May 11, 2016

School Nurse Day was established in 1972 to foster a better understanding of the role of school nurses in the educational setting.  This annual event recognizes the contributions that school nurses make every day to improve the health and success of our nation’s children.  This year’s theme, “School Nurses:  Better Health. Better Learning,” reflects the significant roles that school nurses have in health care in their school communities, as well as in modeling health and resiliency.

School nursing is a specialized practice of professional nursing that advances the health and well-being of students and is an integral part of a quality school health program. School nurses promote health, safety, prevention, and wellness; intervene with actual and potential health problems; and provide case management services.  They are an invaluable bridge connecting health care providers, public health, and families.

The school system works closely with the Fairfax County Health Department to provide quality services to the children of Fairfax County Public Schools and recognized May 11 as School Nurse Day in Fairfax County.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2016

 

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month every year in May. The theme this year is “Early Intervention Counts,” a campaign to make people aware of the warning signs of speech, language, and hearing problems and the positive effect that early intervention can have to reverse and even prevent them. This annual event provides opportunities to promote services that can improve the quality of life for those who experience problems with speaking and listening. An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families. 

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to more than 10,000 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, via base school itinerant services, and in early childhood programs.  FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

Children’s Mental Health Awareness Day – May 5, 2016

 

The Federal Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration has declared May 5 to be National Children’s Mental Health Awareness Day.

The well-being of children has a profound, immediate and lasting impact on our community.  Nationally over one in five children and youth have a mental health issue and only half of children and youth with behavioral health issues receive treatment.

 

Current national statistics show that approximately 36,000 Fairfax County children and youth have a diagnosable mental health condition; including 3,960 with significant impairment and 1,800 with extreme impairment.  Unfortunately, there are multiple barriers preventing families from accessing timely and appropriate behavioral health services, including high deductible/high co-pay insurance plans, and lack of providers that accept Medicaid and insurance.

 

In their FY 2015 budget, the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors approved an expansion of behavioral health services for youth and families in order to address  Fairfax youth behavioral health needs in the community.  Through this expansion, Fairfax County Public Schools , Fairfax  County Human Services, non-profit agencies, family and consumer organizations are together implementing a multi-year Children’s Behavioral Health System of Care Blueprint to address barriers to accessing services through coordinating prevention, early intervention, and intensive treatment services across the human services system, schools and private providers.

RESOLUTION

SUPPORTING THE UNIFIED PREVENTION COALITION OF FAIRFAX COUNTY

AND ITS “PARENTS WHO HOST, LOSE THE MOST” CAMPAIGN

 

WHEREAS, May 2016 begins the tenth year of the Fairfax County “Parents Who Host, Lose the Most…don’t be a party to teen drinking” campaign to educate parents and to increase awareness of and compliance with Virginia underage drinking laws; and

 

WHEREAS, during the time frame of this ten years, data from the 2008 through 2014 Fairfax Youth Surveys, shows that the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County, its collaborative prevention partners, and parents have successfully reduced 30-day use of alcohol for 8th, 10th and 12 grade students from 27.9% to 19.2% overall and binge drinking for the same grade levels from 13.7% to 8.8% overall,

 

WHEREAS, alcohol remains the most commonly used illegal drug by Fairfax County students; and underage alcohol use is associated with other drug use, traffic crashes and fatalities, rape, depression, suicide, educational failure, violence and gang involvement; and

 

WHEREAS underage youth say they can easily get alcohol from family and friends, even though it is illegal in Virginia for a person under 21 years of age to consume or possess alcohol, and it is illegal for an adult to provide alcohol to a person under 21 years of age; and
 

WHEREAS, the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County, an independent non-profit organization of over 60 collaborative partners, including school officials, county government agencies, concerned residents, businesses, students, parents, and community groups, has been instrumental in addressing underage drinking and binge drinking, drinking and driving, impaired driving, and risk and protective factors; and

 

WHEREAS, prevention efforts, including parents talking with their teens and modeling appropriate behavior, are critical and must be consistent, particularly during prom and graduation season;
 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board supports the positive work of the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County and recognizes the month of May 2016 as “Parents Who Host, Lose The Most” month in Fairfax County.

School Board Action Required:  Decision

Key Points:

 

In July 2015, the School Board approved Ignite, the strategic plan for Fairfax County Public Schools, which represents the cooperative work of the School Board and Leadership Team, with significant community input, to create a long term plan for continuous improvement. At the core of this plan is the FCPS Portrait of a Graduate. The Ignite Plan contains four strategic goals: Student Success, Caring Culture, Premier Workforce, and Resource Stewardship. This school year, 2015-2016, marks the first year of implementation of the Strategic Plan.

 

The School Board is to receive quarterly updates/reports on the Strategic Plan, focused on the work accomplished and the results received. In addition, the report will outline major Strategic Plan activities for the coming year that are designed to address areas in need of improvement. This presentation and report will provide an update on the monitoring metrics and current work that support Goal 2, Caring Culture.

 

The following are the three overarching strategies and the desired outcomes for Goal 2, Caring Culture:

 

Overarching Strategy 5

Establish a school division and culture that is highly responsive to stakeholders and creates a model environment in which to learn and work

Desired Outcomes

  • FCPS will utilize a consistent approach to customer service
  • FCPS will demonstrate cultural competency and sensitivity when supporting families, students and employees

Overarching Strategy 6

Foster active and positive communication and engagement among students, staff, families, and the community in order to ensure that stakeholders are engaged and well-informed partners

 

Desired Outcomes

  • Communication tools, processes, and content will enable access to key information anytime, anywhere, on any device
  • Under-engaged communities will be provided additional communication and community support
  • Proactive and dynamic communications will better engage the community in critical issues
  • School-based staff will be better engaged and supported in communications and information sharing with their communities
  • All students will demonstrate digital citizenship skills

 

Overarching Strategy 7

Promote overall health and well-being of students and staff in order to encourage healthy life choices and an increased quality of life

Desired Outcomes

  • FCPS students will demonstrate improved life skills and self-awareness of behaviors and actions
  • FCPS students and staff will feel welcome and supported at school
  • FCPS students and staff will be encouraged and supported in making healthy life choices

Subject:  Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2016-2017

Key Points:

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report for the 2016-2017 school year, as required by VDOE Superintendent’s memo #290-15, dated December 4, 2015.  This report will be used by the Virginia Department of Education in preparing the state plan for special education.  As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-prescribed abbreviated format on a date, no later than May 13, 2016.

 

The 2016-2017 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $33,628,048.  This amount is equal to the final 2015–2016 award as stated in Superintendent’s memo #037-16, dated February 26, 2016.  Estimated expenditures include cost associated with the voluntary 15% set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS), and a mandatory private school set-aside of 0.4%.  Section 611, Part B, award will fund a total of 417.1 FTE positions.

 

The 2016-2017 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $730,514.  This amount is equal to the final 2015-2016 award as stated in Superintendent’s memo #185-15, dated July 31, 2015.  These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for school-age students.  Estimated expenditures include costs associated with a mandatory private school set-aside of 1.7%.  Section 619 funds six FTE positions.

 

The revenue amounts above reflect no change from the 2015-2016 awards.  The 2016-2017 application reflects estimated costs for the current programs funded in the 2015-2016 application.  Small adjustments in both sections of 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, materials/supplies and the state restricted indirect cost rate.

 

The Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) reviewed the plan and funding application at the March 9, 2016 meeting.

 

 

Options:

  2. Amend or reject the plan and application and provide direction to staff for further action.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
