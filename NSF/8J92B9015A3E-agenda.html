African American History Month - February 2012
 

Originally established as Negro History Week in 1926 by Dr. Carter G. Woodson, a noted African-American author and scholar, this event evolved into the establishment in 1976 of February as "Black History Month." This commemoration has increasingly been referred to as "African-American History Month," although both names are currently in use.

When Carter G. Woodson established Negro History week, he realized the importance of providing a theme to focus the attention of the public. The intention has never been to dictate or limit the exploration of the Black experience, but to bring to the public's attention important developments that merit emphasis.  This year’s theme, African Americans and the Civil War, honors the efforts of people of African descent to abolish slavery and inaugurate universal freedom in the United States and encourages all Americans to study and reflect on the value of their contribution to the nation.

 

 

Teen Dating Violence Prevention and Awareness Month – February 2012

 

Teen Dating Violence Prevention and Awareness Month is a national effort began in 2010 to raise awareness about abuse in adolescent relationships and promote programs that prevent it during the month of February.  Teen dating violence is a pattern of abusive behaviors used to exert power and control over a dating partner.  Each year, about one in three teens in the United States report being a victim of verbal, physical, emotional, or sexual abuse by a dating partner.  Abusive relationships can impact adolescent development, and teens who experience dating violence may suffer long-term negative behavioral and health consequences. 

 

The Fairfax County School Board encourages citizens to learn more about preventing dating violence and to show support for the organizations and individuals who provide critical advocacy, services and assistance to victims.

National School Counseling Week - February 6-10, 2012

 

National School Counseling Week 2012, "School Counselors: Helping Students Be Brilliant," will be celebrated from Feb. 6-10, 2012, to focus public attention on the unique contribution of professional school counselors within U.S. school systems. National School Counseling Week, sponsored by the American School Counselor Association, highlights the tremendous impact school counselors can have in helping students achieve school success and plan for a career.  Every day, school counselors work with students, families, and staff to address the academic, personal, social, and career needs of all students. 

 

Fairfax County Public Schools employs over 600 school counseling professionals, including school counselors, directors of student services, and career center specialists.  These professionals are committed to preparing students to become lifelong learners and productive members in a global society.

National Career and Technical Education Month - February 2012

Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from registered nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (office administration and technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (merchandising, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle schools, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

[Updated 1/27/12 - Motion and Voting Below]

 

Staff Contact:        Dean A. Tistadt, chief operating officer, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category: January 26, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                 FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five year enrollment projections and capacity calculations and identifies new construction, capacity enhancement, renovation, and infrastructure requirements.  Detailed background information supporting the CIP recommendations is contained in the Cluster Data compact disc provided with the FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program booklet attached to this agenda item.

 

The Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program Summary can be found in Attachment B of the Capital Improvement Program booklet.

 

Discussion:

 

The school system is facing a challenging situation in which student enrollment growth is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings.  This will necessitate increasing allocations of capital dollars to capacity enhancement projects and continued reliance on temporary classrooms, modular additions, and probable boundary adjustments.

 

The CIP includes funding for two new elementary schools, one in the Route 1 Corridor and one in the eastern Fairfax County area.  In addition, it is anticipated that beyond 2017 additional new elementary school capacity will be needed to address future growth in the Tyson’s Corner area, Fairfax/Oakton area, and in western Fairfax County, in the vicinity of Route 28 and the Dulles Airport Access Road.  Funding for an addition at either Kilmer or Jackson Middle schools, and additional high school capacity in the western portion of the county, are also identified.

 

Staff has briefed the Facilities Planning Advisory Council (FPAC) on the needed capital improvement projects outlined above.  FPAC, which was created to help advise the School Board and staff on facilities related issues, will be working to develop long-term plans that address facility needs in the most effective and efficient way.  Future CIP’s will be based upon strategic facilities plans developed by FPAC and approved by the School Board.  FPAC’s recommendations will reflect public engagement and community input obtained through their outreach efforts.  

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2013-2017 Capital Improvement Program Summary.

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Key points:

 

At the January 12, 2012, School Board meeting, the Board approved adding the following motion for new business:  That high school English and Social Studies honors courses be offered on site, if sufficient enrollment, through the 12th grade for all courses beginning with the 2012-13 school year, and that the 2012-13 Standard Course Offerings Catalog be amended accordingly.

 

The Board then discussed the issue of offering English and Social Studies honors courses at the January 23, 2012, Instruction work session. 

 

Staff has provided the following course descriptions for the five high school English and Social Studies honors courses:

 

ENGLISH 11 LANGUAGE AND COMPOSITION HONORS (115036)

Grades:  11     Credit:  one/weighted +.5

Prerequisite:  English 10

Students study advanced composition and analysis of language.  In refining their awareness of language and the writer's craft, students write works of publishable quality.  Students are required to take the Standards of Learning End of Course Test.

 

ENGLISH 12 LITERATURE AND COMPOSITION HONORS (116036)

Grades:  12     Credit:  one/weighted +.5

Prerequisite:  English 11

Students study composition and literary analysis. Through in-depth studies of literary works, students sharpen their awareness of language and their understanding of the writer's craft. They establish critical standards for appreciating and evaluating literature.

 

WORLD HISTORY AND GEOGRAPHY 2 HONORS (222136)

Grades:  10     Credit:  one/weighted +.5

This course is designed to develop greater understanding of the evolution of global processes and contacts, in interaction with different types of human societies. This understanding is advanced through a combination of selective factual knowledge and appropriate analytical skills. Focused primarily on the past fifteen hundred years of the global experience, the course builds on an understanding of cultural, institutional, and technological precedents that, along with geography, set the human stage prior to the year 600.  Students are required to take the Standards of Learning End of Course Test.

 

VIRGINIA AND UNITED STATES HISTORY HONORS (236036)

Grades:  11     Credit:  one/weighted +.5

In this course students develop the factual knowledge and analytical skills necessary to deal with the issues and problems of American History. They do in-depth analysis of major developments; become familiar with primary sources; assess historical materials, evidence, and interpretations; and learn to write research papers.  Students are required to take the Standards of Learning End of Course Test.

 

VIRGINIA AND UNITED STATES GOVERNMENT HONORS (244036)

Grades:  12     Credit:  one/weighted +.5

In this study of American government, students interpret national, state, and local government and politics through an examination of both general concepts used to interpret U.S. politics and the analysis of specific examples.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board waive Policy 3202, New Course Proposals and Deletion and Consolidation of Existing Courses, to add the following five courses to the 2012-2013 Course Offerings Catalog:  English 11 Honors, World History and Geography 2 Honors, English 12 Honors, Virginia and United States History Honors, and Virginia and United States Government Honors, and that these courses be offered on site, if there is sufficient enrollment.  Further move that the Board initiate an in-depth discussion of the K-12 curriculum and potential revision of the appropriate policies to more fully integrate the Student Achievement Goals in the Program of Studies.  The in-depth review will also address the delivery methodology of standard, honors, and advanced programs of study (to include Advanced Placement and International Baccalaureate), and review how to ensure increased and equitable student access to advanced academic offerings.  The Board shall begin such work at the February 11 retreat and develop an implementation plan by July 2012.

Amend the Fairfax County School Board’s 2012 legislative program to include the following statement: The Fairfax County School Board opposes mandates to require local school boards to allow students who are not currently enrolled full time in a public school in their locality to participate in student athletics and activities (S; work session held 1/23/12)

[Posted 1/26/12]

Staff Contact:         William Curran, Director of Student Activities and Athletics Programs

 

Meeting Category:  January 26, 2012 - Regular Meeting No. 12

 

Subject:                 Amendment to the Fairfax County School Board 2012 Legislative Program

 

School Board Action Required: Decision

 

Key Points:

 

House Bill 905 as currently introduced at the General Assembly would prohibit public schools from joining the Virginia High School League (VHSL) unless the VHSL were to change their rules to allow students who are home schooled to participate in public school athletic programs.  Other similar bills have also been introduced.

 

FCPS staff believes the following eligibility standards would be difficult to enforce with a non-FCPS student:

 

  • Attendance
  • Behavior expectations (SR&R)
  • Academic requirements

 

Attached is a position paper from the High School Principals Association and the Directors of Student Activities on this issue.

 

At the January 23, 2012, work session, the Board discussed this recent legislation. There was consensus to add an agenda item to the January 26, 2012, regular business meeting agenda in order for the Board to take action to amend the 2012 Legislative Program to include a statement of opposition to HB 905.

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board amend the Fairfax County School Board’s 2012 legislative program to include the following statement:  The Fairfax County School Board opposes mandates to require local school boards to allow students who are not currently enrolled full time in a public school in their locality to participate in student athletics and activities.

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Background:

 

Staff routinely monitors enrollments and projections across the county, especially those schools that approach the highest and lowest building utilizations based on projections and capacity.  As can be seen in the following chart, we currently have capacity challenges at a number of schools near Tysons Corner and in some cases, these challenges are expected to increase over the next five years.  For some schools, such as Westgate, we have planned renovations that will address the capacity issues.  For other schools, no such solution is available.

Within this general area, Lemon Road Elementary is the one school that has existing available capacity and which is of a size that suggests that it should be expanded.  Using the existing and expanded capacity at Lemon Road offers the opportunity to solve at least some of the overcrowding at adjacent schools.  The most pressing of the overcrowding issues is at Freedom Hill Elementary where enrollments have risen significantly.  Between September 2006 and September 2011, enrollment at Freedom Hill Elementary increased from 473 to 709.  In September 2016, a total of 828 students are projected.   Currently, Freedom Hill has a capacity utilization of 116% which is projected to increase to 136% by 2016.  To accommodate continuing enrollment pressure, Freedom Hill relies on eight classroom trailers to accommodate students.

 

To address overcrowding in this region and to take advantage of capacity at Lemon Road, staff is recommending the following actions:

 

  1. Construct an eight (8) room addition at Lemon Road which will increase that school’s capacity to approximately 550 students (the School Board has already authorized the architectural contract for this addition).
  1. Conduct a limited boundary study spring 2012 to consider moving approximately 100 students from Freedom Hill to Lemon Road effective the 2012–2013 school year.
  1. Conduct a boundary study in either the spring of 2013 or 2014 to move additional students to Lemon Road once the addition is complete.  At a minimum, the study should involve the following schools:
    1. Freedom Hill
    2. Shrevewood
    3. Westgate

Key Points:

 

For the initial spring 2012 study, staff has focused on a triangular portion of the Freedom Hill attendance area that is situated south of Rt. 7 and east of I-495, and north of I-66.  This section of the attendance areas is closest to Lemon Road (see attached map).  This study area contains approximately 325 elementary students attending at Freedom Hill.

 

For this spring 2012 boundary study staff proposes to move some of this triangular area to Lemon Road Elementary with implementation in fall 2012 to take advantage of existing capacity surplus of approximately 100 spaces.  Limiting the scope to these two schools at this time would allow for some immediate, meaningful enrollment relief for Freedom Hill.  Furthermore, a limited boundary study would allow adequate time for Lemon Road Elementary to position its resources and programs to receive an influx of students, many of whom participate in both ESOL and FRM programs.  For this limited study, no changes would be proposed at the middle or high school level since the study area for Freedom Hill and the majority of Lemon Road both feed to Kilmer Middle and Marshall High Schools.

 

The following schools are in this region but are not recommended, at this time, for inclusion in either boundary study. 

  • Haycock Elementary has a projected capacity deficit which could be addressed with the construction of additional capacity as part of its planned renovation.  Furthermore, enrollment pressures at Haycock could also be relieved with adjustments to their Advanced Academic Program (AAP) which continues to be a significant part of the total enrollment.
  • Westbriar Elementary is shown to also have capacity deficit.  However, the FY 2013-17 Capital Improvement Plan, currently under review by the School Board, proposes a future capacity enhancement project to address the elementary level capacity deficit in Tysons Corner which includes the Westbriar attendance area.
  • Stenwood Elementary has some capacity deficit projected which could likely be accommodated with interior architectural modifications and/or additional classroom trailers. 
  • Vienna Elementary does not have sufficient surplus capacity to be a part of the solution for overcrowding. 
  • Kent Gardens Elementary is projected to have a declining capacity deficit which would not require any solution over the 5-year projection period.

 

Recommendation:  

 

Staff recommends that the School Board authorize a limited scope boundary study for Freedom Hill and Lemon Road Elementary Schools for spring 2012.  Staff further recommends that the School Board direct staff to bring forward an expanded scope of study for consideration in spring 2013 or 2014 in order to take advantage of additional capacity to be constructed at Lemon Road Elementary.
