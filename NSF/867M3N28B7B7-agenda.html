National Career and Technical Education Week - February 12-18, 2006


This year's theme is Career Tech: Education for Success. Career and technical education prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers, from registered nurse to computer technician. These careers may require varying levels of education--from high school to postsecondary certificates to two- and four-year college degrees.

The subject areas most commonly associated with career and technical education are: Business and Information Technology (office administration and technology); Trade and Industrial (skilled trades such as automotive technician, carpenter, cosmetologist, law enforcement); Health and Medical Sciences (nursing, dental, and emergency medical technicians); Family and Consumer Sciences (culinary arts, child development, education); Marketing (merchandising, entrepreneurship); and Technology Education (electronics, engineering).

Career and technical education is offered in middle school, high schools, two-year community and technical colleges and other postsecondary schools. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 18 of the 20 fastest growing occupations within the next decade will require career and technical education.

RESOLUTION
NAMING THE CAFETERIA AT GARFIELD ELEMENTARY SCHOOL
“THE PAUL KNOTT CAFETERIA”

WHEREAS, Paul Knott has served Garfield Elementary School as building supervisor and custodian for 23 years, earning numerous custodial accolades, preparing the building for summer programs and the start of school, and maintaining a safe environment for students and staff members; and

WHEREAS, Mr. Knott interacted positively and patiently with all students, using the proper blend of friendliness and authority, rewarding cafeteria table washers with popular stickers, and being constantly aware of and responsive to students’ needs in the school and cafeteria, particularly, special needs students; and

WHEREAS, Mr. Knott eagerly shared his horticultural expertise with staff members and students, which contributed to the beautification of Garfield Elementary School; and

WHEREAS, Mr. Knott willingly volunteered to help before anyone requested his assistance, and the suggestions he provided to help make after-school activities flow more smoothly and his daily presence in the cafeteria during breakfast and lunch helped to instill a sense of respect and responsibility in the students;

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the School Board of Fairfax County, Virginia, honors Paul Knott by the naming the Garfield Elementary School Cafeteria “The Paul Knott Cafeteria" in recognition of his service to the entire Garfield community through the caring way he has maintained the school and served the students during his time as custodian at Garfield Elementary School.

5.04 Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution - Adopt the revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution (FS; presented as new business 1/26/06)
Meeting
Feb 9, 2006 - Regular Meeting No. 13
Category
5. Consent Agenda - 9:30 p.m.
Type
Action (Consent)

Staff Contact: Deirdra McLaughin, Chief Financial Officer, Department of Financial Services

RECOMMENDATION:

    That the School Board adopt the revised Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution.
EXPLANATION:
    The Fairfax County Board of Supervisors approved revisions to the Fairfax County Purchasing Resolution effective November 21, 2005. This year, only administrative changes were made to the document; no actions on the part of the 2005 General Assembly affected the Purchasing Resolution. The most significant changes include:

Identification of consultant services as a stand-alone definition and process, separate from professional services.
          Professional services and consultant services are now defined as separate service categories. Consultant services are defined as, “any type of services required by the County, but not furnished by its own employees, which is so unique that it should be obtained by negotiation on the basis of demonstrated competence and qualification.” In contrast, professional services are a distinct service category under the Virginia Code, requiring professional licensure and a greater focus on qualifications during the selection process. Establishing a separate definition and process for consultant services will not change the existing contract review and approval requirements.

Modification of the process for negotiating a contract with a consultant, establishing consistency with the Virginia Public Procurement Act §2.2-4301 3b.
          Modification of the process for procurement of consultant services changes the dynamic in which the County negotiates with vendors. Until enactment of the 2005 Purchasing Resolution, the County conducted negotiations serially; that is, negotiations were conducted with the top rated offeror and a contract awarded if one could be agreed upon. If the County was unable to negotiate successfully with the top rated offeror, then negotiations were formally terminated and negotiations commenced with the second rated offeror and a contract awarded if one could be agreed upon, and so forth. The new process allows concurrent negotiations providing the County with the ability to negotiate with multiple offerors. Conducting negotiations with multiple offerors positions the County to compare and contrast competing offers simultaneously, tailoring the final agreement to best meet the needs of the County.
