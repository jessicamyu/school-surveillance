Teacher Appreciation Week - May 6-10, 2013

 

Every day, close to 15,000 teachers in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate their personal and professional dedication to providing an excellent education for the children in our community. Our teachers are committed to ensuring that all children develop their full potentials for academic achievement. Teachers are influential in fostering in their students both healthful self-concepts and a sense of civic responsibility. Fairfax County Public Schools values the skills, creativity, and professionalism that characterize our teachers.

 

Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month - May 2013

 

Americans of Asian and Pacific ancestry have made significant contributions to the history and development of the United States in many fields of endeavor such as science, commerce, the arts, and the humanities. The Fairfax County School Board recognizes that an understanding of these contributions should be an integral part of the year-round education of all students.

Since 1992, the President of the United States has proclaimed May to be Asian- and Pacific-American Heritage Month and has chosen this month to pay tribute to the generations of Asian and Pacific Islanders who have enriched American’s history and are instrumental in its future success.

 

Children's Book Week - May 13-19, 2013

 

Children’s Book Week, a national celebration of reading, storytelling, and book-related events, has been observed since 1919 and is the longest-running national literacy initiative in the country.  Established to promote higher standards in children’s books, the week grew to involve librarians, publishers, and booksellers.  Nationwide, author and illustrator events take place each year and the Children’s Choice Book Awards, the only national child-chosen book awards program, are announced during the week.

Recognition of Better Hearing and Speech Month - May 2013

 

Since 1927, the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA) has celebrated Better Hearing and Speech Month every year in May. The theme this year is “Helping People Communicate.” This annual event provides opportunities to raise awareness about communication disorders and to promote services that can improve the quality of life for those who experience problems with speaking and listening. An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families. 

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools provides speech and language services to more than 10,000 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, via base school itinerant services, and in early childhood programs.  FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the speech-language clinicians, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

School Nutrition Employee Week, May 6-10, 2013

 

Caring. Trained. Thoughtful. Professional. Dedicated.  These are just a few words that describe today's school nutrition professional.

 

School nutrition employees in Fairfax County Public Schools demonstrate daily their professional commitment to providing students with nutritious menu choices that reflect current research and meet the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. School nutrition employees must balance many roles and follow numerous federal, state and local regulations to ensure safe and healthy meals are available in schools. They are trained sanitation and food safety experts and must manage financially self-sufficient programs. School nutrition professionals also provide nutrition education to students, as well as healthy catering services to their communities. They use their creativity to make the cafeteria a fun and welcoming place all year long and perform their jobs each day because they care passionately about the children they serve.

 

FCPS school nutrition employees are committed to imparting food and nutrition knowledge and skills to students, using the school cafeteria as the nutrition laboratory, and are influential in preparing students to learn.

RESOLUTION

SUPPORTING THE UNIFIED PREVENTION COALITION OF FAIRFAX COUNTY

AND ITS “PARENTS WHO HOST, LOSE THE MOST” CAMPAIGN

 

 

     WHEREAS, 65 percent of underage youth say they get alcohol from family and friends, even though it is illegal in Virginia for a person under 21 years of age to consume or possess alcohol, and it is illegal for an adult to provide alcohol to a person under 21 years of age; and
 

     WHEREAS, alcohol is the most commonly used illegal drug by Fairfax County students; and underage alcohol use is associated with traffic crashes and fatalities, other drug use, violence and gang involvement, rape, depression and suicide, and educational failure; and
 

     WHEREAS, the 2011-2012 Fairfax County Youth Survey reported that 37.1 percent of high school seniors and 21.1 percent of tenth graders had consumed alcohol in the last 30 days, and that 20.7 percent of seniors had engaged in binge drinking in the last two weeks; and
 

     WHEREAS, the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County, an independent non-profit organization of over 50 collaborative partners, including school officials, county government agencies, concerned residents, students, and community groups, has been instrumental in addressing underage drinking and binge drinking, and is now in the seventh year of a campaign known as “Parents Who Host, Lose the Most: Don’t be a party to teenage drinking,” to educate parents and to increase awareness of and compliance with Virginia underage drinking laws; and

 

     WHEREAS, prevention efforts, including parents talking with their teens and modeling appropriate behavior, are critical, particularly during prom and graduation season;
 

     NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board supports the Unified Prevention Coalition of Fairfax County and recognizes the month of May 2013 as “Parents Who Host, Lose The Most” month in Fairfax County.

Recommended Action
Adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2013 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $359,110,000 (FTS; presented as new business 4/4/13))

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  April 18, 2013 - Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                    Proposed 2013 School Bond Referendum

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Background:

 

On January 24, 2013, the School Board adopted the FY 2014-2018 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) that identified school facility needs anticipated during the coming five-year period.  Capital improvement programming provides the foundation for cost-effective investment and construction project management of facility needs identified throughout the planning period.  Voter authorization to issue bonds is needed in November 2013 to provide:

 

Construction funds for:

  • 2 new elementary schools
  • 1 elementary school addition
  • 9 elementary school renovations
  • 1 middle school renovation
  • 1 high school renovation

Planning funds for:

  • 1 high school addition
  • 8 elementary school renovations
  • 1 middle school renovation
  • 2 high school renovations

Site Acquisition funds for:

  • 1 new high school

Infrastructure Management funds for:

  • roof replacement
  • HVAC replacement
  • athletic infrastructure
  • asphalt paving
  • security enhancements
  • technology upgrades
  • Americans with Disabilities (ADA) requirements

Key Points:

 

The adopted FY 2014-18 CIP identified substantial enrollment increases over the 5-year planning period and beyond.  These enrollment increases will require FCPS to accommodate more than 15,000 additional students by school year 2017-18, with additional growth expected through the 2021-22 school year when enrollment is projected to exceed 200,000 students.  This bond provides construction, planning, and site acquisition funds for new facilities to help address the expected capacity deficits; in addition it should be noted that renovations continue to include additional capacity to address the student membership growth in many schools.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt the resolution requesting the Board of Supervisors to approve and take appropriate action to place on the ballot for the November 2013 general election, a School Bond Referendum in the amount of $359,110,000.

Staff Contact:          Kim P. Dockery, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services

 

Meeting Category:  April 4, 2013 - Regular Meeting No. 17

 

Subject:                 Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2013-2014

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Related To:             Legal Requirement

 

Key Points: 

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan and Report 2013-2014, as required by VDOE Superintendent’s memo #009-13, dated January 11, 2013.  This report will be used by the Virginia Department of Education in preparing the state plan for special education.  As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-proscribed abbreviated format on a date no later than May 13, 2013.  

 

New this year is a requirement to separate both the portion of the plan for coordinated early intervening services and the mandatory private school set-aside from the FCPS special education portion of the budget.

 

The 2013-2014 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $33,267,068. This amount is equal to the final 2012-2013 award as stated in by Superintendent’s memo #183-12 dated July 27, 2012. Estimated expenditures include cost associated with maintaining a mandatory 15% set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS), and a mandatory private school set-aside of 0.3%.  Section 611, Part B, award will fund a total of 472.6 FTE positions.

 

The 2013-2014 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $787,443. This amount is equal to 2013-2014 award.  These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for preschool-age students.  Estimated expenditures include cost associated with a mandatory private school set-aside of 0.8%. Section 619 funds seven FTE positions.

 

The amounts above reflect no change from the 2012-2013 award.  The 2013-2014 application reflects funding of the same programs funded in the 2012-2013 application.  Small adjustments in both sections 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, and the state restricted indirect cost rate.   None of the above represents new or additional operating resources.

 

The Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) reviewed the plan and funding application at the March 13, 2013 meeting.

 

Options:                     

  1. Approve the plan and application as detailed in the agenda item.
  2. Amend or reject the plan and application and provide direction to staff for further action.

Apr 18, 2013 - Regular Meeting No. 18
Category
5. New Business
Type
Information

[Posted 4/18/13]

 

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  April 18, 2013 – Regular Meeting No. 18

 

Subject:                  Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments and School Closings

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Background:

 

At the December 13, 2012, work session, the Facilities Planning Advisory Council (FPAC) recommended that FCPS Policy 8130.6 be amended in order to provide for a simplified and expedited process for boundary changes affecting between 5% and 15% of student populations at sending and receiving schools. The genesis for the recommendation was based on a desire to make small boundary adjustments to address boundary anomalies that may no longer be efficient or appropriate; balance enrollments; address changing conditions with respect to transportation efficiencies; and to improve pyramid alignments and small split feeders.  The current policy allows for an administrative change provided that no more than 5% of the enrollment of the sending and receiving schools is impacted.  Typically, an administrative change is used for new development and unoccupied housing.  In recent years, the administrative process has been used with more frequency for small boundary adjustments within established neighborhoods.  Under the administrative policy for enrollment change, the limitation is small (5%).  More extensive adjustments require a full boundary study which has evolved over the last several years to include a scope of study period of three to four months (or more), extensive communication efforts with web page development and monitoring, FAQs, multiple community engagement meetings, and the requisite School Board meetings for new business, public hearing(s), work session, and action.  Limited staff resources and time is an obstacle to more quickly addressing smaller single option boundary changes, many of which are sought by communities and School Board members to improve efficiency.  The School Board directed staff to prepare revisions to Policy 8130.6 for review and consideration of a boundary ‘lite’ or expedited process.  

 

Key Points:

 

The proposed revision adds a policy option for an expedited boundary process, effecting less than 15% of affected schools, as permitted by Code of Virginia Sections 22.1-79 (4) and (8).  The proposed policy revision allows the Superintendent to implement such changes, similar to an administrative adjustment.  However, unlike an administrative process, a vote of approval by the School Board would be required; and unlike a standard boundary study, a public hearing would not be required. 

 

Should the School Board adopt this policy revision, the staff regulations for implementing boundary adjustments would also be revised to parallel the three–tier policy options for boundary adjustments, retaining the requirements for meetings at sending and receiving schools, among others. Other minor revisions are incorporated for flow and clarity and to distinguish policy from process (implementation regulations).  Staff believes that the proposed policy revisions are in keeping with the applicable Code of Virginia sections and address the directive to provide for a policy to expedite and implement smaller boundary adjustments in order to achieve greater operation efficiencies.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt Policy 8130.7, Local School Boundaries, Program Assignments, and School Closings, as detailed in the agenda item.

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:
