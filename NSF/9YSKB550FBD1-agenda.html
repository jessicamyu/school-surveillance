The School Board Character Award was initiated by former Student Representative to the School Board Chris Giovarelli and established by the Student Advisory Council in 2001. The Apple Federal Credit Union Education Foundation has generously funded the $500 award since 2007.  The award is given to a junior or senior who demonstrates a continuous record of high morals, strong integrity, and good character, and who is recognized as a role model for students in Fairfax County Public Schools in day-to-day behavior. 

 

The recipient of the 2016 School Board Character award is Abigail Conde, a senior at Stuart High School.

 

Abby embodies all of the traits showcased by this award and demonstrates them daily through her words and actions.  She is a positive role model for her peers, leading with integrity and honesty.  Abby is sincerely interested in others.  With her heartfelt and upbeat attitude, she easily establishes a rapport and builds lasting friendships and is known for putting others’ needs ahead of her own.  As one of her nominators said, “She is simply filled with goodness and kindness.”

 

Abby is an accomplished student who has maintained perfect school attendance and an excellent academic record.  Inducted into the National Honor Society as a sophomore, she has served in an officer role as a junior and senior, while also volunteering and tutoring within the community.  The path to these accomplishments has not been easy.  Abby’s family has faced multiple challenges that required her to accept responsibilities far beyond her years.  Through her maturity, common sense, and compassion, Abby has been a stalwart support to her mother and brother in difficult times.   

 

In addition to her school and home commitments, Abby is actively involved in the community.  She is currently serving as the SkillsUSA Virginia’s Region I Vice President. Through her affiliation with SkillsUSA, she has been an advocate for career and technical education, including speaking on Capitol Hill. In her church, Abby is one of the top youth leaders and an active participant in multiple ministries for children and youth.  She is a co-founder of CAALM, a student partnership advocating for changing the high school name, and a recipient of the NAACP President’s Award in recognition of her efforts.

 

For Abby, a challenge becomes an obstacle only if you let it, and she refuses to be defeated. She faces and conquers each challenge with determination, an exceptional work ethic, and honesty and sincerity in every action. According to one nominator, “Even when things are not going her way, she is able to see the good in everyone and every situation.” Her high morals, strong integrity, and resilience epitomize the character traits we want to see in all of our students.  Abby has earned and truly deserves the School Board Character Award.

The winner of the 2016 Distinguished School Resource Officer award is Police Officer First Class Darrell Estess, Oakton High School.

Fairfax County Public Schools and the Fairfax County and Fairfax City Police Departments have established a mutually beneficial relationship for more than a decade. One of the hallmarks of this relationship has been the School Resource Officer (SRO) program. SROs help ensure a safe and peaceful learning environment for students, and provide school administrators and staff with the law enforcement resources and expertise necessary to maintain order, prevent truancy, and enforce the rules and codes of student conduct.

A committee was created to establish and select the Distinguished School Resource Officer Award to annually recognize the efforts of outstanding SROs. The 2016 selection committee members were Tom Vaccarello, Director, Office of Safety and Security, Jim McLain, Security Coordinator, Office of Safety and Security, Terry Dade, Region 3 Assistant Superintendent, and Lt. Andy Wehrlen, School Liaison Commander.

Three other School Resource Officers were nominated by their principals. These nominees are stellar examples of the outstanding officers working in our schools. They should be very proud of their nomination.

Police Officer First Class Brett Manthe, Madison High School

Police Officer First Class Kimberly Wright, Robinson Secondary School (Middle School Level)

Police Officer First Class Brad Wrobel, Centreville High School

This evening we are so proud to recognize and congratulate the 2016 National School Board Association Innovation Challenge winning team from our own Lanier Middle School.  The Challenge, which was held at the National School Board Association Annual Conference in Boston, is sponsored by the DLR Group | Sorg who are also with us this evening. 

 

Three middle school teams from across the country were selected to compete in the challenge which blended project-based learning, collaboration and teamwork to solve an assigned global issue.  In our ever changing global economy, STEM careers are some of the fastest growing and highest paying jobs available, but there is a noted shortage of women in that workforce.  The teams were challenged to design a solution to increase female leadership surrounding a specific innovation within a STEM field such that a 30% increase of young women graduating college will pursue careers in that field by 2025.

 

When the team arrived in Boston, their already difficult global issue challenge became a bit more complex when this twist was added: In 2040, the world governing council determines population increases can no longer be supported by Earth’s resources.  Develop a plan to begin a colony on another planet, in one year.  

 

Our Lanier students had a great presentation with compelling ideas while incorporating 21st Century skills.  The first place winning team consisted of four students, Ryan Phillips, Luke Hylton, Melissa Trivett & Angelina Joa and two coaches, Faiza Alam and Sam Tennison.  Congratulations to you for your hard work, creativity and collaboration. 

 

At this time I would like to introduce Nikki Sorg of DLR Group Sorg for the presentation of the award.

Subject: Student and Staff Health and Wellness Policy 5008

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:  FCPS staff will present the revised FCPS Student and Staff Health and Wellness Policy (5008.2). This policy has been developed in close collaboration with the School Health Advisory Committee (SHAC) and is in compliance with Public Law 111.296, Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010. The revised policy serves to promote student, staff and community health and well-being. The components include physical education; physical activity; health education; health services; healthy and safe school environment; school counseling, school psychology and school social work services; health promotion for staff; family and community involvement; food and nutrition (general); nutrition education; promotion and food marketing; and nutrition guidelines that influence student and staff health and wellness.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board adopt Policy 5008, Student Health and Wellness Policy, as detailed in the agenda item.

 

Proposed Board member amendments:

 

Five amendments to the Student and Staff Health and Wellness Policy are proposed by Board Member Elizabeth Schultz. The amendments are detailed below and on the attached draft policy. [Clerk’s note: text proposed to be deleted is shown in strikethrough; text proposed to be added is shown in underline.]

 

Proposed Amendment 1
I move to amend the motion by adding the words respect and collaboration with parents, parental engagement to the policy in Item I. C. as follows:

I. Purpose
C. Encourage positive impacts created through continuous improvement of the interrelationship between the of a school’s environment, respect and collaboration with parents, parental engagement, student health, and learning.  [Schultz]

 

Explanation
The draft policy’s Purpose does not mention parents at all, although there are clear elements of parental involvement throughout the policy. Therefore, I am suggesting the ‘respect and collaboration with parents’ portion which represents the division’s responsibility under the policy. The ‘parental engagement’ identifies that the path to success is not solely dependent upon the division and student but also needs parental engagement.

 

 

Proposed Amendment 2
I move to amend the motion by deleting the word “Additional” and adding the word Appropriate to the policy in Item IV. C. 3. as follows:

IV. Policy C. Health Education
3. Additional Appropriate resources will be provided for health education in schools that have high-risk populations. [Schultz]

 

Explanation
High-risk populations may not necessarily require additional resources, but different resources – i.e., connections with county services, etc. Additional means ‘more’, appropriate could mean more but also could simply be ‘aligned to identified needs’. ‘Appropriate’, thus, is more appropriate in this case.

 


Proposed Amendment 3
I move to amend the motion by deleting the words “Access” and “on available,” and adding the words Assist with information and access to the policy in Item IV. D. 1. as follows:

IV. Policy D. Health Services
1. Access  Assist with information on available and access, or referral, to primary health care and behavioral health care will be made available to all students. [Schultz]

 

Explanation
Based on the conversation from the Board work session on May 31, 2016, it still doesn’t seem the division doesn’t (can’t) ‘provide access’.

 

 

Proposed Amendment 4
I move to amend the motion by deleting Item IV. E. 3. in the policy as follows:

IV. Policy E. Healthy and Safe School Environment
3. Mentorship programs will be encouraged as a way to help students develop strong, safe, and trusted relationships with a respected adult.  [Schultz]

 

Explanation
Recommend deletion as this is not policy-level language. There is no clear intent of what this intends to do: for whom? by whom? which grades? why? when? where?. This sentence would be more appropriately included in the corresponding regulation as detail about how to implement policy work.

 

 

Proposed Amendment 5
I move to amend the motion by adding the words voluntary and with parental consent to the policy in Item IV. F. 4. as follows:

IV. Policy
F. School Counseling, School Psychology Services, and School Social Work Services
4. The use of voluntary student surveys with parental consent and other data sources will be used to inform suicide prevention efforts. [Schultz]

 

Explanation
The addition of this text will provide clarity at a policy level that student participation is voluntary and that the Board is committed to respecting parental involvement.

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Related To:  School Board Policy 5011.3 Authority to Contract

 

                 

Key Points:

 

Funding for this project will be provided from the Fairfax County Infrastructure Replacement and Upgrade Funds. The Office of Facilities Management (OFM) has an urgent requirement to replace the boilers, associated pumps and expansion tanks (collectively, the “Boilers Replacement Project”) at Bren Mar Park Elementary School in order to maintain the reliability of the heating system in the school building. The above-referenced system components have exceeded their useful life. The reliability of the boilers is unpredictable, and the cost of continuing to maintain them can be expected to increase significantly if they remain in use.  Because the Boilers Replacement Project must be completed prior to the 2016-17 heating season, and because of the long lead time for ordering certain items, there is insufficient time to use the traditional design-bid-install methodology.

 

Therefore, through cooperative procurement as well as a joint procurement agreement with the County pursuant to the Code of Virginia and to the Joint Purchasing Resolution, OFM proposes to utilize the contractor, which was awarded the competitively procured, Fairfax County Job Order Contract #4400001417.

 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board award the contract for the Boilers Replacement Project at Bren Mar Park Elementary School to Centennial Contractors Enterprises, Inc., in the amount of $255,095.21, and authorize the Division Superintendent, or the Assistant Superintendent of Facilities and Transportation Services, to execute and administer the contract on behalf of the School Board.

Key Points: 

 

According to the Fairfax County Ordinance, Chapter 3, Section 3-4-3, the ERFC Board of Trustees consist of seven (7) persons, one of whom is referred to as the “individual Trustee.”  The individual Trustee may not be affiliated with or employed by Fairfax County, the Fairfax County School Board, or any union or similar organization which represents teachers or other employees in Fairfax County.  The individual Trustee is chosen by unanimous vote or consent of the other six trustees subject to the approval of the Fairfax County School Board, on the basis of the fact that such individual Trustee possesses competent financial and/or investment counseling skills.

 

At a recent meeting of the ERFC Board of Trustees, Michael Burke was unanimously recommended for appointment as the “individual Trustee.”  Mr. Burke is a Managing Director in the Washington, D.C. Private Client Group of Stifel, a wealth management and investment firm.   If approved by the School Board, Mr. Burke will serve for his fourth year on the ERFC Board.  Mr. Burke meets all of the requirements of the position.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
