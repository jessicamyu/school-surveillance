Veterans Day - November 11, 2021

 

Veterans' Day (formerly Armistice Day), is the anniversary of the Armistice which was signed in the Forest of Compiegne by the Allies and the Germans in 1918, ending World War I, after four years of conflict.

At 5 a.m., on Monday, November 11, 1918, the Germans signed the Armistice, an order was issued for all firing to cease; so, the hostilities of the First World War ended. This day began with the laying down of arms, blowing of whistles, impromptu parades, and closing of places of business. All over the globe there were many demonstrations; no doubt the world had never before witnessed such rejoicing.

 

Veterans' Day is observed annually in the United States in honor of all those, living and dead, who served with the U. S. armed forces.

 

The School Board directs school personnel to provide students with instructional activities that honor veterans and recognize the role of the military in American democracy. Many schools have special events, to which they invite veterans and active-duty military members of the school community to join them.

 

 

American Education Week - November 15-19, 2021

 

American Education Week was established in 1921 by the National Education Association (NEA) to raise public awareness of the importance of public education in America. This year’s theme, Reach. Educate. Inspire - reminds all Americans of the importance of public education and offers the opportunity to celebrate public education and honor those who ensure that every child receives a quality education. As part of the observance, community leaders are invited to serve as educators for a day in order to get a glimpse at a day in the life of a school employee. American Education Week features a special observance on each day of the week, including parents, education support professionals, educators, and substitute educators.

 

 

Geography Awareness Week - November 15-19, 2021

 

Established by presidential proclamation more than 25 years ago, this annual public awareness program encourages citizens young and old to think and learn about the significance of place and how we affect and are affected by it. Too many young Americans are unable to make effective decisions, understand geo-spatial issues, or even recognize their impacts as global citizens. Geography Awareness week is observed in November and promotes what geography is, why it is important, and the relevance of a geographic education in preparing citizens to understand and debate pressing social and environmental issues and problems.

National School Psychology Week, November 8-12, 2021

 

School psychologists are uniquely qualified members of school teams serving students, staff, and families. School psychologists receive specialized advanced graduate preparation that includes coursework and practical experiences relevant to both psychology and education. They apply expertise in mental health, learning, and behavior, to change lives by taking actions to help children and youth succeed academically, socially, behaviorally, and emotionally. In alignment with the FCPS Strategic Plan and Portrait of a Graduate, school psychologists help individuals realize their own abilities, develop the skills they need to cope with the normal stresses of life, work productively and fruitfully, and be valuable contributors in the community.

 

School psychologists help students develop the social, emotional, and life skills necessary to realize personal achievement and growth, and to build resilience and a sense of belonging. Through the delivery of direct services using evidenced-based practices, school psychologists help students build social and emotional competence, and the skills required to actively engage in learning.

 

School psychologists collaborate with school teams to develop universal prevention and intervention practices across a multi-tiered system of supports. These practices foster resilience, increase educators’ understanding of social and emotional development, reduce barriers to learning, and enhance students’ protective factors. School psychologists bring knowledge of data collection and analysis to team discussions, which informs the development of interventions and monitoring of student progress.

 

School psychologists intervene when students experience mental health issues, which may require a thorough risk assessment, intervention through individual or group counseling, and helping to connect families to community resources. They are members of crisis response teams and risk assessment teams, they assess and assist students at risk of harming themselves, and they teach and implement trauma-informed practices.

 

School psychologists consult with school staff and parents to further their understanding of youth mental health and empower them to develop the necessary skills to respond to complex student needs.

 

All children and youth can face problems from time to time related to learning, social relationships, making difficult decisions, or managing emotions such as feeling depressed, anxious, worried, or isolated. School psychologists help students, families, educators, and members of the community understand and resolve both long-term, chronic problems and short-term issues that students may face. They are a highly skilled and ready resource in the effort to ensure that all FCPS children and youth thrive in school, at home, and in life.

Subject:    Contract to George Mason University for the Provision of Course Work to Eligible FCPS Instructional Employees

 

Staff Contact:    Leigh Burden, assistant superintendent, Department of Financial Services

                          Dr. Lisa Williams, chief equity officer

 

Other Staff Present:  Michelle R. Pratt, director, Office of Procurement

                                  Kathleen Walts, executive director, Office of Professional Learning and Family Engagement

Rationale:

 

FCPS continues to seek innovative solutions to recruit, hire, and retain an exceptional workforce. Success markers include having a minimum number of qualified candidates for each position, and the diversity of the qualified applications will match the diversity of our community. The nationwide teacher shortage, exacerbated by multiple contributing factors, has significantly limited FCPS’ ability to recruit and hire special education teachers. FCPS believes that effective educators are essential to student success and identifying and retaining quality teachers surfaced as stakeholder’s highest priority in the 2019 refocus of the strategic plan. This priority, specifically special education teachers, was further underscored during ESSERIII community meetings as a part of the needs assessment process. Taking action to increase the number of qualified candidates is imperative and essential to address acute and continuing critical shortages in special education.

 

Secondly, but no less important, is building a pool of qualified candidates that mirrors the diversity of the community. To a degree, this type of diversity is reflected in current employee groups such as classroom support. Improving access to career growth for these groups builds capacity in underrepresented groups in the teacher workforce and demonstrates the value they bring to the division. The tuition sharing aspect of the Grow Your Own (GYO) initiative relieves financial burdens among the lower paid employees who desire to move into higher paying positions but are held back by the high cost of tuition. Research shows GYO programs are more likely to attract a diverse teaching pool (Muniz, 2018; Carver-Thomas, 2018) and are better able to target recruitment efforts (Texas Comprehensive Center, 2018). The most effective GYO programs offer financial assistance (PESB, 2016).

 

Key Points:

 

The Chief Equity Officer recommends that the School Board award a contract to George Mason University (“GMU”) for the provision of course work to eligible FCPS instructional personnel. GMU will offer courses for FCPS classroom support staff, teachers, and others who aspire to obtain endorsements, certification and/or degrees under the Grow Your Own Pathway and other tuition benefits programs. Tuition Benefits for FCPS employees include tuition sharing, tuition reimbursement as well as full tuition for select courses that serve to qualify individuals for provisional licensure in Special Education.  Each tuition benefit program is referred to herein as the “Program”. Courses eligible for tuition benefits are to be taken in GMU’s College of Education and Human Development (“CEHD”).

 

The Grow Your Own Pathway (GYO) program was established to address critical vacancies in Special Education and is funded through FCPS’s ESSER III plan. Using a 50-50 tuition sharing model, participating employees will cohort to achieve full certification or endorsement in Special Education-General Curriculum or Special Education-Adapted Curriculum. The pathway includes both master’s degree and bachelor’s degree to enhance opportunities for current Classroom Support staff to achieve full licensure in critical shortage areas. By providing an enhanced tuition benefit in a 50-50 tuition sharing model, financial barriers are mitigated and access to qualifying programs is improved. Participating employees are provided enhanced wrap around supports through mentoring and timely, tailored job-embedded professional development. Participants are required to commit to a Professional Development School (PDS) experience during FCPS’ summer Enhanced School Year (ESY), participate in mentoring and other job-embedded professional development, as well as commit to special education positions for a minimum of three additional years with FCPS in return for the benefit.

 

FCPS employees attending graduate programs in GMU’s College of Education and Human Development are afforded educator discounts, with additional discounts available for cohort programs. Those discounts are outlined in FCPS’s Tuition Reimbursement program provides an additional benefit that serves to encourage and support staff who seek opportunities to achieve personal professional learning goals. All contracted employees are eligible for the benefit with priority given to provisionally licensed teachers in critical fill areas such as special education followed by employees seeking degrees in critical shortage areas in education. Employees enrolled in other degree or college credit continuing education are eligible to request reimbursement. Employees may request and receive up to $1400 per year ($700 per term, per course). Reimbursement is not guaranteed due to funding limitations, however, 100% of eligible requests have been fulfilled in FY20 and FY21. GMU cohorts for FCPS employees include, but are not limited to, Education Leadership, and Special Education Specialties in Early Childhood, General Curriculum and Adapted Curriculums. Employees participating in master’s level cohorts may receive additional discounts beyond GMU’s standard educator discount.

 

Finally, GMU will provide flat rate, contract courses for eligible FCPS employees that can be used as qualification for a provisional license in Special Education with the State of Virginia. The goal of this program is to fill urgent vacancies in special education classrooms across Fairfax County Public Schools. FCPS is invoiced for and obligated to pay 100 percent of the “contract course” tuition rate. The program allows up to 150 FCPS staff per year to meet the eligibility requirement for provisional licensure and fill an urgent need in special education classrooms in FCPS. Participants are required to commit to filling an immediate FCPS Special Education position and join a Special Education cohort (at their own cost) to obtain full licensure within three years.

 

Estimated contract expenditures by FCPS over the three (3) year term is a maximum of $1.556 million, or a range between $298,000 and $663,000, per academic year based upon the level of participation in the cohorts and contract courses.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve and award a contract to George Mason University for the provision of course work to eligible FCPS instructional employees effective as of this date through June 30, 2027, and authorize the Division Superintendent or Deputy Superintendent to execute and administer such contract on behalf of the School Board.

Summary/Background:

 

The Department of School Improvement and Supports recommends that the School Board award a contract to the University of Wisconsin for the provision of English language proficiency (ELP) assessments. The University of Wisconsin assessments are mandated by the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) for use by all public schools in Virginia.

 

The total estimated payment to the University of Wisconsin for this assessment for the 2021-2022 school year is expected to be $1.1M; the payment amount is based upon the total number of assessments submitted for scoring in a given school year. Policy 5011 requires that the School Board approve “any contract or contract amendment awarded between the School Board and other governmental entity that exceeds $250,000.” Policy 5011.3 (III)(A)(1).

 

These assessments also comply with federal legislation [Section 3113(b)(2) and Section 1111(b)(7) of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 2015] and related guidance from the VDOE, which requires that all English learners (ELs):

  1. be assessed in English language proficiency upon entry into a Virginia public school; and
  2. be assessed annually in English language proficiency.

 

The assessment suite from the University of Wisconsin, developed by the World-Class Instructional Design and Assessment (WIDA) Consortium, measures an English learner’s proficiency in English reading, writing, speaking, and listening skills for the purpose of establishing the student’s eligibility for English for Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) services and monitoring the student’s progress in acquiring English proficiency.

 

Assessments within the WIDA suite are:

  • WIDA Assessing Comprehension and Communication in English State to State for English Language Learners® (WIDA ACCESS for ELLs), used for annual assessment of most ELs
  • WIDA Alternate ACCESS for ELLs, used for annual assessment of certain ELs with significant cognitive abilities
  • WIDA-ACCESS Placement Test (W-APT), used for entering kindergarten ELs
  • WIDA Screener, used for ELs entering or re-entering FCPS from outside Virginia public schools
  • WIDA Measure of Developing English Language (MODEL), used for ELs entering in second semester of kindergarten or first semester of grade 1

 

The Virginia Board of Education adopted the WIDA ACCESS for ELLs in 2007 and the WIDA Alternate ACCESS for ELLs in 2013 as the state approved ELP annual assessments for EL students.

 

In FCPS, these assessments are used to:

  • Determine students’ ELP level, eligibility for ESOL services, and eligibility for testing accommodations and exemptions;
  • Calculate EL progress as part of the combined rate for English Achievement and English Achievement Gap school quality indicators within the Standards of Accreditation (SOA);
  • Help teachers and families understand students’ current level of English language proficiency and progress toward proficiency along the developmental continuum;
  • Provide teachers with information they can use to plan for and support student learning;
