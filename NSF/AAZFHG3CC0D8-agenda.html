Crossing Guard Appreciation Day - February 8, 2017

 

Crossing Guard Appreciation Day, a statewide event to say thank you to the individuals who help students cross the street safely on their way to and from school, will be celebrated in Virginia on Wednesday, February 8.  Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) will observe the day by honoring the 80 crossing guards who manage 174 school-related crossings every day.  FCPS crossing guards are employees of the Fairfax County Police Department.

 

Schools and communities can plan their own Crossing Guard Appreciation Day activities to recognize the valuable role crossing guards play in ensuring student safety.  The FCPS Safe Routes to School program and the Virginia Department of Transportation Safe Routes to School website offer may ways to show your appreciation to a crossing guard in your neighborhood.

Custodian Appreciation Week - February 20-24, 2017

The School Board recognizes the hard work and unending dedication of the custodians who work for Fairfax County Public Schools. Our custodians are the first employees to arrive in the morning and the last employees to leave at night. By providing clean and safe school environments, the custodians at FCPS have a direct impact on the educational success of our students. Many custodians take additional training leading to certification as professional custodians.  The week of February 20-24 has been named Custodian Appreciation Week to recognize and thank our dedicated custodians whose attention to detail and high standards affect all students, employees, and many members of the community.

                                     Chace Ramey, assistant superintendent, Department of Human Resources

Meeting Category:  February 9, 2017 – Regular Meeting

 

Subject:  ERFC 2001 Plan Modifications                 

 

School Board Action Required:  Action  

 

Related To:  Legal Requirement

 

Key Points:

 

FCPS hired Aon Hewitt, a provider of retirement consulting and actuarial services, to conduct a study which benchmarked FCPS’ retirement benefit levels to surrounding school divisions, Fairfax County and the federal government.  The study also included the adequacy of income replacement at retirement provided by the retirement programs that FCPS offers. 

 

On May 12, 2016, ERFC presented the history of the FCPS retirement programs along with the key provisions of the existing plans.  Aon Hewitt then provided a briefing on the results of its study, illustrating retirement benefit levels for FCPS employees at various age and service combinations with comparisons to the peer group.  Aon Hewitt also presented ERFC options for possible future consideration.  The options presented included:  lowering the interest crediting rate on ERFC member accounts from 5 percent to 4 percent, instituting a minimum retirement age of 55, increasing the averaging period for the final average salary from 3 years to 5 years and changing the cost-of-living adjustment to equal 100 percent of the Consumer Price Index with a cap of 4 percent. 

 

On July 21, 2016, the School Board considered the benefit impact of the options for possible consideration.  At work sessions in September, November and December, 2016, the School Board reviewed the employer cost savings associated with those options for new hires, new hires and non-vested employees and expanding the options to all members except those currently eligible for full service retirement.  On January, 19, 2017, the School Board considered the employer cost savings associated with those same options with increasing the minimum retirement age from age 55 to age 60 for new hires and non-vested employees. The School Board arrived at consensus to move forward with the proposed ERFC changes for new hires and non-vested members with a minimum age requirement of 55.    

 

Recommendation:  That the School Board direct the ERFC Board of Trustees to initiate the process to implement the following ERFC Plan modifications, effective July 1, 2017:

 

All ERFC members:

      1. Lowering the interest crediting rate on ERFC member accounts from 5 percent to 4 percent

 

ERFC 2001 members hired on or after July 1, 2017, and non-vested members as of June 30, 2017:

      2. Instituting a minimum retirement age of 55

      3. Increasing the period for calculating the final average salary from 3 years to 5 years

      4. Changing the cost-of-living adjustment to equal 100 percent of the Consumer Price Index (CPI-U) with a cap of 4.0 percent; and present the ERFC 2001 amended plan documents to the School Board for approval, by June 2017.

6.03 Alternative Accreditation Plan Requests for Key and Kilmer Centers, Bryant and Mountain View Alternative High Schools, and Fairfax Adult High School
Meeting
Feb 9, 2017 - Regular Meeting
Category
6. New Business - 9:45 p.m.
Type
Information

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      Updated 2/8/17                                                                               

 

Staff Contact:  Francisco Durán, Chief Academic Officer

 

Meeting Category:  February 9, 2017 - Regular Meeting

 

Subject:  Alternative Accreditation Plan Requests for Key and Kilmer Centers, Bryant and Mountain View Alternative High Schools, and Fairfax Adult High School

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Related To:  Strategic Plan, Goal 1 Student Success

 

Key Points:

 

Every three years VDOE allows any “Special Purpose School” to submit an alternative accreditation plan.  Five schools in Fairfax County - Key Center, Kilmer Center, Mountain View Alternative High School, Bryant Alternative High School, and Fairfax Adult High School – once again are pursuing the approval of alternative accreditation plans in order to be able to be “Fully Accredited” in 2017-2018, 2018-2019, and 2019-2020.

Because the five schools listed above are classified as “Special Purpose” schools, serving unique populations of students for whom the measures of accreditation are inappropriate, staff in the Office of School Improvement at the Virginia Department of Education recommend that alternative accreditation plans be submitted to the Virginia Board of Education for approval.  This recommendation is based on the Regulations Establishing Standards for Accrediting Public Schools in Virginia, Section 8 VAC 20-131-280.C. The regulation states: 

"Subject to the provisions of 8 VAC 20-131-350, the governing school board of special purpose schools such as those provided for in § 22.1-26 of the Code of Virginia, Governor’s schools, special education schools, alternative schools, or career and technical schools that serve as the student's school of principal enrollment may seek approval of an alternative accreditation plan from the Board of Education. Schools offering alternative education programs and schools with a graduation cohort of 50 or fewer students as defined by the graduation rate formula adopted by the board may request that the board approve an alternative accreditation plan to meet the graduation and completion index benchmark. Special purpose schools with alternative accreditation plans shall be evaluated on standards appropriate to the programs offered in the school and approved by the board prior to August 1 of the school year for which approval is requested. Any student graduating from a special purpose school with a Standard, Advanced Studies, or Modified Standard Diploma must meet the requirements prescribed in 8 VAC 20-131-50."

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the Alternative Accreditation Plan requests for Key Center, Kilmer Center, Mountain View Alternative High School, Bryant Alternative High School, and Fairfax Adult High School for submission to the Virginia Board of Education, as detailed in the agenda item.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
