Autism Acceptance Month Recognition 2022

 

 

 

April is National Autism Acceptance Month, and the fifteenth annual World Autism Day is April 2.  The Autism Society of America recognizes that the prevalence of autism in the United States has risen from 1 in 125 children in 2010, to 1 in 44 in 2021. Autistic students attend many of our schools in Fairfax County. Autism can affect an individual's social interaction and communication skills. It is considered a spectrum because each child is different and is affected in unique ways, with many strengths and challenges. As the saying goes, "if you have met one person with autism, you have met one​ person with autism." National Autism Acceptance Month provides an opportunity for families, friends, and local communities to increase awareness and acceptance of autism and celebrate the unique aspects and talents of all autistic individuals. The Autism Society’s “Celebrate Differences” campaign focuses on providing information and resources for communities to be more aware of autism, promote acceptance, and encourage increased community partnerships with businesses and organizations dedicated to building more inclusive experiences. Many also believe it is time to move from autism acceptance is to promoting neurodiversity, which is the concept that there are many diverse ways of experiencing the world, of thinking and behaving, and these differences are not deficits but part of the diversity of the human experience.

Better Hearing and Speech Month Recognition – May 2022

 

Better Hearing and Speech Month is observed in May annually to increase awareness about communication disorders and hearing health.  An estimated 42 million people have communication disorders and they impact one in ten families.

 

Currently, Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) provides speech and language services to more than 10,000 students.  Audiologists and special education teachers also serve more than 500 deaf and hard of hearing students in class-based programs, via base school itinerant services, and in early childhood programs.  FCPS annually observes and celebrates Better Hearing and Speech Month and encourages all citizens in our school community to recognize the efforts, dedication, and skills of the Speech-Language pathologists, audiologists, and teachers of students who are deaf and hard of hearing.

Children’s Mental Health Awareness Week Recognition – May 1-5, 2022

 

Positive mental health is essential to a child’s healthy development.  Yet over one in five children and youth in the U.S. have a mental health issue, and only half of children and youth with behavioral health issues receive treatment.  This has a profound, immediate, and lasting impact on our community.  The COVID-19 pandemic created additional barriers to accessing treatment and had a wide-ranging impact on the mental health of children and youth.

 

Fairfax County Public Schools is a partner with county agencies and non-profit organizations in the Healthy Minds Fairfax initiative to increase awareness of signs of mental distress and to address barriers to accessing services through the coordination of prevention, early intervention, and treatment services.

 


The School Board is pleased to recognize May 1-5, 2022, as Children’s Mental Health Awareness Week in Fairfax County.  This is an opportunity to celebrate the work of our school division to increase awareness of the connection between mental health and social, emotional, and psychological well-being.

Asian American and Pacific Islander Heritage Month

May 2022

 

WHEREAS, Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders make up 20 percent of the population in Fairfax County according to the US Census Bureau including 35,000 students in Kindergarten through twelfth grade; and,

 

WHEREAS, Asian Americans established their first settlement in 1763 and over time have endured such injustices as the Asian Exclusion Act that prohibited immigration from Asia and the Japanese Internment that established concentration camps that incarcerated Asian U.S. citizens from 1942 until 1946; and,

 

WHEREAS, Asian American and Pacific Islander students and their families reflect the rich and vibrant diversity of cultures, histories, and languages of the many different countries of Asia and the Pacific Islands, and are valued members of our community; and

 

WHEREAS, Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders have made significant contributions in areas of infrastructure, economy, and technology that have played a pivotal role in America’s history; and

 

WHEREAS, Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders have achieved great accomplishments in fields of math, science, education, the arts, and civil rights that benefit past, current and future generations; and

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board joins Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in celebrating May as 2022 Asian American and Pacific Islander Heritage Month, in recognition of the many contributions Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders have made to our society.

Historical Markers Contest Resolution

 

WHEREAS, the Black History Month and American and Asian American Pacific Islander (AAPI) Heritage Month Historical Markers Contests, sponsored by former Governor Northam and former Secretary of Education Atif Qarni, were created to recognize important Black / African and AAPI figures and events in Virginia history, shedding light on important histories and narratives that have previously been excluded from or underrepresented in our curriculum; and

 

WHEREAS, in February of 2020, 85 students from four Fairfax County schools participated in the first Black History Month Historical Marker contest, which was open to all Virginia students in Kindergarten through 12th grade.  Of the 10 new Black History historical markers selected by the Governor’s commission in 2020, five resulted from successful submissions from FCPS students. 55 4th graders from Laurel Ridge Elementary School successfully nominated Barbara Rose Johns for a historical marker that can now be seen in Farmville. One first grader and six 4th graders from Laurel Ridge Elementary successfully nominated Sergeant William H. Carney for a marker that has been erected in Norfolk.  One Falls Church High School student and three 4th graders from Laurel Ridge successfully nominated Katherine Johnson for a marker that will soon be unveiled in Hampton.  One Falls Church High School student nominated “Angelo” for a new historical marker that was installed in Jamestown.  And three Laurel Ridge 4th graders successfully nominated Ona Judge for a new historical marker that was unveiled here in Fairfax County; and

 

WHEREAS, in February of 2021, 15 students in the History Hunters and History Makers Club at Kings Glen Elementary School participated in Governor Northam’s second Black History Historical Markers Contest. Of the five new Virginia historical markers resulting from this contest, three came from this Kings Glen Elementary virtual club. 4 students in 4th, 5th, and 6th grade at Kings Glen successfully nominated Mary Bowser Denman for a marker that will be unveiled in Richmond later this year.  One Kings Glen 4th grader nominated Dr. Edwin Bancroft Henderson for a historical marker that was unveiled in Falls Church last fall.  And three students in 4th and 5th grade nominated Dangerfield and Harriet Newby for a marker that will be unveiled this coming June in Rappahannock County; and

 

WHEREAS, in May of 2021, students from Hunters Woods Elementary School and Cherry Run Elementary School in FCPS participated in Governor Northam’s first Asian American and Pacific Islander Heritage Month Historical Markers Contest.  Of the 5 new Virginia historical markers resulting from this contest, two were nominated by FCPS students. Students from Cherry Run Elementary School nominated “Filipinos in the U.S. Navy” for a marker that will be installed in Virginia Beach. Students from Hunters Woods Elementary successfully petitioned for a roadside marker honoring W.W. Yen in that will be erected in Albemarle County; and

 

WHEREAS, the hard work and dedication of these students, with the guidance and support of teachers, have led to the long-overdue formal recognition of Black and Asian American Pacific Islander historical figures in Fairfax County and around the Commonwealth, celebrating not only Virginia history, but also American history; and

 

WHEREAS, the fact that, of all the nominations from students all around the Commonwealth, exactly half of the 20 new historical markers come from the research and writing of Fairfax County Public Schools students, a particular source of pride for FCPS; and

 

WHEREAS, the curiosity and determination of these students and their teachers to learn about and amplify the untold stories of Virginia’s history helped to inspire Fairfax County’s Board of Supervisors and School Board to establish the Fairfax County Historical Markers Contest; and

 

NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that Fairfax County Public Schools hereby officially recognizes students and educators from Laurel Ridge Elementary School, Kings Glen Elementary School, Hunters Wood Elementary School, Cherry Run Elementary School and Falls Church High School in appreciation for their extraordinary achievement in uncovering and amplifying the histories of underrepresented communities in Fairfax County and across the Commonwealth of Virginia.

Summary/Background (Key Points)

In November 2021, the School Board Governance Committee, in collaboration with FCPS Division Counsel, developed an initial draft of a Trust Policy, to align with the Fairfax County Trust and Confidentiality Policy, with the goal of ensuring FCPS families feel comfortable that their personal information will not be shared with immigration officials. Since that time, the draft policy has been revised and updated through the Governance Committee processes, and has been assigned to the Office of Professional Learning and Family Engagement, to align with Strategic Plan Goal #2-Caring Culture.

Recommendation:

That the Board approves the Trust Policy.

 

Board Member proposed follow-on motion.

1.  I move to direct the Superintendent to implement as much of the School Trust Policy as possible for the 2022 summer school session and conduct all necessary preparations for the policy to be fully implemented for the start of the 2022-2023 school year. (KF/RA)

 

2. I move to direct the Superintendent to:

  • By mid-July August, provide guidelines for the Department of Human Resources to promote compliance with this policy, including investigative procedures and potential disciplinary actions for the unauthorized sharing of personally identifiable information and citizenship or immigration status.
  • By May 10, provide the Board with updated language to the “Rights and Responsibilities of Students” section of the draft SR&R, that states students have an unambiguous right to have their personally identifiable information and citizenship or immigration status protected from unauthorized sharing.
  • Beginning in mid- July August, provide regular written updates to the Board and public regarding noncompliance with this policy, progress on communications to students and families as outlined in this policy to include information about what is and is not covered/protected, and other pertinent information as needed.  Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

The Department of Special Services (DSS) has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan/Part B Flow-Through Application and Report for the 2022-2023 school year, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004 (IDEA), and the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) State Superintendent's Memo #319-21, dated December 3, 2021. This report will be used by VDOE in preparing the state plan for special education. As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-prescribed abbreviated format on a date, no later than May 13, 2022.

 

The 2022-2023 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $37,845,107. This amount projects a 2022-2023 award amount equal to the final IDEA Part B, Section 611 Flow-Through allocation that FCPS received for Federal Fiscal Year (FFY) 2022, as communicated in the VDOE Superintendent’s Memo #198-21 dated July 23, 2021. Estimated Section 611 expenditures include costs associated with the voluntary 15 percent set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS); a mandatory proportionate set-aside, which ensures that FCPS spends a proportionate share of funds on special education services for parentally-placed private school children with disabilities; materials and supplies; and state restricted indirect costs of 1.0 percent. The IDEA Section 611 Part B, award will fund a total of 295.60 FTE positions.

 

The 2022-2023 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $842,851. This amount projects a 2022-2023 award amount equal to the final IDEA Part B, Section 619 Flow-Through allocation that FCPS received for Federal Fiscal Year (FFY) 2022, as communicated in the VDOE Superintendent’s Memo #198-21 dated July 23, 2021.These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for preschool-age students. Estimated expenditures include costs associated with a mandatory proportionate set-aside and state restricted indirect costs of 1.0 percent. The IDEA Section 619 (Preschool), award will fund 6.0 FTE positions.

 

The 2022-2023 application reflects estimated costs for the current programs funded in the 2021-2022 application. Small adjustments in both sections 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, and materials and supplies. The plan projects a state restricted indirect cost rate of 1.0 percent, consistent with the rate for FY 2022.

 

The 2022-2023 Annual Plan was presented to the Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) at the March 9, 2022 meeting.

 

Recommendation: It is recommended that the School Board approve the 2022-2023 Local Special Education Annual Plan/Part B Flow-Through Application and Report, Section 611, Part B grant funding application and Section 619, Part B Preschool grant funding application, as detailed in the agenda item.

Key Points:

 

FCPS previously purchased basal resources for elementary language arts in 2020. Schools are using school-curated texts and the core units and lessons developed by FCPS as well as print and digital works available through various sources. To provide equitable resources for students in elementary Language Arts, Instructional Services began the process for new basal instructional materials as defined in FCPS Regulation 3004 Adoption Basal Instructional Materials in 2020.

 

A timeline of the process for the Language Arts basal resource review can be found in attachment A.  In October 2021, the School Board approved the committee members to review and recommend basal instructional materials for elementary Language Arts curriculum. The Language Arts Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee convened 6 times between February 9, 2022, and March 9, 2022, to review resources for Language Arts that were submitted by vendors in response to an RFP for consideration as basal instructional materials for elementary Language Arts as well as to review the over 300 community review submissions.

 

During the last months of the adoption process, the Virginia legislature approved the Virginia Literacy Act, which requires evidence-based literacy instruction be implemented in all early learning classrooms, including new and amended requirements for instruction and intervention programs.  Staff has been notified that the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) will provide a list of approved resources in the coming months. In order to ensure compliance and maximize FCPS investment, the recommendations before the School Board (Attachment B) are a subset of the recommendations of the Language Arts Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee (Attachment C).

 

In accordance with the Code of Virginia, regulations of the Virginia Board of Education, and Fairfax County Public Schools regulations, the titles listed in Attachment B are recommended for adoption at the division level in conjunction with the Basal Instructional Materials Adoption Committee, the Selection Advisory Committee, and the Technical Advisory Committee.   Additionally, a summary of the committee rationales for the recommended materials and community input received are provided in attachments C and D.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the titles listed for elementary Language Arts as recommended by Staff.

Summary/Background (Key Points): FY 2023 Approved Budget

 

FY 2023 Advertised Budget

On February 24, 2022, the School Board adopted the FY 2023 Advertised Budget. The budget totaled $3.3 billion, a decrease of $78.5 million, or 2.3 percent, over the FY 2022 Approved Budget. For FY 2023, funds are budgeted to prioritize investments to ensure all students receive the rigorous instruction and comprehensive supports they need to thrive.

 

Remaining School Board Meetings to discuss the FY 2023 Approved Budget are as follows:

 

DATE           ACTIVITY

April 28       Approved Budget Presented to School Board

May 5          School Board Holds Public Hearing on Budget

May 10        School Board Conducts Budget Work Session

May 12        School Board Holds Public Hearing on Budget (if needed)

May 26        School Board Adopts Approved Budget

 

County Budget Impact

The County Executive presented the FY 2023 Advertised Budget Plan on February 22, 2022, which included a School Operating Fund transfer increase of $112.6 million, 5.2 percent, as compared to FY 2022. On April 26, the County Board of Supervisors will markup the County Executive’s FY 2023 Advertised Budget Plan, determine the budget package, and the transfer to Fairfax County Public Schools.

 

Impact of State Budget to FCPS

The General Assembly adjourned March 12, 2022 without adopting a state budget.  The General Assembly met briefly on April 4, 2022, to convene a Special Session, for the purpose of finishing the biennial budget but did not act on any legislation and instead went immediately into recess to be recalled at an uncertain future date once budget conferees have come to consensus. The Governor had until April 11, 2022 to sign, propose amendments, or veto legislation from the General Assembly’s Regular Session.  The General Assembly will return on April 27, 2022, in a Reconvened Session to consider the Governor’s actions. 

 

On May 10, 2022, the School Board will conduct a budget work session where the Superintendent will provide recommendations to the budget based on county and state funding levels.
