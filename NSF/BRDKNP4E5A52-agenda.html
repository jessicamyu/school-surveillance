National Social Work Month - March 2021

 

“March is National Social Work Month and School Social Worker week is March 7th -13th. This year’s National Association of Social Workers theme is “Social Workers are Essential”. School Social workers are master degree professionals who are trained to examine and address life’s challenges in a holistic way. They have expertise in many areas including mental health, human development and behavior, trauma and family systems. School social workers are needed now more than ever as we support students and families during the COVID-19 pandemic. They increase student and family engagement by fostering positive relationships and creating hope and opportunity for youth and families in need. School social workers facilitate skill building and strengthen support systems to help students cope, problem solve, overcome barriers and access resources to support distant learning and the return to school buildings.

 

Social workers tackle some of the toughest challenges facing our society. In every city and every community they develop solutions to increase access to both mental health and physical health care, reduce poverty, eliminate injustices and protect vulnerable children from harm. School social workers are essential members of the school multidisciplinary team, working with school faculty and families to provide coordinated interventions and consultation designed to keep students in school and help families access the supports and resources needed to ensure student success. They promote a positive school climate and culture through a trauma-sensitive lens to maximize student learning.

 

School Social workers in Fairfax County Public Schools use their training, experience, and skills to provide services to children, families, and school staff members in an effort to remove personal and environmental obstacles for supporting student academic performance. They are committed to helping every child achieve, ensuring equitable access and opportunity, building relationships to strengthen student and family engagement, and supporting mental health wellness to help students succeed in school and in life.”

National Athletic Training Month - March 2021

 

March is National Athletic Training Month and this year’s theme is “Essential to Health Care,” an appropriate slogan reflecting the important role certified athletic trainers employed by Fairfax County Public Schools play in keeping our students safe. This slogan perhaps carries a bit more weight this year given all that has happened since last March and the important role our Certified Athletic Trainers have played in our efforts to resume athletic opportunities within FCPS.

 

Nationally certified and state licensed medical professionals, FCPS athletic trainers dedicate themselves to promoting the health and safety of our student-athletes before, during and after practices and competitions. While the last year has forced our athletic trainers to adapt to a changing environment, FCPS athletic trainers remain committed to providing the full complement of athletic healthcare services that has defined this group of professionals over the nearly 40 years that FCPS has employed athletic trainers in our high schools. In fact, even with the interruptions in our spring athletic season, over 8700 students received care from an FCPS athletic trainer during the 2019-20 school year, accounting for 67,000 visits to our 25 athletic training facilities.

 

Our ability to resume out-of-season workouts last August and competitive athletics in December has required all involved with our athletic programs to navigate a host of challenges to support our student-athletes. FCPS athletic trainers, in partnership with Directors of Student Activities, Assistant Directors of Student Activities and athletic coaches have been at the forefront of these efforts, developing and implementing a host of new policies and protocols designed to keep students safe.

 

FCPS athletic trainers consistently go above and beyond to support our athletic programs and without question are essential members of each school’s activities program. Even when faced with unprecedented circumstances, FCPS athletic trainers play a vital role in our overall efforts to support athletic opportunities for FCPS students.

WOMEN’S HISTORY MONTH - MARCH 2021

 

 

WHEREAS, March is Women’s History Month and this year’s theme “Valiant Women of the Vote”, marks the 101st anniversary of women achieving the right to vote.

 

WHEREAS, we honor those who gathered at Seneca Falls in 1848 for the first women’s rights convention, organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott, launching the women’s suffrage movement. The convention’s manifesto “The Declaration of Sentiments,” drew its inspiration from Thomas Jefferson’s Declaration of Independence but added the words “woman” and “women” throughout to call upon “women to fight for their Constitutionally guaranteed right to equality as U.S. citizens.” (history.com)

 

WHEREAS, Susan B. Anthony, an abolitionist, gave many “passionate” speeches against slavery at a time when most “thought it was improper for women to give speeches in public.” Anthony later joined Stanton and became one of the most visible leaders of the women’s suffrage movement, traveling the country and giving speeches in favor of a woman's right to vote. In 1872, Anthony was arrested for voting and fined $100, which helped bring national attention to the issue. Anthony and Stanton spent 50 years together fighting for women’s rights. (womenshistory.org)

 

WHEREAS, Ellen Glasgow, is a native Virginian, a Pulitzer Prize-winning author, and the namesake of Glasgow Middle School. She wrote some 20 novels, many with strong women characters like the women who sought the right to vote. In the early 1900s, she spoke at the first women’s suffrage meeting in Virginia and helped establish the Equal Suffrage League of Virginia. (glasgowms.fcps.edu) (libraryofva.gov)

 

WHEREAS, the Silent Sentinels, organized by Alice Paul and Lucy Burns, picketed outside the White House six days a week from dawn until dusk in the bitter cold, starting in January 1917. They called upon President Woodrow Wilson to support a women’s suffrage amendment to the Constitution. Once the US entered WWI, public opinion regrettably turned against these demonstrations because they were seen as unpatriotic. On Nov 10, 1917, 33 women, including Lucy Burns, were arrested and sentenced to jail at the Occoquan Workhouse in Lorton, Virginia. The night of Nov 14 became known as the Night of Terror. (history.com) (washpost.com)“When word leaked out about this inhumane treatment, it became the turning point in winning the right to vote.”

 

WHEREAS, Women’s History Month began as “Women’s History Week” in Santa Rosa, California in 1978, to coincide with “International Women’s Day” on March 8. The goal of this event was to bring attention to women’s historical achievements. Many communities across the country adopted this special recognition.This movement became so wide spread that in 1987 Congress ultimately designated the month of March as Women’s History Month. (nationalwomenshistoryalliance.org)

 

WHEREAS, the newly created national Turning Point Suffragist Memorial is located in Lorton, VA and pays tribute to the “millions of women who fought more than 7 decades” to win the women’s right to vote.

 

WHEREAS, Kamala Harris made history this year when she was sworn in as Vice President of the United States. She became the first woman, first African-American, and first Asian American to hold this office. Her election coincided with the the 100th anniversary of women achieving the right to vote.


NOW THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board, on behalf of the Fairfax County Commission for Women and all its residents, hereby proclaims March 2021 as Women’s History Month, and a time to remember and honor the countless women who fought relentlessly for the rights of all women in our great Nation.

Summary/Background:

 

The Office of Counseling and College and Career Readiness recommends that the School Board extend the agreement with George Mason University (GMU) for an additional five (5) year period.  The agreement provides first-generation students and families in select Fairfax County Public Schools the opportunity to participate in GMU’s Early Identification Program (EIP).  EIP is a multi-year, regional program supported by George Mason University, and other participating local school divisions. EIP supports FCPS’ goal to close gaps in access, opportunity, and achievement.    

 

The mission of EIP is to improve students’ access to higher education and quality of life by providing academic enrichment, personal development, and community engagement programs that produce global citizens.  Established in 1987 through a grant from the State Council of Higher Education for Virginia, George Mason University and Fairfax County Public Schools partnered to provide college access programming to first-generation college bound students in Fairfax County. For over thirty years, this GMU and FCPS partnership has resulted in 100% high school graduation rates and a 95% postsecondary attendance rate for students who complete the EIP program. Currently, over 700 FCPS students are EIP alumni.   

 

Students are nominated for EIP in the spring of their seventh-grade year. Currently students from the following FCPS Middle Schools: Glasgow, Holmes, Jackson, Lanier, Poe, Sandburg and Whitman, are eligible to participate in the program.  One hundred percent of the students selected are first-generation, in addition many are from single parent homes, qualify for free or reduced meals and come from underrepresented student groups.  Over the course of their five-year commitment to the program, students receive academic mentoring and tutoring, attend three weeks of Summer Academy as well as a variety of Saturday programing that includes: STEM enrichment, Parent Engagement, and College and Career preparation and awareness.  Upon completion of the program each student who graduates with 3.2 GPA will receive a guaranteed acceptance to GMU and students who graduate with a 3.5 GPA can apply for EIP scholarships. 

 

Due to the impact of COVID-19 programing will take place in a virtual environment until it is deemed safe to return to in-person events by both FCPS and GMU.   

 

The total estimated payment to George Mason University for the 2020-2021 school year is approximately $130,000, or $650,000 over the 5 year term; Policy 5011 requires that the School Board approve “any contract or contract amendment awarded between the School Board and other governmental entity that exceeds $250,000.”  Policy 5011.3 (III)(A)(1).

Subject: Policy 2202, Eligibility for Enrollment

 

Staff Contact: Dr. Michelle Boyd, Assistant Superintendent

 

Other Staff Present: Lea Skurpski, Director of Operations and Strategic Planning

Dave Anderson, Coordinator Student Registration

 

Meeting Category: February 18, 2021 – Regular Meeting

 

Ignite Link: Caring Culture, Student Success
 

Summary/Background: In order to support students and families who may be relocating during the COVID-19 emergency, for the remainder of SY 2020-2021, FCPS is requesting a temporary exception to Policy 2202.

Currently, P2202, Section III. I., allows for families becoming residents in another jurisdiction within 60 calendar days of the end of the school year to request to continue attending his or her previously assigned school on a non-tuition bases until the completion of the school year. For SY 2020-2021, this would allow for these requests to be considered as of April 13, 2021. However, in order to support students and families, FCPS is requesting we make a temporary exception to this policy to allow these requests to begin immediately, following School Board approval.

This temporary exception will allow for families who are permanently relocating outside of Fairfax County to request for their child to remain enrolled in their current school of enrollment within FCPS (either virtually or in-person) for the remainder of SY 2020-2021. Per P2202, students would no longer be eligible for enrollment after the end of SY 2020-2021 and will be required to transition to their new district school of residence in SY 2021-2022.

 

Recommended Action: The School Board to review P2202, Section III. I. to consider a temporary exception that will allow for families who are permanently relocating outside of Fairfax County to request for their child to remain enrolled in their current school of enrollment within FCPS (either virtually or in-person) for the remainder of SY 2020-2021.
