Fire Prevention Week - October 3-9, 2010

Fire Prevention Week 2010 focuses on the importance of keeping home smoke alarms in good working condition.  The official theme, Smoke Alarms:  A Sound You Can Live With!, encourages citizens to take the steps necessary to update and maintain their home smoke alarm protection. 

 

During Fire Prevention Week, October 3-9, fire safety educators around the country will stress installing smoke alarms, making sure they are working properly, testing them regularly, and replacing batteries when necessary.  New technologies—including interconnected smoke alarms for large or multi-story homes—are now available for residential use.  When one of these interconnected alarms sounds, they all do. Statistics from the National Fire Prevention Association show that working smoke alarms cut the chance of dying in a fire nearly in half.

Subject:                       Southwestern Boundary Study

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

The purpose of this study is to address overcrowding in a number of elementary schools in the southwestern part of the county.  The boundary study will also address capacity under utilization at several schools.  The proposal to undertake a southwestern boundary study as a solution to this overcrowding is based on community planning efforts which began under the Southwestern Regional Planning Study Committee (SWRPSC), an ad hoc citizen planning committee, in October 2009. 

 

The SWRPSC reaffirmed on-going current and projected overcrowding problems at Eagle View, Colin Powell, Greenbriar East, Centreville, Bull Run, Greenbriar West, and Providence Elementary Schools, all of which have projected building utilizations ranging from 156% to 107%, respectively.  

 

Further, the SWRPSC study noted that the overcrowding solution of building a new school on the current Liberty Middle School campus was not advantageous since that site is not located sufficiently proximate to the areas of severe overcrowding.  Research conducted under the ad hoc planning study revealed limited and constrained options for other elementary school sites. However, the study also indicated that some combination of building additions, program and boundary changes could largely address the overcrowding in the region. (See Executive Summary at Attachment A)

Staff does not recommend that this boundary study include the five schools that were added into the ad hoc study at the recommendation of the SWRPSC.  Staff does not believe those schools—Lees Corner, Navy, Silverbrook, Terra Centre, and Waples Mill—will contribute to solving overcrowding in this region.  Staff notes that none of the potential solutions identified by the ad hoc committee included any involvement of these schools. 

 

While this is an elementary school study, middle and high school changes to accommodate feeder re-alignments may be considered as part of the study provided that the receiving middle and high schools have existing and projected capacity.  In addition, it is possible that the final boundary changes might include moving Advanced Academic Program students from schools within the scope of this study to immediately adjacent Advanced Academic Program centers.   

 

Staff is recommending the following study objectives and parameters to guide the boundary study as supplements to existing School Board Policy 8130:

 

Objectives:     To maximize efficient use of funding for both capital and operating budgets

 

                     To achieve building utilization at all elementary schools within the study area between

                          approximately 95% - 105% of program capacity 

                               

Parameters:  Building additions may be considered at one or more of the following elementary schools:  Bonnie Brae,

                        Cub Run, Fairfax Villa, Fairview, Greenbriar East and Union Mill.*

 

                        No elementary school will receive building additions that take that school’s program capacity above 950

                        students.

 

                        No new schools will be built as part of the overcrowding solution.

 

*These six schools were identified for potential additions as a part of the boundary study for the following reasons:

  • The sites allowed construction of additional square footage per Fairfax County zoning standards.
  • Additions would not make any of these schools physically too large (100,000 square feet was used as the maximum desired size of an elementary school).
  • These sites are located where they might provide solutions to overcrowded schools proximate to them.

 

The objectives and parameters outlined above should be considered concurrently with School Board Policy 8130.5 (attached) which provides factors that may be considered as part of a boundary study.  Specifically, the policy states:

 

The following examples of these factors are not presented in priority order. Any or all of these factors may be relevant in a particular consolidation, redistricting, or assignment plan: the proximity of schools to student residences; projected school enrollment and capacity; walking distances; busing times and costs; walking and busing safety; natural and man-made geographic features; the impact on neighborhoods; school feeder alignments; contiguous school attendance areas; long-range capital plans; the socioeconomic characteristics of school populations; the distribution of programs and resources; the overall impact on families and students; and comparative long-term costs. Adjustments shall be made without respect to magisterial districts or postal addresses and, whenever possible, shall not affect the same occupied dwellings any more often than once in three years. The consideration of these factors and such adjustments shall involve affected communities to the extent reasonable.

Subject:                              Facilities Planning Advisory Council

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

On May 20, 2010, the School Board approved the formation of a Facilities Planning Advisory Council (FPAC) as a new standing committee of the Board.  FPAC will consist of 13 appointed members, one from each Fairfax County magisterial district, three at-large representatives, and one representative from the City of Fairfax. This initiative is a recommendation from the School Board’s Comprehensive Planning Development Committee (CPDC), which had been charged with designing a comprehensive facilities planning process.

 

The establishment of the FPAC will allow its members to advise and inform the School Board and school staff in the development of comprehensive, long-term plans for facilities needs in the most effective and efficient way.  Issues which may be considered by the advisory council shall include, but not be limited to, Capital Improvement Plan prioritization, program based school capacities, size and location of schools, and periodic boundary adjustments.

 

Applications for appointment to FPAC were accepted until August 15, 2010, and approximately 50 applications were received.  Each School Board member was requested to forward a maximum of three preferred candidates to the CPDC for consideration in the development of a slate of candidates representing all magisterial districts, at-large members, and the City of Fairfax.
