National School Nurse Day has recognized the significant role of school nurses in the educational setting since 1972.

National Children’s Mental Health Awareness Day – May 7, 2021

 

Positive mental health is essential to a child’s healthy development. Yet over one in five children and youth in the U.S. have a mental health issue, and only half of children and youth with behavioral health issues receive treatment. This has a profound, immediate, and lasting impact on our community. The COVID-19 pandemic created additional barriers to accessing treatment and had a wide-ranging impact on the mental health of children and youth.

 

Throughout the pandemic, Fairfax County Public Schools invested in school wide programming to support students’ mental health and well-being. School counselors, social workers, and psychologists provided more intensive support to students experiencing mental health challenges. School staff also engaged in extensive and ongoing outreach to families and communities, providing timely information and guidance related to students’ mental wellness, and offering strategies and tools that parents can use to foster mentally healthy youth.

 

In addition, Fairfax County Public Schools is a partner with county agencies and non-profit organizations in the Healthy Minds Fairfax initiative to increase awareness of signs of mental distress, and to address barriers to accessing services through the coordination of prevention, early intervention, and treatment services. This system of care empowers families with the knowledge and resources they need to support their children’s mental wellness and teaches children that caring for their mental health is a vital part of living a healthy, fulfilling life.


The School Board is pleased to recognize May 7, 2021 as National Children’s Mental Health Awareness Day in Fairfax County. This is an opportunity to celebrate the work of our school division to increase awareness of the connection between mental health and social, emotional, and psychological well-being. Our students are contributing to this increased awareness through their work on the Student Advisory Council and through participation in wellness clubs and activities in their schools. Together, we are committed to offering acceptance, respect, and support to all children and their mental health needs.

Other Staff Present: Noel Klimenko, director, PreK-12 Curriculum and Instruction, Instructional Services Department

 

Meeting Category: April 22, 2021- Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required: Action (Consent)

 

Ignite Link: Goal 1: Student Success

 

Key Points :

 

The School Divisions of Innovation (SDI) designation is an initiative from the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) intended to encourage divisions to develop innovative practices that challenge current methods and strategies to support the academic success of all students and educators. Each year, VDOE invites divisions to apply for a SDI designation by submitting their innovation plan. Division applications are reviewed and approved by the Virginia Board of Education and are valid for three years.

 

To apply for SDI designation in 2021, FCPS’ innovation plan seeks to extend and expand the use of authentic assessments, wherein students are engaged in application of knowledge and skills, complex thinking, and problem-solving to create a unique product or response.

 

SDI designation would give added weight to scaling up the FCPS Portrait of a Graduate Presentations of Learning (POG-POL) Goal 1 metric divisionwide. SDI designation would additionally provide FCPS the opportunity to extend temporary waivers that VDOE offered related to performance assessments in 2020-21, leveraging the time and effort division staff (school and central) put into performance assessment practices this year.

 

Recommendation:

That the School Board approve FCPS’ 2021 application for a School Division of Innovation (SDI) designation.

Other Staff Present:

 

Deb Scott, director, Office of Intervention and Prevention Services

John Foster, division counsel, Division Superintendent Office

Liz Noto, principal, Herndon High School

Meeting Category: April 22, 2021- Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Ignite Link: Caring Culture

 

Problem Statement: Regulation 2601, Student Rights and Responsibilities annual review

 

Outcome: Review changes to regulation and receive input from board member.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points): Executive Summary

The presentation of the Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R), Regulation 2601, provides general updates based upon board feedback, state legislative requirements, changes made to ensure alignment with the state model guidance, updates on the district wide behavior referral implementation and proactive supports. The SR&R is aligned with the Virginia Board of Education Model Guidance for Positive and Preventative Code of Student Conduct Policy and Alternatives to Suspension and is reviewed each year and approved by the school board.

 

Significant changes were made to the 2020-21 SR&R. These changes include:

 

  • a hybrid distribution model
  • dress code changes to align with the state Dress Code Equity Act
  • implementation of the new Student Behavior and Administrative Response (SBAR) codes and Leveled Behavior Response Charts
  • the Reflective Considerations for Disciplinary Actions Flowchart for administrators
  • narrowing the mandatory police reporting requirements

 

The total number of behavior incidents entered to date during the 2020-21 school year have been significantly impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, because of this, the impact of the most recent changes to the Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R) is not fully known. The division has less than 3 days of discipline data in comparison to a typical school year. Detailed data points and notes were recently shared with the board and public in a Brabrand Briefing dated March 19, 2021.

 

The changes proposed with regards to the 2021-22 SR&R reflect the motions and further notes from the school board. In addition, a few language changes to note needed updates and definitions. No significant changes are proposed due to the need to implement the prior years’ changes as the division returns to in-person instruction five (5) days per week.

 

Notable changes that have been made this year include:

 

  • language has been edited throughout the document to reflect gender neutrality
  • each section in Chapter 1 has been clearly outlined as a right or a responsibility
  • the Chapter 2 title has been changed to align with the order of the content more clearly in the chapter
  • throughout Chapter 2, language has been expanded to minimize the use of the words inappropriate and disruptive
  • language around the SRO’s primary role (Page 39, II.B.3) in schools has been expanded and examples of safety concerns have been provided
  • the definition of aggravating circumstances has been added to the Suspension of Students in Grades K-3 (page 43, II.C.3) paragraph in Chapter 2 for clarity
  • Title IX language and codes have been moved from the addendum into the main document along with information on retaliation to align with current federal requirements and Regulation 2118

 

No changes were necessary to the SR&R this year based upon the outcomes of the 2021 legislative session. To prepare for the new state data collection requirements and codes, FCPS implemented the Student Behavior and Administrative Response (SBAR) codes this school year and has anecdotally seen the benefit of focusing behavior conversations on the impact of the behavior on the learning environment and addressing it with a leveled response that includes appropriate sanctions and interventions. Per the VDOE, additional codes have been added to the SBAR data collection to reflect Persistently Dangerous behaviors that require a referral to the division superintendent and additional documentation for state reporting. These codes have been added as Category 6, which have the highest impact, in the code charts and require a referral to the Division Superintendent. The new codes will be used across the Commonwealth by all school divisions next school year and will provide consistency and transparency.

 

The focus for the 2021-22 school year, as students return to buildings 5 days per week, is to re-establish Tier 1 classroom routines and expectations while providing proactive social emotional support for our students. The division-wide implementation of the online behavior referral in the fall, paired with teacher and administrator training to support implementation at Tier 1, will provide more accurate data at the classroom, school, region, and division levels. The behavior incidents will populate the MTSS Workflow feature in SIS which will provide a school level dashboard for easy access to data that is updated each night.

 

Recommendation:  That the School Board adopt Regulation 2601.34P, Student Rights and Responsibilities, as detailed in the agenda item.

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

The Department of Special Services, as required by Public Law 108-446, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act of 2004, has completed the Local Special Education Annual Plan/Part B Flow-Through Application and Report for the 2021-2022 school year, as required by the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE) Superintendent’s Memo #314-20,

2021-2022 Special Education Annual Plan/Part B Flow-Through Application, dated December 4, 2020. This report will be used by VDOE in preparing the state plan for special education. As required by VDOE, this report has been prepared and will be submitted entirely online in a VDOE-prescribed abbreviated format on a date, no later than May 14, 2021.

 

The 2021-2022 Section 611, Part B application estimates revenue to Fairfax County Public Schools (FCPS) of $37,414,692. This amount projects a 2021-2022 award amount equal to the final IDEA Part B, Section 611 Flow-Through allocation that FCPS received for Federal Fiscal Year (FFY) 2021, as communicated in the VDOE Superintendent’s Memo #182-20 dated July 17, 2020. Estimated Section 611 expenditures include costs associated with the voluntary 15 percent set-aside for Coordinated Early Intervening Services (CEIS); a mandatory proportionate set-aside, which ensures that FCPS spends a proportionate share of funds on special education services for parentally-placed private school children with disabilities; materials/supplies; and state restricted indirect costs of 1.1 percent. The IDEA Section 611 Part B, award will fund a total of 295.60 FTE positions.

 

The 2021-2022 Section 619, Part B Preschool application estimates revenue to FCPS of $836,008. This amount projects a 2021-2022 award amount equal to the final IDEA Part B, Section 619 Flow-Through allocation that FCPS received for Federal Fiscal Year (FFY) 2021, as communicated in the VDOE Superintendent’s Memo #182-20 dated July 17, 2020. These funds support Child Find activities as well as parenting and transition services for school-age students. Estimated expenditures include costs associated with a mandatory proportionate set-aside and state restricted indirect costs of 1.1 percent. The IDEA Section 619 (Preschool), award will fund 6.0 FTE positions.

 

The 2021-2022 application reflects estimated costs for the current programs funded in the 2020-2021 application. Small adjustments in both sections 611 and 619 have been made for incumbent salaries, employee benefit costs, and materials/supplies. The plan projects a state restricted indirect cost rate of 1.1 percent, which is unchanged from FY 2021.

 

The 2021-2022 Annual Plan was presented to the Fairfax County Advisory Committee for Students with Disabilities (ACSD) at the March 10, 2021 meeting.

 

Recommendation: It is recommended that the School Board approve the 2021-2022 Local Special Education Annual Plan/Part B Flow-Through Application and Report, Section 611, Part B grant funding application and Section 619, Part B Preschool grant funding application, as detailed in the agenda item.

Subject: Regulation 2601, Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R)

 

Staff Contact: Dr. Michelle Boyd, assistant superintendent, Department of Special Services

                       

Other Staff Present:

 

Deb Scott, director, Office of Intervention and Prevention Services

John Foster, division counsel, Division Superintendent Office

Liz Noto, principal, Herndon High School

Cindy Conley, principal, Irving Middle School

Marie Lemmon, principal, Baileys Upper Elementary

 

Meeting Category: April 22, 2021- Regular Meeting

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Ignite Link: Caring Culture

 

Problem Statement: Regulation 2601, Student Rights and Responsibilities annual review

 

Outcome: Review changes to regulation and receive input from board member.

 

Summary/Background (Key Points): Executive Summary

The presentation of the Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R), Regulation 2601, provides general updates based upon board feedback, state legislative requirements, changes made to ensure alignment with the state model guidance, updates on the district wide behavior referral implementation and proactive supports. The SR&R is aligned with the Virginia Board of Education Model Guidance for Positive and Preventative Code of Student Conduct Policy and Alternatives to Suspension and is reviewed each year and approved by the school board.

 

Significant changes were made to the 2020-21 SR&R. These changes include:

 

  • a hybrid distribution model
  • dress code changes to align with the state Dress Code Equity Act
  • implementation of the new Student Behavior and Administrative Response (SBAR) codes and Leveled Behavior Response Charts
  • the Reflective Considerations for Disciplinary Actions Flowchart for administrators
  • narrowing the mandatory police reporting requirements

 

The total number of behavior incidents entered to date during the 2020-21 school year have been significantly impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, because of this, the impact of the most recent changes to the Student Rights and Responsibilities (SR&R) is not fully known. The division has less than 3 days of discipline data in comparison to a typical school year. Detailed data points and notes were recently shared with the board and public in a Brabrand Briefing dated March 19, 2021.

 

The changes proposed with regards to the 2021-22 SR&R reflect the motions and further notes from the school board. In addition, a few language changes to note needed updates and definitions. No significant changes are proposed due to the need to implement the prior years’ changes as the division returns to in-person instruction five (5) days per week.

 

Notable changes that have been made this year include:

 

  • language has been edited throughout the document to reflect gender neutrality
  • each section in Chapter 1 has been clearly outlined as a right or a responsibility
  • the Chapter 2 title has been changed to align with the order of the content more clearly in the chapter
  • throughout Chapter 2, language has been expanded to minimize the use of the words inappropriate and disruptive
  • language around the SRO’s primary role (Page 39, II.B.3) in schools has been expanded and examples of safety concerns have been provided
  • the definition of aggravating circumstances has been added to the Suspension of Students in Grades K-3 (page 43, II.C.3) paragraph in Chapter 2 for clarity
  • Title IX language and codes have been moved from the addendum into the main document along with information on retaliation to align with current federal requirements and Regulation 2118

 

No changes were necessary to the SR&R this year based upon the outcomes of the 2021 legislative session. To prepare for the new state data collection requirements and codes, FCPS implemented the Student Behavior and Administrative Response (SBAR) codes this school year and has anecdotally seen the benefit of focusing behavior conversations on the impact of the behavior on the learning environment and addressing it with a leveled response that includes appropriate sanctions and interventions. Per the VDOE, additional codes have been added to the SBAR data collection to reflect Persistently Dangerous behaviors that require a referral to the division superintendent and additional documentation for state reporting. These codes have been added as Category 6, which have the highest impact, in the code charts and require a referral to the Division Superintendent. The new codes will be used across the Commonwealth by all school divisions next school year and will provide consistency and transparency.

 

The focus for the 2021-22 school year, as students return to buildings 5 days per week, is to re-establish Tier 1 classroom routines and expectations while providing proactive social emotional support for our students. The division-wide implementation of the online behavior referral in the fall, paired with teacher and administrator training to support implementation at Tier 1, will provide more accurate data at the classroom, school, region, and division levels. The behavior incidents will populate the MTSS Workflow feature in SIS which will provide a school level dashboard for easy access to data that is updated each night.

 

Recommendation:  That the School Board adopt Regulation 2601.34P, Student Rights and Responsibilities, as detailed in the agenda item.
