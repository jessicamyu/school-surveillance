3.04 2018 National Hometown Hero Award
Meeting
Jan 10, 2019 - Regular Meeting
Category
3. Meeting Opening - 7 p.m.
Type

It is my greatest honor to speak tonight on behalf of the School Board in recognition of Coach Darrell General, who has been named as America’s 2018 “Hometown Hero,” a competitive nationwide award sponsored by FloSports live digital sports company. The award recognizes “the finest coaches across the country who have made an impact in their community by changing high-school-age athletes’ lives for the better.” Coach General, who was one of the five finalists, took home $25,000 for winning the award. I can safely say that here is no one more qualified for this award. If I can think of one word to describe Coach General, it is selfless. His care for others is infectious, always prioritizing the success of others before himself. I should say, of course, that he never puts himself first unless he’s winning a marathon.

 

And I should know about the difference that he has made in the lives of countless students. After all, he was my running coach for three years at Marshall. General has coached at Marshall since 2002, now for 16 years, and he has coached for 32 years overall. Don’t let his size deceive you. This man has won two Marine Corps Marathons—in 1995 and 1997—he was the top American finisher at the Boston Marathon in 1990, and he has run in about 35 marathons across the country and overseas. And he still runs Marathons to this day.

 

When he was 21, he was the youngest person to qualify for the 1988 Olympic trials, and since then he has qualified for five Olympic trials, including when he was serving as my coach in 2004. He has twice been ranked among the top ten runners in the United States. In 1989, he ran his personal best marathon-time of 2 hours, 14 minutes, and 42 seconds.

 

But again, Darrell’s life has been first and foremost about helping others. He believes that coaching allows him to give back to the sport he picked up as a kid growing up in Southeast D.C. He has said, “I know I was fast enough and strong enough to challenge for an Olympic sport but helping out and coaching was more important to me than trying to be an Olympian.”

 

In 2017, I had the honor of presenting a different recognition to Darrell on behalf of the School Board after he had been named All-Met Girls’ Cross-Country Coach of the Year. In Fall 2016, he led his star runner Heather Holt to the individual state cross country championship and the Marshall girls team to the school’s first state championship in any sport in 40 years, going all the way back to girls’ basketball in 1976. That same year, he also led the boys to a regional title. The Marshall cross-country team has gotten progressively bigger and better under his leadership.

 

When he was my coach, my peers and I had no idea what kind of crazy life he was leading because he would never talk about it. To support his family, he was working three jobs in addition to coaching Marshall students and keeping up his personal training. His daily schedule went like this: he worked at Whole Foods from 1 a.m. to 8 a.m., helped out with a D.C. running group from 8 to 9:30am, worked at Metro Run & Walk from 10 to 3pm, then came to coach at Marshall. After going on his own run, he would sleep maybe 3-4 hours each night. To this day, he still crosses the Potomac, driving a long distance to support our schools and our kids.

 

But Darrell’s service to Fairfax County Public Schools is not limited to coaching. This past fall, a custodial position opened at Marshall High School. Principal Jeff Litz and DSA Joe Swarm asked Darrell if he would be interested in the position. And of course, Darrell answered yes. I think it’s fair to say that he also holds the unofficial designation as the fastest custodian in FCPS.

 

And when the opportunity to recognize Darrell as a hometown hero arose this past year, his students leapt into action—creating promotional campaigns including Twitter and Instagram accounts to secure him the online vote totals to win.

 

He has said that he will use his $25,000 prize to help pay off his bills, and he may eventually throw a party for the Marshall track teams to celebrate. He has assured me that School Board members will also be invited to the party.

 

Congratulations, Coach General, for all you have done for Marshall and Fairfax County Public Schools over the years. Sometimes the little guys do, in fact, win.

Now, before we come together for a photo, I want to recognize the staff of Marshall High School and Darrell’s family members who are here with us. From Marshall we have Principal Jeff Litz, Director of Student Activities Joe Swarm, and Director of Student Services Cindy Blakeley.

RESOLUTION

ON

HUMAN TRAFFICKING AWARENESS MONTH

January 2019

 

 

 

WHEREAS, students in Fairfax County continue to fall victim to the enduring and growing threat of human trafficking, a threat that remains largely under the radar, understood little or not at all by many students and a majority of the community, this widespread complacency endures despite the expanding reach of traffickers around the world and here in our county, with criminals skillfully exploiting the lack of knowledge of students and their families; and

 

WHEREAS, human traffickers continue to seek out students that are vulnerable in county middle and high schools and even elementary schools, a group which includes nearly every student at some point during their academic career, so that victims have been identified from every high school, most middle schools, and multiple elementary schools across the school district; and

 

WHEREAS, these targeted children and young people come from every background and social and economic group, and are pursued in person at all the places that students gather or spend time, including: shopping malls, restaurants, and bus stops, and on-line through virtually every social media application, especially those that mask the user’s identity; and

 

WHEREAS, the trafficking awareness curriculum pioneered by Fairfax County Public Schools, with the support of the Just Ask Prevention Project, remains the “Gold Standard” for building a realistic sense of the danger among students and their families and educating them on warning signs to look for, with other districts across the nation continuing to reach out to learn from the example set here; and

 

WHEREAS, engaged and informed young people are one of the best weapons against teen trafficking—as evidenced by the Just Ask Teen Advisory Board, which is made up of high school students from Fairfax County and across the regions and is empowering the next generation in the fight—and these young people are having a real impact every day through school campaigns, social media outreach, and their support events such as the Youth Cares Conference on February 2, 2019.

 

WHEREAS, working in conjunction with these dedicated young people, educators, parents, medical professionals, law enforcement officials, civic organizations, and the business community are also playing an invaluable role in spreading the word and protecting the community, with a shared goal of establishing a community free from this dire threat to our students and caring for the victims;

 

NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board again joins the county Board of Supervisors in recognizing the month of January 2019 as Human Trafficking Awareness Month in Fairfax County and supports the efforts of all those who are seeking to eradicate human trafficking and end its devastating impact on our students and our community. I so move

Summary/Background (Key Points):

 

Marimae Land, L.L.C. ("Marimae"), the owner of certain land adjacent to Liberty Middle School, proposes to grant certain easements on its land to the School Board, in return for the School Board’s granting a sanitary sewer and temporary construction easement (the “Easements”) on the Liberty Middle School site which are needed in order for Marimae to develop its property. The School Board would formally grant the Easements to the Board of Supervisors, as is customary for sanitary sewer related easements.

 

In exchange for the School Board's grant of the Easements, Marimae has agreed to grant an ingress-egress easement (the "Ingress-Egress Easement") and a temporary construction easement (the "Temporary Construction Easement") to the School Board for the potential future construction of entrance improvements benefitting the Liberty Middle School site.

 

DFTS staff has determined that the Easements will have no negative impact on School Board property and that the Ingress-Egress Easement and the Temporary Construction Easement will benefit Liberty Middle School site and the operation of the school division. All of the above described easements are shown on the plats, attached hereto.

  1. None

 

 

Recommendation:

That the School Board authorize the Division Superintendent and the Assistant Superintendent, Department of Facilities & Transportation Services, (either of whom may act without the other) to take all action necessary and appropriate, in consultation with legal counsel for the school division, to negotiate, execute, grant, and administer such deeds and agreements (as well as any other documents) to grant certain easements on the property of Liberty Middle School to the Fairfax County Board of Supervisors, and to receive in return from the adjoining landowner certain easements for the benefit of Liberty Middle School and the school division.
