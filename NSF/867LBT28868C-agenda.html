Agenda Item:

Update on Assessment and Instructional Coaches

Staff Contact:

Ann Monday, Assistant Superintendent, Instructional Services
Pat Murphy, Assistant Superintendent, Accountability
Ellen Schoetzau, Assistant Superintendent, Cluster II

Background:

In the FY 2006 budget, 24 instructional coach positions and 25 assessment coach positions were approved.

Key Issues:

Instructional coaches are charged with building school and teacher capacity to increase student achievement so identified schools can meet AYP subgroup requirements in language arts and math. Coaching involves activating, supporting, and providing professional development to teachers and teams of teachers in assessing student needs, modeling effective instructional practices, and implementing differentiated instruction.

Assessment coaches coordinate the high school testing program. They access, interpret, and communicate student achievement data; work with counseling staff to identify at-risk students, recommend students to appropriate support/remediation programs, and maintain a system to ensure that students and parents are informed of and prepared for testing opportunities. Assessment coaches collaborate with school staff to plan and implement school-based staff development that addresses instructional needs identified in student achievement data. They are the liaison between their high school and all offices involved with testing. Assessment coaches work with the school testing team to ensure appropriate test-related software is loaded and functioning. In addition, assessment coaches function as team leaders for the school online testing team.

School Board Discussion/Decision:

Information session

Staff Recommendation:

Continue support for assessment and instructional coaches

Options:

None recommended.

Discussion:

Dan Storck's amendment to SR&R: I move to amend Chapter 1, Section H. The Rights of Adult Students and Their Parents, by deleting the third paragraph.
    H. The Rights of Adult Students and Their Parents or Guardians

      With certain exceptions, 18-year-olds are considered adults under the law of Virginia. Those 18-year-olds who wish to act in lieu of their parents or guardian regarding field trips, access to student records by third parties (except those persons who have access without consent), part-time employment, use of motor vehicles, and questioning by police, must sign a declaration. Students who want to exercise this right may do so by following the procedures outlined in the current version of Regulation 2604. Parents or guardians of such students shall be notified of their children's decisions in this regard.

      As provided by federal law, the parent or guardian of a student who is a dependent for tax purposes may have access to the student's record. Further, as required by the Code of Virginia, the parent or guardian will be notified by letter of any instance of the student's suspension from school. School personnel will continue to contact the student's parent or guardian regarding the student's academic performance in school and any suspension from school even though a declaration is filed by the student. Unless otherwise noted, adult students are subject to all rules and regulations of the school.

      The SR&R booklet does not apply in its entirety to students who are older than persons of school age as defined in the Code of Virginia and who are not eligible for special education services pursuant to the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act, or students for whom English is a second language who entered school in Virginia for the first time after reaching their twelfth birthday, and who have not reached 22 years of age on or before August 1 of the school year (collectively adult students). While adult students are expected to comply with the rules of conduct as stated in the SR&R, the hearing and appeal process applicable to them is more limited and is as follows. The determination of whether an adult student has violated one or more rules of conduct, whether the adult student should be disciplined (up to and including expulsion), and whether readmission should be allowed, shall be made by the school principal whose decision in that regard shall be final unless–as to a long-term suspension or expulsion–the adult student appeals in writing within four days to the cluster hearing officer. If an appeal is made by the adult student, the cluster hearing officer may decide the appeal by reviewing only the written record or may elect to hold an additional hearing. The cluster hearing officer’s decision shall be in writing and shall be final. No further appeal will be allowed.

    Response to Questions from School Board

    1. What students “sign” the SR&R?
        Parents of students of school age must sign and return to the school in which the student is enrolled the statement included in the SR&R acknowledging the receipt of the school board’s standards of conduct. (Children of school age, as defined in the Code of Virginia, are those who will have reached their fifth birthday on or before September 30 of the school year and who have not reached twenty years of age on or before August 1 of the school year and who are not eligible for special education services or are not students for whom English is a second language who entered school in Virginia for the first time after reaching their twelfth birthday, and who have not reached 22 years of age on or before August 1 of the school year.) Adult students are those who have reached twenty years of age on or before August 1 of the school year. Adult students attending alternative high schools are required to sign the SR&R because they are attending classes with students who are of school age and must abide by the same rules of conduct. Beginning this school year (2006-2007) school age students as well as their parents will be expected to sign the SR&R.
    2. Who is bound by ALL the SR&R? (e.g., NOT Students in Summer School)
        Students attending regular and alternative school during the regular school year are bound by the rules of conduct set forth in the SR&R. Adult students are expected to comply with the rules of conduct as stated in the SR&R. According to the Code of Virginia, adult students are not required to be afforded the same rights as students of school age. For the past year, the right of appeal of adult students who have been expelled by a principal has been limited to the cluster hearing officer, whose decision is final.
    3. If the SR&R was amended as suggested, what adult students would be affected?
        It would affect only those students who exceed the age of the Code’s definition of a student of school age.
    4. In what substantive ways are those adults different from the other students in our school system?
        Students of school age are entitled to a free public education as required by section 22.1-5 of the Code of Virginia. Adult students do not have such an entitlement.
    5. What additional burden does granting appeal to the School Board impose on principals and central office staff?
        Principals would be required to develop disciplinary expulsion packets as well as devote time to attend hearings at the hearings office. The hearings office would need to hold hearings on the recommendation for expulsion.
    6. How many adult students are expelled for conduct for which the Board usually expels school age children?
        2003-2004: 3
        2004-2005: 3
        2005-now: 0
    7. Are adult GED students required to sign the SR&R?
        No, they are not given the SR&R.
      8. Are adults in adult education programs required to sign the SR&R?
          Adults taking classes through Adult & Community Education ( e.g. heating and air conditioning, wood working, ESOL for adults, cake decorating, computer classes) are not given the SR&R.
      9. Do adult students in alternative high schools sign it?
          Yes, because they are attending school with students whose parents are required to sign the SR&R and must abide by the same rules of conduct. There are 356 adult students enrolled this year.
      10. What’s the maximum age?
          FCPS has no maximum age for which adult students are unable to enroll.
      11. What is the cost of a School Board hearing?

      Attorney fees: $395 per hours; court reporter: average cost, $170 per case

          Agenda Item: School Board Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries

          Staff Contact: Phillip Niedzielski-Eichner, Providence District
          Stuart Gibson, Hunter Mill District

          Background

          The recently approved 2007-2011 Capital Improvement Program anticipates that Fairfax County Public Schools will start classes this fall with overcapacity in its middle and high schools of approximately 1,340 and 2,060 students respectively. While some parts of Fairfax County are realizing decreases in enrollment, pockets of significant student growth continue in other areas of the County. We are responsible for being prudent stewards of taxpayer resources and to make optimal use limited capital resources. This responsibility dictates the need for us to realign existing attendance areas to balance enrollment in relation to capacity for a number of high schools (and possibly for middle and elementary schools).
          The changes proposed to Policy 8310 will provide the School Board with an additional method for conducting a boundary study. The current practice – designated in the proposed language as “the Internal Analysis Method” – appears best suited for a study that is limited in scope, such as for single school that is overcrowded. A limited scope study often involves only one (or sometimes two) magisterial districts. The School Board in this situation looks to the district School Board member for an assessment of the Superintendent’s recommendation and any additional factors that he or she believes should be considered in the final boundary decision.

          The proposed additional method – designated as the “External Analysis Method”-- provides the School Board an alternative approach to setting boundaries when the scope is substantially larger, is inherently more complex, and involves a much broader array of community interests. In this scenario, Board members from multiple magisterial districts will be responsible for representing the interests of their district. The potential for competing interests will be increased significantly. The External Analysis Method offers the School Board an alternative approach to conducting a boundary study if we believe that the community may not trust the results of an internally managed process. The External Analysis Method – by virtue of its transparency and its being conducted by respected, disinterested, and impartial members of the community -- will assure the community that all factors leading to recommendations are known publicly and all variables were considered objectively, without regard to perceived political considerations.

          Finally, the proposed language will clarify that the Internal Analysis Method has two distinct phases, with Phase I being the responsibility of the Superintendent, and Phase II being the responsibility of the School Board. The language makes clear that the Board will apply any additional factors it deems appropriate before making a final decision on the Superintendent’s recommendation. In this, the language suggests that School Board member engagement during Phase I is unnecessary, and perhaps counterproductive.

          Key Issues

          Should the School Board add an additional method – using a Board appointed task force -- for potential use in consolidating schools, redistricting school boundaries, or adopting pupil assignment plans when a regional or county-wide study is required?

          School Board Discussion/Decision
          Proposal to add the following language to Policy 8130, Local School Boundaries.

          “The School Board will designate one of two methods for consolidating schools, redistricting school boundaries, or adopting pupil assignment plans. The first method –The Internal Analysis Method -- requires the Division Superintendent under Phase I of the process to develop a recommendation to the School Board after appropriate, open, and transparent engagement with the potentially affected community. Under this method, the School Board will consider the Superintendent’s recommendation and, as Phase II of the process, consider any other matters the School Board deems appropriate prior to making a final decision.

          Under the second method, The External Analysis Method -- the School Board will appoint a task force made up of respected, disinterested, and impartial members of the community to develop a recommendation to the School Board after appropriate, open, and transparent engagement with the potentially affected community. This method will be considered by the Board when a regional or County-wide study is required. A region for this purpose involves potentially three or more magisterial districts, clusters, or high-school pyramids.

          Under The External Analysis Method, the Superintendent will provide the task force appointed by the School Board with the resources necessary for the effective conduct of its charter. The task force, under Phase I of the process, will develop at least two options for consideration by the School Board. Under this method, the School Board will consider only the options presented to it by the task force and select only one of those options or reject all of them.”
