The Board listens to the community’s issues of concern in Public Comment on its agenda. Sign up online here to speak to the Board of Education.   (will be available 7 am Sunday)  
 

At this time, the Board listens to concerns on topics related to items on the agenda for up to one hour.  If your topic is unrelated to the meeting's agenda please submit written comments to board@jeffco.k12.co.us. We listen with respect and ask those who address the Board to do the same. In order to respect all persons addressing the Board, we request that issues, rather than personalities, be addressed. Any resident, staff member or parent of the district who has completed the requested information on the public comment roster and has the necessary equipment and internet access to join a virtual meeting via Zoom may speak. There is a three minute time limit per individual speaker. Additional guidelines for public comment.  Additional direction will be emailed to those who sign up for public comment (i.e., link to join the board meeting) prior to the meeting.

 

If there are a number of speakers commenting on the same topic, we ask that remarks be consolidated and new thoughts or concepts be shared. It is not the number of people who address the Board that leads to better decision-making, rather the content of your presentation. So, we respectfully ask that you do not repeat the same message of an earlier speaker. If there are four or more people who sign up to address the Board they will have 10 minutes for that group's view to be presented. All those who sign up as a group must be able to accompany the speaker virtually (see additional emailed direction). We respectfully ask that you not repeat the same message, but rather present varying concepts on the same theme.  If more than 20 individuals sign up to speak to the Board, individuals will have two minutes and groups will have five minutes for a collective message to the Board.

The Board will listen to Public Comment for up to one hour if necessary and, by a majority vote, may extend the time for high volume meetings.  Thank you.

 

Hora estimada: 6:15 p.m. (duración estimada: hasta 1 hora)
 

La Junta Directiva escucha los temas de interés de la comunidad en los Comentarios del Público de su agenda. Registrarse en línea aquí para dirigirse a la Junta Directiva de Educación. (estará disponible a las 7 a.m. Sunday)

Por el momento, la Junta atiende inquietudes sobre temas relacionados a items on the agenda hasta por una hora. Si su tema no está relacionado con la agenda de esta reunión, rogamos enviar sus comentarios por escrito a board@jeffco.k12.co.us. Escuchamos con respeto y pedimos lo mismo a los que se dirigen a la Junta Directiva. A fin de respetar a todas las personas que se dirigen a la Junta Directiva, pedimos que se enfoquen en los asuntos más que en las personalidades. Cualquier residente, miembro del personal o padre de familia del distrito que ha completado su información en la lista para Comentarios del público y tiene el equipo e internet necesarios para participar de una reunión virtual mediante Zoom puede hablar. Hay un límite de tres minutos por orador individual. Directrices adicionales para comentarios del público. Se enviarán instrucciones adicionales por correo electrónico a aquellos que se registraron para los Comentarios del público. (ej.: enlace para la participar de la reunión) antes de la reunión.

 

Si hay varios oradores con comentarios sobre el mismo tema, pedimos que se consoliden las observaciones y que se compartan nuevas ideas o conceptos. No es el número de personas que se dirigen a la Junta Directiva lo que guía en la mejor toma de decisiones, sino el contenido de su presentación. Por esta razón, pedimos respetuosamente no repetir el mismo mensaje de un orador anterior. Si hay cuatro personas que como grupo se registraron para dirigirse a la Junta Directiva, ellos tendrán 10 minutos para presentar su punto de vista. Todos aquellos que se registran como grupo deben acompañar de manera virtual al orador (ver instrucciones adicionales enviadas por correo electrónico). Respetuosamente pedimos que no repitan el mismo mensaje, más bien presenten conceptos distintos sobre el mismo tema. Si se registran más de 20 individuos para dirigirse a la Junta, los individuos tendrán dos minutos y los grupos tendrán cinco minutos para dar un mensaje colectivo hacia la Junta.

La Junta escuchará los comentarios del público hasta por una hora si es necesario y, por mayoría de votos, puede extender el tiempo para reuniones de alta participación. Gracias.

 

Rogamos abrir el anexo siguiente el día 11 de agosto después de las 8 a.m., para conocer el orden de oradores invitados a la reunión para el comentario público.

To be informed of the supplemental funds awarded to Jeffco Public Schools for September 2021

PERTINENT FACTS:

In accordance with district policy regulation DD-R, Funding Proposal, Grants and Special Projects, the grant acquisition department shall inform the Superintendent and Board of Education of all grant awards greater than $10,000. Included in notification is background information, amounts awarded, identification of the grant manager, evaluation criteria, and any other pertinent information.  

 

The Board of Education is hereby notified that:

 

  1. The Colorado Department of Education (CDE) released the district’s 2021/22 allocation for the Individuals with Disabilities Act (IDEA) – Part B of $18,402,752. Funds provide services to students with disabilities such as hearing, vision, speech or language impairment; emotional or intellectual disabilities; or autism spectrum disorder. An additional allocation of $617,394 is specifically for identifying, screening and serving preschool children. Services align with Strategic Plan objective Conditions for Learning, Commitment to Equity. Chief Student Success Officer Susan Leach administers the program.
  2. Gifted & Talented (GT) received a 2021/22 allocation of $813,413 from CDE. Funds cover professional development, identification of gifted students, development and support for advanced learning plans, direct services to students, consultants and coaching. Students have the option and choice to attend a GT Center school for intensive programming if they qualify with a body of evidence that suggests this is an appropriate learning environment. For students who do not choose to attend a GT Center school, an Advanced Learning Plan (ALP) is developed to bolster their areas of strength. Services target the Strategic Plan Objective Conditions for Learning, Commitment to Equity. GT Director Roger Dowd supervises the program.
  3. The US Department of Agriculture via CDE awarded $696,753 to Food and Nutrition Services through a noncompetitive process. Funds provide additional revenue for local child nutrition program operators whose revenues declined or were temporarily interrupted during the early months of the pandemic due to COVID-19 related restrictions and closures. Funds are for operational costs related to food service activities. Food and Nutrition Services Executive Director Beth Wallace will manage the funds.
  4. Student Services received $825,164 from CDE’s School Health Professional Grant Program (year two of three years). Eleven elementary schools and three high schools, located in Jefferson, Lakewood, and Wheat Ridge articulation areas will receive education to address, prevent and decrease substance abuse, specifically related to the legalization of marijuana. Students with substance abuse and other behavioral needs will receive coordinated referral care. Funds pay the salary of a Social Emotional Learning Specialist (half time) at each elementary school and a full-time nurse at each high school. The project supports Strategic Plan Objective Readiness for Learning, Social-Emotional Support. Student Services Director Terry Walderman administers the funds.
  5. The CDE Office of Adult Education awarded $345,401 to the district’s Adult English as a Second Language (ESL) and Literacy Program. Funds support English language instruction to prepare adults (ages 18 and older) for employment and further education. Approximately 300 adults participate in year round daytime and evening classes at multiple school sites. Adult classes promote Readiness for Learning by preparing adults to better support their children’s education. Coordinator Karen Caddoo, from the ESL/Dual Language Team, oversees the program.
  6. The Colorado Department of Human Services (CDHS), Office of Refugee Services, provided $173,133 to support English as a Second Language (ESL) instruction for immigrants and refugees with a focus on college and career readiness. Daytime, evening, in-person and online classes are offered in multiple locations. Funds cover instruction, interpretation, and student support. Coordinator Karen Caddoo, from the ESL/Dual Language Team, oversees the program.
  7. Jefferson Jr/Sr High, Lumberg and Stevens Elementary received a 21st Century Community Learning Center grant for $450,000 (year one of a five-year grant). Non-school activities include STEM, cultural and physical-movement activities. 21st Century grants support Strategic Plan Tactic for Readiness for Learning: Schools as Community Hubs. Grant Manager Weston Scott oversees services.
  8. Peak Expeditionary (formerly Pennington) Elementary was awarded a 21st Century Community Learning Center grant of $150,000 (year one of a five-year grant). Services include expanded learning time, enrichment activities and family engagement. Principal Tim Carlin and Grant Manager Erika Williams manage the program.
  9. Arvada K-8 and Thomson Elementary received a $300,000 21st Century Community Learning Center grant (year three). Funds support non-school activities to boost academic achievement; social-emotional health; physical health; and family engagement/education. Grant Manager Weston Scott leads the program.
  10. Alameda International Jr/Sr High School received year 3 of a 21st Century Community Learning Center grant for $150,000. Funded non-school activities include STEM, math courses, 7th and 9th grade transition courses and family enrichment activities. Principal Susie Van Scoyk oversees the program.
  11. Student Services was awarded $180,000 (year one of four years) Counselor Corps grant to Provide counseling support in three schools: Lasley, Slater and Rose Stein. In alignment with the Strategic Plan’s Conditions for Learning, High Expectations, services are designed to positively affect graduation rates and postsecondary matriculation in articulation areas. Director of Student Services Terry Walderman oversees the program.
  12. Student Services received $360,000 (year two of four years) to continue counseling support in four elementary schools: Emory, Deane, Lumberg, and Molholm. Director of Student Services Terry Walderman oversees the program.
  13. Student Services received $360,000 (year three of four years) to continue counseling support in four schools: Arvada K-8, Bear Creek K-8, Foster K-7 and Long View High School. Director of Student Services Terry Walderman oversees the program.
  14. Student Services received $270,000 (year four of four years) to continue counseling support at Belmar, South Lakewood, and Westgate Elementary. Director of Student Services Terry Walderman oversees the program.
  15. Student Services received a year two award of $839,806 from the CDE’s School Health Professional Grant Program. Through this three-year grant, eleven high schools will receive support to address, prevent and decrease substance abuse, specifically related to the legalization of marijuana. Funds pay the salary of a full or part time staff member (e.g., counselor, social worker or psychologist) at each school. The project supports Strategic Plan Objective Readiness for Learning, Social-Emotional Support. Student Services Director Terry Walderman manages the initiative.
  16. Doral Academy received an award of $157,560 from the CDE’s School Health Professional Grant Program (year one of a two-year grant). Funds will cover the salary of a full or part time staff member (e.g., counselor, social worker or psychologist). Principal Arley Blanco will oversee the grant.
  17. CDE awarded a $28,500 Comprehensive Health Education grant to Healthy Schools. Funds will support local comprehensive health education plans to foster healthy behaviors through increasing student’s knowledge of physical, emotional, social and mental health and wellness in addition to modifying risky behavior. The initiative addresses Strategic Plan Objectives Conditions for Learning through support of school-based health and wellness teams, and Readiness for Learning because healthy choices increase student access to learning. Director of Health Services Julie Wilken supervises this work.
  18. CDE awarded a $50,000 Concurrent Enrollment Expansion and Innovation grant. In partnership with Red Rocks Community College, funds will pay RRCC faculty to offer 5-6 courses to Jeffco students. The district will hire a part-time project coordinator. The project supports Strategic Plan Objective Learning, Customized Pathways. Director of Choice Programs Marna Messer will manage the funds.
  19. CDE awarded $198,557 through the Student Re-engagement Grant Program (year three of a three-year grant). Funds will support staff to offer leadership, social emotional and experiential learning activities at Arvada High School. McLain High School will use funds to develop programs for dropout prevention and re-engagement. The program addresses Strategic Plan Objective Readiness for Learning. Director of Student Engagement Dave Kollar supervises the project.
  20. The U.S. Department of Education awarded the Title VI 2021/22 allocation of $82,930 for the Indian Education program. Funds support the mission to meet the educational and culturally related academic needs of eligible students. Services are part of the district’s Commitment to Equity under Strategic Plan Objective Conditions for Learning. Manager of Student Engagement Jason Firestone supervises the program.
  21. The CDHS, Office of Behavioral Health awarded a third Opioid Response Grant to Student Engagement, Indian Education. Funds of $99,009 are for prevention activities focusing on reducing substance abuse and suicide attempts. A Prevention Specialist and Indian Education Liaison implement the program. This initiative aligns with Strategic Plan Objective Readiness for Learning, Social-Emotional Supports. Manager of the Student Engagement Office Jason Firestone supervises the project.
  22. Free Horizon Montessori received $49,323 through the Emerging and Expanding Grant for Early Childhood. The CDHS funds, awarded by Tri-County Early Childhood Council, will pay for costs related to adding a new preschool classroom at Free Horizon. Principal Kresta Vuolo will direct the expansion.
  23. Early Learning accepted $33,800 from Invest in Kids to offer a parent education program called Incredible Years. The program supports parents as they learn skills to promote children’s social competence and reduce behavior problems, aligning with Strategic Plan Objective Readiness for Learning through meaningful family engagement. Director of Early Learning Lisa Schell administers the program.
  24. Home Instruction for Parents of Preschool Youngsters (HIPPY) received a year two award from the Tony Grampsas Youth Services funds, via intermediary organization Parent Possible. The $25,000 grant will support the home-based, peer-delivered, early intervention program that fosters parents’ skills to teach their children. HIPPY aligns with Readiness for Learning, Family and Community Engagement. Coordinator Katherine Harrison from the Early Learning Department manages the program.
  25. HIPPY received an award from the Buell Foundation. The $45,236 grant will support the home-based, peer-delivered, early intervention program for parents and children. Coordinator Katherine Harrison from the Early Learning Department leads the program.

  3. The West Woods (WW) PTSA, in conjunction with the school, is in year 5 of an Outdoor Learning and Playspace Initiative. This initiative has focused on increasing movement and healthy choices in kids by improving and updating the outdoor learning environment and play spaces for students. It includes replacing two play structures, adding a learning garden, adding two outdoor classroom spaces, painting the blacktop with interactive games, and replacing six-eight basketball hoops. The initiative also includes updating the cafeteria with murals of a garden-to-table theme and creating a Healthy Schools student club and Learning Garden club. The purpose for adding the artificial turf is that the area is the only flat play area for students other than the adjacent field. However, during the winter months when the field has snow on it, the kids don’t have any flat space to play. The natural grass and vegetation is muddy and wet and we can’t allow kids on it to play for a large chunk of the winter months. The WW PTSA has done fundraising for the above improvements over the past five years and the artificial turf is the final phase of the initiative. They have had the $50,000 in a separate account for a couple of years but we haven’t been able to find a company that will install the turf. With the help of the district, this will finally be able to complete this project. 
     
  4. A donation of $50,000.00 was given to West Woods Elementary from the West Woods PTSA to be used for purchase and installation of artificial turf in a section of the playground.

Recommended Action
To authorize the director of Purchasing to negotiate and execute an agreement with Jefferson Center for Mental Health for school based counseling and prevention specialist services in an estimated annual amount of $1,300,000.

estimated time: 8:30 p.m. (estimated duration: 5 minutes)

PERTINENT FACTS:

  1. In accordance with Board executive limitation, EL-7, Asset Protection, "the superintendent may not contractually obligate the Board to an expenditure greater than $500,000."
     
  2. Since August 1, 2010, the district has entered into a formal agreement with the Jefferson Center for Mental Health (JCMH) to provide school based mental health and counseling programs to participating schools.  The agreement was in effect for the 2020/21 school year.
     
  3. For the 2021/22 school year, approximately 40 schools will use JCMH services. JCMH is a not-for-profit community mental health center serving Jefferson, Clear Creek, and Gilpin counties since 1958.
     
  4. Jefferson Center for Mental Health also serves to support district mental health crisis response activities.
     
  5. Jefferson Center for Mental Health serves as a district mental health partner and supported services include licensed mental health professionals who provide school-based counseling services to students in the school setting and an avenue to connect students and family members to ongoing clinical mental health services. JCMH licensed mental health providers establish an integrated community model of care that supports consistency for families and schools as well as early interventions related to individual mental health needs. JCMH school-based counselors also provide affective education and have demonstrated positive student growth in achieving personal wellness goals.
     
  6. JCMH school-based counseling programs and prevention programs provide classroom based affective education to promote critical thinking, healthy communication and problem-solving skills. Prevention specialists utilize education in the classroom to improve student’s self-esteem, teach emotional regulation techniques, and help children identify and appropriately express their feelings. This is achieved by using research-based curriculum, practicing the skills in role-plays as well as discussion and problem solving. School-based counseling program provides both counseling services and prevention services. The counseling services include a Center therapist placed at the school who provides family, individual, and group therapy. They provide community information and referral and consultation with teachers and administrators. These counselors also attend parent-teacher conferences, back-to-school night, and other community events. 
     
  7. This year, a Deductible and Co-Pays Pilot Payment Program will be implemented.  JCMH must collect applicable co-pays and deductibles from students and their families receiving Intervention Services, the payment of which may be a financial barrier to students seeking access to mental health support.  To support continued access to the Intervention Services while partnering with JCMH to evaluate the long-term, sustainable funding for the Program, the district has agreed to pay deductibles and co-pays on a pilot basis and subject to certain conditions.  The maximum expenditure for the Pilot is set at $100,000 and will be paid from discretionary 5A funds earmarked for mental health.
     
  8. JCMH provides a series of parent and community nights each year entitled “Helping Kids Thrive” to the Jeffco school community. These conferences provide parents and those who work with youth practical skills and resources to raise healthy kids.
     
  9. The Health Services Department will continue to support this work with a $75,000 contribution from Medicaid grant funds to this means. 
     
  10. The balance of this work, except for the Pilot program, is funded through SBB at the respective schools that utilize the services.
     
  11. Renewal of this agreement is essential in that it affords schools a mechanism for filing gaps in important mental health services.
     
  12. JCMH provides a resource for Jeffco students and their families to receive individual, group and family therapy at school.  This is of substantial benefit to those families who experience hardships with securing and transporting students to therapy appointments with outside providers.  The JCMH providers become an integral part of the school culture and work to bridge school and home environments.
