RESOLUTION CONCERNING THE FUNDING OF PUBLIC EDUCATION IN VIRGINIA

 

FAIRFAX COUNTY SCHOOL BOARD

 

     WHEREAS, the Virginia General Assembly has the Constitutional obligation to “… provide for a system of free public elementary and secondary schools for all children of school age throughout the Commonwealth, and shall seek to ensure that an educational program of high quality is established and continually maintained,” as well as to “…determine the manner in which funds are to be provided for the cost of maintaining an educational program meeting the prescribed standards of quality, and shall provide for the apportionment of the cost of such program between the Commonwealth and the local units of government comprising such school divisions;”  (Constitution of Virginia, Article VIII, Sections 1 and 2) and

 

     WHEREAS, Virginia’s Standards of Quality require the General Assembly to provide for the support of public education as set forth in the Constitution of Virginia, which finds that the “…quality of education is dependent upon the provision of (i) the appropriate working environment, benefits, and salaries necessary to ensure the availability of high-quality instructional personnel; (ii) the appropriate learning environment designed to promote student achievement; (iii) quality instruction that enables each student to become a productive and educated citizen of Virginia and the United States of America; and (iv) the adequate commitment of other resources;” (Code of Virginia 22.1-253.12:1) and

 

     WHEREAS, Virginia’s state aid to public education has failed to keep pace with ever increasing and evolving accountability requirements within Virginia’s Standards of Accreditation and Standards of Learning; with changes in student population and demographics, particularly growth in special needs populations such as students from low income households and students learning English; as well as with new mandates imposed upon school divisions by Virginia and federal policymakers; and

 

     WHEREAS, state direct aid K-12 appropriations have been reduced from 35 percent of the general fund in FY 2009 to below 30 percent in FY 2014 and beyond with the state’s reduction in funding for K-12 occurring through structural policy changes since FY 2009, such as reduced state funds for school “support” personnel and administrative and employee benefits; by systematically underestimating the true overall costs of providing a high quality education; and by using state-operated Lottery proceeds to supplant general fund support for K-12 education instead of serving their original purpose as additional resources for public education; and

 

     WHEREAS, state underfunding has left localities to pay for the increased costs of education required by state mandates to improve public school performance with localities currently paying 56 percent of the state versus local share of public education rather than the 45 percent they should be paying as defined by the Standards of Quality, which has forced localities to budget $3.55 billion in FY2013 above their state required local effort (RLE) to maintain the actual costs of public education; and

 

     WHEREAS, real estate values in Virginia and, therefore, local government revenues continue to be depressed in Virginia - increasing only 2 percent in fiscal year 2013 – with little prospect for even average growth rates in future years without significant local tax rate increases; and

 

     WHEREAS, increased funding for public education is needed to ensure that the state meets its responsibility to provide a high-quality educational system that prepares students for college and careers as a core function of state government and to promote economic development in Virginia, and that without such investments Virginia’s economic competitiveness and ability to attract new business will falter;

    

     NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED that the Fairfax County School Board and its division superintendent, in conjunction with the Virginia School Boards Association and the Virginia Association of School Superintendents, call upon the Virginia General Assembly to immediately increase the state’s share of funding for public education to levels commensurate with the true and fairly calculated costs required to fully implement the requirements envisioned by Virginia’s Standards of Quality, Standards of Accreditation and Standards of Learning, as expected by all of the Commonwealth’s citizens.

Subject:                     FY 2015 Midyear Budget Review

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key Points:

 

In the School Operating Fund, funds available is increasing by $6.1 million.  The FY 2015 beginning balance increased by $2.5 million due to final year-end audit adjustments made after the FY 2014 Final Budget Review. Audit adjustments include an increase of $1.6 million in sales tax revenue based on actual sales tax receipts offset by decreases in other revenue of $0.5 million and expenditure decreases of $1.4 million. In addition to the beginning balance adjustment, other FY 2015 revenue adjustments include a net increase in grant revenue totaling $0.5 million and an increase in projected FY 2015 receipts from sales tax of $3.1 million. 

 

Recommended FY 2015 expenditure adjustments reflect a net decrease of $7.1 million resulting primarily from: a net increase of $0.5 million in grant expenditures, including positions, due to an increase in the federal award for the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA); $0.4 million in audit adjustments for multiyear projects; and savings of $8.0 million resulting from health insurance participation rates in the first six months and calendar year 2015 rates remaining level with calendar year 2014. 

 

Funding of $5.0 million is needed to meet the projections for the FY 2016 beginning balance as detailed in the fiscal forecast presentations shared with the School Board in September and November.  This funding will increase the FY 2016 beginning balance from $22.8 million to $27.8 million as projected.

 

One-time funding of $8.2 million is recommended to be used for accelerations from FY 2016. Funding of $0.8 million will fund an additional 10 teacher positions in the staffing reserve specifically to address class size in FY 2016 and $7.4 million will be used for the required replacement of transportation public safety radios in a joint initiative with the County. By accelerating these expenditures, FCPS is using one-time funding to meet the very high priority need of addressing class size and the required and unavoidable cost of replacing public safety radios while reducing the expenditure requirements for FY 2016 and minimizing the long-term impact on the budget.

 

Position authorization is required for: a 1.0 auditor general to be funded utilizing the placeholder previously approved for additional School Board staffing; a 0.5 specialist funded with the Perkins grant; and 2.0 special education mentor coaches funded with IDEA.

 

Changes to other School Board funds are detailed in the attachment.

 

Recommendation:

Subject:                 Revised Audit Committee Charge

 

School Board Action Required:  Decision

 

Key points:

 

At the October 6, 2014, all-day work session, there was Board consensus to request that the Governance Committee review the charge of the Audit Committee (in the Strategic Governance Manual) and clarify the role of the Audit committee.  Based on recent Board action to create a new position of Auditor General in the Office of Internal Audit, the charge was outdated.  The Governance Committee discussed a draft revision to the charge on October 20, and there was agreement to recommend the revisions to the full Board. 

 

At the November 10, 2014, all-day work session, the Board discussed the revised committee charge, and agreed to schedule Board approval at the December Board meetings.  Both the current and proposed charges are as follows:

 

CURRENT CHARGE:

1.         Audit Committee

Charge:  To oversee the Office of Internal Audit and conduct an annual evaluation of the School Board Auditor; to review the annual audit plan and audit budget prepared by the office and submit its recommendation to the School Board; and to review audit reports and proposals and take action to implement recommendations or forward them to the School Board, as appropriate. The School Board shall have final approval authority in regard to the audit plan, the audit budget, and the implementation of any significant recommendations.

 

PROPOSED CHARGE:

1.         Audit Committee

Charge:  The Audit Committee serves as the Board’s liaison with the Internal Audit Office by:  1) reviewing the annual audit plan prepared by the Internal Audit Office and submitting its recommendation to the School Board for approval; 2) mediating the auditors’ relationship with management; 3) reviewing audit reports and management responses, and reporting to the Board monthly; 4) following up on the implementation of internal audit recommendations; 5) monitoring progress against the approved annual audit plan; 6) acting as a conduit for School Board Member requests for adhoc reviews; 7) periodically reviewing audit-related policies for approval by the Board; and 8) assisting the Board with the evaluation of the Auditor General in accordance with Human Resource guidelines, policies, and regulations. The School Board shall have final approval in regard to the audit plan, the audit budget, and the implementation of any significant recommendations.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the revised charge of the Audit Committee, as detailed in the agenda item, and update the Strategic Governance Manual accordingly.

Staff Contact:         Jeffrey K. Platenberg, assistant superintendent, Facilities and Transportation Services

 

Meeting Category:  December 18, 2014 - Regular Meeting No. 10

 

Subject:                 FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Key Points:

 

The FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program (CIP) provides five-year enrollment projections and capacity calculations, as well as identification of new construction, capacity enhancement, renovation, special program facilities, and site acquisition.

 

The Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program Summary can be found in the Capital Program Recommendations chapter of the Proposed Capital Improvement Program booklet.

 

Discussion:

 

The school system continues to face a challenging situation in which student enrollment is projected to exceed the school system’s ability to accommodate students within existing school buildings. This will necessitate an increase in allocation of capital dollars to build new facilities, provide capacity enhancement projects, and it will require continued use of temporary classrooms, modular additions, and boundary adjustments to accommodate growing enrollment needs.

 

The CIP proposes four new elementary schools. The new elementary school needs are identified as follows: one in the Richmond Highway Corridor (Route 1 area) to relieve overcrowding in the Groveton/Hybla Valley area; one in the Northwest County area in the vicinity of Route 28 and Dulles Airport Access Road; one to relieve overcrowding in the Fairfax/Oakton area; and one on the army base to relieve overcrowding at Fort Belvoir Elementary School which is jointly funded with the Department of Defense. 

 

Funding for additions at South Lakes High School and Westbriar are also identified. Capacity enhancements are proposed at Langley, West Springfield, Herndon and Oakton High Schools as part of their renovations. Furthermore, the CIP, from 2013 bond referendum, includes construction funds for renovation at nine elementary and one middle school, plus a renovation at one high school, as well as planning funds for renovations at eight elementary, one middle, and two high schools. Many of the renovation projects will also include capacity enhancements.

 

Project costs have also been updated in this document to reflect those currently being experienced. The current and anticipated enrollment increases continue to present a major challenge as the school system struggles to provide sufficient capacity in our schools. Providing the additional new schools and capacity enhancements required to accommodate enrollment growth will cause delays in the schedule of many future renovation projects. We anticipate the next bond referendum will be in the fall of 2015. In addition, beginning in FY 2016, the Board of Supervisors approved an additional $13.1 million to offset future infrastructure replacement requirements. 

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program as described in the narrative and in the Superintendent’s Proposed FY 2016-2020 Capital Improvement Program Summary.

Key points:

 

At the School Board’s retreat on July 18-19, 2014, there was a review of the Board’s beliefs, vision, and mission.  There were suggested revisions to the beliefs and mission, and agreement that those revisions would be discussed at a future date.  There were no recommended changes to the vision statement.

 

The Governance Committee reviewed the suggested revisions on November 24, 2014, and at the December 8 work session, the Committee recommended to the full Board that the revisions to the beliefs and mission be approved. 

 

The changes to the beliefs included a new list order of the beliefs; the proposed beliefs and the current beliefs are listed below, with changes marked. One additional revision—to add the word “and ethical” in the last belief—was to be consistent with Board action when the Portrait of a Graduate was approved.

 

BELIEFS (proposed changes)

Listed in new proposed order; former belief # noted

 

Each student is entitled to an excellent education that meets his or her individual needs. (former #1 no changes)

 

A Dynamic and supportive partnerships among students, parents, educators, and the community is are critical to meet student needs and provide enriching experiences. (former #6 with changes)

 

Effective educators are essential to student success.  (former #2 no changes)

 

Families play a fundamental role in their children’s education.  (former #7 no changes)

 

High expectations inspire high performance. (former #9 no changes)

 

Everyone we thrives in a vibrant, healthful, safe, enriching, and respectful environment. (former #3 with changes)

 

Our diversity is a strength that creates resilient, open and innovative global citizens. (former #8 no changes)

 

Quality early childhood education is crucial to school readiness and future success. (former #12 with changes)

 

Literacy is an essential life skill. (former #13 with changes)

 

A well-rounded education enables students to lead productive, fulfilling, creative and culturally rich lives. (former #4 no changes)

 

An educated citizenry enhances everyone’s quality of life, improves sustains our economy, and sustains our system of self-governance. (former #10 with changes)

 

A successful education system develops students who are fosters effective communicators; communication collaborators; creative critical thinkers; thinking, creativity and collaboration global and ethical citizens; and goal-directed, resilient learners. (former #5 with changes)

 

BELIEFS (current version)

  1. Each student is entitled to an excellent education that meets his or her individual needs.
  2. Effective educators are essential to student success.
  3. We thrive in a vibrant, healthful, safe, enriching, and respectful environment.
  4. A well-rounded education enables students to lead productive, fulfilling, creative and culturally rich lives.
  5. A successful education system fosters effective communication, critical thinking, creativity and collaboration.
  6. A dynamic partnership among students, parents, educators and with the community is critical to meet student needs and provide enriching experiences.
  7. Families play a fundamental role in their children’s education.
  8. Our diversity is a strength that creates resilient, open and innovative global citizens.
  9. High expectations inspire high performance.
  10. An educated citizenry sustains our economy and our system of self-governance.
  11. Self-motivation and personal responsibility are keys to future success. (Deleted in new version)
  12. Early childhood education is crucial to school readiness and future success.
  13. Reading proficiency by third grade is critical for the academic success of all students.

The changes to the mission statement included adding the word “healthy”:

 

Fairfax County Public Schools inspires and empowers students to meet high academic standards, lead healthy, ethical lives, and be responsible and innovative global citizens.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the revised beliefs and mission statements, as detailed in the agenda item, and update the Strategic Governance Manual accordingly.

Subject:                    Joint Budget Development Committee Recommendations

 

School Board Action Required:  Information

 

Key Points:

 

The Joint Budget Development Committee (JBDC) has concluded its work and provided both the School Board and the Board of Supervisors with a recommended multi-year framework and suggestions for continued cooperative work on budget issues. 

 

The JBDC recommendations were shared with the School Board at the November 17, 2014, work session.  In addition, the JBDC’s work was discussed at the joint budget work session held with the School Board and Board of Supervisors on November 25, 2014.

 

The JBDC recommendations include the following categories: improve the budget process; identify opportunities for smart savings and smart services; improve financial management; and establish shared vision, priorities, and goals.

 

Recommendation:

 

That the School Board approve the recommendations of the Joint Budget Development Committee, as detailed in the agenda item.

DEBRIEFING FORMATS

Three simple questions:

  1. What did the Board do well in our meeting?
  2. What did not work well for us?
  3. What do we want to do to improve?

Quick Whip Technique--around the room, back, and around again:

  1. What I contributed to the meeting to help us as a board.
  2. What I did that detracted from good board accountability and what I intend to do to not let that happen again.


One member takes the lead:
One board member monitors the meeting and gives feedback to the board. Rotate the responsibility. Other members respond to the leader's assessment.
